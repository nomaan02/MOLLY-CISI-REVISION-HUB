## **Legal Actions** 

**Legal actions** (in a court of law) 

- **Criminal** (State acts as prosecutor) (e.g. ‘R v. Smith’ or ‘R v. Jones plc’) 

   - Proof required: ‘Beyond all reasonable doubt’ 

   - Punishment: Jail and/or fine 

      - ‘Summary conviction’ – magistrates court, used for minor offences 

      - ‘Conviction on indictment’ – Crown court, used for more serious offences 

- **Civil** (usually private – plaintiff/defendant) (e.g. ‘Smith v. Jones plc’) 

   - Proof required: ‘Balance of probability’ 

   - Outcome: Damages (monetary compensation) or other remedy 

- The FCA/PRA have civil enforcement powers for certain financial market offences (e.g. market abuse, under s123 FSMA 2000) 

- The Upper Tribunal (Tax and Chancery Chamber) hears appeals against FCA, PRA or pensions regulator decisions

## **Original Regulatory Structure under FSMA 2000** 

## **(1.1.1) The ‘Tripartite Authorities’** 

## • **Treasury (HMT)** 

- Responsible for the legal framework within which regulation is conducted (including international issues) 

## • **Bank of England (BoE)** 

- Responsible for monetary policy (via Monetary Policy Committee) and payment systems 

- Responsible for maintaining the stability of the financial system as a whole 

## • **Financial Services Authority (FSA)** 

- Responsible for authorisation and day-to-day supervision of ALL regulated firms 

- Responsible for regulating all financial markets

## **Weaknesses of Tripartite System** 

## **Treasury** 

- No clear responsibility for dealing with a crisis which put tens of billions of pounds worth of public funds at risk 

## **Bank of England** 

- Not provided with the tools to carry out their financial stability role effectively 

   - Lack of microprudential viewpoint 

   - Lack of power to intervene into individual banks’ operations 

## **FSA** 

- Expected to deal with issues ranging from the safety and soundness of the largest global investment banks to the customer practices of the smallest highstreet financial advisor 

   - Lack of macroeconomic viewpoint 

   - Lack of ‘fit’ between systemic and consumer protection supervision roles 

   - Sheer volume of information caused information overload and bureaucratic paralysis 

   - FSA became entirely reactive, and far too slow, in dealing with risks

## **Changes to FSA Approach Following the Financial Crisis** 

## **Approach of FSA** 

- End of ‘light touch’ regulation of banks and other large financial institutions 

   - Eliminating the somewhat naïve assumption that larger firms must be safer and better- 

      - run than smaller firms 

- Introduction of Supervisory Enhancement Programme (SEP) 

   - More intrusive (i.e. aggressive and interventionist) supervision of high-impact firms and senior managers 

- Increased focus and concentration on risk 

   - Not just checking the mere presence of systems and controls (‘box-ticking’ approach) 

   - A move to making judgements about the riskiness of business models, aims and strategies

## **Legislative Changes Following the Financial Crisis** 

## **(1.1.13) Banking Act 2009** 

- Introduced Special Resolution Regime (SRR) for dealing with banks in financial difficulty – three ‘resolution mechanisms’ 

   - Sale to private sector buyer 

   - Transfer to subsidiary of Bank of England prior to dismemberment and sale 

   - Temporary nationalisation 

- **(1.1.11) Financial Services Act 2010** 

- Introduced new powers for FSA 

   - New financial stability objective 

   - New powers to oversee remuneration of senior managers (evolved into SYSC 19) 

   - New enforcement powers, and powers to require firms to improve consumer redress

## **Legislative Changes Following the Financial Crisis** 

- **(1.1.14) Financial Services Act 2012** 

- Dismantled the failed ‘tripartite’ system, and replaced it with the ‘twin peaks’ approach, by amending FSMA 2000 (see next slide) 

   - Put the Bank of England back at the centre of financial regulation 

- **(1.1.15) Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013** 

- Introduced Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR) to banks 

   - To enhance individual accountability and raise standards in banking 

- Introduced ‘ring-fencing’ – to protect vital banking services 

- **(1.1.16) Bank of England and Financial Services Act 2016** 

- Made changes to the structure of the Bank of England/PRA 

- Gradually extended SMCR to all other authorised firms

## **Principles of Good Regulation** 

- **(1.1.1) Principles of good regulation** 

In discharging their general functions, the regulators must pay regard to: 

1. Efficient and economic use of their resources 

2. Burden of regulation to be proportionate to expected benefit 

3. Desirability of sustainable growth in UK economy 

4. General principle that consumers should be responsible for their decisions 

5. Responsibilities of firms’ senior management to comply with regulatory 

   - framework 

6. Regulator to recognise differences in nature and objectives of different businesses 

7. Desirability of publishing (or requiring regulated persons to publish) information 

8. Regulatory functions to be exercised as transparently as possible

## **Types of Specified Activity** 

- **(1.1.2) Specified activities** can be divided into: 

- Designated investment business 

   - These are the activities that COBS applies to; for example stockbroking, investment advice, fund management, etc. 

- Other specified activities 

   - e.g. retail banking, mortgages, general insurance, etc. – these activities are covered by other FCA sourcebooks, not COBS 

Also, it is worth noting that there are separate sub-definitions for: 

- **MiFID business** (these are the business activities to which the MiFID rules apply – these are narrower in scope than UK specified activities) 

- **Eligible counterparty (ECP) business** (relevant for determining ‘eligible counterparty’ status under COBS)

## **FSMA Penalties: Authorisation Offences** 

**(1.1.2) Section 23: unauthorised regulated activity (breach of s19 General Prohibition or s21 Financial Promotions restriction)** 

- Summary (magistrates court): six months in jail/£5,000 fine 

- Indictment (crown court): Two years in jail/unlimited fine 

**Section 24: false claim to be authorised/exempt** 

- Six months in jail/£5,000 fine 

**Section 26: civil consequences of breaching s19/s21** 

- Contract unenforceable 

- Money/other property recoverable 

- Compensation for any loss arising 

- Court may allow the agreement to be enforced by customer (if it considers this to be ‘just and equitable’)

## **FSMA Penalties: Breaches of Statutory Duty** 

## **(1.1.5) Section 56: prohibition order** 

- The regulator can issue a Prohibition Order if an individual is not fit and proper 

   - This prevents the subject of the Order from working for, directing, or owning more than 20% of, an authorised firm 

## **Section 59: controlled functions** 

- A firm must ensure that an individual does not perform a controlled function unless they are an approved person 

   - This means the individual has been assessed as ‘fit and proper’ by the Regulator to carry out the relevant senior manager function 

## **Section 71: suing firms for breaching s56 or s59** 

- A private person may sue a firm for breach of s.56 (employing a prohibited individual) or s.59 (allowing an unapproved person to perform a controlled function) 

## **Section 138D: suing firms for breaching rules** 

- A private person may sue a firm for breach of a rule (e.g. COBS rule) if they suffer loss as a result

## **Brexit overview** 

## **(1.1.19) Brexit implications and the Financial Services and Markets Act 2023** 

## • Britain voted to leave the EU in 2016 

- Much financial regulation in UK stemmed from EU law, which continued to apply in the UK until 31 December 2020 

- UK firms lost their passporting rights into the EEA (and vice versa) 

## • Onshoring 

   - EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 drafted all EU Regulations into UK law 

   - Parliament can pass laws to ‘diverge’ from EU regulations over time 

- Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 (amongst other things) 

   - Maintain UK’s position as an open and global financial hub 

   - Harness opportunities of innovative technologies in financial services 

   - New FCA/PRA secondary objectives to boost UK economy’s international competitiveness 

   - Support levelling up, promote financial inclusion and consumer protection
