## **PRA – Objectives** 

## **(2.1.4) General objective of the PRA** 

- To promote the safety and soundness of PRA-authorised persons (PRA firms) 

   - To be advanced primarily by: 

      - Ensuring that the business of PRA firms does not destabilise the system 

      - Minimising any adverse effect on stability caused by the failure of a PRA firm 

      - Protecting the continuity of provision of core services 

   - Key point: this is **not** to be regarded as requiring PRA to ensure that no PRA firm fails! 

## **Secondary objective of the PRA** 

- To facilitate effective competition (this is subordinate to the general objective) 

## **Insurance objective of the PRA** 

- To contribute to securing an appropriate degree of protection for those who are or may become policyholders (applies only to insurance firms)

## **Handbook Provisions** 

**S138 FSMA 2000 gives legal effect to the rules and guidance of the regulators, set out in their handbooks** 

## • Provisions of the handbooks 

- (R) Rules – rules are binding on authorised firms 

   - Contravention of R may lead to enforcement action by the regulator and, in most cases, will give a right of action to private persons under s.138D 

- (E) Evidential provisions – non-binding, but show evidence required to demonstrate compliance with a rule 

   - Compliance with E creates an assumption of compliance with the linked rule 

- (G) Guidance – non-binding, recommends means of compliance or courses of action to take 

   - G is used to explain and throw light on the application of a rule; it is not binding and need not be followed to achieve compliance with the rule 

- (UK) Text from UK law – the FCA provides links to relevant laws, rather than copy and paste large chunks of text

## **Conduct Rules (COCON)** 

## **(2.2.3) Individual Conduct Rules** 

## • Individual (‘First Tier’) Conduct Rules 

1. You must act with integrity 

   - A breach would be to deliberately mislead by act or omission 

2. You must act with due skill, care and diligence 

   - A breach would be to act without full understanding or approval 

3. You must be open and co-operative with the FCA, the PRA and other regulators 

   - A breach would be a failure to inform the FCA of anything of a nature that it would reasonably expect to be informed 

4. You must pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly 

5. You must observe proper standards of market conduct 

   - A breach would be a failure to comply with the Code of Market Conduct 

6. You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers 

## OR 

Where Consumer Duty applies Rule 6 replaces Rule 4

## **A Note on the Approved Persons Regime (APER)** 

## **(2.9.1) Before SMCR regime** 

- Under APER, FCA set out a list of **controlled functions** (significant influence functions and customer functions) for authorised firms and appointed representatives 

   - All staff performing any of these functions had to be approved by the FCA 

   - APER included a list of **Statements of Principle** for approved persons and a **Code of Practice** 

- SMCR has gradually replaced APER. 

   - March 2016: banks, building societies, credit unions and 

      - PRA-designated investment firms (and UK branches of foreign firms) 

   - December 2018: insurers 

   - December 2019: all other (‘solo-regulated’) authorised firms 

- **(2.2.1) And now?** 

- APER still exists, but has been superseded by SMCR for all authorised firms 

   - APER continues to apply to appointed representatives only

## **Consumer Duty and Vulnerable Clients** 

## **(2.14.4) Vulnerable Clients**

## **Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC)** 

## **(2.14.2) Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC)** 

- Principle for Businesses 6 – customer’s interests 

- **N.B. Now only applies to firms outside of scope of Consumer Duty**

## **COBS Applications (COBS 1)** 

## **(2.14.1) General application of the COBS** 

- The Conduct of Business Rules (COBS) apply to firms conducting designated investment business or conducting long-term insurance business in relation to life policies from an establishment maintained by them (or their appointed representatives) in the UK. 

   - Designated investment business is a subset of regulated activities, including MiFID business plus a few other (‘non-MiFID’) activities 

## **(2.14.2) Acting in the client’s best interests** 

- All firms must ensure that they act honestly, fairly and professionally. 

   - Clients’ best interests rule 

   - Since MiFID II, this rule covers **all clients** (i.e. RC, PC and EC) 

## **Exclusion of liability** 

- Firm may **not** seek to exclude its liability to: 

   - FSMA and the FCA rules for **any client** 

   - The general law for **retail clients** (unless honest/fair/professional to do so)

- A legal order instructing the recipient to stop doing something 

   - Can be applied by the civil court upon anybody (whether an authorised firm or not) 

   - If breached, this is contempt of court 

## **Restitution orders** 

- A legal order instructing the recipient to hand over money to compensate for damages caused to victims of recipient’s actions 

   - Can be applied by the civil court upon anybody (whether an authorised firm or not) 

      - If breached, this is contempt of court 

      - Money is paid to regulator, for distribution to victims 

   - Can also be applied by FCA/PRA upon authorised firms 

      - If breached, this is a regulatory offence – firm is no longer ‘fit and proper’ 

      - Money is paid directly from firm to victims 

      - Can be appealed to Upper Tribunal

## **The Cost of Non-Compliance** 

## **(2.18.1) The cost of non-compliance** 

- The FCA has a five-step penalty-setting framework for rule-breaking firms 

   - Removing any profits made from the misconduct (‘disgorgement’) 

   - Setting a figure to reflect the seriousness of the breach 

   - Considering any aggravating and mitigating factors (‘discipline’) 

   - Achieving the appropriate deterrent effect (‘deterrence’) 

   - Applying any settlement discount 

## **s.166 Senior manager attestations** 

- FCA uses attestations to place accountability for firm actions directly at the feet of senior managers 

   - A senior individual puts his or her name to a particular measure, effectively giving an implementation guarantee 

   - A personal commitment by an approved person at a regulated firm that a specific 

      - action has been, or will be, taken

## **Complaints and the FOS** 

## **(2.19.1) Complaints and the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)** 

- Eligible complainants are retail clients who are also: 

   - A consumer 

   - Small- and medium-enterprises (SMEs) with annual turnover less than £6.5 million and either fewer than 50 employees or balance sheet less than £5 million 

   - Charities with annual income of up to £6.5 million 

   - Trustees of trusts with net assets of up to £5 million 

- Firm has eight weeks to resolve complaint 

   - If unresolved, complainant has six months to take matter to FOS for arbitration 

## • FOS compensation 

- Maximum award to complainants: £445,000 (and reasonable costs) 

   - Financial loss, pain and suffering, damage to reputation and distress and inconvenience 

- If the complainant **accepts** the award, it is binding on the firm 

- If the complainant **rejects** the award, they can pursue the matter through the courts

## **Super Complaints and Mass Detriment References** 

- **(2.19.4) Super complaints and mass detriment references** 

- Provisions introduced due to FSA 2012 s43, inserted into FSMA s234 

- Super complaints can be made by consumer bodies directly to the FCA 

   - Significant damage to consumer interests 

   - A recent example is ‘Which?’, complaining about home and travel insurance 

- Mass detriment references can be made by FOS, or by firms themselves 

   - Regular failures by a firm (or firms) to comply with regulatory requirements, resulting in loss or potential loss to consumers 

   - Firm acting in a way in which, if a complaint was to be made to FOS, the FOS is likely to find in favour of the complainant 

   - A recent example is the ‘Motor Finance Redress Scheme’ 

- FCA must respond within 90 days to super-complaints or mass detriment references 

   - Must set out, with reasons, the action the regulator proposes to take

## **Whistleblowing: The Law** 

## **(2.23.1) Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA)** 

- If a worker informs a regulatory authority about a relevant concern arising at their workplace (a ‘ **protected disclosure** ’), their employer may not take action against them in retaliation 

   - Any clause in a contract of employment is void if it precludes a worker from making a protected disclosure 

## **Protected disclosures** 

- : 

- A protected disclosure is one made **in the public interest** 

   - A criminal offence 

   - A failure to comply with any legal obligation 

   - A miscarriage of justice 

   - Endangering an individual’s health and safety 

   - Damage to the environment 

   - Deliberate concealment of any of the above

## **Whistleblowing: Appropriate Procedures** 

- **(2.23.1) Whistleblowing – internal arrangements (under SYSC 18.3)** 

- A firm must establish, implement and maintain appropriate and effective arrangements for the disclosure of reportable concerns by whistleblowers 

- These arrangements must cover: 

   - Confidentiality (if requested) for whistleblowers 

   - Assessment of disclosures and escalation to FCA/PRA, if appropriate 

   - No victimisation of whistleblowers 

   - Feedback for whistleblowers 

   - Preparation and maintenance of records 

   - Annual report to the firm’s board on operation of the system 

   - Training for employees and managers 

   - Employees may disclose direct to FCA/PRA, bypassing the firm

## **Whistleblowing: Other factors** 

## **(2.23.2) Link to fitness and propriety** 

- The FCA would regard as a serious matter any evidence that a firm had acted to the detriment of a whistleblower 

- Such evidence could call into question fitness and propriety: 

   - Firm (Threshold Condition 5: Suitability) 

   - A staff member (status as an approved person or a certification employee) 

## **Whistleblowers’ Champion** 

- Under the Senior Managers Regime, banks and insurers must appoint a director or senior manager as the firm’s Whistleblowers’ Champion 

   - This is an FCA-prescribed senior management responsibility
