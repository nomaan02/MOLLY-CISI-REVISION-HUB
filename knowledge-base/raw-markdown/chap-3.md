## **Insider Dealing** 

## **(3.3.4) Defences to Insider Dealing (s.53 CJA 1993)** 

What are the **general** defences? 

- Dealing offence/encouragement offence: 

   - No advantage was expected (did not expect dealing to result in a profit or avoidance of a loss) due to information possessed 

   - Would have acted like that anyway, even without the info (e.g. a ‘forced sale’) 

   - Reasonable belief that the information had been widely disclosed 

## • Disclosure offence: 

- Did not expect any dealing to take place due to the disclosure 

What are the **special** defences? 

- Market maker (acting in good faith in ordinary course of business) 

- Stabilisation (if done in terms of EU MAR rules) 

- Market information

## **Misleading Statements and Impressions (FSA 2012)** 

## **(3.3.2) Misleading Statements and Impressions** 

- **Misleading statements** (FSA 2012, s.89) 

   - Covers deliberate deceit and recklessness 

   - Covers things said and things not said (concealed) 

   - Intention: Cause investor to alter their position for the worse, i.e. to enter (or not) investment agreement or to exercise (or not) investor rights 

- **Misleading impressions** (FSA 2012, s.90) 

   - Act/conduct (e.g. starting a false rumour) intended to: 

      - Give a false impression about: 

         - Market in investments; or 

         - Price/value of investments 

## • **Criminal penalty** 

- Summary: Six months in jail/£5,000 fine 

- Indictment: Ten years in jail/unlimited fine

## **Definition of Insider Dealing under MAR** 

A person who is in possession of inside information uses that information: 

- To acquire or dispose of financial instruments to which the information relates (whether it is for their own account or for a third party, and whether it is done directly or indirectly) 

- To cancel or amend an order to which the inside information relates, where the order was placed before the person possessed the information 

Such a person in possession of inside information may be any of: 

- Director (or equivalent) of an issuer 

- Shareholder of an issuer 

- Access to the information through employment, profession or duties 

- Access through criminal activities 

- Knowing tippee

## **Definition of Inside Information under MAR** 

Inside information has the following elements: 

- The information is **of a precise nature** (1) 

- It has not been made public 

- It relates directly or indirectly to one or more financial instruments 

- If it were made public, it would be likely to have a **significant effect** (2) on the price of the financial instrument (or of a related derivative) 

## **Notes** 

1. Information is ‘of a precise nature’ when it would enable a specific conclusion to be drawn about its effect on the price 

2. Information would have a ‘significant effect’ on the price if it would be likely to be used by a reasonable investor to help in making a decision

## **Control of Inside Information** 

## **(2.4.1) Disclosure of inside information** 

- Issuers must publish inside information as soon as possible 

- The information must remain on the issuer’s website for at least five years 

## **Delaying disclosure of inside information** 

- Delaying disclosure is permissible if: 

   - Immediate disclosure would prejudice the legitimate interests of the issuer; and 

   - Delayed disclosure is not likely to mislead the public; and 

   - The issuer can ensure the confidentiality of the information 

- The FCA must be notified (and may ask for full written explanation)

## **Insider Lists** 

- **(3.2.4) Insider Lists** 

In outline, issuers and persons acting on behalf of issuers must: 

- Maintain (and keep up to date) a list of persons with access to inside information (distinguishing between permanent and temporary insiders) 

- Ensure that the persons on the list acknowledge, in writing, their duty of confidentiality (and are aware of the sanctions that apply) 

- Remain fully responsible for the list, even where it is maintained by an adviser (e.g. an accountancy or legal firm) 

- Keep the list for at least five years

## **Safe Harbours** 

## **(3.3.1) Safe Harbours** 

This is the term used to describe behaviour which might have a direct or indirect effect on an issuer’s share price, but which would not be subject to sanctions under the market abuse regime.

   - Disclosures in accordance with the UKLA’s Disclosure Guidance and Transparency Rules (DTR) 

   - Disclosures in accordance with the Takeover Code’s rules on disclosure during a takeover bid 

   - Market soundings

## **What is ‘corporate governance’?** 

- The structure of rules, practices and processes used to direct and manage a company

## **(3.4.2) Proceeds of Crime Act 2002** 

- POCA 2002 establishes offences relating to money laundering 

   - Concealment (max. 14 years/unlimited fine) 

      - Conceal, disguise, convert, transfer, or remove criminal property from the UK 

   - Arrangements (max. 14 years/unlimited fine)

## **Terrorist Financing** 

## **(3.4.8) Money laundering and terrorist financing (TF)** 

## • Terrorism 

   - Violence or fear of violence by organised groups, generally aimed at civilians 

   - Aimed at compelling a government to change policies or actions 

   - Faith- or religion-based, ideological, political, ethnicity etc. goals 

- Money used in terrorism 

   - Terrorist funding – collection of funds for terrorist groups 

   - Terrorist financing – movement of money by terrorists to undertake specific operations 

- Difference between money laundering and terrorist financing 

   - Money laundering – taking ‘dirty’ money and making it ‘clean’ 

   - Terrorist financing – taking ‘clean’ money and making it available for ‘dirty’ purposes 

   - Obviously these often overlap, but they are not the same thing 

   - TF typically involves far smaller sums of money than money laundering 

   - TF can be from legitimately obtained income

## **UK Bribery Act 2010** 

## **(3.5.1) Bribery Act 2010: Offences** 

- s1: Bribing another person (‘active bribery’) 

   - Offering, promising or giving a financial or other advantage to another person 

   - Intent to induce or reward the other person for improper performance 

- s2: Receiving bribes (‘passive bribery’) 

   - Requesting or accepting a financial or other advantage from another person 

   - Intent that, in consequence, a relevant function or activity will be performed improperly 

- s6: Bribery of a foreign public official 

   - Intention to influence a foreign public official to obtain or retain business, or an advantage in the conduct of business 

   - Unlike offences defined in sections 1 and 2, there is no requirement to show improper performance 

## **Penalty (maximum):** 10 years in jail and unlimited fine
