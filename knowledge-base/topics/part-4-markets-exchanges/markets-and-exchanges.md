## 1. Recognised Investment Exchanges and Clearing Houses

- 1.1 Recognised Investment Exchanges (RIEs) and Recognised Overseas Investment Exchanges (ROIEs)

Since a large proportion of trades in financial instruments is carried out via established exchanges, such as the London Stock Exchange (LSE), the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) has the responsibility of recognising and supervising them under the Financial Services and Markets Act (2000) (FSMA).

Any body corporate or unincorporated association may apply to the FCA for an order declaring it to be a recognised investment exchange (RIE). The FCA will seek to establish whether the applicant is fit and proper to operate as an exchange, including whether it has sufficient financial resources to properly carry out its activities. The applicant must be willing and able to share information with the FCA, and to promote and maintain high standards of integrity and fair dealing, including laying down rules for activities on the exchange. It must record, monitor and enforce compliance with these rules.

Once recognised, these exchanges are subject to supervision and oversight by the FCA. Being granted recognised status relieves the organisation of the requirement to be an authorised person to conduct financial services business.

RIEs can be UK- or overseas-based. In the latter case, they are often referred to as **recognised overseas investment exchanges (ROIEs)** .

There are currently five RIEs based in the UK, offering membership and access to their market to UK firms:

1. **The London Stock Exchange (LSE)** – this is the largest formal market for securities in the UK. It facilitates deals in shares, bonds and some derivatives (for example, those that take the form of covered warrants).

2. **Intercontinental Exchange (ICE)** – this exchange is owned by a company listed on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), called Intercontinental Exchange – hence ICE. It deals in futures for energy products, such as crude oil and gas, and also in such new instruments as carbon emission allowances.

3. **The London Metal Exchange (LME)** – this exchange provides trading in a variety of futures and options on base metals and some plastics.

4. **Aquis Stock Exchange Limited** – this is an exchange for ambitious growth enterprises. Both equities and debt instruments are traded on the exchange.

5. **Cboe Europe Equities Regulated Market** – it operates two equity exchanges, in the UK (Cboe UK) and the Netherlands (Cboe NL). These represent one of the largest pan-European equities exchange groups by market share and value-traded.

ROIEs, in contrast, are based outside the UK, but carry on regulated activities within the UK (for example, by offering electronic trading facilities to members in the UK) and, to this extent, are regulated and supervised by the FCA. They do not have physical UK operations (except for some support representation), so they are necessarily electronic marketplaces. They include the National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations (NASDAQ), established in the US, and the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX).

At the time this workbook was published there were 32 ROIEs that are regulated and supervised by the FCA.

_Source of information – FCA website (financial services register/other registers)_

## 1.2 Recognised Clearing Houses (RCHs) and Recognised Overseas Clearing Houses (ROCHs)

Recognised clearing houses (RCHs) are supervised by the Bank of England under FSMA 2000. Clearing houses facilitate the clearing and sometimes also the settlement of trades.

There are currently four recognised clearing houses in the UK. Of these, Euroclear UK & Ireland is not a recognised central counterparty – rather it is a recognised central securities depository:

1. **LCH Limited** – this acts as central counterparty (CCP) for trades executed on Euronext.LIFFE, the LME and ICE Futures, and for certain trades executed on the LSE. LCH stands for London Clearing House.

2. **Euroclear UK & Ireland (formerly CRESTCo)** – this firm is owned and operated by Euroclear, and offers the facility, via the CREST system – to settle trades in dematerialised form. It is mainly known for UK and Irish equity clearing, and also provides clearing and settlement for a variety of other equities, bonds and funds.

3. **LME Clear Limited** – launched in 2015 by the LME for its members.

4. **ICE Clear Europe Limited** – a recently formed subsidiary of the Intercontinental Exchange group of companies (ICE), a group that operates global electronic marketplaces for trading futures, options and over-the-counter (OTC) energy and chemical contracts. It acts as a clearing house and CCP specifically for contracts executed on, or through, ICE Futures.

As with an RIE, recognition as an RCH means that the clearing house does not need to become an authorised person in order to conduct financial services business in the UK.

Recognised overseas clearing houses (ROCHs) are similar to RCHs, except they are based overseas, rather than in the UK. Examples of ROCHs include ICE Clear US Inc and the CME.

The current list of RCHs and ROCHs can be found on the Bank of England website – http://www. bankofengland.co.uk/financial-stability/financial-market-infrastructure-supervision.

## 1.3 Obtaining Recognised Status

FSMA (Recognition Requirements for Investment Exchanges and Clearing Houses) Regulations 2001 set out the requirements that enable recognised status to be obtained and provided.

In order for recognition to be provided by the FCA for the RIEs, ROIEs, RCHs and ROCHs (other than those RCHs and ROCHs which are central counterparties), the following must be complied with, where they must:

- be fit and proper

- maintain sufficient financial resources

- have proper systems and controls

- cooperate and share information

- maintain high standards

- record and monitor trades

- have proper procedures

- investigate complaints

- take appropriate measures to reduce the use of the exchange or clearing house for financial crime or market abuse

- have in place arrangements for settlement and appropriate arrangements for safe custody.

Once they have been granted recognised status, the FCA will undertake continuous supervision.

## 1.4 Designated Investment Exchanges (DIEs)

The FCA also recognises some overseas exchanges where no business is undertaken from the UK.

By granting **designated investment exchange (DIE)** status, the FCA assures UK market participants and/or investors that they consider the overseas market to be equivalent to the UK in terms of local regulation and investor rights.

Examples of DIEs include:

- Tokyo Stock Exchange

- NYSE, and

- Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing ltd.

At the time this workbook was published there were 29 DIEs.

## 1.5 Quote- and Order-Driven Markets

Financial markets are operated in two different ways: order-driven (the most common), and quotedriven (the market maker system).

In an order-driven market, buyers and sellers disclose the prices at which they are willing to trade. When the price matches, then a trade is undertaken. There may be periods when buyers' and sellers' views on the value of companies and/or shares diverge and no buyers are prepared to buy at the prices sellers are seeking.

In contrast, in a quote-driven market the continuity of bid and offer prices is ensured by having certain exchange members (market makers) who are obliged by the exchange rules to provide buying and selling prices throughout the day. The main advantage of this approach is that investors have a guaranteed pool of liquidity.

For this obligation, and for taking on this risk of always having to buy or sell, market makers are compensated by access to inter-dealer broker systems and relaxed disclosure requirements on transactions carried out, and the taking of temporary short positions in equities, sovereign debt and credit default swaps (CDSs).

## 1.6 Liquidity

In the context of regulated markets, liquidity is defined as the ability to enter or exit the market, in reasonable size, at an acceptable price spread, and create only modest price distortion.

Traditionally the quote-driven market was described as being the best way to provide liquidity, because market makers are obliged to buy and sell the stock. However, a quote-driven dealing system means that investors suffer the bid/offer spread when dealing, thus introducing a cost to the investor. Also,

liquidity is derived from more than just a trading method; there are a number of other important prerequisites such as:

- 'free' availability of the security being traded

- the ability of the stock to be freely borrowed or repo'd

- the existence of suitable derivatives for trading and hedging purposes

- well-capitalised exchange membership and investment community of both principals and agents, and

- efficient settlement systems to minimise failures (reduce counterparty risk).

## 2. UK Markets

## 2.1 London Stock Exchange (LSE)

The LSE operates as both a primary market and a secondary market. The majority of members can act in both capacities, agency, or principal.

The most relevant parts of the LSE rulebook are the following sections:

- Definitions.

- Core rules.

- Order book trading.

- Off order book trading rules.

- Market maker.

- Settlement, clearing and benefits.

- Compliance procedures.

- Default.

LSE rules require all market makers to disclose and trade-report all transactions carried out. Trades carried out via SETS (Stock Exchange Electronic Trading System) or other order books are automatically trade-reported. The following provides examples of the required information:

- Member firm reporting the transaction.

- Date and time of the transaction.

- Whether the security is a buy/sell.

- The security (name).

- The price and amount/nominal bought/sold.

- Trade reporting conditions, ie, is security/share cum-dividend.

- Settlement due date.

- Dealing capacity (principal/agent).

## 2.1.1 Transaction Reporting

Transaction reporting is required under the FCA's Supervision sourcebook (derived from UK MiFIR), and is for market surveillance/market manipulation purposes, rather than for market transparency purposes. When a firm provides a report of its transactions to an approved reporting mechanism (ARM)

it is fulfilling its obligations to report to the FCA as required by the Supervision (SUP) Sourcebook of the FCA's Handbook. The information contained in a transaction report is not disclosed to the market.

Subject to some limited exceptions (such as an entity being a non-UK entity that is either, not an investment firm or is an investment firm that does not have a presence in the UK, ie, it is not regulated/ does not operate out of a branch/subsidiary in the UK or the underlying financial instrument is not reportable), if both parties to a transaction are domiciled in the UK, they are required to submit transaction reports containing specific information, which includes:

- reporting firm's identity – legal entity identifier (LEI)

- trading venue identifier code

- executing entity identifier code

- trading date and time

- whether the transaction is a buy/sell

- instrument identification code (name and ISIN of the instrument)

- trading capacity

- quantity – number of shares/nominal/amount of stock purchased/sold

- settlement date

- identity of the counterparty (other party to the transaction)

- price and currency

- unique transaction identifier

- buyer/seller identification code

- buyer/decision-maker, first name, surname and date of birth, and

- seller/decision-maker code, first name, surname and date of birth.

Following Brexit, UK branches of EEA firms are now UK branches of third country entities and are, therefore, required to report under UK MiFIR. This also means that these branches will have dual reporting obligations – under UK MiFIR and also under EU MiFIR due to their parents' obligations.

Transaction reporting is one of the key areas that the FCA review and monitor. There have been a number of fines with respect to underreporting and incorrect reporting of transactions.

Firms are also able to report directly to the FCA, rather than use an ARM.

## 2.2 Alternative Investment Market (AIM)

The **AIM** market offers access for smaller, fast-growing companies to obtain access to the market at a lower cost and with a lighter regulatory burden.

To be listed on AIM, there are no requirements for the minimum percentage that must be held by the public, or a minimum market value of securities. The company is not required to have a lengthy trading history. However, all securities must be freely transferable.

AIM companies must have a **nominated adviser (Nomad)** to advise the company and/or its directors of their responsibilities, and a nominated broker to support trading on the company's shares.

In addition, there are ongoing obligations to publish price-sensitive information immediately and disclose details of significant transactions.

Refer to chapter 3, section 2.9 for further details.

## 2.3 Acquis (NEX Exchange)

Acquis operates two 'markets' or 'exchanges'. Aquis Stock Exchange (AQSE) provides primary and secondary markets for equity and debt products. The AQSE Growth Market is divided into two segments 'Access' and 'Apex', with different levels of admission criteria. The Access market focuses on earlier stage growth companies, while Apex is the intended market for larger, more established businesses.

## 2.4 Commodities Exchanges

The term 'commodities' means physical goods such as agricultural products, metals and energy commodities (ie, oil/gas). Commodities exchanges operate with both spot markets (immediate delivery) and futures markets (prices agreed for delivery in the future, on an agreed date now).

The following are UK exchanges where commodity futures are traded:

- **ICE Futures Europe** – for trading energy derivative products, for example, Brent Crude Oil, ICE Futures.

- **LME** – for trading base metal derivative products.

## 3. Multilateral Trading Facilities (MTFs)

An MTF is a specific type of UK and European financial trading system. The concept was introduced in 2007 by MiFID I, which describes a trading venue that brings together buyers and sellers in a nondiscretionary way, according to a defined set of rules resulting in trades. The United States equivalent is an alternative trading system (ATS).

The MiFID legislation laid out a number of obligations for an MTF to operate:

- It must be **pre-trade transparent** – the price of existing orders must be made available on market data feeds.

   - An MTF may be exempted from pre-trade transparency via the use of an appropriate waiver, such as a large-in-size waiver or price referencing waiver: in this case the MTF will be known as a 'dark pool'.

- It must be **post-trade transparent** – any trades carried out on the platform must be published in real time.

- **Prices and charges** must be public and applied consistently across all members.

- There must be a **rule book** advising how the system works and a means for applying for membership.

MTFs are notable for the following:

- **High trading speeds** , using technology to make their platforms attractive to high-frequency traders.

- **Low cost-bases** , running their organisations with minimal headcount.

- **Maker/taker pricing** , paying members to trade on the platform as long as the trading adds liquidity rather than takes it.

- **Trading incentives** , often called jump-balls, in which stakes are given to trading members in return for volume traded.

This made them highly attractive and enabled them to take market share. In turn, existing venues were forced to discount heavily, significantly impacting revenues.

With the implementation of MiFID II, a number of previously unregulated platforms converted to MTFs, across a range of asset classes (ie, interest rate derivatives, FX derivatives). MTFs are offered by companies such as Bloomberg, MarketAxess, Tradeweb, 360T and FX Connect.

At the time this workbook was published there were 36 MTFs regulated and approved by the FCA.

_[Source of information: FCA website (financial services register/other registers)]_

## 4. Organised Trading Facilities (OTFs)

MiFID II introduced a new category for trading venues, called an 'organised trading facility (OTF) which is a multilateral system that is not a regulated market nor an MTF.

Within an OTF, multiple third-party buying and selling interests in bonds, structured finance products, emission allowances or derivatives are able to interact in a way that results in a contract. Equities are not permitted to be traded through an OTF. MTFs and OTFs are similar multilateral systems that bring together third-party buying and selling interests in financial instruments in a way that results in a contract.

The requirements that apply to OTFs and their transactions are generally the same as the requirements for MTFs. As with MTFs, OTFs must establish clear rules and processes around trading under MiFID. OTFs are also subject to the same transparency requirements as regulated markets and MTFs, and are required to establish and maintain effective arrangements and procedures to enable the regular monitoring of compliance by the members.

At the time this workbook was published there were 20 OTFs regulated and approved by the FCA.

_[Source of information: FCA website (financial services register/other registers)]_

## 5. Systematic Internalisers (SIs)

The systematic internaliser regime, or trading on own account acting on a 'principal' basis, was not a new concept. It was introduced by UK MiFID, only applying to shares and did not have a quantitative test. The reason behind amending the SI regime to include post-trade reporting requirements was to enhance transparency around trading undertaken outside of trading venues (ie, OTC), thus helping with the price discovery process for investors.

MiFID II enhanced the SI regime and applied it to non-equity financial instruments (such as bonds and derivatives).

An 'SI' is defined as an investment firm which, on an organised, frequent, systematic and substantial basis, deals on own account by executing client orders outside a regulated market, MTF or OTF without operating a multilateral system in financial instruments that are admitted to trade or are traded on a UK trading venue (sometimes referred to as 'ToTV' – traded on a trading venue).

Furthermore, UK MiFID introduced quantitative criteria to make the definition more objective. The relevant period for assessment of the conditions detailed is quarterly based on the previous six months' trading activity in the UK.

## 5.1 Pre-Trade Requirements

- **Equities** – the quantitative thresholds are:

   - **frequent/systematic basis (liquid instrument)** – the number of OTC transactions undertaken by a firm is equal to or larger than 0.4% of transactions in the same financial instrument in the UK (on a venue or OTC) and the firm carries out a transaction in the financial instrument at least daily

   - **frequent/systematic basis (illiquid instrument)** – the firm carries out a transaction in the financial instrument at least daily

   - **substantial basis** – the size of OTC trading carried out on own account or on behalf of clients in last six months is equal to or larger than:

      - 15% of the total nominal amount in the same financial instrument executed by a firm on a trading venue or OTC

      - 0.4% of the total of the total nominal amount in the same financial instrument executed in the UK by the firm on a trading venue or OTC.

- **Non-equities** – the quantitative thresholds which apply are as follows:

   - **frequent/systematic basis (liquid instrument)** – the number of OTC transactions undertaken by a firm is equal to or larger than 2.5% or 4% (asset class-specific) of transactions in the same financial instrument in the UK (on a venue or OTC) and a firm carries out a transaction in the financial instrument at least weekly

   - **frequent/systematic basis (illiquid instrument)** – a firm carries out a transaction in the financial instrument at least weekly

   - **substantial basis** – the size of OTC trading carried out on own account or on behalf of clients in the last six months is equal to or larger than:

      - 25% or 30% (asset class-specific) of the total nominal amount in the same financial instrument executed by a firm on a trading venue or OTC.

The extent to which an investment firm meets the organised, frequent, systematic and substantial benchmarks will be determined per financial instrument (eg, at an ISIN code level).

Registration as an SI is undertaken at the legal entity level.

An entity can determine that it wishes to opt into the SI regime where it does not meet the specified thresholds; there is, however no opt-out once the criteria are met. The obligations introduced can essentially be summarised as follows:

- **pre-trade** – the provision of quotes:

   - **equities** :

      - continuous two-way 'firm' price quotes for liquid instruments

      - disclose 'firm' quotes upon request to the client for illiquid instruments

   - **non-equities** :

      - provide 'firm' quotes to all clients and make public for liquid instruments

      - provide 'firm' quotes request from a client for illiquid instruments

- **post-trade** – the reporting of trades executed as near to real-time as possible:

   - reported to made public – 'price discovery'.

There are certain waivers in place when having to provide firm quotes to clients where the size of the order is deemed to be 'large in scale' and/or above the size specific to the financial instrument.

## 5.2 Post-Trade Requirements

There are a number of deferrals from the post-trade reporting requirements. The normal timeframe for reporting is within one minute for equity/equity like instruments, within five minutes for non-equity instruments deemed liquid and by 7pm local time on a T+2 basis for illiquid instruments.

The FCA triggered the discretion clause in the legislation that delays the reporting for certain illiquid non-equity financial instruments for up to four weeks.

Reporting is single sided, by one of the firms – either the trading venue, the SI or in the case that both firms are SIs/neither are SIs, then the seller of the financial instrument.

Reporting identifies the firm reporting the trade and the trade economics of the transaction; **it does not contain any details on who the other counterparty to the trade was** .

Reporting only applies in respect of financial instruments that are subject to being traded on a trading venue/have been admitted to trading on a trading venue (referred to as 'ToTV'). The following would be exempt transactions in respect of equity transactions:

- Primary market transactions.

- Allocations to underlying clients/funds.

- Two matching trades entered into at the same time and for the same price (only report one side).

- Where a trade is passed along a chain and concluded on a trading venue/with an SI.

- Equity give-ups when completed in the market (and not internally filled).

The following was exempt transactions in respect of non-equity transactions:

- Primary market transactions, securities financing transactions and UK listed exchange-traded derivatives.

- Allocations to underlying clients/funds.

- Certain transactions where the counterparty is a member of the European System of Central Banks.

- Certain transactions where the counterparty is one of a number of specified third country central banks.

## 5.2.1 Removing the Link between an SI and Post-Trade Reporting

Following an earlier consultation paper published in 2022, the FCA published a Policy Statement (PS 23/4 – improving equity secondary markets), with final rules in May 2023. The Designated Reporter Regime (DRR) came into effect on 29 April 2024.

The FCA DRR regime de-links the post-trade reporting obligation from a firm's SI 'status' and creates an entity level designated reporter regime.

Although an SI register was developed by the approved publication arrangement (APAs), and managed independently, the FCA noted in their consultation that buy-side firms had been concerned that at the point of execution they did not know if their counterparty was an SI in the 'MiFID' financial instrument or whether they would be responsible for undertaking the post-trade reporting.

The FCA is responsible for maintaining the 'designated reporter' register. With regards to who will have the post-trade reporting obligation under the DRR, where both firms are either 'designated reporters' or neither is registered as designated reporters – the seller will be obligated to undertake reporting. However, the legislation does permit firms to bilaterally agree who should report.

Source of information: https://register.fca.org.uk/s/resources#Other_registers (Selecting the 'DRSP, MTF, OTF, SI and DR Register')

## 5.3 SI Calculation

In order to assess whether a firm is captured by the SI regime, it must undertake a quarterly calculation to assess the quantitative threshold limits. However, the FCA is only undertaking these calculations annually – as noted in their 'UK MiFID transparency calculations' webpage https://www.fca.org.uk/ regulation-markets-financial-instruments/uk-mifid-transparency-calculations.

The FCA is now responsible for publishing this data for firms to use to determine whether they would qualify as an SI based on the SI calculations.

Firms cannot opt out of the SI regime if they meet the quantitative threshold conditions.

## 5.3.1 Why would Firms be a Systematic Internaliser?

Being an SI identifies the firm as being a liquidity provider in a range of financial instruments and indicates that it is willing to trade with clients outside a trading venue. It also used to mean that clients who trade with a SI will not have any post-trade reporting obligations – something of interest to buyside clients and non-financial entities who would otherwise need to develop the framework to be able to undertake reporting obligations where they would apply. Although that has now changed with the implementation of the DRR, as noted  above in section 5.2.1.

Obviously, best execution obligations would apply and could determine whether a buy-side firm would trade on a trading venue or outside a trading venue with an SI.

## 5.3.2 SI Commercial Policy

The legislation creates the concept of a commercial policy that firms must have in place and provide to clients. As a systematic internaliser, the firm can decide in an objective non-discriminatory way:

- the clients to whom it will grant access to its quotes

- the process and circumstances for updating/withdrawing quotes

- the length of time a quote is 'firm' and can be dealt against by clients

- the number of transactions the firm will undertake with clients for any given quote

- the circumstances under which the firm will provide a quote for financial instruments deemed to be 'illiquid', and

- the obligations when dealing in sizes above the 'size specific to the financial instrument'.

The commercial policy needs to be provided to clients in advance of trading with them in the capacity of an SI. One way would be to put this on the firm's website.

## 5.4 Developments with the SI Regime

## 5.4.1 Definition of a Systematic Internaliser

In 2023 the FCA published some proposals on amending the definition of the SI regime, with the Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 amending the definition as follows:

_'systematic internaliser' means an investment firm which deals on own account when executing client orders outside a UK regulated market, UK MTF or UK OTF without operating a multilateral system and which:_

- _a. does so on an organised, frequent, systematic and substantial basis, or_

- _b. has chosen to opt in to the systematic internaliser regime;_

_for the purposes of whether dealing is taking place on a basis that is organised, frequent, systematic and substantial is to be determined in accordance with rules made by the FCA.'_

The FCA will be removing the quantitative criteria, keeping the qualitative criteria as noted above.

In the FCA's proposed glossary definition, the FCA defined dealing as organised, frequent, systematic, and substantial when the following are true.

- It is carried on in line with rules and procedures in an automated technical system, such as an electronic execution system, which is assigned to that purpose.

- It is available to counterparties on a regular or continuous basis.

- It is held out as being carried on by way of business, in a manner consistent with Article 3 of the Business Order in respect of the relevant financial instrument. On this point, firms may refer to our new proposed guidance in PERG 13.2 Q10a for guidance on meaning.

However, to ensure a consistent approach is adopted, the above will not come into effect until 1 December 2025 – which is the same timeframe for the implementation of the FCA's transparency requirements for bonds and derivatives markets (PS24/14).

## 5.4.2 FCA Discussion Paper on the Future of the SI Regime

The FCA are looking at the future of the SI regime, in particular for bonds and derivatives in light of recent changes under FSMA 2023, Policy Statement PS23/4 (Designated Reporter Regime)

## Areas being considered by the FCA

- The effectiveness of the current transparency regime for SIs and whether any changes need to be made to the threshold under which the pre-trade transparency regime applies to SIs and the minimum quote size for SIs (equities).

- Whether the SI flag on post-trade transparency reports 'SINT' provide useful information.

- The case for adding any new trade flags to post-trade reports to help identify addressable liquidity if firms trading bonds and derivatives OTC no longer have to identify themselves as SIs.

- Implications for best execution if firms are no longer identified as SIs in respect of bonds and derivatives.

The FCA expects to publish a consultation paper on the future of the SI regime in Q2/3 2025, changes to the SI regime will take effect alongside the new approach to determining SIs on 1 December 2025 – when the post trade transparency requirements for bonds and derivatives comes into effect.

## 6. Derivatives Markets

The main purposes and aims of regulation, in all markets globally, are to:

- maintain and promote the fairness, efficiency, competitiveness, transparency and uniformity of the securities and futures industry

- promote understanding by the public of the operation and functioning of the securities and futures industry

- provide protection for members of the public investing in or holding financial products

- minimise crime and misconduct in the securities and futures industry

- reduce systemic risks in the securities and futures industry, and

- assist in maintaining the market's financial stability by taking appropriate steps in relation to the securities and futures industry.

## 6.1 UK and EU

While each country has its own financial regulator (such as the FCA in the UK), which sets out the rules for all, including the derivatives markets, the EU, through EMIR and MiFID II, introduced more extensive rules and regulations regarding the conduct of business and organisational requirements of firms within the financial sector.

Following Brexit, UK firms must comply with the onshored version of EU regulations. Therefore, UK firms would have to comply with UK EMIR and UK MiFID and UK MiFIR.

## 6.2 Global Regulatory Reforms

At international level, the Financial Stability Board (FSB) set up a work stream in order to address the challenges related to the implementation of the G20 commitments. In addition, the OTC Derivatives Regulators' Forum was established to promote cooperation between regulators.

In line with the EU's G20 commitments, there have been a number of initiatives both by individual jurisdictions and international bodies.

## 6.2.1 OTC Derivatives

With OTC derivatives, settlement is direct with the counterparty rather than through an exchange.

Since 2009, there have been moves, not just in the US and Europe, but also in G20 countries more widely, for OTC derivatives to be cleared through clearing houses/CCPs. This increases transparency and reduces the risk of counterparty default, therefore reducing systemic risks arising from trading in OTC derivatives.

Before the financial crisis of 2007–08, the OTC market grew rapidly without significant regulatory scrutiny.

In late 2009, the G20 concluded that four changes to such markets were needed:

- All standardised OTC derivative contracts should be traded on exchanges or electronic trading platforms, where appropriate.

- All standardised OTC derivative contracts should be cleared through CCPs.

- OTC derivative contracts should be reported to trade repositories.

- Non-centrally cleared contracts should be subject to higher capital requirements.

## 6.3 European Market Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR)

The EU implemented a regulation aimed at increasing transparency in derivatives and reducing risk in the OTC derivatives market.

UK EMIR applies to UK firms and requires the following:

- The **clearing** of standardised OTC derivative contracts through CCPs in order to reduce counterparty risk. This is aimed at preventing the default of one market participant causing the collapse of other market players, thereby putting the entire financial system at risk. To be authorised, a CCP will have to hold a minimum amount of financial resources.

- The **reporting** of all derivative contracts to trade repositories (ie, central data centres). Trade repositories will have to publish aggregate positions by class of derivatives, thereby offering market participants a clearer view of the derivatives market.

- To put in place certain **risk management procedures** for OTC derivative transactions that are not cleared through a CCP, for both financial and non-financial counterparties.

The FCA is now responsible in the UK for the surveillance of trade repositories and for granting and withdrawing their registration. It is also responsible for the identification of contracts subject to the clearing obligation, while national competent authorities will be responsible for the authorisation and supervision of CCPs, except in the case of CCPs from third countries, which will have to be recognised by ESMA, providing that they meet certain conditions.

If a contract is not eligible for clearing by a CCP, the regulation requires the application of different risk management techniques, including the exchange of collateral and the holding of additional capital.

The regulation applies to CCPs and their clearing members, to financial counterparties and to trade repositories, as well as to non-financial counterparties and trading venues where so provided.

## 6.4 US: Dodd-Frank Act

Following the end of the global financial crisis of 2007–08, after a lengthy and sometimes contentious legislative process, the US Senate approved the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (the Act). Title VII of the Act sets forth the legislative framework for derivatives.

The Act creates an extensive regulatory framework for swaps and security-based swaps, capturing substantially all derivatives transactions that previously were exempt from regulation under the Commodity Futures Modernization Act.

The Act provides for mandatory clearing and trading on regulated facilities for many derivatives contracts, with an exception for non-financial end users.

Swap dealers and major swap participants are subject, among other things, to capital and margin requirements, business conduct rules and special duties in their dealings with governmental entities, the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) and governmental plans and endowments. While many end users will not be directly regulated, they often will be affected indirectly as their counterparties are subject to the requirements, in particular with respect to margin rules.

Other significant provisions include the swaps 'pushout' rule, collateral segregation and real-time swap transaction reporting requirements, position limits and large trader reporting, and the application of the securities laws to security-based swaps.

## 6.4.1 Swaps and Security-Based Swaps

The Act aims to sweep the universe of previously unregulated derivatives into the new regulatory framework, and divide this universe into two broad categories:

- **Swaps** – the term 'swap' is defined broadly and includes options, swaps and other transactions based on rates, commodities, securities, debt instruments, indices, quantitative measures and other financial or economic interests, subject to certain exceptions. Building upon the definition of previously exempt swap agreements under Section 206A of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, the Act

brings previously unregulated derivatives into the new framework. Swaps are subject to Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) jurisdiction. Swaps do not include security-based swaps, as discussed below.

- **Security-based swaps** – swaps based on individual securities or loans, on narrow-based securities indices, or on events affecting individual issuers of securities, or issuers of securities in a narrowbased securities index. Security-based swaps are subject to Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) jurisdiction.

The Act seeks to establish parallel rules for swaps and security-based swaps. Mixed swaps, which combine features of swaps and security-based swaps, are to be regulated jointly by the CFTC and SEC. Parties seeking to list or trade novel derivatives products having both commodities and securities features, may petition the CFTC and SEC for a determination of the product's regulatory status. The CFTC and SEC are required to consult and coordinate with one another (and with the federal banking regulators) in exercising their jurisdiction over swaps, and may challenge each other's actions in court in the event of a dispute.

While the basic approach is comprehensive and quite straightforward, the definitional provisions are complex in detail. Care must be taken in evaluating the status of individual derivatives products. For example, the swap definition excludes certain transactions that are already regulated as securities, including options on securities and foreign exchange options traded on registered securities exchanges.

Other exclusions remove from the scope of the Act commodity and security futures, leverage contracts, and forward transactions for non-financial commodities intended for physical settlement, as well as deposit and savings accounts, certificates of deposit and other 'identified banking products'. Swaps based on government securities (other than municipal securities) are excluded from the security-based swap definition, and thus are subject to regulation by the CFTC.

Foreign exchange swaps and forwards, which were the subject of considerable debate during the legislative process, will be considered swaps.

## 6.4.2 Clearing and Trade Execution

The effort to require central clearing and exchange trading for many derivatives transactions is at the heart of two basic purposes of the Act, reducing systemic risk and increasing market transparency. At the same time, the mandatory clearing and exchange-trading provisions have been controversial, in particular due to the increased margin requirements (and hence increased costs) associated with central clearing.

The Act requires the CFTC and SEC to, on an ongoing basis, review swaps and categories or classes of swaps with a view to determining whether clearing should be mandatory. Factors to be considered include the existence of significant outstanding exposures, trading liquidity and the availability of appropriate operational expertise and resources. When the CFTC or SEC determine that a particular type of swap should be cleared, but no clearing organisation accepts the swap for clearing, the CFTC or SEC are directed to investigate and take appropriate action. Mandatory clearing will not be applied to existing swap positions, so long as the positions are reported to swap data repositories in a timely fashion under rules to be publicised by the CFTC and SEC.

Clearing organisations will be required to offset swaps with the same terms and conditions on an economically equivalent basis within the clearing organisation, and to provide for non-discriminatory clearing of transactions executed bilaterally or on unaffiliated facilities. In a similar vein, the Act calls on the CFTC and SEC to adopt rules, including possible ownership and control limitations, to mitigate conflicts of interest that may arise with respect to ownership of regulated clearing, and trading facilities by bank holding companies, certain non-bank financial institutions, swap dealers, and major swap participants.

## 6.5 Clearing and Settlement

LCH, as a central clearing counterparty (CCP) acts as principal to trades, therefore members of exchanges who buy or sell a contract end up with a position with the LCH either directly, if they themselves are clearing members, or indirectly, through the clearing member of the exchange who clears their trades.

LCH takes the risk of counterparties failing to honour obligations. The LCH reduces its risk by requiring cash or collateral deposits (initial margins) for the clearing members' positions.

Looking at how this operates from a practical perspective for NYSE Liffe, only members can trade on NYSE Liffe, and anyone else wishing to trade a Liffe contract must do so through such a member who takes responsibility for the registration, margining and settlement of the trade.

Individuals using the services of NYSE Liffe members include the following:

- **Traders** – acting on their own behalf, commonly known as 'locals', or on their company's behalf. Traders make a profit from the positions taken.

- **Brokers** – acting for a third party and making profits by charging commission to trade.

Day-to-day supervision on NYSE Liffe is carried out by the Market Supervision Department of the exchange.

## 7.1 FX Global Code ('Code')

The 'Code' was developed by a partnership between central banks and market participants from 16 jurisdictions around the globe. It is a voluntary code.

The purpose of the FX Global Code is to promote a robust, fair, liquid, open, and appropriately transparent market in which a diverse set of market participants, supported by resilient infrastructure, are able to confidently and effectively transact at competitive prices that reflect available market information and in a manner that conforms to acceptable standards of behaviour.

The FX Global Code does not impose legal or regulatory obligations on market participants, nor does it substitute for regulation, but rather it is intended to serve as a supplement to any and all local laws, rules and regulation by identifying global good practices and processes.

The FX Global Code covers topics such as ethics, governance, information sharing, as well as risk management and compliance and confirmation and settlement.

In 2021, the FX Global Code was revised following a formal review. Although there were no updates to the leading principles, there were minor updates made to 11 of the 55 principles and some of the guidance text in the FX Global Code – further strengthening guidance on anonymous trading, algorithmic trading and transaction cost analysis, disclosures and settlement risk.

A copy of the FX Global code can be located at https//www.globalfxc.org/docs/fx_global.pdf.

A revised FX Global Code was published in December 2024, although there were no changes to the overarching principles, additional text and guidance has been provided to assist market participants.

## 7.1.1 Ethics

Market participants are expected to behave in an ethical and professional manner to promote the fairness and integrity of the FX market. They should:

- act honestly in dealings with clients and other market participants

- act fairly, dealing with clients and other market participants in a consistent and appropriately transparent manner, and

- act with integrity, particularly in avoiding and confronting questionable practices and behaviours.

Maintaining high standards of behaviour is the responsibility of:

- firms, which should promote ethical values and behaviour within the organisation, support efforts to promote high ethical standards in the wider FX market, and encourage involvement by personnel in such efforts

- senior and front-line management, who should be proactive in embedding and supporting the practice of ethical values within the firm's culture and be prepared to give appropriate advice to personnel, and

- personnel, who should apply judgement when facing ethical questions, expect to be held responsible for unethical behaviour, and seek advice where appropriate. Personnel should report and/or escalate issues of concern to appropriate parties internally or externally, having regard to the circumstances.

## 7.1.2 Governance

Market participants should have sound and effective governance frameworks to provide for clear responsibility for, and comprehensive oversight, of their FX market activity; and to promote responsible engagement in the FX market.

## 7.1.3 Execution

Market participants must exercise care when negotiating and executing transactions in order to promote a robust, fair, open, liquid, and appropriately transparent FX market.

Irrespective of their role, market participants handling orders should:

- have clear standards in place that strive to ensure a fair and transparent outcome for the client

- be truthful in their statements

- use clear and unambiguous language

- make clear whether the prices they are providing are firm or merely indicative

- have adequate processes in place to support the rejection of client orders for products they believe are inappropriate for the client

- not enter into transactions with the intention of disrupting the market, and

- provide all relevant disclosures and information to a client before negotiating a client order, thereby allowing the client to make an informed decision as to whether to transact or not.

## 7.1.4 Information Sharing

Market participants must be clear and accurate in their communications and must protect confidential information to promote effective communication that supports a robust, fair, open, liquid, and appropriately transparent FX market.

## 7.1.5 Risk and Compliance

Firms should have appropriate risk management, compliance, and review structures to manage and mitigate the risks that arise from a market participant's activities in the FX market.

## 7.1.6 Confirmation and Settlement

Market participants must have robust, efficient, transparent, and risk-mitigating post-trade processes to promote the predictable, smooth, and timely settlement of transactions in the FX market.

## 7.2 UK Money Markets Code

The UK Money Markets Code is a voluntary code, written by market participants, and was originally published in 2017, and recently updated in 2024.

The UK Money Markets Code sets out the standards and best practice expected from participants in the deposit, repo and securities lending markets in the UK. It supersedes existing guidance for participants in these markets provided by the NIPS Code, the Gilt Repo Code and the Securities Borrowing and Lending Code. By bringing these together it more clearly establishes the framework for transacting in UK money markets.

The UK Money Markets Code is endorsed by the Bank of England's Money Markets Committee (MMC), made up of market participants from a wide range of banks and other financial and non-financial institutions. The high standards it promotes are designed to build greater trust and certainty throughout these markets, bringing clear benefits for all involved.

The UK Money Markets Code applies to:

- a. execution of transactions in the deposit markets, specifically in the sterling and foreign currency wholesale unsecured deposit market (including certificates of deposits and commercial paper:

   - but not general transactional banking activity (ie, an individual's deposit or savings account with their bank)

- b. the repo market, and

- c. securities lending transactions.

The UK Money Markets Code was first updated in 2021 to reflect 'new' remote working practices following the COVID-19 pandemic, the ongoing development of environmental, social and governance criteria, and the increasing electronification and use of platforms in money markets.

The 2024 update reflected changes following developments in UK Money Markets, such as reminding market participants of the importance of making every reasonable effort to settle transactions conducted in UK Money Markets, including a new section on settlement discipline being added to the 'Securities Lending' chapter.

## 7.3 Global Precious Metals Code

The London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) introduced the Global Precious Metals Code in 2017. The Code applies to all precious metals market participants, and provides participants with guidance on best practice; again, it is a voluntary code.

The Global Precious Metals Code sets out best practice in the market with a high level of detail and therefore will be more comprehensive than the previous version. In addition to market conventions, the Code covers principles that should be adopted by members, including compliance, governance, risk management and pre- and post-trade execution. Illustrative examples of acceptable practices will be included.

All market participants involved in the global wholesale precious metals market are expected to act according to the principles of the Global Precious Metals Code.
