## 1. Current UK Regulatory Developments

## 1.1 Markets Effectiveness Review

Following a number of recent Government endorsed reviews, changes have been made to the listing requirements in the UK – these were covered in section 2 of chapter 3.

In January 2025 the FCA published a further consultation paper proposing changes to the rules to align disclosure requirements for low denomination bonds with those for higher denominations to make capital raising easier for issuers. The FCA also proposes further changes to the UK Listings Rules to make them more efficient for companies issuing further securities and to remove Listings Particulars to streamline the rules.

The proposals put forward by the FCA, include:

- Aligning prospectus requirements for non-equity securities to a single standard which is based on the current disclosure requirements for 'wholesale' denominations, rather than having separate standards for lower denomination bonds.

- Guidance that defines which certain (non-complex) low denomination corporate bonds issued by listed companies can be appropriate for the 'mass market'.

- Changes to the listings applications process for further issuances to reduce frictions.

- Remove Listing Particulars as a listing admission document to simplify the listing framework and better align with the POATRs reforms.

The FCA noted in the consultation paper that these proposed changes aim to further promote efficient and effective capital raising by companies across debt and equity capital markets in the UK, and encourage wider access for investors to investment opportunities.

## 1.2 Wholesale Markets Review

The purpose of the 'Wholesale Markets Review' is to determine how much of retained EU law needs adapting to reflect the UK's approach to financial regulation, while looking ahead to future challenges.

Although the UK played a significant role in designing the MiFID II framework, the UK government believed that although the transposed regime is working well in many areas, the EU approach to regulation (ie, all EU member states have to agree to legislation) meant that regulations were not always calibrated for the uniqueness of UK markets.

The UK government saw the departure from the EU as an important opportunity to ensure regulation is appropriate and proportionate for the UK. The government believes that this is best achieved by amending the regime for secondary markets to ensure that it reflects the UK's position as a global capital market.

The UK government has on more than one occasion stated that it does not intend to make changes for the sake of change; this review is about making regulation nimble and fit for purpose for UK markets.

The proposed reforms to the UK's wholesale market have been informed by discussions between HM Treasury, the FCA and market participants. They have also been guided by the following objectives and principles:

- Upholding high regulatory standards.

- Promoting openness and competitiveness.

- Delivering fair and proportionate regulation.

- Supporting economic growth.

## 1.2.1 Roadmap to Delivery

The regulatory framework underpinning secondary markets policy sits across primary (which is the government's responsibility) and secondary legislation (The FCA is responsible for developing rules and guidance).

## 1.2.2 Areas of 'MiFID II' under Review

## Trading Venues

The government believed that the current market structure for trading venues is generally sound; however, it identified several areas where the regime could be simplified, or where requirements have led to unnecessary costs and burdens on firms but has provided benefits in terms of market integrity.

MiFID II expanded the trading venue perimeter to include multilateral trading systems where trading interests can interact, regardless of whether interaction is achieved through the use of the operator's discretion or according to non-discretionary rules. It is neutral as to whether transactions are finalised within the 'trading system' or bilaterally between users.

MiFID II introduced restrictions on the ability of operators of trading venues to deal on their own account. Therefore, an investment firm operating an MTF cannot execute clients' orders in a matched principal trading capacity and an investment firm cannot act as a systematic internaliser within the same legal entity operating an OTF.

## Systematic Internalisers (SIs)

The SI regime was originally determined on a qualitative basis and limited to equity markets. Its objective was to ensure that OTC trading could contribute to price formation. MiFID II expanded the regime to cover non-equity instruments, including bonds and derivatives.

SIs are determined on an instrument basis. When acting as an SI, investment firms – in addition to the other requirements such as best execution and post-trade trade reporting obligations – are required to make public the quotes they provide to their clients when dealing in liquid instruments and up to certain sizes (ie, pre-trade transparency).

MiFID II introduced quantitative thresholds, requiring firms to determine if they should be an SI with respect to different asset classes. To identify and confirm whether they exceed the thresholds, firms are expected to perform calculations for each financial instrument they deal in on a quarterly basis covering the previous six-month period.

The government is aware that many firms avoid having to undertake the quarterly calculations, on the basis that they have 'opted in' to the regime for all instruments that they trade in, thereby removing post-trade reporting requirements for their firm's clients (ie, buy-side firms).

The government considers that this suggests the current definition is not working well and could be disincentivising firms from operating as an SI. The government noted that as SIs are currently determined on an instrument-by-instrument basis, therefore, at the point of execution it can be difficult to know which counterparty has the obligation to undertake the post-trade reporting.

The relevant questions asked by the government included the following:

- Should the definition for SIs be based on qualitative criteria?

- Should SIs be determined at entity level instead of an instrument-by-instrument basis?

## Equities

MiFID II extended the transparency, market integrity and competition by adding equity like instruments (ie, depository receipts and ETFs).

The government believed that there is scope to simplify the MiFID II transparency regime by removing restrictions that have not aided in price formation, while at the same time as ensuring the market structure is balanced between investors' choice and market integrity.

The government also noted that it identified some amendments that could increase competition and openness, such as removing requirements that limit a firm's ability to execute transactions where it can get the best outcome for investors.

Although the government noted that the MiFID II equity pre-trade transparency regime has had a positive effect on price discovery, it believed that the double volume cap (DVC) is operationally costly and does not achieve its objective of pushing equity trading onto venues and that amendments could be made to the reference price waiver to encourage innovation and enhance best execution.

The government also believed that amending the transparency regime to encourage SIs to quote in meaningful sizes could help increase transparency and therefore price formation and best execution.

The government is of the view that the DVC is not an appropriate tool to protect price formation in UK markets and it is proposing to repeal it. However, to ensure market integrity, the government is proposing that the FCA continues to monitor the level of dark trading in markets and retains its ability to limit it if there is evidence that the volume of trading is undermining the efficiency of the price formation process.

Not included in the consultation were thresholds for equity waivers; these will be reviewed separately by the FCA.

The government is keen to understand what incentives can be offered to SIs to increase the number of quotes that they disclose prior to trading, as they are aware that an SI would only have to do this if such SI is quoting up to 10% of standard market size for liquid instruments. This can lead to inaccurate transparency if SIs quote in smaller sizes.

The share trading obligation (STO) is an extension of the derivatives trading obligation (DTO), which was implemented to fulfil a G20 commitment to bring more derivatives trading onto exchanges or electronic platforms. However, unlike the DTO, the STO is not based on international standards or best practices.

When it was implemented, policy makers understood it would increase activities on trading venues, thereby increasing transparency. However, the government noted that there has been no evidence that the objectives have been met. Other than the UK, only the EU has such a regime. The government noted that it does not believe that the way the STO restricts trading is appropriate, effective or conducive to price formation or stability. Therefore, proposals are being developed to remove this requirement.

## Fixed Income and Derivative Markets

MiFID II significantly extended the transparency regime – which originally applied only to equities – to include bonds, exchange-traded commodities, structured finance products, emission allowances and derivatives. It also introduced the mandatory DTO in an attempt to bring more derivatives trading onto venues with the aim of increasing transparency.

Evidence shows that the MiFID II regime has achieved little meaningful transparency and limited impact on price formation at a high cost to industry. The government noted in the report that this is because the regime is not appropriately calibrated and does not account for the diverse nature of non-equity markets.

The scope of the DTO was designed to align with the scope of the Clearing Obligation which is set out in the European Market Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR). In 2019 the 'EMIR Refit' legislation amended the scope of the Clearing Obligation by excluding small financial counterparties. Because the scope of the DTO has not been updated, this has created a misalignment. As the scope of the DTO for financial counterparties is defined with a cross-reference to the definition in EMIR, discrepancy between the two obligations has caused ambiguity for industry.

Although the FCA has mitigated this through transitional relief, the government intends to formally realign the counterparties in scope of the DTO with those in scope of the UK EMIR Clearing Obligation.

MiFID II provides an exemption from the DTO for the risk reduction techniques, namely 'portfolio compression'. But other post-trade risk reduction services, including cover rebalancing and optimisation services, do not currently benefit from the DTO exemption.

While MiFID II included non-equity instruments in its transparency regime, it did not go far enough in accounting for fundamental differences between equity and non-equity markets. The current framework is based on the (inaccurate) assumption that transparency, especially pre-trade, plays the same role in the fixed income and derivatives markets as it does in the equity markets.

Whilst equity markets are typically executed on electronic order books, most fixed income and derivatives transactions occur through voice, request-for-quote (RFQ) systems, or bilateral negotiation – with the notable exceptions of some exchange-traded and OTC derivatives.

The type of financial instruments that are in scope of the pre-trade and post-trade transparency is determined, according to MiFIR, on the basis of whether the instrument or class is admitted to trading or traded on a trading venue (ToTV). The concept of ToTV is relevant for other provisions in MiFID II, such as transaction reporting, but it is not a defined term under MiFID II. This has created ambiguity in relation to the boundaries of the transparency regime for OTC derivatives.

The current regulatory guidance for OTC transactions is based on the assumption that any OTC derivatives are in scope where they share the same characteristics of derivatives traded on trading venues (ie, instruments that are captured in the Financial Instruments Reference Data System (FIRDS)). To compound the confusion, many OTC derivative contracts that are subject to transparency requirements use post-trade deferrals, further compounding the lack of transparency around price formation and discovery.

The government is considering how it can amend the scope of the transparency regime to increase OTC derivatives transparency while removing contracts that do not need to be included.

The government considers that liquidity calculations for non-equity instruments do not work for fixed income markets because instruments are traded infrequently. For commodity derivatives, it is because the aggregation of instruments overstates the liquidity in individual contracts and subjects them to transparency requirements that are not appropriate for their true liquidity profile.

With regards to pre-trade transparency, MiFID II established a very broad pre-trade regime for fixed income and derivatives markets – requiring bid and offer prices that are broadcast through a trading venue to be disclosed to the public. However, waivers provide exemptions from pre-trade transparency requirements if:

- there is no liquid market for the instrument in question

- an order is large in size

- the instrument is held in an order management facility; and

- it is part of a package order or an exchange for physical (EFP) transaction.

Because a lot of fixed income and derivative instruments are bespoke, illiquid and complex, the government does not believe that pre-trade transparency works well for non-equities. They have the same views on post-trade transparency due to the complex exemption in the form of 'deferrals' – which can range from the standard T+2, to four weeks or indefinitely in the case of sovereign bonds meeting certain criteria.

Therefore, the government believes that the long length of deferral has compromised the aim of the transparency objectives and is looking to reduce the number of deferrals that are available, and also reduce the length of deferred reporting in some instances.

## Commodity Markets

The UK government noted that it always considered the MiFID II commodities regime poorly designed and inefficient.

The MiFID II definition of a 'commodity derivative' determines which instruments are subject to the position limits and position reporting requirements, but is wide-ranging, complex and is not strongly linked to the MiFID II definition of a commodity.

OTC contracts that are economically equivalent to exchange-traded commodity derivatives are also captured by the MiFID II regime. However, identification of these contracts has proven difficult, and they have only been reported in a very small number of instances. In line with feedback from market participants, the government believes that the inclusion of these contracts imposes increased legal risk and potential compliance costs for firms.

Prior to MiFID II, trading venues were responsible for setting controls to ensure orderly trading, settlement and delivery. As part of MiFID II, responsibility for position limits was moved from trading venues to local regulators within EU Member States. Therefore, the FCA was required to establish and enforce position limits for all exchange-traded commodity derivative contracts – for around 800 contracts in the UK. Additionally, UK trading venues were required to establish and operate their own position management controls.

The government recognises that this approach is overly complicated, when compared to the pre- MiFID II approach, and has led to position controls being duplicated across trading venues and the FCA.

The government proposed revoking the requirement for position limits to be applied to all exchangetraded contracts, and to transfer the setting of position controls from the FCA to trading venues.

The government is of the opinion that position reporting generally works effectively and provides a valuable source of market data to trading venues and the FCA, which use the data for market monitoring.

## Market Data

Market data is fundamental in helping market participants identify investment opportunities and evaluate positions, essential for price formation/discovery and best execution.

In an attempt to make data more accessible, MiFID II requires trading venues and approved publication arrangements (APAs) to make data available on a 'reasonable commercial basis' – although this must be made free after 15 minutes.

However, the government believed that MiFID II has not achieved its aim of making standardised data easily accessible to market participants and is keen to improve the quality and usability of market data and progress the emergence of a consolidated tape.

## 1.2.3 FCA Publications to Date

## Guidance on the Trading Venue Consultation Paper (CP22/18)

The FCA consulted on providing new guidance on the regulatory perimeter for trading venues, to ensure that they have greater certainty about the permissions they require to carry on their business.

The FCA proposed adding this guidance in the Perimeter Guidance Manual (PERG) to clarify the definition of a multilateral system, as well as Questions and Answers (Q&As) on the application of the general guidance to specific types of arrangements in financial markets.

The FCA also sought views on, but only for information purposes and not to make final rules, on whether the regulatory obligations for trading venues could be made proportionate for smaller firms, while maintaining high standards of market integrity.

The FCA set out the key areas that it would look to provide guidance:

- **Investment-based crowdfunding firms** operating primary market platforms.

- **Bulletin boards** – clarifying the demarcation between multilateral systems and bulletin boards.

- **Technology providers** – draws a distinction between general purpose communications systems and multilateral systems.

- **Voice brokers** – arranging or executing client orders over the telephone without operating a trading system or facility does not constitute a multilateral system.

- **Portfolio managers operating internal matching systems** – the execution of trading interests by a portfolio manager does not constitute a multilateral system.

- **Blocking onto trading venues** – where a firm operates a system for the purpose only of blocking trades onto a trading venue consistent with the intentions of the parties to the underlying transactions to trade on a trading venue, these arrangements do not amount to the operation of a multilateral system.

In its Policy Statement, the FCA did not modify the perimeter, which would require legislative change. Instead, it issued new guidance in PERG 13.3 in the form of Q&As. This covers the different elements of the definition of a multilateral system and how this applies to specific types of arrangements, where the FCA's supervisory experience and discussions with firms suggest that further clarity would be beneficial. The final guidance came in effect in October 2023.

The FCA set out key areas that its guidance covers, including:

## **Technology Providers**

- Distinguishes between general purpose communications systems and multilateral systems.

- Sets out a range of factors that the FCA would have regard to when considering whether a service has the characteristics of a trading system or facility.

## **Voice Brokers**

- Clarifies that arranging or executing client orders over the telephone does not constitute a multilateral system unless the voice trading system has the characteristics of a system or facility.

## **Portfolio Managers Operating Internal Matching Systems**

- Clarifies that internal matching systems operated by firms engaged in portfolio management, where shares or other financial instruments are transferred from one fund to another, do not constitute a multilateral system.

## **Systems Operated for the Sole Purpose of Executing Trades on Regulated Trading Venues**

- Trading firms often arrange deals in large blocks of derivatives outside the systems operated by trading venues and then report them to the exchange for execution.

- Clarifies that where a firm operates a system for the sole purpose of executing trades on a trading venue in accordance with the venue's rules, these arrangements do not amount to a multilateral system.

## **Primary Market Activity by Crowdfunding Platforms**

- Clarifies that a crowdfunding platform which allows clients to participate in company fundraisings by buying shares or bonds from the issuer does not amount to a multilateral system.

## **Bulletin Boards**

- Clarifies that for these arrangements to be within the perimeter, trading interests must be able to interact within the system (for example, where users are able to respond to requests to deal).

## Consolidated Tape – Policy Statement on the framework for UK Consolidate Tape (CT)

In July 2023, the FCA published a consultation paper (CP 23/15) on a proposed framework for a UK consolidated tape for bond market data, with a section of the paper also discussion a consolidated tape for equities.

The FCA sees the role of a consolidated tape provider (CTP) as collecting market data from relevant trading venues and approved publication arrangements (APAs) and then disseminate the 'tape' in a standardised electronic data feed to market participants.

The outcomes that the FCA are seeking from the design of the consolidated tape framework are:

- a consolidated tape for bond data operating from 2025, following a tender process and subsequent authorisation or verification of the chosen CTP

- enhanced market quality through an improved understanding of execution costs, leading to greater market participation, and a more efficient allocation of investments, and

- improved outcomes in wholesale markets in terms of addressable liquidity, market transparency and market access.

In December 2023 the FCA published a further consultation paper (CP23/33), which included its policy statement, and final rules, on the framework for a UK consolidated tape for bonds, along with a consultation on payments to data providers and forms for data reporting services providers.

The rules for the consolidated tape framework, came into effect on 5 April 2024.

The consultation on payments to data providers and forms for data reporting service providers closed on 9 February 2024.

## **Improving Transparency for Bond & Derivative Markets (CP23/32)**

In December 2023, the FCA published it proposals for amending the pre-trade and post-trade requirements for non-equity financial instruments under MiFID.

The current MiFID requirements apply to any bond or derivative admitted to trading or traded on a trading venue ('ToTV') in the UK. Once an instrument is ToTV, investment firms must comply with the transparency requirements, including when dealing OTC.

The FCA is proposing to reduce the scope of instruments subject to transparency requirements via the introduction of new Category 1 and 2 groups.

Bonds traded on UK trading venues, and certain derivatives subject to the UK EMIR clearing obligation, will be Category 1. The FCA noted that significant parts of the OTC derivatives market, namely FX derivatives and single-name credit default swaps, will, therefore, be outside the scope of Category 1. All other instruments – including other derivatives, structured finance products, and emission allowances - will be Category 2.

Whilst trading venues will need to comply with the pre- and post-trade transparency rules that apply to Category 1 instruments, investment firms will only need to meet the post-trade requirements for Category 1 instruments (they will not have any pre-trade transparency obligations).

## **Transparency Calculations**

The FCA currently identifies liquid and illiquid instruments for the purpose of setting the thresholds for transparency exemptions or deferrals. The FCA is proposing to delete its financial instruments transparency system (FITS). Rather, calculations for derivatives will be based on the tenor of the instrument, and for bonds and credit default swaps on a number of characteristics including type of issuer, currency, issuance size, maturity, and credit rating.

## **Pre-Trade Transparency**

The FCA proposes to retain the requirement for trading venues to publish adequate information about bid and offer prices and the depth of trading interest on a continuous basis during normal trading hours.

However, detailed pre-trade requirements will be limited to fully transparent trading systems, and other systems (ie, voice or request-for-quote) will not need to follow these requirements.

## **Post-Trade Transparency**

The FCA proposes the following:

- To replace the current system of deferrals from publishing real-time post-trade transaction reports, which it has stated as being as overly complex:

   - The new framework for deferrals would apply only to large trades and remove the ability to aggregate post-trade information.

- To prioritise information being made public on prices, over size or volume of trades

   - The largest trades would benefit from an extended deferral time, or permanent reporting exclusion if capped.

- Two models for setting the thresholds and length of deferrals, which will be dependent on the liquidity of the instrument in question:

   - The FCA believes both models will provide protection to market participants who conduct large trades, while still providing transparency.

- Exemptions from post-trade transparency:

   - Current exemptions from post-trade reporting, which apply to certain non-price forming trades, will be retained or expanded.

   - The changes include an expanded definition of give-up and give-in trades, and a new exemption for investment firms that execute OTC transactions between entities within the same group.

- Data reporting

   - The FCA proposes a number of changes to the reportable fields in post-trade transaction reports.

      - removing some fields and flags, and adding a new field to identify the central counterparty used to clear the trade, where relevant.

   - The FCA also proposes to introduce a new unique product identifier field, alongside several further data fields, while still retaining the use of international securities identification number codes.

## SI definition

- The Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 ('FSMA 2023') confirmed that FCA rules will determine when trading is taking place on an organised, frequent, systematic and substantial basis.

- The FCA proposes to use qualitative terminology to define those terms in its handbook, and work with HM Treasury to revoke the current quantitative calculations firms must perform.

   - the FCA will provide additional interpretive guidance in its Perimeter Guidance Manual.

The FCA published its policy statement, with final rules in November 2024, with the final rules coming into effect on 1 December 2025.

The FCA noted that they were seeking a more proportionate and better calibrated transparency for bond and derivative markets, with requirements tailored to different asset classes and market structures, delivering:

- Greater transparency, in terms of timeliness and content of the information, for those financial instruments which would benefit most from increased disclosures, which will in turn support market liquidity.

- A lower cost of complying with the transparency regime for trading venues and investment firms.

- Adequate protection to market makers when providing liquidity to clients.

- Improved post-trade data will also support the creation of a CT for bonds in the UK.

Alongside the FCA's final rules on pre-trade and post-trade transparency, including the revised framework for waivers and deferrals for bonds and derivatives, the FCA also published a discussion paper on the future of the SI regime.

The closing date for comments on the SI Discussion Paper was 10 January 2025, the FCA noted that they would publish their formal consultation in Q2 2025 and the final requirements would come into to effect at the same time as the transparency requirements for bonds and derivates on 1 December 2025.

At the time that the workbook was updated and published the FCA had not published its consultation on the SI regime.

The SI Discussion Paper looked at the following topics:

## **Pre-trade transparency**

   - Following the removal for bonds and derivatives.

   - An obligation still applies for equities.

- **Post-trade transparency**

   - Following the introduction of the Designated Reporting Regime, there is no longer a link between a firms 'SI' status and the post-trade reporting obligation.

   - The FCA is looking to improve the quality of post-trade reporting – by identifying 'liquidity' providers.

- **Trading venue perimeter**

   - Looking to identify the distinction between the typ4es of trading venue:

      - 'multilateral' vs 'bilateral'.

- **Other issues**

   - SFTS and SIs

      - An entity is not permitted to operate both an OFT and act as an SI.

   - Transaction reporting

      - Looking how best to identify reports made by firms.

         - Undertaken bilaterally and off exchange – 'liquidity provider'.

- **Reporting to clients**

   - Would a change in the 'execution venue' identification field create issues for the requirements to provide an execution note to retail clients, when an investment firm carries out client orders.

- **Best execution**

   - Whether a client is relying on the firm to provide 'best execution' when acting as an SI.

## Reforming the Commodity Derivatives Regulatory Framework (CP23/27)

The FSMA 2023 transferred the principal responsibility for setting position limits from the FCA to trading venues/exchanges ('trading venues'). Although, to ensure that trading venues determine and operate position limits consistently, the FCA was tasked with developing rules on how the limits should be determined.

The FCA will therefore proactively monitor and supervise the trading venues; this approach was supported by stakeholders in response to the HM Treasury 2021 consultation on the WMR.

FSMA 2023 also revoked the requirement that position limits must be applied to all commodity derivatives traded on a trading venue and to EEOTC (economical equivalent OTC) contracts. It gave the FCA powers to establish the scope of the position limits regime, ie, to specify the commodity derivatives to which limits must apply, with the exception of OTC contracts, for which it is not possible to apply position limits.

FSMA 2023 also enables the FCA to establish a framework outlining how trading venues should apply position limits.

The FCA consultation sets out proposals concerning the key pillars of the regime:

- Setting of position limits by trading venues

   - The FCA will retain the power, under certain circumstances, to set position limits.

- Applying position limits only to certain commodity derivatives contracts:

   - The FCA will maintain a 'critical contracts register.

   - The application of position limits to certain critical commodity derivatives, which they have identified as:

      - a. the risk from abusive practices or disorderly trading carries the greatest potential negative impact to relevant markets in a way that threatens the objectives of the regime, and

      - b. position limits and the accompanying position management controls are effective arrangements to mitigate those risks.

- Enhanced position management controls and reporting:

   - trading venues must establish accountability thresholds for the contracts for which the positions list will apply:

   - a. accountability thresholds are defined as position management controls that support the early identification of risks before they crystallise.

- Exemptions from position limits:

   - Two 'new' exemptions from the requirements:

      - a. financial firms providing risk-mitigation services to non-financial firms hedging their commercial risk ('pass-through hedging exemption')

      - b. the second exemption for liquidity providers.

- Ancillary activities test (AAT).

In February 2025 the FCA published its Policy Statement with final rules, although there is a split implementation, the majority of the requirements come into effect on 6 July 2026.

Although the FCA proceeded with the requirements as consulted on, the industry did lobby on issues relate to the reporting by members of trading venues on their and their clients 'OTC' positions that are linked to contracts traded on trading venues.

While the final rules require trading venues to have powers in place under which they can obtain OTC position data, they did not specify in what circumstances they would need to collect such data – thereby giving freedom to trading venues to decides when and whether OTC positions would pose a risk to their specific market.

Prior to the rules coming into effect in July 2026, trading venues will be required to notify the FCA as to whether they consider that additional reporting is needed, and the details of additional reporting requirements.

_Source of information: FCA website_

## Improving UK Transaction Reporting Regime (DP24/2)

In November 2024 the FCA published a discussion paper on options for improving the transaction reporting and instrument reference data regimes.

The FCA noted in the discussion paper that their approach to developing the MiFID transaction Reporting regime is based on two main objectives:

- Improve the usefulness of transaction reporting data through better data quality.

- Support the competitiveness of UK markets by ensuring that the requirements remain appropriate for firms.

Therefore, the FCA is seeking feedback on a range of issues on the transaction reporting regime, issues considered include:

- whether transaction reporting requirements should be extended to CPMI firms

   - ie, AIFMs and UCITS managers

- whether OTC derivatives should be identified through an alternative identifier (rather than ISIN)

   - knock-on consequences this may have (including additional data requirements and potentially extending the instruments in scope of transaction reporting)

- whether SIs should be subject to reference data reporting requirements going forward, and

- whether existing transaction reporting fields should be amended and supplemented with new fields such as fields recording aggregate client link codes and digital token identifiers.

The FCA is expected to publish its formal consultation in Q2/Q3 2025, followed by a Policy Statement, with final rules, in Q4 2025 or Q1 2026.

## 1.3 UK Regulatory Reforms

## 1.3.1 Smarter Regulatory Framework (Financial Services and Markets Act 2023)

The 'Future Regulatory Framework' (FRF) review was announced by the Chancellor of the Exchequer in 2019; its aim was to ensure that, following Brexit the UK financial services regulatory framework was fit for purpose outside the EU. It is now referred to as the Smarter Regulatory Framework ('SMF') on the basis that, following the publication of the FSMA 2023, the UK regulatory framework is being redeveloped now, not in the future.

In a consultation published in 2020, by HM Treasury, it discussed the approach of having FSMA at the heart of all future UK financial services regulations and the importance of having expert and independent regulators.

In 2021, HM Treasury published their proposals for reform, stating that they will develop standards that allow a greater focus on growth and competitiveness in the UK by introducing new, statutory secondary objectives for the PRA and the FCA. It is also clear that the government is looking to replace or amend much of the EU retained law to make it fit into the 'FSMA model' and the UK market.

HM Treasury now has the powers to make the required primary legislation – this has provided new powers to the PRA and the FCA (as noted below). Following this, the PRA and the FCA would publish their own consultations in order to fulfil the purpose of the Act and the reshaping of the UK regulatory landscape.

The summary of the 16 'reform' proposals which make up the FSMA 2023 are set out below:

- **Growth and international competitiveness objectives**

      - Secondary objectives for the PRA and the FCA.

   - This will complement the existing three operational objectives of the FCA and sit alongside the PRA's secondary objective.

- **Incorporation of climate change into the regulatory principles**

   - Amending regulatory principles to identify that 'growth' must occur in a sustainable manner that is consistent with the UK government's commitment to a net zero economy by 2050.

- **Requirements for regulators (PRA/FCA) to respond to HM Treasury recommendations letters**

   - A new statutory requirement for the PRA and the FCA to respond to government recommendations.

      - Currently HM Treasury must make at least one recommendation per Parliament sitting, but the PRA and the FCA are not obliged to respond.

      - The FCA and the PRA (Prudential Regulatory Committee) response should contain an overview of:

         - how they have taken into account each of the recommendations, and

         - the impact on policy that has resulted from recommendations.

- **Power for HM Treasury to require the PRA and the FCA to conduct a rule review**

   - HM Treasury will be granted powers to require (force) the PRA/FCA to undertake a review of their rules when it is considered in the public interest.

   - The PRA and the FCA are not currently required to formally review their rules.

- **Overseas deference arrangements and trade agreements accountability mechanisms**

   - As the PRA and the FCA will be responsible for setting the rules that apply to firms, they will now have an obligation for considering potential impacts on deference arrangements and assess compliance with trade agreements:

      - FSMA does not currently require the PRA or the FCA to assess the impact of their activities on HM Treasury's 'deference' arrangements.

- **Requirement to notify the relevant committee of a consultation**

   - The PRA and the FCA must inform the relevant Parliamentary Committee when they publish a consultation.

- **Statutory requirement for the PRA and the FCA to respond to Parliament**

   - The PRA and the FCA must respond in writing to all formal statutory consultations from parliamentary committees.

- **Placing the FCA's Listing Authority Advisory Panel and the PRA Practitioner Panel insurance sub-committee on a statutory panel**

   - Levelling up to have a consistent approach with the existing FCA panels

- **Statutory requirement for the PRA and the FCA to publish information on their engagement with the panels**

   - To ensure clear and consistent communication on their engagement with panels.

- **Statutory requirement for the PRA and the FCA to maintain a statement on appointment for the panels**

   - To ensure that panels are diverse and to strengthen the role of the panels

- **Statutory requirement for the PRA and the FCA to publish a CBA framework**

   - The PRA and the FCA will be required to publish and maintain a public version of their CBA framework.

   - The government will expect the framework will include the following:

      - establishing when a CBA is required

      - methodology involved in different aspects of CBA

      - how representations at consultations are considered.

- **Establish a statutory panel to support development of regulators approach to CBA**

   - The creation of a statutory panel to support the development of the PRA's and FCA's CBA framework.

- **Requirement for the regulator to publish and maintain a framework for reviewing its rules**

   - The PRA and the FCA will be required to publish a framework on how they will conduct reviews on their rules.

   - The criteria will be developed by the PRA and the FCA, but must include:

      - differences between a purpose of review and what review was conducted

      - criteria for assessing and deciding what type of review is undertaken

      - criteria for deciding when the review will be undertaken.

   - This is something that happens in EU legislation, where within a newly published Directive or Regulation there is a formal review process that must be undertaken within a set timeframe by the European Commission.

- **Power to repeal parts of EU law, including the direct regulatory requirements that apply to firms**

   - The move to a comprehensive 'FSMA model' of regulation.

   - The PRA and the FCA will take responsibility for setting many of the direct regulatory requirements which are currently set out in the retained EU law:

      - changing the statutory objective and principles of the PRA and the FCA.

   - The government intends to formulate a power to repeal the 'direct' regulatory requirements that apply in retained EU law – the PRA and the FCA will then be responsible for determining rules applying to firms via their rulebook (PRA) and handbook (FCA).

- **Create a new 'Designated Activities Regime' to enable the PRA and the FCA to make rules for such activities**

   - The creation of a designated activities regime will allow the regulation of these activities that will sit outside the FSMA authorisation process:

      - there are some pieces of retained EU law which are not FSMA regulated activities: ◊ ie, short selling regulation, margin rules.

- **The ability to establish activity specific 'have regards' and obligations**

   - Placing an obligation on the PRA and the FCA relating to making rules in specific areas of regulation.

- Bringing financial market infrastructure firms into FSMA regulation:

   - ie, central counterparties, central securities depositories, trade repositories, credit rating agencies, recognised investment exchanges, payments systems and e-money.

## 1.3.3 Independent 'Investment Research Review'

Following on from the proposals noted in the Edinburgh Reforms, an independent investment research review was undertaken, with a report published in July 2023. The report made the following recommendations:

1. Introduce a research platform to help generate research.

2. Allow additional optionality for paying for investment research.

3. Allow greater access to investment research for retail investors.

4. Involve academic institutions in supporting investment research initiatives.

5. Support issuer-sponsored research by implementing a code of conduct.

6. Clarify aspects of the UK regulatory regime for investment research and consider introducing a bespoke regime.

7. Review the rules relating to investment research in the context of IPOs.

The report identified that the main concerns relating to the impact of MiFID are as follows:

- It has not necessarily led to more transparent pricing of research, nor to greater availability of different sources of research.

- The main basis on which client funds can be used to pay for research under MiFID II (the research payment account) is considered unnecessarily complex and, therefore, is rarely used.

- The decision of many asset managers to pay for external research from their own resources (the P&L model) and to increase their own internal research capabilities has reduced the amount spent on external research and, therefore, the quality and availability of that research.

   - There are arguments that this may have a detrimental effect on investment returns.

   - The increased use of the P&L model has also meant that the research that has been commissioned is, understandably, less likely to become widely available.

- Payment for investment research through the P&L model may prove unsustainable or subject to further reduction – for example, in the event of a market downturn that leads to a reduction in asset manager fee income, with the availability of research being arguably most important in times of market stress.

   - Similarly, if demand for investment research was to increase significantly, for example due to increased investment in smaller cap companies by DC pension schemes or because of increased ESG requirements, not all buy-side firms may be able to sustain the financing of research payments through the P&L model.

- Greater regulatory requirements regarding the process for the appointment of researchers have led to some asset managers limiting the number of providers from whom they receive research.

- The reduction in research spending has led to less willingness to engage in speculative and innovative research, including in respect of niche and growth areas, including ESG-related companies and issues.

In April 2024 the FCA published a consultation paper titled 'Payment Optionality for Investment Research'. The FCA proposals follow the independent Investment Research Review, which noted that the MiFID II unbundling requirements deteriorated the quality and availability of investment research, capturing UK portfolio managers (ie, managers of pension schemes and other funds). The new requirements came into effect from 1 August 2024.

In addition, the FCA also proposed (in consultation paper CP24//21) to apply the requirements to UK asset managers, to pay for investment research, enabling them to purchase investment research using 'bundled' payments for third-party research and execution services.

This option will be in addition to those already available (such as paying from their own resources or charging clients through a Research Payment Account).

In scope UK portfolio and asset managers would be able to use this option, providing they meet certain requirements, for example:

- A formal policy describing the firm's approach.

- A budget for the amount of third-party research to be purchased (which is to be reviewed annually).

- Ongoing assessments on value and price.

- An approach to allocate the costs across their clients, ensuring the outcome is fair and the costs commensurate with the benefits.

- A structure for the allocation of payments across research providers.

- Operational procedures for the administration of accounts used to purchase research.

- Disclosures to clients on the firm's use of bundled payments, the key features of its approach, if/ how bundled payments are combined with other payment options, their most significant research providers, and the costs incurred.

The new requirements are aimed at the buyers of research (ie, UK asset managers/fund managers), not the producers of research (ie, banks, brokers and independent research providers).

The FCA noted that the new requirements will have no impact on providers of research who do not generate research on equities which are traded on public markets (ie. Providers of 'FICC' only research and/or macroeconomic research).

The FCA noted that this consultation paper only focused on recommendation 2, wider points noted ion the IRR report (such as recommendations 3, 6 & 7) will be considered in due course by the FCA.

## 1.3.4 UK Short Selling Regulation – HM Treasury 'Call for Evidence'

In December 2022 following the announcement of the Edinburgh Reforms, HM Treasury published a call for evidence on the 'Short Selling Regulation' (SSR).

HM Treasury noted their intention to design a 'short selling regulation' tailored to the needs of UK markets, companies and investors.

It did acknowledge the need for regulation of short selling in a way that supports market integrity and public confidence.

The review of UK SSR intends to ensure the UK's approach to regulating the short selling of shares admitted to trading reflects the specificities of UK markets, whilst protecting market participants and supporting market integrity.

However, the call for evidence did not explore other provisions in UK SSR – such as UK sovereign debt and UK sovereign credit default swaps. HM Treasury noted that it will consider this in the later part of the process to repeal the retained EU law and replace it with a regulatory framework tailored to the UK market.

It followed this up with a responses publication in 2023, where it noted that it the intention to remove requirements currently placed on investors when taking out short positions in sovereign debt or sovereign CDS, and the related reporting requirements under the UK's new short selling regime.

## 1.3.5 Accelerated Settlement Taskforce (T+1)

In 2024 HM Treasury published the report from the 'Accelerated Settlement Taskforce', which it set up in following the announcement of the Edinburgh Reforms.

The key recommendations are noted below, which the UK government accepted in full and subsequently set up the Technical Group to take this work forward.

- the UK should commit to moving to a T+1 settlement cycle

- this move should take place no later than 31 December 2027

- the UK and other European jurisdictions should collaborate closely to see if a coordinated move to T+1 is possible, and if other European jurisdictions commit to a transition date then the UK should consider whether it wishes to align with that timeline

- a Technical Group of industry experts should be set up to determine the technical and operational changes necessary for the transition to T+1 happen and how these should be implemented. This group should select a date in 2025 for these changes to be mandated, and a date before the end of 2027 for the UK transition to T+1

- irrespective of the migration date to T+1 appropriate operational changes should be mandated with effect from a date in 2025 determined by the Technical Group including the following:

   - market standards for onboarding all new accounts to include all data necessary to settle a trade;

   - electronic processes for sharing SSIs

   - market standards for allocations, confirmations and trade level matching to take place on trade date

   - market standards to be established for Settlement instructions to be sent on trade date

   - market standards for securities lending recalls

   - CREST to remain open beyond 8pm for transaction input until a time agreed with the Technical Group, and

   - the UK time at which the Trade Date should be deemed to end.

It is no surprise that the Taskforce recommended a move to T+1, especially as the EU is also looking at this issue.

ESMA recently published feedback and findings from its recent consultation, it is now formally responding to the European Commission, ESMA will be recommending a move to T+1 within a suitable timeframe.

In September 2024 the Accelerated Settlement Taskforce (technical group) published a draft recommendations report and consultation.

The report recommended a move to T+1 settlement in the UK, no later than the end of 2027.

The report identified the following 'principal' and 'additional' recommendations, that would enable a smooth transition to the implementation of T+1:

- **Success Criteria**

   - what this looks like, and how it can be achieved

      - regulatory and supervisory support, industry 'Code of Conduct'

- **Settlement**

   - what this looks like, and how it can be achieved

      - settlement deadlines, performance benchmarks/monitoring, contractual arrangements

- **Financial Market Infrastructure**

   - Rulebook reconciliation and supervision

- **Static Data**

   - SSI and KYC market best practice

- **Corporate Actions**

   - Dividend processing and claims

- **Securities Financing**

   - Recall process and impacts for stock lending

- **'FX'**

   - Settlement deadlines / cut off times

- **Environmental (additional recommendations)**

- Trading hours, impact of the consolidated tape, cash management, onboarding process

- Regulatory scope.

In February 2025 the Accelerated Settlement Taskforce Technical Group published its UK implementation plan for the UK's move to a T+1 settlement cycle. The Technical Group's report recommended 12 'critical' and 27 'highly recommended' actions to facilitate a successful transition to T+1.

## **The report recommended that the UK move to T+1 on 11 October 2027.**

Following the publication, HM Treasury welcomed the report and agreed with its proposals, they noted that they will bring forward the required primary legislation to facilitate the move to a T+1 settlement cycle (amending UK CSDR). On this basis, they stated that firms, and the industry, should begin preparations for 11 October 2027 to be the first day of trading under a T+1 standard.

HM Treasury also noted another important factor is the potential for alignment with other European jurisdictions. An aligned move to T+1 across Europe is a highly desirable outcome which would minimise costs for firms. The government confirmed that it is engaging with European partners to support this outcome and notes the growing consensus that 11 October 2027 is an appropriate implementation date.

## 1.3.6 The MiFID Organisational Regulation

In November 2024 HM Treasury committed to make further changes to the MiFID framework as part of the Smarter Regulatory Framework, one element of this was the revoking of the MiFID Organisational Regulations, retained EU Law, so that they can be moved into the FCA's Handbook.

The MiFID Organisational Regulation ('MiFID Org Reg') contains firm-facing conduct rules and systems and controls rules to ensure market integrity and investor protection, the UK version applies directly to UK MiFID investment firms.

The MiFID Org Reg provides detailed requirements for firms and contains key conduct and systems and controls obligations for investment firms. The regulation is broad in scope, covering various requirements including:

- client categorisation

- disclosure

- suitability

- reporting to clients

- best execution

- inducements

- research

- conflicts of interest

- business continuity

- outsourcing

- control procedures and security mechanisms

- risk, compliance and internal audit functions

- remuneration policies

- telephone taping.

In implementing MiFID II in 2018, the FCA replicated parts of the MiFID Org Reg into their Handbook for certain sourcebooks so that firms could see all the relevant MiFID requirements relating to a particular topic in one place.

The consultation sets out the FCA's proposals for replacing the firm-facing requirements in the MiFID Org Reg with Handbook rules, once HM Treasury commences the repeal of the MiFID Org Reg.

The FCA has set out specific changes made to each sourcebook and the rationale behind these changes. The sourcebooks and non-Handbook guidance manuals include:

- The Conduct of Business sourcebook (COBS)

- The Senior Management Arrangements, Systems and Controls sourcebook (SYSC)

- The Market Conduct sourcebook (MAR)

- The Recognised Investment Exchanges sourcebook (REC)

- The Dispute Resolution sourcebook (DISP).

The origin of the MiFID Org Reg as a directly applicable EU regulation meant that in relation to the scope and application of the provisions, the FCA could only replicate in its Handbook with the abbreviation 'EU' at the start of the provision (becoming a 'UK' abbreviation after the EU withdrawal implementation period). This denoted that the MiFID Org Reg provisions were copied out legislative material from the EU, rather than UK (FCA)-made rules.

In addition, the FCA is seeking feedback on potential improvements of the rules – Chapter 4 of the consultation paper is effectively a 'discussion paper'.

## FCA Approach

The FCA has noted that they are intending to restate the existing firm-facing requirements from the MiFID Org Reg into their Handbook as rules and guidance with no or minimum changes.

They are not proposing any new requirements on firms as part of these changes, nor do they propose removing any existing requirements.

They have noted that provisions which will not be replaced in regulatory rules in their Handbook, will either be restated or repealed by HM Treasury.

## **Chapter 4 - Future Amendments to EU Derived Organisational and Conduct Rules**

The majority of the FCA's conduct and organisational rules are derived from EU Directives and their implementing measures. A number of EU directives, such as UCITS Directive, AIFMD, IDD, MiFID and the Capital Requirements Directive (CRD), included common operating standards for firms, such as requirements to maintain adequate policies, procedures and control functions and responsibilities of management bodies ('organisational rules').

They also included broadly similar standards on how firms conduct their activity with clients, such as conflicts of interest rules and information to be provided to clients.

The FCA's approach on implementation of EU derived Directives (rules) was to apply a common standard to firms conducting similar activities. For example, the common platform requirements in SYSC were devised to ensure a single set of organisational requirements would apply to firms subject to either or both MiFID and the CRD, rather than applying similar but slightly different requirements from these directives on the same business functions.

However, the FCA have noted that this has become increasing challenging over time. This has resulted in duplicate rules across the FCA Handbook. Therefore, as part of the process of moving assimilated laws (retained EU rules) into the FCA Handbook, the FCA are looking at simplifying some of these requirements to make them more relevant to the UK Market, removing them where they are not relevant to the UK market.

The FCA noted potential areas in which they could rationalise, or improve the rules, for the benefit of firms without negatively impacting client protections, which include:

- Reducing the complexity and length of the Handbook by rationalising largely duplicative conduct and organisational rules derived from various EU directives (mostly in COBS and SYSC) and removing the distinctions in rules between types of firms if there is no substantive difference in obligations.

- Replacing ineffective distinctions between different types of firms by tailoring or calibrating rules to better suit different sectors, or to better enable technological development and innovation.

- Introducing flexibility on how firms can meet existing obligations, taking a more outcomes-based approach where this may allow firms to better meet the needs of clients – including retail and wholesale.

- Considering whether certain rules do not meet their intended objective, either because they have never been effective (either generally or when applied in particular context) or because they have become redundant due to market or technological development.

- Rationalising the body of level 3 and other materials firms may rely on to interpret our rules.

The closing date for comments on the main body of the consultation is the 28 February 2025. However, the closing date for comments on Chapter 4 (Future amendments to EU derived Organisational and Conduct Rules) is 28 March 2025.

Separately, the the PRA published a consultation paper in April 2025. Both the FCA and the PRA will publish separate Policy Statements, with final rules in H2 2025.

## 1.4 Future Disclosure Framework

## 1.4.1 A New Retail Disclosure Framework

In November 2023, HM Treasury published it long awaited UK retail disclosure framework, which will see 'Consumer Composite Investments' (CCIs) replace the UK PRIIPS regime.

HM Treasury proposed to:

- replace the concept of a PRIIP and define products in scope of the UK framework as 'consumer composite investments'

- regulate manufacturing, advising and offering a CCI to a UK retail investor via the designated activities regime and provide the FCA with rule-making powers

- restate FCA supervisory and enforcement powers on product intervention and suspension

- set civil liability for pre-contractual disclosures, and

- provide a transition period for UCITS KIIDs.

Overall, the instrument scope of the new regime appears to be very similar to the existing UK PRIIPs rules. However, the draft Statutory Instrument could result in a much broader population of firms captured as 'manufacturers' of CCIs, as the published draft Statutory Instrument borrows from the Consumer Duty the definition of a 'manufacturer'.

The HM Treasury draft Statutory Instrument does not prescribe the actual disclosure requirements, this will be undertaken by the FCA.

In terms of timing, it is anticipated that the new framework will come into effect from 1 January 2027.

## 1.4.2 FCA Consultation Paper – A New Product Information Framework for Consumer Composite Investments

In December 2024 the FCA published a consultation paper (CP24/30) with proposals, and draft rules, for the new Consumer Composite Investments (CCI) regime, which will replace PRIIPS.

The key parts of the CCI regime are contained in secondary legislation that was made by HM Treasury in November 2024. It provides a definition of a CCI, along with exclusions. It also sets out the relevant designated activities under the regime and grants the FCA power to make rules for the new framework.

The legislation defines a CCI as:

- a. an investment

- b. a contract of insurance, or

- c. any right to or interest in anything listed in sub-paragraphs (a) or (b),

where the value or amount payable to the investor is subject to fluctuations because of exposure to reference values or to the performance of one or more assets which are not directly purchased by the investor.

- The below is a summary of the FCA proposals contained in the consultation paper:

The FCA will clarify the products it envisages to fall within scope, which include:

- structured deposits

- structured products — securities which embed a derivative, or include features equivalent to a derivative contract

- debt securities that are currently classified as PRIIPs

- securities issued by open-ended funds, or rights to or interests in such securities

- securities issued by closed-ended investment funds (including investment trusts and venture capital trusts)

- contingent convertible securities

- contracts for difference

- insurance-based investment products (with-profits polices, unit-linked policies, and Holloway sickness policies).

The FCA has Acknowledged the difficulties issuers face ensuring that their securities intended for non-retail investors are not sold to any retail investors, therefore, the FCA is proposing to add rules disapplying the CCI when an offer of readily realisable securities meets all of the following criteria:

- The marketing materials (including prospectus, where relevant) feature clear and prominent disclosures that the product is not intended for retail investors, and the CCI is only being offered to professional clients or eligible counterparties.

- The issuer has taken reasonable steps to ensure the offer and associated promotional communications are only directed at non-retail investors.

- A minimum investment of £50,000 applies for each end-investor.

The FCA is proposing that the following requirements for manufactures and distributors, will include:

## Manufacturers

- Calculate core product information in line with detailed methodologies in FCA rules.

   - Core information disclosures would include basic information on the CCI, as well as more standardised information on costs and charges, risk, and performance.

- Prepare a machine-readable file.

- Produce a product summary for each CCI.

- Publish the machine-readable file and product summary on a publicly accessible website.

- Share additional detail with distributors and respond to feedback from distributors.

- Ensure the product summary and underlying core information that manufacturers produce remain accurate as long as a CCI continues to be distributed to retail clients.

   - review and update product summary and core product information as necessary, and at least every 12 months

   - flag any material changes in the product information to distributors.

## Distributors

- Review product information and product summary prepared by the manufacturer.

   - verify that the product summary remains up to date.

- Decide whether additional explanatory information is appropriate to support consumer understanding.

- Decide whether to use the manufacturer's product summary or amend it.

   - Where the distributors create their own product summaries, they are responsible for ensuring they contain the required information and meet regulatory requirements.

- Communicate relevant information to customers pre-sale.

- Provide a durable medium product summary to customers after every sale.

- Share feedback and information with manufacturers on their communications and products

## Disclosure Obligations

The FCA proposes that the CCI product summary would replace the PRIIPs KID and UCITS KID.

The FCA is proposing that the product summary contains certain prescribed information:

- issue date

- investment objectives

- investment policy

- information about risk

- past performance

- costs and charges

- Financial Ombudsman Service/Financial Services Compensation Scheme eligibility.

With regards to the disclosure of costs and charges, the FCA's proposed rules will only to the calculation and presentation of product costs. The proposed costs and charges requirements:

- Provide distributors with the product's aggregated ongoing cost percentage for each CCI.

- **Summary cost illustration** must show product costs over a single holding period:

   - consists of entry costs, exit costs, ongoing costs, and transaction costs

   - presented as a percentage and as GBP figures over a 12-month period

   - £10,000 as the representative investment, or £1,000 each year for CCIs with regular contributions

   - performance fees or carried interest are not included in this figure.

- A **baseline cost methodology** for all products and specific considerations for certain product types. Firms must disclose all direct and indirect costs and charges associated with a product. The types of costs and charges to disclose would remain broadly unchanged from existing requirements:

   - one-off entry, one-off exit, ongoing and transactional costs, each of which would need to be calculated and disclosed as a percentage figure, alongside an aggregated summary of these costs

   - performance fees and carried interest, which would need to be identified and explained in easily understandable language.

- **Transaction costs** – while firms should calculate and disclose transaction costs for CCIs, the FCA will consult on amendments to the existing methodology (used under the PRIIPs regime) in early 2025.

- **Ongoing costs** – requirement to present a single, aggregated ongoing costs percentage for CCIs. The FCA is not proposing to require that specific ongoing cost components must be highlighted to investors.

- **One-off and other costs** – requirement for separate disclosure of any one-off entry or exit costs, contingent costs like performance fees and carried interest, and transaction costs:

   - transaction costs should be disclosed separately to other ongoing costs

   - Performance fees or carried interest should be communicated through a simple explanation of how the performance fee is structured, alongside a practical example of what this fee could amount to.

## Risk

In relation to risk, the FCA proposes that the existing 1–7 risk metric be replaced with a 1–10 risk metric based on product volatility, along with descriptions which balance the material risk and potential rewards to provide a more holistic picture. The FCA proposes a minimum risk score for certain products that are deemed high-risk or those of unknown volatility.

## Past Performance

The FCA is proposing that product summaries would need to present past performance of products in a standardised graphic manner covering a ten-year period (unless that data is not available). The FCA is not proposing that firms provide general information about the key factors that determine the performance of the product for all products.

In April 2025 the FCA published a following up consultation, with further proposals on product information for Consumer Composite Investment (CP25/9).

The consulted on remaining issues to support the 'CCI' regime:

- Proposals on the calculation of transaction costs to remove the requirement for firms to calculate and disclose implicit transaction costs:

   - this simplification would remove a significant compliance burden for firms, while ensuring that consumers are still provided with the most relevant information about the transaction costs of their chosen product.

- Proposed revisions to simplify current cost disclosure rules reflecting assimilated requirements of the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID) Organisation Regulation (MiFID Org Reg) and bring them into line with our proposals for CCIs.

- Provide more detail, and set out proposed drafting, on transitional provisions to allow firms flexibility to move across to the new regime as soon as they are ready, and on basic complaints handling requirements in the Product Disclosure Sourcebook (DISC) for non-authorised persons in scope of the CCI regime.

- Proposed consequential amendments to the FCA Handbook that result from the replacement of the previous regimes with new CCI rules.

The FCA noted that they will publish a single Policy Statement, with feedback, to both consultations, along with final rules in late 2025.

The FCA noted that the new regime will come into force when the Policy Statement is published, subject to a proposed 18-month transition period.

During the transition period, the FCA plans to allow firms to follow either the new CCI rules or continue to comply with the PRIIPs rules.

## 1.5 Prospectus Regime Review

Following on from the HM Treasury consultation of 2021 and its review outcome report published in March 2022, further details were included in the Edinburgh Reforms concerning the UK Prospectus Regime.

Included in the Edinburgh Reforms, announced in December 2022, was a _'Public offers and admissions to trading statutory instrument'_ , which will replace the current UK Prospectus Regulation and introduce a new framework governing public offers and the admission to trading of securities.

The draft legislation provides the FCA power to redesign prospectus contents and will also result in a reduction of the instances that a prospectus is required.

In particular the draft instrument will create a new 'designated activity of:

- offering relevant securities to the public in the UK and communicating advertisements or other information in relation to such offers

   - these include transferable securities and other securities and investment products

- admitting, or seeking or obtaining admission, of, transferable securities to a regulated market and communicating related advertisements or disclosures,

- admitting, or seeking or obtaining admission of, transferable securities on a 'primary MTF'

   - communicating related advertisements of disclosures

   - primary MTFs include AIM and other markets that impose obligations on traded issuers and are not merely secondary trading venues.

The Financial Services and Market Act 2023 provides the FCA with powers to make designated activity rules. The FCA's rules for prospectuses and admissions to a regulated market will be called the 'admission rules'.

## 1.5.1 Regulated Markets

Admission rules made by the FCA will specify when a prospectus is required, the content and form and the procedures for submission to and approval by the FCA and how and when the prospectuses must be published.

Despite the proposed changes, many of the core principles and definitions are expected to remain in place, including the need for a prospectus for IPOs. However, a prospectus will not necessarily be required by the offering to the public.

## 1.5.2 Junior Markets

The FCA will have the powers to require primary MTF operators to develop rules that impose an obligation on issuers, to have a 'prospectus' as a condition of admission of securities to trading.

In November 2023, as part of the next steps of replacing the EU derived prospectus rules, HM Treasury published a draft Statutory Instrument titled _'The Public Offers and Admissions to Trading Regulations 2023'_ , which has been laid before Parliament for approval. The application of the Instrument will happen in two phases, firstly the FCA will be granted new powers enabling them to make rules, provide guidance and directions and issue statement of policy. The requirements of the new regime will come into force when the FCA makes its rules using these powers, which it will do so following the formal consultation process.

## 1.6.1 HM Treasury Consultation and Call for Evidence – 'Future Financial Services Regulatory Regime for Cryptoassets'

In February 2023 HM Treasury published a consultation on its proposed financial services regime for cryptoassets. It is noted in the consultation that the UK's aim is to be the home to the most open, wellregulated and technology advanced capital market in the world.

HM Treasury's objective with the consultation is to establish a proportionate and clear regulatory framework which enables firms to innovate while maintaining financial stability and clearly focused regulatory standards. This will include bring into financial services regulatory scope centralised cryptoasset exchanges as well as core activities such as lending and custody.

The consultation closed in April 2023.

The below is a summary of the HM Treasury consultation and call for evidence:

## Regulatory Framework

- The consultation proposed to expand the list of 'specified investments' in the Regulated Activities Order (RAO) to include cryptoassets, which forms the basis of the rest of the proposal.

- Amendments to FSMA, made through the Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 affirms the use of the RAO power for the financial services regulation of cryptoassets:

   - This clarifies that individuals who carry out certain activities involving cryptoassets would be performing regulated activities and therefore require authorisation under FSMA:

      - HM Treasury confirmed that it does not intend to expand the definition of 'financial instrument' in the RAO to include presently unregulated cryptoassets.

- The FCA will need to consider whether to update the SYSC sourcebook, which contains the SM&CR regime, and other financial crime rules to apply to the new cryptoasset activities that are outlined in the Consultation.

- HM Treasury confirms that firms which are FSMA authorised and intend to undertake these new activities will need to apply for a variation of their permission from the FCA (and the PRA for dualregulated firms):

   - But, it noted that permission would not be automatically granted for firms which are already authorised:

      - it notes that the FCA will adopt a _'timely and proportionate authorisation process'_ for existing registered firms, in order to avoid duplicative information requests of businesses, taking into account the supervisory history of businesses during the authorisation process.

## Cryptoasset Activities

- Although it is proposed that cryptoassets be a specified investments, the consultation is clear that it is activities in relation to cryptoassets that will be regulated rather than the underlying tokens:

   - asset- and crypto-backed tokens, as well as algorithmic stablecoins are not given any special prominence, but will be subject to the same requirements as 'unbacked crypto assets'

   - a separate FCA regime (similar to the payments and e-money rules) will apply to fiat-backed stablecoins, which is to be defined in the statutory instrument expected to be laid in 2023:

      - HM Treasury confirmed that further details on the approach to fiat-backed stablecoins will be published

   - all cryptoassets, including NFTs and utility tokens, have the potential to be included in the future regulatory perimeter if a specified activity is carried out in relation to them.

## Territorial Scope and Equivalence

- The consultation proposes to bring all activities in relation to cryptoassets within scope to the extent they are carried out in or to the UK:

   - under FSMA, a requirement to be authorised applies to firms that carry on activities by way of business 'in the United Kingdom'. When an activity is deemed to be carried on 'in' the UK is different for different activities.

- This is a significant shift, and will capture both activities provided by UK firms (whether to clients in or outside of the UK) as well as activities provided by overseas firms to UK persons:

   - the approach being proposed by HM Treasury is that the ability to operate offshore and service UK customers will be pretty limited.

- It is not clear yet whether firms carrying out these type of activities would be required to have a physical presence in the UK in order to obtain authorisation, the consultation does suggest that it may be possible for equivalence type arrangements, whereby firms authorised in third countries can provide services in the UK without needing a UK presence, provided they are subject to equivalent standards.

## FSCS Protections

- The consultation suggests that it is not their intention for the FSCS protections to apply to investor losses arising from cryptoasset exposures – although this is a decision that the PRA and FCA will make.

- However, FSCS protection could be available for claims against failed authorised cryptoasset custodians.

## Exemptions

- The consultation also suggests formalising the concept of reverse solicitation for cryptoasset activities that are provided from overseas companies, ie, if a UK customer accessed a particular cryptoasset service entirely at their own initiative from an overseas firm and the firm does not solicit from the customer, then the firm will not be require FSMA-authorisation.

## The Activities

The Consultation provides a helpful table summarising the activities covered, both existing and new, as well as providing further proposals in relation to some specific activities.

## Operating a Cryptoasset Trading Venue

- Operating a cryptoasset trading venue will become a regulated activity under the RAO.

- The consultation proposes to establish a regulatory framework which is based on existing RAO activities of regulated trading venues – including the operation of an MTF.

- It is not clear if exchanges will have to be in the UK:

   - HM Treasury's view is that trading venues have a critical role in the cryptoasset value chain

   - there are, however, wider 'arranging' permissions, similar to those currently in existence for securities

   - it may be possible for offshore firms to obtain permissions may be available to obtain for offshore firms.

## Cryptoasset Intermediation Activities

- The proposals that apply to analogous regulated activities – such as 'arranging deals in investments' and 'making arrangements with a view to transactions in investments' (set out in the RAO) will be adapted for cryptoasset market intermediation activities.

## Cryptoasset Lending Platforms

- The proposals introduce a new activity of 'operating a cryptoasset lending platform':

   - this will include facilitating collateralised and uncollateralised borrowing of cryptoassets or borrowing of fiat currency with collateral provided in cryptoassets.

## Cryptoasset Custodians

- HM Treasury is proposing to apply and adapt the CASS Rules for cryptoasset custody activities, making suitable modifications to accommodate unique cryptoasset features:

   - the consultation proposes that the activity in relation to cryptoassets would be broader than the current RAO, as it will capture firms that only safeguard (but do not administer) assets (eg, firms that solely safeguard cryptographic private keys which provide access to cryptoassets)

   - this broader definition is in line with the definition of cryptoasset custodians in the Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (Amendment) Regulations 2019 (MLRs).

- The Consultation also notes that there is a Law Commission consultation currently exploring whether English and Welsh law needs to be adapted to accommodate digital assets – this looks specifically at custody arrangements:

   - HM Treasury confirmed that they will consider the findings of this consultation when drafting legislation specific to the custody of cryptoassets.

- HM Treasury is also considering what liability standards will need to be applied to custodians:

   - this leads to the suggestion that the government is looking to take a proportionate approach which may not impose full, uncapped liability on the custodian in the event of a malfunction or hack that was not within the custodian's control.

## Cryptoasset Issuance and Disclosures

- The consultation proposes to establish an tailored issuance and disclosures regime for cryptoassets

- There will be two regulatory trigger points as to when a cryptoasset issuance has deemed to have occurred:

   - admitting (or seeking the admission of) a cryptoasset to a cryptoasset trading venue

   - making a public offer of cryptoassets (including ICOs).

- Where cryptoassets are admitted to a cryptoasset trading venue, the FCA would specify principles in their rule book for admission and disclosure requirements that cryptoasset trading venues would then be responsible for administering:

   - cryptoasset trading venues would be responsible for writing more detailed content requirements for admission and disclosure documents as well as performing due diligence on the entity admitting the cryptoasset

   - liability will be attached to the preparer of the documents (this could be the issuer or the trading venue)

   - where there is no issuer (eg, Bitcoin), the trading venue would be required to take on the responsibilities of the issuer if they wish to admit the asset to trading

   - trading venues would be required to reject the admission of cryptoassets where they consider that it may result in investor detriment.

## Market Abuse

- The consultation proposes to introduce a cryptoassets market abuse regime based on elements of UK MAR:

   - this will include offences covering insider dealing, market manipulation and unlawful disclosure of inside information.

- HM Treasury intends that offences against market abuse would apply to all persons committing market abuse on a cryptoasset that is requested to be admitted to trading on a UK trading venue:

   - exchanges and venues will have to develop and out in place suitable systems and controls to monitor and report on trading activities in order to detect these abuses:

      - this would apply regardless of where the person is based or where the trading takes place

      - the consultation notes that there are significant challenges dealing with market abuse in the cryptoasset industry, due to its globalised, fragmented, and borderless nature of cryptoasset markets – HM Treasury notes that they do not expect to be able to achieve the same outcomes as UK MAR.

## 1.6.2 Digital Pound: A New Form of Money for Households and Businesses – HM Treasury & Bank of England Consultation

The Bank of England and HM Treasury noted that based on work undertaken to date, it is likely a digital pound will be needed in the future.

A UK central bank digital currency – a 'digital pound' – would be a new form of digital money for use by households and businesses for their everyday payments needs. As part of the wider landscape of money and payments it would sit alongside, not replace, cash – a digital counterpart to familiar, trusted banknotes and coins, subject to rigorous standards of privacy and data protection.

A digital pound would help to ensure that central bank money remains available and useful in an ever more digital economy, continuing to bolster UK monetary and financial stability while safeguarding the UK's monetary sovereignty in a changing global financial system. It could provide a platform for private sector innovation, promoting further choice, competition, efficiency and innovation in payments. It could also have further benefits for the resilience and functionality of payments in the UK.

Any future digital pound would be a major piece of national infrastructure which would likely take several years to complete. Its launch would require deep public trust in this new form of money – trust that their money would remain safe, accessible, and private.

A digital pound would be a retail central bank digital currency (CBDC) – digital money for use by households and businesses for their everyday payments, issued by the central bank, the Bank of England.

The consultation notes that it is too early to take the decision on whether to introduce the digital pound, rather that decision will be made in the coming years taking account of developments in money and payments and based the findings of this consultation as well as the investigation into the operational features and technology needed to deliver a digital pound.

However, for now, the Bank and HM Treasury's priority is to step up development work, build the necessary skills and put in place the technical capability to introduce the digital pound in a timely manner, should a decision be made in future to do so.

The Consultation Paper (CP) focused on a proposal for a retail CBDC, designed for everyday payments by households and businesses. By contrast, a 'wholesale CBDC' would be used to settle high-value payments between financial firms.

In 2024 the Bank of England and HM Treasury published an update on the feedback to their consultation paper. They advised that they received over 50,000 responses to the consultation, noting that many respondents raised concerns about the implications of such a digital pound for access to cash users' privacy, and control of their money.

Since the Consultation Paper was published, the Government has committed to introducing primary legislation before launching such a digital pound, ensuring Parliamentary input into any decision to proceed. Reflecting respondents' feedback to the Consultation Paper, this response makes clear that legislation introduced by the Government for a digital pound would need to provide protections to guarantee users' privacy and control of their money.

Respondents will have further opportunities to share their thoughts on a digital pound as there will be further public consultation prior to the introduction of primary legislation. Future work and decisions on a digital pound will continue to be informed by dialogue with the public, business, civil society, Parliamentarians, and experts, as the Bank and HM Treasury continue to develop its design.

## The Consultation Paper

The Bank and HM Treasury have progressed to the design phase of work on a digital pound and expect to decide whether to proceed to the build phase around the middle of the decade. The priority of the design phase is to develop further, in both policy and technology terms, the in-depth design of such a digital pound. Work during the design phase will focus on developing in detail the digital pound proposition, with a particular focus on the operational, functional and technology model for a digital pound.

## 1.6.3 FCA Discussion Paper – 'Regulating Cryptoassets Phase 1: Stablecoins'

The FCA is seeking to design a regulatory regime based on their statutory objectives, managing the risk of both fiat-backed stablecoins and those within fiat-backed stablecoins issuers.

The FCA noted that it recognises that many of the risks and issues involved are not new and apply to existing assets and markets (eg, the need to protect ownership rights).

It will adopt a outcomes-based approach, formed within a clear principles-based policy framework – but with sufficient detail and guidance for legal clarity (eg, as to what happens on insolvency), which will be technology-agnostic and it will not explicitly or otherwise endorse any particular technology.

It will look to achieve a balance between allowing innovation while protecting consumers and market integrity.

A stable coin issuer will need to be authorised by, and regulated by, the FCA in order to issue fiat-backed stablecoins in or from the UK - even if they are not marketed to UK consumers.

The Consumer Duty & Financial Promotion requirements will be the key focus of the FCA attention within firms.

Although the regime will be designed for the retail market, the FCA have asked whether there is appetite for institutional and wholesale use cases within the regime.

The Proposed 'Future Joint stablecoin Regulatory Regime' will come under the FCA's remit:

- **Payment Services Regulations** – UK Payment chain & Payment arrangers.

- **Regulated Activities Order** – issuing a UK based fiat-backed stablecoin, custody of regulated stablecoins, requirements in FCA Handbook.

The FCA noted that the 'fiat-backed' stablecoins will be the next phase of cryptoasset regulation.

## 1.6.4 HM Treasury – Smarter (Future) Financial Services Regulatory Regime for Cryptoassets (response to consultation and call for evidence) [including HM Treasury publication 'update on plans for the Regulation of Fiat-backed Stablecoins']

In October 2023 HM Treasury published a feedback response and a call for evidence, following its earlier consultation in February 2023.

HM Treasury noted that since its original (noted above in 1.6.) proposals, there have been a number of policy developments in the cryptoassets space:

- The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Financial Promotion) (Amendment) Order 2023 (Financial Promotions SI) – which brings financial promotions relating to qualifying cryptoassets within the existing financial promotions regime - was made on 7 June 2023.

   - the FCA published its Policy Statement on financial promotion rules for cryptoassets and the Guidance Consultation on cryptoasset promotions in June 2023 – the regime came into force in October 2023.

- The Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 contains important powers confirming the government's ability to specify cryptoasset activities within the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Regulated Activities) Order 2001 (RAO)

   - and to designate activities as part of the Designated Activities Regime (DAR) as well as providing the regulatory powers necessary to deliver a regulatory regime for the use of 'digital settlement assets' (DSA) in payments.

- HM Treasury published (noted below) an update on plans for the regulation of fiat-backed stablecoins (Stablecoins Update) which provides further clarity on activities relating to fiat-backed stablecoins and the delineation of phase 1 and phase 2) legislation.

HM Treasury noted that they intend to proceed as proposed in the Consultation, expanding the list of 'specified investments' in Part III of the RAO and thereby require firms undertaking relevant activities involving cryptoassets by way of business to be authorised by the FCA under Part 4A of FSMA. FSMA is well-established and understood by firms. HM Treasury believes that developing a fully bespoke regime outside of the FSMA framework would risk creating an un-level playing field between cryptoasset firms and the traditional financial sector. The government's approach would also be in line with the approach taken by numerous other jurisdictions.

At the same time, HM Treasury confirmed that they will not expand the definition of 'financial instruments' in Part 1 of Schedule 2 of the RAO to include presently unregulated cryptoassets.

The proposal to ban cryptoassets similarly fails to address other risks associated with the industry. There would be adverse consequences in terms of innovation, and there is no guarantee that banning cryptoassets would protect consumers, given high public demand and the availability of services in other jurisdictions. Therefore, HM Treasury noted that having robust and effective regulation will boost innovation, by giving people and businesses the confidence they need to use new technologies safely. By making the UK a safe place for the use and development of these technologies, HM Treasury's aim is to attract investment, generate new jobs, benefit from tax revenues, and promote innovation in new products and services.

Regarding the FSMA authorisation process, the new regime will be broader in scope than the current MLR registration regime and will include aspects of regulatory compliance that have not previously been assessed. Authorisation will therefore not be automatically granted to Money Laundering Regulations (MLR) registered firms.

Similar to the current MLR registration regime, the FCA will seek to provide feedback on good and poorquality applications, engage with industry to ensure clarity of regulatory expectations and relevant provide data on the number of applications received and the outcomes of those applications.

The Phase 2 secondary legislation is expected to be laid before Parliament in 2024, subject to Parliamentary time, but this may be delayed depending on the timing of a general election which must be called by the end of 2024.

The accompanying 'Stablecoins Update' outlines the government's intention to prioritise:

- the creation of FCA regulated activities under the RAO for the issuance and custody of fiat-backed stablecoins which are issued in the UK

- the regulation of payment services relating to certain fiat-backed stablecoins when used in a UK payment chain under the Payment Services Regulations 2017.

## 1.6.6 HM Treasury Policy Note – Future Financial Services Regulatory Regime for Cryptoassets (Regulated Activities)

In May 2025 HM Treasury published a policy note and a draft Statutory Instrument (SI), following on from their October 2023 consultation on proposals for the creation of a financial services regulatory regime for cryptoassets.

The policy note and draft SI provisions relate to 'new' regulated activities to be created under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Regulated Activities) Order 2001 (the RAO), and their associated consequential amendments to other instruments such as existing anti-money laundering and financial promotions requirements for cryptoasset firms. HM Treasury noted that market abuse and admissions and disclosures provisions will be published separately.

The Draft Order:

- includes certain cryptoassets (and stablecoins) in the list of "specified investments" in the UK

- designates certain activities in respect of such investments as "regulated activities" so that carrying them on in the UK by way of business triggers a licensing requirement

- makes consequential changes to legislation, bringing in a revised territorial scope, new exclusions to regulated activities and new exemptions to avoid other frameworks inadvertently overlapping.

The draft provisions amends the RAO to include 'cryptoasset activities, which are:

## **Specified Investments**

- Qualifying cryptoassets

   - A subcategory of FSMA 'cryptoassets' which are fungible and transferable, and which includes 'qualifying stablecoin'; excluded from this definition are 'specified investment cryptoassets' and other instruments that could meet the definition of a 'cryptoasset' under FSMA, such as tokenised e-money or tokenised deposit.

- Qualifying stablecoin

   - An asset that references one or more fiat currencies, and seeks to hold those fiat currencies or fiat currencies and other assets as backing assets to maintain a stable value.

## **Specified Activities**

- Stablecoin issuance

   - The issuance of 'qualifying' stablecoin in the UK – offering, redemption and maintaining the value of the qualifying stablecoin.

- Safeguarding.

- Operating a qualifying cryptoasset trading platform.

- Dealing in qualifying cryptoassets as principal.

- Dealing in qualifying cryptoassets as an agent.

- Arranging deals in qualifying cryptoassets.

- Qualifying cryptoasset staking.

- Decentralised Finance (DeFi).

The draft Statutory Instrument is noted by HM Treasury as 'near-final', with the finalised version expected to be published in Q3 2025.

## 1.8 Diversity and Inclusion in Financial Services

The FCA is proposing to provide additional guidance on 'non-financial misconduct' (which was one of the key responses the industry made to the recent FCA and PRA discussion papers on the SM&CR regime) and the PRA is proposing that all firms appoint an individual (who is an SMF) to be responsible for the firm's D&I development and implementation as a prescribed responsibility.

In addition, the FCA is proposing some minor amendments to their FIT (Fit and Proper Test for Employees and Senior Personnel) Sourcebook relating to when firms should take into account 'Non-Financial Misconduct' in their assessment of an individual being deemed to be 'fit and proper'.

In March 2025, the FCA submitted a letter to the Treasury Select Committee to advise them that they would not be taking forward their proposals on diversity and inclusion.

## 1.8.1 SM&CR (Non-Financial Conduct) Proposals

## FCA

Expansion of the Code of Conduct for Staff sourcebook (COCON) to make it clear that COCON covers serious instances of bullying, harassment, and similar behaviour towards fellow employees and employees of group companies and contractors.

- Guidance sets out the types of behaviour that will fall within the expanded scope of COCON and what conduct is out of scope because it relates to an employee's personal or private life.

   - Additional guidance is included to show how conduct in relation to an individual's private life is out of scope.

   - Whether this 'conduct' should be treated as a private life matter (eg, whether the conduct took place on firm premises/using firm equipment/involved work colleagues/was intended to benefit the firm).

   - Not every instance of misconduct towards a fellow member of the workforce will amount to a breach of COCON – only serious misconduct would amount to a breach of COCON.

- Additional guidance provided on conduct at a client/firm-organised social event will be regarded as in scope of the conduct rules.

- The FCA is also proposing to introduce guidance to indicate:

   - the sorts of factors that might indicate Non-Financial Misconduct that is a breach of Individual Conduct Rule 1 (integrity), and

   - the factors that might indicate Non-Financial Misconduct that is a breach of Individual Conduct Rule 2 (due skill, care and diligence).

The FCA will provide guidance on how Non-Financial Misconduct should be incorporated into regulatory references.

## PRA

Clarifying that the prescribed responsibilities in relation to culture, including responsibility for developing and implementing the firm's D&I strategies, must be allocated to an SMF:

- No requirements currently exist for UK branches.

- For UK banks, this is normally allocated to the Chair and CEO respectively.

- Therefore, UK branches will need to allocate this responsibility to the Head of the branch, having this reflected in their Statement of Responsibilities:

   - responsibility for overseeing the adoption of the firm's culture in the day-to-day management of the firm (prescribed responsibility 'h'), and

   - responsibility for leading the development of the firm's culture by the governing body as a whole (prescribed responsibility 'i').

SMFs will not be accountable for a failure to meet D&I targets – however, they are expected to understand and be able to discuss with regulators why targets have been set at a particular level and, if they will not be met, the reasons for this.

- The relevant SMF will be expected to take reasonable steps to design and implement a 'well developed and evidenced based' D&I strategy.

The FCA is not proposing to introduce equivalent requirements for FCA authorised firms.

The letter from the FCA to the Treasury Select Committee (referred to in section 1.8) noted that they are prioritising their work to tackle non-financial misconduct, noting that it was important that their approach was proportionate and aligned with planned legislation (ie, the review of the SM&CR), and that they would publish the next steps by the end of June 2025.

## 2.1 Central Securities Depositories Regulation (CSDR)

The CSDR is a regulation aiming to improve the securities settlement process in Europe and introduces a common authorisation, supervision and regulatory framework for central securities depositories (CSDs). Although aimed at EU central securities depositories (CSD) and central clearing counterparties (CCPs), it impacts market participants and firms globally in relation to the settlement of trades conducted in securities which are electronically transferable (settled) via EU CSDs.

The CSDR will apply to UK regulated entities to the extent that they are undertaking transactions in transferable securities on EU trading venues and which are settled at a EU CSD (such as Euroclear, Clearstream).

The main aim of the CSDR is to increase the safety and efficiency of securities settlement and settlement infrastructure (ie, CSDs) in the EU, providing shorter settlement cycles and settlement discipline measures including penalties and mandatory buy-ins for settlement fails.

The CSDR applies to all financial instruments that settle at an EU CSD and which are defined as MiFID financial instruments (as per Annex I, Section C of MiFID).

Under the Central Securities Depositories Regulation (CSDR), the following applies:

- Any participant in a securities settlement shall settle such transactions on the intended settlement date.

- Trading venues shall establish procedures to enable the confirmation of relevant details of transactions on the date when the transaction was executed.

- A CSD will provide regular reports to the relevant competent authority (ie, the FCA) of failed trades.

- A CSD will establish procedures in respect of penalties for failed settlement.

- A mandatory 'buy-in' process that takes effect from a specific number of days following the intended settlement date, based on whether the financial instrument is deemed to be 'liquid' or 'illiquid. Daily cash penalties will apply until the transaction settles and additional costs and charges resulting from the 'buy-in' process are to be settled by the failing counterparty.

- CSDs, CCPs and trading venues shall establish procedures that enable them to suspend (in consultation with the firms' respective competent authorities) any participant or firm that fails consistently and systematically to settle trades on the intended settlement date. The details will be made public.

One of the most contentious pieces of the legislation, the 'settlement discipline regime' (cash penalties and the mandatory buy-in process) was published in the EU Official Journal in September 2018. However, its implementation has been the subject of much debate and the 'mandatory buy-in' requirements have been delayed until November 2025.

Cash penalties came into effect in February 2022 and are seen by the industry as the main tool to change market participants behaviour in relation to increasing settlement efficiency and reducing settlement fails.

The UK decided against applying the 'Settlement Discipline' requirements, meaning that the requirements do not apply to securities settled at UK CSDs.

In 2022 the European Commission published formal proposals for changes to the CSDR Level 1 legislation – which include changes to the buy-in provisions.

The proposals are referred to as 'CSDR Refit'. In December 2023, the CSDR Refit legislation was published in the EU Official Journal, coming into effect 20 days later.

The following changes have been made to CSDR by the 'CSDR Refit legislation':

## Cash Penalties

ESMA, in close cooperation with the members of the ESCB, have been tasked with developing draft regulatory technical standards to specify the measures to prevent settlement fails in order to increase settlement efficiency and in particular:

- the measures to be taken by investment firms

- the details of the procedures that facilitate settlement – which could include the shaping of transaction sizes, partial settlement of failing trades and the use of auto-lend/borrow programmes provided

- the details of the measures to encourage and incentivise the timely settlement of transactions

ESMA must submit the draft regulatory technical standards to the Commission by 17 July 2025.

The European Commission must develop a Delegated Act to supplement the CSDR Refit by specifying parameters for the calculation of a deterrent and proportionate level of the cash penalties, based on all of the following:

- asset type

- liquidity of the financial instrument

- type of transaction

- duration of the settlement fail.

## Mandatory Buy-ins (MBI)

A new Article was developed and adopted under the CSDR Refit, specifically to cover the buy-in process (Article 7a – 'Mandatory Buy-in process').

Mandatory Buy-ins will only apply to transactions executed between counterparties that relate to equities (and equity like financial instruments) and bonds.

MBI's will only apply as a last resort, where both the following conditions are met:

- the application of the penalty mechanism has not resulted in a long-term sustainable reduction of settlement fails in the EU, or in maintaining a reduced level of settlement fails in the EU, even after a review and change to the structure and level of cash penalties, and

- the level of settlement fails in the EU has or is likely to have a negative effect on the financial stability of the Union.

The European Commission must consider the following when making its decision on whether MBI's should apply:

- the potential impact of the mandatory buy-in process on financial markets in the EU

- the number, volume and duration of settlement fails, including the number and volume of settlement fails outstanding at the end of the extension period, and

- whether a particular financial instrument or category of transactions in that financial instrument is already subject to appropriate contractual provisions that provide a right for receiving participants to trigger a buy-in.

Where the Commission decides that MBI's should apply to an asset class, and/or a specific sub-set of financial instruments, the application date of the MBI's must not be earlier than one year from the publication of the Delegated Act.

Mandatory buy-ins do not apply to:

- settlement fails the underlying cause of which is not attributable to the participants in the transaction

- operations that are not considered as trading

   - ie, 'margin transfers' under SFTR and EMIR

   - transactions where the failing participant is a CCP, except for transactions entered into by a CCP where it does not interpose itself between the counterparties

   - transactions where insolvency proceedings are opened against the failing participant

   - securities financing transactions (i.e. repos and securities lending)

      - other types of transactions that render the buy-in process unnecessary) ie, 'margin transfers' under SFTR and EMIR)

   - transactions that fall within the scope of Article 15 of Regulation (EU) No 236/2012

      - 'buy-in' procedure under the Short Selling Regulation (covering EU shares, EU Government Bonds and CDS).

## Settlement Chains and Pass-on Mechanism

Where there is a settlement fail in a chain of transactions resulting in settlement fails of subsequent transactions in the chain, each participant shall have the right to pass on their obligation to initiate the mandatory buy-in to the next participant in the chain.

## Power to Suspend MBIs

Where the buy-in process has been instigated, ESMA can recommend that the Commission suspend the buy-in mechanism for specific categories of financial instruments where necessary, and proportionate to avoid or address a serious threat to financial stability or to the orderly functioning of financial markets in the EU.

## Buy-in Agent

The requirement to appoint a 'buy-in' agent to complete the mandatory buy-in has been removed.

The trading counterparties to the transaction are responsible for the buy-in process, specifically the party awaiting delivery will have the responsibility for undertaking and executing (or appointing a counterparty to undertake the buy-in on their behalf) the buy-in.

## Symmetry of Payments (Buy-in Notice Issued)

Where the price of the financial instruments agreed at the time of the trade is different from the price paid for the execution of the 'buy-in', the difference shall be paid by the participant benefitting from the price difference to the other participant no later than on the second business day after the financial instruments have been delivered following the buy-in.

ESMA must, in close cooperation with the members of the ESCB, develop draft regulatory technical standards to further specify:

- details of the operation of the appropriate buy-in process

   - including appropriate timeframes

   - taking into account the asset type and liquidity of the financial instruments, for the delivery of the financial instrument following the buy-in process

- circumstances under which the extension period could be prolonged according to asset type and liquidity of the financial instruments

   - ie, taking into account the liquidity status of the underlying financial instrument

- details of the pass-on mechanism

- other types of transactions that render the buy-in process unnecessary

   - ie, financial collateral arrangements (margin transfers)

   - transactions that include close-out netting provisions (subject to a Master agreement, such as an ISDA CSA)

- methodology for the calculation of the cash compensation

- details of how the participants of the CSDs, the CCPs and the trading venue members are to take into account the specificities of retail investors when executing mandatory buy-ins.

## 2.2 European Markets Infrastructure Regulation – 'EMIR III'

In December 2022, as part of its 'Capital Markets Union', the European Commission published proposals, to create an EU central clearing framework by forcing Euro denominated derivative products to be cleared at Euro Central Clearing Counterparties.

It has been a long-standing aim of the European Commission to reduce dependency on third country central clearing counterparties since Brexit.

In its proposal it noted the following:

_'In addition, since 2017, concerns have been repeatedly expressed about the ongoing risks to the EU financial stability arising from the excessive concentration of clearing in some third country CCPs, notably in a stress scenario.'_

The legislative text for EMIR III was published in the official journal in December 2024, coming into force on 24 December 2024. Alongside the Regulation (EMIR III)  a Directive was also published, the measures must be transposed into national law by the EU Member States by 25 June 2026.

The following is a summary of the new requirements:

## 2.2.1 The EU Clearing Framework

## Active Account at an EU CCP

- EU entities subject to the mandatory clearing obligation (FCs and NFC+) must clear a proportion of such contracts at accounts with EU CCPs:

   - interest rate derivatives denominated in euro and Polish zloty

   - credit default swaps (CDS) denominated in euro, and

   - short-term interest rate derivatives (STIR) denominated in euro.

- The Commission is empowered to amend the list of products by a delegated act.

- ESMA has been mandated to draft regulatory technical standards (Level 2) specifying the proportion of activity to be cleared at EU CCPs:

   - what is not clear is if this would be just 'new' contracts or also existing contracts which have been cleared at Third Country CCPs

   - it is possible that these proposals could make EU firms less competitive than Third Country firms

## Clearing Thresholds

- This calculation of clearing thresholds distinguishes between transactions cleared by an EU CCP or a recognised third country CCP, and uncleared transactions:

   - rather than between exchange traded derivatives (ETDs) and OTC derivatives

- ESMA will be given more flexibility to review the clearing thresholds periodically.

## Exemption from the Clearing Obligation with Respect of Third Country Pension Schemes

- This will apply a level playing field to benefit EU FCs who enter into transactions with Third Country Pensions Schemes, which would not need to be mandatory cleared by the EU FC.

## Equivalence and Intragroup Exemptions

- Replacement of the requirement for an equivalence decision with a list of third countries classified as high risk or non-cooperative with respect of money laundering, terrorist financing or tax evasion or otherwise identified by the Commission by way of delegated act, with the exemption not being available for transactions with entities in those countries.

In November 2024 ESMA published a consultation paper, looking at several key aspects of the AAR including: the operational conditions, the representativeness obligation, as well as the reporting requirements.

## 2.3 European Commission Proposals for Changes to MiFID II and MiFIR

In 2021, the European Commission published a set of legislative proposals under the Capital Markets Union Action Plan. The proposals were aimed at the wholesale markets, and are being referred to as 'MiFID 3' and 'MiFIR 2'. The legislative text has been published in the EU Official journal.

MiFID 3 has an 18-month implementation period for EU Member states to transpose in law, meaning it comes into effect in September 2025.

For MiFIR 2, the requirements came into effect on 28 March 2024. However, as noted in the legislative text, a number of Delegated Acts need to be drafted by the European Commission and Level 2 measures (Regulatory Technical Standards) by ESMA.

The European Commission and EMSA have both published statements regarding the application and outstanding work with regards to MiFIR 2, acknowledging that:

_'several provisions in 'MiFIR 2' need to be supplemented by Commission Delegated regulations to become fully operational. Therefore, for the transitional period the existing provisions in MiFIR 2 will continue to apply until the date of application end effective date of the new Delegated Acts and Regulatory Technical Standards'._

The following are the main changes:

## Consolidated Tape

- The creation/emergence of a single consolidated tape for each asset class, including bonds and derivatives.

- Trading venues, SI's and other investment firm will be required to provide trade data to Consolidated Tape Providers (CTPs) free of charge.

- Only derivatives that are subject to the EMIR clearing obligation would need to be reported/ provided to a consolidated tape provider.

- OTC and exchange-traded derivatives would not be required to be provided.

- Information to be provided to a consolidated tape provider will need to be in a harmonised form (templates will be provided) and close to real time.

## Pre-trade and Post-trade Transparency

Removal of the ability of trading venues to obtain a waiver of pre-trade transparency for actionable indications of interest for trades above the size specific to the financial instrument (SSTI).

- This will mean that SIs' pre-trade transparency obligations would also apply to bond and derivatives trades above SSTI

   - with no exception for large-in-scale trades as ESMA had recommended

      - ESMA will be required to draft RTS specifying the content, format and terminology of the pre- and post-trade data that trading venues, APAs, CTPs and SIs are required to make available on a reasonable commercial basis.

- The requirements have been interpreted in different ways, notwithstanding the existing ESMA guidelines.

- ESMA would be required to deliver the draft RTS to the Commission within nine months of 'MiFIR2' becoming law.

## Derivative Trading Obligation

- Alignment of entities subject to the derivatives trading obligation to those subject to the clearing obligation under EMIR, as amended by EMIR Refit:

   - EMIR Refit exempted certain smaller financial counterparties from the clearing obligation and changed the process in which the clearing thresholds apply to non-financial counterparties (ie, asset class specific).

- The European Commission is empowered to suspend the derivatives trading obligation when the EMIR clearing obligation is suspended under the powers afforded to the Commission under EMIR Refit.

- The European Commission is empowered to suspend the derivative trading obligation, exercisable at the request of a member state competent authority, for investment firms that regularly act as market makers in derivatives subject to the trading obligation and that regularly receive requests for quotes for those derivatives from non-EU counterparties which do not have an active membership in an EU trading venue that trades those derivatives.

## Best Execution Reports

- The removal of the requirement to provide the annual Top 5 execution report to clients.

   - often referred to as RTS 28.

- The removal of the requirement to undertake the quarterly reports on the quality of execution.

   - often referred to as RTS 27.

Both of the above (RTS 27 and RTS 28) are currently subject to a statement by EMSA on the depriortisation of supervisory actions in light of the agreement on the MiFID II/MiFIR review.

In order the develop the Level 2 measures, ESMA has been mandated to draft regulatory technical standards, on subjects such as:

- pre-trade transparency requitements for equity and non-equity instruments

- transaction reporting

- consolidated tape/data reporting service providers

- order execution/best execution

- the Commodity Derivative regime.

Regulatory divergence between the UK and EU MiFID/MiFIR regimes will mean the potential for firms operating in both regions facing operational challenges.

## 2.4 ESG

## 2.4.1 Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

The Directive was published in the EU Official Journal in December 2022, coming into effect in January 2023.

The CSRD was developed to provide high quality data across a wider range of EU companies, requiring detailed disclosures on sustainability matters.

Companies are required to report through the lens of materiality, disclosing information about the sustainability risks and opportunities that they face – as well the impacts they have on society and the environment.

It also has an element of extraterritoriality, requiring in some circumstances reporting at full group level where Third Country entities have EU subsidiaries.

## Undertakings Captured by the CSRD

The CSRD will apply to:

- **Large undertakings** (including EU subsidiaries of non-EU parent companies)

   - Exceed at least two of the following three thresholds:

      - 250 employees on average during the financial year

      - net turnover of €40 million, and

      - total assets of €20 million.

- **Listed undertakings**

   - Undertakings with securities listed on an EU regulated market (including where they are incorporated outside the EU):

      - will include small and medium-sized entities (SMEs)

      - but not micro-undertakings which do not exceed two of the following three thresholds:

         - 10 employees on average during the financial year

         - net turnover of €700,000, and

         - total assets of €350,000.

- **Non-EU undertakings with substantial activity in the EU**

   - Where net turnover generated within the EU exceeded €150 million (in each of the last two consecutive years), provided the non-EU undertaking has:

      - an EU subsidiary that is listed or a large undertaking (ie, within CSRD's scope as per the above two bullets), or

      - an EU branch that generated net turnover of at least €40 million, the EU subsidiary or branch must report at the non-EU parent's group level.

- **Parent undertakings of large groups**

   - Groups that satisfy, on a consolidated basis, the criteria set out in the first bullet.

With regards to the exterritoriality issue, where an in-scope EU company (or branch) has an ultimate parent incorporated outside the EU, then the parent will be captured. The EU subsidiary must report at a consolidated level for the entire group. However, this this group-level reporting will be slightly less burdensome.

Reporting for non-EU undertakings will not be required until 2029, subject to the entity meeting the threshold conditions.

## Disclosure Requirements

- **Business model and strategy** – comprising their resilience to risks from sustainability matters and opportunities arising from sustainability matters, how their strategy accounts for the interests and impact upon other stakeholders.

- **Transition plans** – are they compatible with limiting global warming to 1.5°C and achieve net zero by 2050.

- **Targets** – where appropriate (and where set by the undertaking), absolute greenhouse gas emission reduction targets for 2030 and 2050, and a description of the progress made in achieving the targets.

- **Governance** – description of the role, skills and expertise of the administrative, management and supervisory bodies, as well as any incentive schemes linked to sustainability matters.

- **Policies** – regarding sustainability matters.

- **Due diligence process** – with respect to sustainability matters.

- **Principal adverse impacts** – actual and potential, connected to the entities own operations as well as its value chain, and any actions taken to prevent, mitigate, remediate or end such impacts.

Specific details on the reporting standards will be provided through Delegated Acts.

- Reporting in 2025 on the financial year 2024 for entities with more than 500 employees already subject to the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD).

- Reporting in 2026 on the financial year 2025 for large entities and parents of large groups that are not currently subject to the NFRD.

- Reporting in 2027 on the financial year 2026 for listed SMEs (except micro undertakings):

   - prior to financial years starting in 2028, SMEs can opt out and choose not to publish a sustainability report.

- Reporting in 2029 on the financial year 2028 for group-level reporting of non-EU entities with in-scope EU subsidiaries (or branches).

## 2.4.2 Corporate Sustainability and Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

In 2022 the European Commission proposed a Directive on Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence, where EU entities would:

- undertake due diligence across their value chains to identify the adverse impacts on their business

- implement processes to mitigate those impacts, and

- integrate sustainability and human rights considerations into their corporate governance and management systems.

Although the legislation is aimed at EU entities, it is extraterritorial as Non-EU entities who generate a net turnover in the EU of more than €450 million will be in scope.

The CSDDD was published in the EU Official Journal in July 2024, EU Members States must transpose it into national law by 26 July 2026. As part of a compromise from the original European Commission consultation, in the published Directive, Financial services will be temporarily excluded from the CSDDD.

The European Commission has been tasked with undertaking a review before the CSDDD can be applied to financial services. It must report to the Parliament and the Council between 25 July 2024 to 26 July 2026, the co-legislators will then decide if the CSDDD shall apply to credit institutions, develop and publish legislative proposals – these proposals could further delay the application.

In March 2022 the European Commission published proposals that are being called the ' _Omnibus Directive_ ' which are aimed at removing regulatory burdens for EU and Non-EU firms that would be subject to the CSRD and the CSDDD.

The European Parliament and European Council will need to develop their approach and positions to the proposals, the trilogue negotiations with the European Commission should take place in late H2 2025.

In February 2025 the European Commission published an 'Omnibus' simplification package which included 'stop the clock' application of the CSRD and the CSDDD, as well as substantive amendments to the CSRD, CSDDD legislation.

The European Council and the Parliament have reached swift agreement on the 'stop the clock' proposals – therefore, EU Member States have until December 2025 to change national laws to give this effect.

## **What has been agreed?**

The entry into application of the CSRD for large companies will be delayed by two years:

- The obligation on large EU companies to prepare reports on financial years starting in 2025 will be delayed by two years, with the first reports required for financial years starting in 2027.

- There will be no immediate delay to reporting obligations on Public Interest Entities (PIEs) for financial year 2024 reporting.

- The STC does not impact the current application thresholds (changes to those are being sought in the separate substantive proposal).

The transposition deadline and the application of the CSDDD to the largest companies will be delayed by one year:

- Member States are no longer required to transpose CSDDD by July 2026, rather by July 2027.

- The obligations on the largest companies (ie, EU-headed groups with over 5,000 employees and €1.5 billion net turnover worldwide, or non-EU-headed groups with over €1.5 billion turnover in the EU) are delayed for one year:

   - this means that substantive obligations will apply from July 2028 instead of July 2027 and that the reporting obligation will apply for financial year 2029 instead of financial year 2028.

- The entry into application of obligations for large companies, ie, EU groups with over 3,000 employees and €900 million net turnover worldwide, or non-EU-headed groups with over €900 million turnover in the EU, remains unaltered: substantive obligations will start to apply in July 2028 and reporting obligations will start to apply for financial year 2029.

- The entry into application of substantive obligations for EU companies with over 1,000 employees and €450 million net turnover worldwide, or non-EU-headed groups with over €450 million turnover in the EU also remains unaltered:

   - obligations will start in July 2029.

   - reporting obligations for these companies, however, are delayed by one year, starting financial year 2030 instead of financial year 2029.

## 2.5 Cryptoassets (MiCAR)

The Markets in Crypto Assets Regulation (MiCAR) is an EU regulatory framework for the issuance of, intermediating and dealing in, cryptoassets. It introduces licensing and conduct of business requirements as well as a market abuse regime with respect to cryptoassets.

The MiCAR legislation has been agreed and approved by both the European Parliament and the European Council. MiCAR came into force in June 2023, although there will be a phased implementation (coming into effect).

The first element of MiCAR (applying to EMTs and ARTs) came into effect on 30 June 2024, while the remainder of the Regulation came into effect at the end of 2024.

Both the EBA and ESMA were mandated to create Level 2 measures, and during 2023 and 2024 they published a number of consultations, and final reports, to develop regulatory technical standards that sit under the legislation.

MiCAR creates a broad regulatory framework for cryptoassets in the EU which:

- regulates the issuance of, and admission to trading of, cryptoassets, including transparency and disclosure requirements

- introduces licensing of cryptoasset service providers, issuers of asset-referenced tokens and issuers of electronic money tokens

- clarifies the regulatory obligations applicable to issuers of asset-referenced tokens, issuers of electronic money tokens and cryptoasset service providers, including consumer protection rules for the issuance, trading, exchange and custody of cryptoassets

- creates a market abuse regime prohibiting market manipulation and insider dealing, and

- clarifies the powers, including the co-operation and sanctions framework, available to competent authorities.

The requirements under MiCAR are similar to requirements under the existing EU financial services regimes, including requirements relating to disclosures, governance and licensing.

However, there are some nuances between MiCAR and the existing regime, firms engaging in cryptoasset activities will need to consider whether they will fall under the MiCAR definition of 'cryptoassets' or whether they are subject to another regulation, to ensure that they adhere to the appropriate regulation.

MiCAR forms part of the EU's digital finance strategy, which also includes a regulation on digital operational resilience (DORA) and a Regulation on a distributed ledger technology (DLT) pilot regime focused on financial market infrastructures based on DLT.

## 2.5.1 Who Does MiCAR Apply to?

- Issuers of cryptoassets:

   - this might not mean the entity or firm that has created the cryptoassets

   - the issuer of a cryptoasset is the 'legal person who offers to the public any type of cryptoassets' or 'seeks the admission of such cryptoassets to a trading platform for cryptoassets'.

- Cryptoasset service providers (CASPs):

   - includes any person whose occupation or business is the provision of one or more cryptoasset services to third parties on a professional basis.

- Any person:

   - with respect to acts that concern trading in cryptoassets that are admitted to trading on a trading platform for cryptoassets operated by an authorised cryptoasset service provider:

      - including where a request for admission to trading on such a trading platform has been made.

## 2.5.2 What Type of Cryptoassets are in Scope?

Any type of cryptoassets:

- which are defined as a:

   - _'digital representation of a value or a right that uses cryptography for security and is in the form of a coin or a token or any other digital medium that can be stored electronically using distributed ledger technology'_

- this means it captures not just cryptocurrencies (ie, Ethereum or Bitcoin), but also stablecoins and tokens.

## 2.5.3 What is Not Covered by MiCAR?

- Security tokens which would quality as transferable securities and other cryptoassets that qualify as financial instruments for the purposes of MiFID II, deposits, securitisation positions, insurance or pension products.

- Central Bank digital currencies (CBDCs) issued by the ECB and digital assets issued by national central banks of EU Member States when acting in their capacity as monetary authority, or to services related to cryptoassets that are provided by such central banks.

MiCAR introduces three sub-categories of crypto-assets that are subject to different requirements adjusted to the risks they entail:

- 'Electronic money tokens' or 'e-money tokens' (EMTs):

   - these purport _'to maintain a stable value by referencing to the value of one official currency'_

   - like traditional e-money, EMTs are electronic surrogates for coins and banknotes and are likely to be used for payment purposes.

- 'Asset-referenced tokens' (ARTs)

   - these aim ' _to maintain a stable value by referencing to any other value or right or a combination thereof, including one or more official currencies'_

   - ARTs could be backed by a basket of different fiat currencies, commodities or crypto assets.

- All other crypto-assets that are not EMTs or ARTs

   - covers a wide variety of crypto-assets, including non-pegged payment tokens (ie, cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin or Ether) and utility tokens

   - MiCAR lays down a few specific rules for utility tokens, defined as:

      - _'a type of crypto-asset which is only intended to provide access to a good or a service supplied by the issuer of that token'._

## 2.5.4 Obligations for Issuers and Service Providers

## Issuers of Crypto Assets

- One core obligation under MiCA is that issuers of all three types of crypto assets must issue a 'prospectus' for crypto-assets before they may offer a token to the public or list it on a trading platform:

   - informs potential holders of the characteristics of the issued crypto-asset

   - MiCAR introduces minimum standards for these 'prospectus' in order to achieve a standardisation and reliability

   - it must include information on the issuer, the crypto-asset project or token, risks and the rights and obligations attached to the crypto-asset but notably also the adverse environmental and climate-related impacts of the consensus mechanism used to issue the crypto asset.

- For crypto assets other than ARTs or EMTs, MiCAR mainly stipulates disclosure, transparency and governance rules:

   - offering such crypto assets to the public or listing them on a trading platform does not require prior authorisation

   - issuers only need to notify and publish the 'prospectus' up front

   - retail holders have a right to withdraw from the purchase of such a token on the primary market within 14 days if the respective crypto asset was not yet traded on a trading platform at the time of purchase.

## Issuers of EMT and ART

- For issuers of the stablecoin variants EMTs and ARTs, MiCAR sets out stricter rules due to related concerns with regard to financial stability and monetary sovereignty.

- Publicly offering or listing ARTs or EMTs within the EU requires prior authorisation and may only be done by the issuer itself unless the issuer gives another person written permission to do so.

- EMTs may only be offered/listed by authorised credit institutions or e-money institutions upon prior notification of their supervisory authority as well as notification and publication of the required white paper.

- Issuers of ARTs must be established in the EU to receive authorisation:

   - they must notify and submit the required 'prospectus' for approval to the competent authority before publishing it.

- MiCA effectively limits the daily average number of transactions and trading volume associated to uses of EMTs and ARTs as means of exchange to 1 million and €200 million, respectively. For an EMT, this only applies where the token is denominated in a currency which is not an official currency of an EU member state. It has been feared that these rules may put popular USD-pegged stablecoins like Tether's USDT, Circle's USD coin and Binance's Binance USD at risk of being stifled in the EU due to their high trading volumes. However, the regulation clarifies that not all kinds of transactions will be considered to be associated to uses as a means of exchange.

## Crypto Asset Service Providers

- The provision of crypto-asset services in the EU will generally require prior authorisation as a 'crypto asset service provider'

- MiCAR stipulates a broad set of general and service specific rules CASPs will need to adhere to, these include:

   - governance, capital/insurance and transparency requirements

## Market Abuse Rules

- MiCAR introduced tailored market abuse rules.

- A duty of issuers, offerors and persons seeking admission to trading to disclose inside information and prohibitions of insider dealing, unlawful disclosure of inside information and market manipulation as well as rules for the prevention and detection of market abuse.

- As under MAR, these rules apply potentially worldwide with regard to crypto-assets that are admitted to trading on a trading platform operated by a CASP or for which a respective request to be traded has been made, irrespective of whether the action or omission actually takes place on the trading platform.
