## 6. Financial Benchmarks 

Following the end of the Brexit transitional period, the EU BMR legislation was onshored and adopted into UK law – it is now known as ‘UK BMR’. 

The Regulation imposes requirements on firms that provide, contribute to or use a wide range of interest rate, currency, securities, commodity and other indices and reference prices. The legislation Regulation is a key part of the EU’s response to the ‘LIBOR’ scandal and the allegations of manipulation of foreign exchange and commodity benchmarks. Its main aim being to reduce the risk of manipulation of benchmarks by addressing conflicts of interest, governance controls and the use of discretion in the benchmark-setting process. It will achieve this by requiring administrators of a broad class of benchmarks to be authorised or registered by a regulator and to implement governance systems and other controls to ensure the integrity and reliability of their benchmarks. 

The Regulation replaced the then UK framework regulating the administrators of LIBOR and other specified benchmarks. It takes a very different approach from those jurisdictions, such as Japan and Singapore, that – like the UK – have so far chosen to regulate a limited range of critical benchmarks and those jurisdictions, such as the US, that are relying on self-regulation and robust enforcement action to achieve the objectives of the IOSCO Principles. 

The UK BMR covers a very wide range of indices including indices linked to interest rates, currencies, securities, commodities and even factors such as the weather. An index will be regulated as a ‘benchmark’ where it determines amounts payable under, or sets the value of, financial instruments or financial contracts; or is used in investment funds. The aim is to cover indices used in securities 

and derivatives traded on a regulated trading venue under UK MiFID II and the **Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (UK MiFIR)** , including securities and derivatives traded outside a venue via an investment firm that is a ‘systematic internaliser’. 

The Regulation also includes indices used to set interest rates under certain consumer loans and consumer mortgages as well as the indices used in index tracking and some other funds. There are exemptions for certain kinds of providers and indices from the scope of the Regulation, covering, in particular, central banks and public authorities (third countries) that provide indices for public policy purposes. 

There is an exemption for single reference prices for an individual security or derivative, but this does not include commodities. 

## How Are Firms Affected? 

A firm will be benchmark administrator if they provide indices that are used in: 

- financial instruments traded on trading venues or via systematic internalisers in the UK 

- mortgage or consumer credit contracts, or 

- investment funds. 

A firm will be a supervised contributor if they are an authorised person and: 

- contribute input data that is not readily available to the administrator, and 

- provide the input data for the purpose of a benchmark determination. 

A firm will be a benchmark user and be subject to additional requirements if it is supervised under UK MiFID, UK CRD, UK UCITS and they: 

- issue a financial instrument that references an index 

- determine the amount payable under a financial instrument or a mortgage or consumer credit contract by referencing an index 

- are a party to a mortgage or consumer credit contract that references an index 

- provide a borrowing rate calculated as a spread or mark-up over an index or a combination of indices and that is solely used as a reference in a consumer credit contract to which the creditor is a party, and 

- measure the performance of an investment fund through an index either to track the return of the fund or to define its asset allocation. 

## Restrictions on Use of a Financial Benchmark 

UK firms are prohibited from ‘using’ indices as a benchmark unless they are produced by an independent administrator included on the UK register of administrators authorised or registered under the Regulation or in an approved third country (qualified for use in the UK under the Regulation’s third country regime). 

The UK BMR groups benchmarks into six different types: 

1. **Critical benchmarks** – where the value of contracts underlying the benchmark is at least €500 billion, or where a benchmark has been recognised as critical in a member state. 

2. **Significant benchmarks** – where the value of contracts underlying the benchmark is at least €50 billion, or where there are no or very few market-led substitutes and there would be an impact on financial stability if the benchmark ceased to be produced. 

3. **Commodity benchmarks** – where the underlying asset of the benchmark is a commodity as defined by MiFID II. Commodity benchmarks are subject to the requirements set out in Annex II of the Regulation unless they are regulated data benchmarks, or are based on submissions, the majority of which are supervised entities. The articles relating to significant and non-significant benchmarks do not apply to commodity benchmarks. 

4. **Regulated data benchmarks** – where the input data to the benchmark is provided directly from regulated venues. Certain provisions of the Regulation do not apply to regulated data benchmarks, and they cannot be classified as critical. 

5. **Interest rate benchmarks** – where the benchmark is determined on the basis of the rate at which banks may lend to, or borrow from, other banks or agents in the money markets. Interest rate benchmarks are subject to the requirements set out in Annex I of the Regulation. The articles relating to significant and non-significant benchmarks do not apply to interest rate benchmarks. 

6. **Non-significant benchmarks** – where the value of contracts underlying the benchmark is less than €50 billion, and the benchmark is not a commodity or interest rate benchmark. 

## Equivalence, Recognition and Endorsement 

In order for supervised entities in the UK to be able to use benchmarks produced by third country administrators (eg, EU countries, US, Japan, Australia), those administrators must apply to be added to the FCA’s list of benchmarks in one of three ways: 

1. **Equivalence** – where an equivalence decision has been approved/provided. 

2. **Recognition** – where an administrator located in a third country has been recognised. 

3. **Endorsement** – where an administrator or supervised entity located in the EU with a clear and welldefined role within the control or accountability framework of a third-country administrator and is able to monitor effectively the provision of a benchmark, applies for endorsement in accordance with the requirements set out in Article 33 of the Regulation.
