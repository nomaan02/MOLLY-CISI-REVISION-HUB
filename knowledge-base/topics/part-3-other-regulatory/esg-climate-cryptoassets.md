## 9. Climate Financial Risk Forum (CFRF) 

Climate change and society’s response to it presents financial risks and opportunities that are relevant to the objectives of both the PRA and the FCA. The Climate Financial Risk Forum’s (CFRF) aim is to develop practical tools and approaches to address climate-related financial risks. 

The objective of the CFRF is to build capacity and share best practice across financial regulators and industries to advance the financial services sector’s responses to the financial risks from climate change. It brings together senior representatives from across the financial sector, including banks, insurers, and asset managers. The Forum will also include observers from trade bodies to represent a broader range of firms and ensure the outputs of the CFRF are communicated to their members. 

The forum is chaired by individuals from the PRA and the FCA. It is scheduled to meet three times a year and will report to the CEO of the PRA and the FCA. 

Climate change will have a significant impact on the financial services sector and will increasingly influence consumer decision making. For example, it could affect the values of all types of financial assets and how investment managers deliver long-term sustainable value to investors. 

It could also increase the cost of insurance for some consumers and reduce the availability of insurance to offer products and cover for others. 

The CFRF is an industry-led forum, bringing together senior representatives from across the financial sector to produce practical tools and recommendations for firms in response to climate-related financial risks and capturing the opportunities arising from climate change. The aim of the forum is to advance thinking and share good practice about how firms can improve their responses and overcome barriers to dealing with climate-related financial risks. 

Membership of the forum is drawn widely from across the sector and includes banks, insurers and asset managers, with trade bodies observing and feeding in the views of a broader range of firms. 

## 9.1 CFRF Guide 

In 2020, the CFRF published their guide to climate-related financial risk management. The guide aims to help financial firms understand the risks and opportunities that arise from climate change, provides support for how to integrate them into their risk strategy and decision-making processes. 

The guide contains four industry-produced chapters: 

- **Risk management** – by appropriately embedding climate-related financial risk into its governance and risk management processes, a firm can make informed business decisions and improve its resilience. 

- **Scenario analysis** – by appropriately modelling and considering a range of potential futures, a firm can better understand and manage future risks today, whilst capturing opportunities to support the transition to a net-zero carbon economy. 

- **Disclosures** – by making effective climate-related financial disclosures, a firm can improve transparency regarding the impact of material financial risks from climate change on their business, thereby helping the market appropriately assess the true future value of assets. 

- **Innovation** – by developing novel products, services, policies and approaches, a firm can adapt its business to respond to the potential impacts of climate change, benefit consumers and deliver the change required to meet climate goals.

The guides are written by industry and focus on risk management, scenario analysis, disclosure, innovation and climate data and metrics. They build on the previous guides (published in 2020). 

The five CFRF working groups, as noted below, published ten deliverables (reports) which are summarised as follows:

   - Practical examples on how firms can incorporate sector specific points when developing an effective approach to scenario analysis.

## 9.2.1 FCA ‘ESG Strategy’ 

In 2021, the FCA published its strategy for positive change with respect to its ‘ESG strategy’ – key themes from the strategy are ‘transparency’ and ‘trust’. The FCA work is based on five core themes: 

- **Transparency** – promoting transparency on climate change and wider sustainability along the value chain. 

- **Trust** – building trust and integrity in ESG-labelled instruments, products and the supporting ecosystem. 

- **Tools** – working with others to enhance industry capabilities and support firms’ management of climate-related and wider sustainability risks, opportunities and impacts. 

- **Transition** – supporting the role of finance in delivering a market-led transition to a more sustainable economy. 

- **Team** – developing strategies, organisational structures, resources and tools to support the integration of ESG into FCA activities. 

## 9.2.2 Sustainability Disclosure Requirements 

In 2021, the FCA published a discussion paper on Sustainability Disclosure Requirements and investment labels. 

The FCA invited views on the potential criteria for classifying and labelling investment products. The aim is to help consumers navigate their sustainability characteristics. The FCA is also interested in obtaining feedback with respect to supporting entity-level and product-level disclosures. 

In 2021, the FCA published a Policy Statement, with feedback and final rules, relating to enhanced climate-related disclosures for asset managers, life insurers and FCA-regulated pension providers. 

The rules require in-scope firms to make disclosures on an annual basis based on the following: 

- **Entity level** 

   - Annual Taskforce Climate-related Financial Disclosure (TCFD), displayed on the firm’s main website: 

      - setting out how they take climate-related matters into account in managing or administering investment on behalf of clients and consumers. 

- **Product level** 

   - Disclosures on the products and portfolios made publicly in a prominent place on the firm’s main website: 

      - cross referenced in an appropriate client communication, or 

      - made available upon request to certain eligible institutional clients. 

The above disclosures applied from 1 January 2022 for the largest in-scope firms, one year later for smaller firms above the £5 billion exemption threshold (ie, 1 January 2023).

In 2022, the FCA published a consultation paper (CP22/20) on sustainability disclosure requirements. 

The proposals were to advance the FCA’s approach on market transparency on the sustainability profile of products and firms and reduce the harm that could arise (in the future in the form of misselling) from greenwashing. 

The proposals in the consultation are only relevant to funds and portfolio management based in the UK. 

For the investment market (and firms operating in it) to flourish there needs to be trust and transparency, the FCA has concerns around greenwashing and how this may erode trust from consumers and investors in the purchase of goods and services from investment firms. The purpose of the rules is therefore to assist consumers and investors to navigate the increasingly complex investment product landscape and protect them from ‘greenwashing’. 

## Outcomes the FCA is Seeking 

The FCA was very clear in the consultation that the proposals are intended to advance its strategic objective to make markets function well (through increasing transparency on the sustainability profile of products) and the reduction of harm arising from greenwashing. 

- The disclosure requirements will facilitate the flow of consistent information along the investment chain, increasing transparency in the market and furthering our objective to promote market integrity. 

- Sustainable investment labels, consumer-facing disclosures, and rules for distributors will help to protect consumers by equipping them to navigate the market more effectively and identify products that meet their needs and preferences. 

- The naming and marketing rules will similarly help to protect consumers by reducing the risk of greenwashing. 

- The labels and consumer-facing disclosures will help consumers to compare products more effectively, and efficiently, potentially leading to greater competition among similar products (ie, where consumers are comparing like for like). 

In 2023, the FCA published its long awaited Policy Statement, along with final rules, as well as a consultation on anti-greenwashing guidance. 

With regards to the anti-greenwashing rule, and guidance, this came into effect from May 2024 for all regulated firms when undertaking client communication, marketing or making a statement about the ‘sustainability characteristics’ about a product or service. 

The purpose of the rules are to ensure that such claims or reference to ‘sustainability characteristics’ of a product or service are: 

- consistent with the sustainability characteristics of the products to services, and 

- are fair, clear and not misleading. 

The requirements apply to all FCA regulated firms and apply no matter where the communication was developed or written – if it is distributed by a FCA regulated firm to its clients (both retail and professional clients), then the FCA rules apply. 

Client communications include statements, assertions, strategies, targets, policies, information and images. 

In its Guidance Consultation, the FCA noted that Sustainability references should be: 

- correct and capable of being substantiated 

- clear and presented in a way that can be understood 

- complete – they should not omit or hide important information and should consider the full life cycle of the product or service 

- comparisons to other products or services are fair and meaningful. 

The FCA also noted in its published Finalised Guidance (FG24/3) that there are existing sections of their Handbook that require firms to ensure information client communications are **fair, clear and not misleading** (the Principles for Businesses, Conduct of Business (4.2) and Consumer Credit (3.3)). The FCA further noted that the anti-greenwashing rule is intended to complement and be consistent with these rules, stating that they are not intended to override or substitute any of the rules. 

In addition, the FCA noted that they consider the anti-greenwashing rules are consistent with the Consumer Duty, where firms must act in good faith towards their retail customers. 

The ‘Sustainability Disclosure Requirements and Investment Labels’ regime focuses on the following main areas regarding disclosure requirements: 

- Establish four sustainable investment labels to help consumers navigate the investment product. 

- Landscape and enhance consumer trust, coming into effect from 31 July 2024: 

   - sustainable focus 

   - sustainable improvers 

   - sustainable impact 

   - sustainability mixed goals – only applicable where the fund sustainability objective is to invest in accordance with two or more of the other three sustainability objectives. 

- Setting naming and marketing rules, coming into effect from 2 December 2024. 

- Consumer-facing disclosures. 

- Simple consumer-facing disclosures to assist consumers in understanding the main sustainability related features of a product. 

- Detailed disclosures for a wider targeted audience (institutional investors and retail customers who wish to find out more information), coming into effect from 2 December 2025: 

   - pre-contractual disclosures 

   - sustainability product report 

   - sustainability entity report. 

- Restrictions on the use of sustainability related terms unless the product has a ‘sustainable investment label’. 

- Distributors of investment products must ensure that product entry level information, including investment labels, are made/provided to customers. 

- These rules come into effect from 2 December 2025 for firms with AUM greater than £50 billion, or from 2 December 2026 for firms with AUM greater than £5 billion, but less than £50 billion. 

In April 2024 the FCA published a consultation (CP24/8) with proposals to extend the Sustainability Disclosure Requirements (SDR) regime to Portfolio Management. 

The FCA defines portfolio management as a service provided to a client which comprises either: 

- managing investments 

- private equity or other private market activities, consisting of advising on investments or managing investments (on a discretionary and/or advisory basis). 

As noted above, the rules for investment labels, accompanying disclosures, came into effect from December 2024. The FCA proposed that these requirements, and timeframes, will apply to firms providing portfolio management. Thereby, having one ‘SDR’ framework for forms offering firms offering/ 

providing portfolio management and asset management (ie, management of collective investment schemes). 

## 10. Cryptoassets 

Information on regulatory developments with regards to ‘cryptoassets’ can be found in section 6 of chapter 5. 

Financial promotion requirements relating to ‘cryptoassets can be found in section 14.4.13 in chapter 2. 

The only regulation in respect of cryptoassets per se in the UK at present is under the MLRs, in connection with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF). Under the MLRs, the FCA (as noted below) is the AML and CTF supervisor for certain cryptoasset service providers in the UK. 

However, certain types of cryptoasset fall within the existing FSMA regulatory perimeter set by the Regulated Activities Order (RAO), as they qualify as ‘specified investments’. 

For instance, security tokens provide rights and obligations akin to shares, debt instruments or other securities, which are specified investments as set out in the RAO – meaning they are already within the regulatory perimeter and that firms carrying on specified activities involving security tokens need to ensure that they have the correct permissions and are following the relevant rules and requirements. 

Also, other types of cryptoasset may also fall within other elements of existing financial services regulation, notably where forming part of (or being the subject matter of) a financial instrument within Part 1 of Schedule 2 to the RAO, e-money under the E-Money Regulations 2011 (EMR 2011) or, where facilitating regulated payment services, the Payment Services Regulations 2017 (PSR 2017). 

Borrowing/lending of cryptoassets is not explicitly regulated in the UK. It is arguable that lending of cryptoassets to consumers could fall within scope of consumer credit regulation, but the FCA does not make any public announcements in this regard. 

## 10.1 Anti-Money Laundering & Terrorist Financing 

In 2020 the FCA became the AML and TF supervisor in the UK; therefore any cryptoasset business needs to register with the FCA under the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 if they wish to provide cryptoasset services by ‘way of business’ and if this service is to be carried on in the UK.

## 10.3 FCA Approach to Supervision 

The FCA’s approach is based on the same as other businesses under the MLRs. It is risk based so that businesses which pose the greatest money laundering and terrorist financing risk receive an increased level of supervisory focus. 

The FCA‘s supervisory assessment includes a requirement for a business to show that it has policies, controls and procedures in place to effectively manage money laundering and terrorist financing risks proportionate to the size and nature of the business’s services. 

Cryptoasset businesses should also carry out regular assessments of their policies, controls and procedures to ensure that these remain relevant and appropriate. They must be alert to any change in their operating model that may have an impact on the way they conduct their business. 

Some requirements are set out below. It is not an exhaustive list and does not include the wider rules and guidance that a cryptoasset business, which is also authorised under FSMA by the FCA, must adhere to in addition to the MLRs. 

Businesses must: 

- take appropriate steps to identify and assess the risks of money laundering, proliferation financing and terrorist financing which the business is subject to 

- assess the money laundering, terrorist financing and proliferation financing risks related to any new technologies prior to launch and take appropriate measures to manage and mitigate those risks 

- have in place policies, systems and controls appropriate to mitigate the risk of the business being used for the purposes of money laundering, terrorist financing and proliferation financing. This riskbased approach should seek to mitigate the risks identified in the business’ risk assessment 

- where appropriate, for the size and nature of its business, appoint an individual who is a member of the board or senior management to be responsible for compliance with the MLRs and the nominated officer. The nominated officer is also the person responsible for reporting suspicious activity to the NCA under Part 7 (Money Laundering) of the POCA 2002 

- where appropriate, for the size and nature of its business, establish an independent internal audit function with responsibility for examining and evaluating the adequacy and effectiveness of the policies, controls and procedures, and making recommendations, as well as monitoring the controls 

- • undertake screening of employees 

- undertake customer due diligence when entering into a business relationship or occasional transactions 

- apply more intrusive due diligence, known as enhanced due diligence, when dealing with customers who may present a higher money laundering, terrorist financing and proliferation financing risk. This includes customers who meet the definition of a politically exposed person 

- undertake ongoing monitoring of all customers to ensure that transactions are consistent with the business’s knowledge of its customer, the customer’s business and risk profile. 

## 10.4 Consumer Protections and Disclosure 

The FCA’s responsibility under the regime is limited to AML/CTF registration, supervision and enforcement only. 

Registration does not mean that customers of cryptoasset businesses will benefit from the protection of the FOS or the FSCS. 

One of the priorities for the FCA is to ensure that cryptoasset businesses do not mislead customers about what consumer/investor protections apply and the status of their FCA registration. 

As most cryptoassets are not specified investments under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA), it is unlikely that customers will have access to the Financial Ombudsman or the FSCS. 

The FCA has provided guidance setting out their position on the types of cryptoassets which would fall within their regulatory remit and the implications this has on consumer protections: 

- cryptoasset services involving security tokens, for example, are regulated tokens which will provide the same protections as specified investments set out in the Regulated Activities Order 

- cryptoasset services which fall into unregulated space will not have the same consumer protections.
