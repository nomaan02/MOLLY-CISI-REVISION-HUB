## 1.10 Collective Investment Schemes (CISs) (Part XVII)

FSMA defines what is meant by the arrangements for and identifies the restrictions in place in relation to the promotion of CISs.

FSMA also provides powers in order to review, approve, and refuse applications on authorised unit trust (AUT) schemes.

## 1.11 Additional Powers Granted under the Financial Services Act 2010

The Financial Services Act 2010, effective from 8 June 2010, gave additional powers to the then regulator, the FSA, (which also included amendments to FSMA). The additional powers were:

- the power to impose suspensions or restrictions on authorised persons, under s206A FSMA, and on **approved persons** , under s66 FSMA (the suspension power), and
- the power to impose penalties on persons who perform controlled functions without approval, under s63A FSMA (the non-approved person’s penalty power).

## 1.11.1 The Power to Suspend

If any authorised person breaches any regulatory rules or requirements, the power to suspend enables the regulator to suspend any permissions the person may have to carry on a regulated activity, or impose restrictions on that person carrying on a regulated activity.

The suspension power enables the regulator to suspend an approved person from performing one or more controlled functions for which they are approved, or restrict the performance of the controlled function by them.

In addition, the regulators can impose a suspension on an authorised person for a period of up to 12 months and on an approved person for a period of up to two years.

The suspension power is an alternative sanction to a financial penalty which could be imposed by the regulator.

The suspension power has been seen as a major change of emphasis and focus by industry, including the way in which the regulator can operate. To alleviate industry concerns, the regulator states that it would only use this power when it considers that the imposition of a suspension would be the most effective and persuasive deterrent, rather than the imposition of a financial penalty alone.

The **Regulatory Decisions Committee (RDC)** is responsible for providing warning and decision notices when the suspension power is used.

## 1.11.2 The Non-Approved Persons Penalty Power

The non-approved persons penalty power provides the regulator with the ability to impose a financial penalty on a person, of an amount the regulator considers appropriate, when it is satisfied that:

- the person has at any time performed a controlled function without approval, and
- at that time the person knew, or could reasonably be expected to have known, that they were performing a controlled function without approval.

## 1.12 Financial Promotions Order (FPO)

A financial promotion is defined in Section 21 of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) as:

*‘an invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity, communicated by a person in the course of business’.*

The effect of section 21 of the Act (Restrictions on financial promotion) is that in the course of business, an unauthorised person must not communicate an invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity or to engage in claims management activity unless either the content of the communication is approved for the purposes of section 21 by an authorised person or it is exempt.

The FPO is made up of the following parts:

- **Part I** – Citation, Commencement and interpretation
- **Part II** – Controlled Activities and Controlled Investments
- **Part III** – Exemptions: Interpretation and Application
- **Part IV** – Exempt communications: All controlled Activities
- **Part V** – Exempt Communications: Deposits and Insurance
- **Part VI** – Exempt Communications: Certain Controlled Activities
- **Part VIA** – Exempt Communications: Controlled Claims Management Activities.

In the UK, it is a criminal offence to communicate a financial promotion unless it has been made or approved by a firm authorised under the UK Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) or it qualifies for an exemption.

An investment activity is defined as a controlled activity that is performed in respect of a controlled investment. This means that the regime not only covers promotions for the straightforward sale of a controlled investment, but also promotions as it pertains to investments.

Controlled investments include shares, bonds and derivatives, and Controlled activities include dealing, providing portfolio management or providing investment advice.

The UK regime covers all financial promotions that are capable of having an effect in the UK, including adverts from abroad that are targeted at UK investors.

## 1.13 Bank of England (BoE) Act 1988

The Bank of England (BoE) Act makes provisions in relation to the constitution, regulation, financial arrangements and functions of the BoE. This also includes the provision for the transfer of supervisory functions and to amend the Banking Act 1987.

The Act provides an objective in relation to the Bank to protect and enhance the stability of the financial systems of the UK.

In doing so, the Bank created an oversight committee, the functions of which are to keep under review the Bank’s performance in relation to:

- the Bank’s objective
- monitoring the extent to which the Bank’s financial management has been met in relation to the court’s set objectives for the directors of the Bank, and
- keeping the internal controls of the Bank under review, with a view to securing the proper conduct of its financial affairs.

The Bank must provide HM Treasury with a copy of reports prepared by the oversight committee.

The Bank must also make available to the Chancellor of the Exchequer a report of its activities.

The court of directors determines the Bank’s strategy in relation to the financial stability objective, and from time to time reviews this strategy. Before determining or revising the Bank’s financial strategy, the court of directors must consult on the revised strategy with the **Financial Policy Committee (FPC)** and HM Treasury.

The Bank set up an ‘FPC’ as a subcommittee of the Bank.

## 1.14 Banking Act 2009

The Banking Act 2009 created a Special Resolution Regime (SRR) which gave the UK regulators a permanent framework, providing the regulatory resolution tools for dealing with UK banks and building societies at serious risk of failure. The SRR powers came into force in 2009.

The Act sets out seven key objectives which must be considered when choosing which resolution tools to use to:

- protect and enhance the stability of the UK financial system
- protect and enhance public confidence in the stability of the banking systems of the UK
- protect depositors and investors covered by relevant compensation schemes
- protect, where relevant, **client assets**
- protect public funds, and minimise reliance on public financial support
- avoid interfering with property rights in contravention of the Human Rights Act 1998, and
- ensure the continuity of banking services and critical functions in the UK.

The SRR powers allows the regulatory authorities to:

- transfer all or part of a bank’s business (its shares or property, ie, assets and liabilities) to a private sector purchaser
- transfer all or part of a bank’s property to a bridge bank – a subsidiary of the BoE – pending a future sale
- place a bank into temporary public ownership (this is a Treasury decision)
- apply to place a bank into the bank insolvency procedure (BIP), which is designed to allow for rapid payments to the **Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)** , insured depositors (or transfer of their accounts to a healthy bank), and/or
- apply to use the bank administration procedure (BAP) to deal with a part of a bank which has not transferred and has been put into administration.

The Banking Act creates clearly defined roles for the operation of the SRR. The PRA, in consultation with the Bank and Treasury, will make the ultimate decision whether to place a bank into the SRR. The Treasury decides whether to place a bank into temporary public ownership.

## 1.15 Financial Services Act 2012 – Twin Peaks Regulatory Structure

## The Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)

The Bank of England and Financial Services (BEFSA) Act 2016 (the Act) ended the status of the PRA as a subsidiary of the BoE by making the BoE the PRA. The Act also created the Prudential Regulation Committee (PRC), through which the BoE exercises its functions as the PRA.

The PRA is a focused prudential regulator, with responsibility for the prudential supervision of banks, building societies, insurers, friendly societies, credit unions, Lloyd’s of London and its managing agents, and certain significant investment firms.

The PRA’s role is to contribute to the promotion of the stability of the UK financial system through the microprudential regulation of those firms within its regulatory remit. It has an overall objective to promote the safety and soundness of regulated firms and will meet this objective primarily by seeking to minimise any adverse effects of firm failure on the UK financial system, by ensuring that firms carry on their business in a way that avoids adverse effects on the UK financial system.

In relation to insurance supervision, the PRA has two complementary objectives:

- Firstly, to **secure an appropriate degree of protection for policyholders** .
- Secondly, and as needed, to **minimise the adverse impact** that the failure of an insurer, or the way in which an insurer carries out its business, could have on the stability of the UK financial system.

The PRA will achieve this by:

- aiming to avoid failures which are a cost to the economy
- emphasising resolution planning in order to permit orderly failure
- cooperating closely with the FPC and the FCA to ensure macro and microprudential regulation is aligned across all markets, and
- working with the FCA and others to ensure the UK authorities have a strong voice in international policy-making.

## The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)

The FCA’s role is to ensure that the relevant markets for financial services in the UK function well. It will achieve this strategic objective by:

- securing an appropriate degree of protection for consumers
- protecting and enhancing the integrity of the UK financial system, and
- promoting effective competition in the interests of consumers.

It takes a proportionate approach to regulation, adapting to the needs of different consumers and market participants, and sets and enforces clear expectations for firms and market participants.

The FCA has a duty to promote competition unless this is incompatible with its strategic and operational objectives. It also has a separate duty to minimise the extent to which regulated businesses may be used for purposes connected with financial crime.

In addition to its stated objective, the FCA:

- focuses on the conduct regulation of all firms, covering their dealings with retail customers through to their activities in the wholesale markets. The FCA regulates over 50,000 firms, including those prudentially supervised by the PRA
- is responsible for the prudential supervision of all firms not prudentially supervised by the PRA
- supervises trading infrastructure, including the investment exchanges and over-the-counter (OTC) markets, and monitors firms’ compliance with the UK Market Abuse regime
- has criminal powers to investigate and prosecute insider dealing
- acts as the UK Listing Authority (UKLA), and
- is responsible for overseeing the **Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)** and jointly responsible with the PRA for the FSCS.

Following the **Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 (FSMA 2023)** being published, and the publication of the FSMA (Commencement No.1) Regulations – from 29 August 2023, the FCA (along with the PRA) have a new secondary objective to facilitate the international competitiveness of the UK economy (in particular, the financial services sector) and its medium-to long-term growth, subject to aligning with relevant international standards.

## 1.15.1 The Financial Services Act 2012

The Act is divided into two volumes. Volume I contains the clauses and Volume II contains the Schedules to the Act. The Act replaces and amends parts of FSMA 2000.

Below is a list of the main changes contained in Volume I of the Act which impact FSMA-regulated firms. *Note: large parts of the Act refer simply to the ‘authority’, which meant at the time the ‘Financial Services Authority (FSA); following the restructure of financial services in the UK in 2013, the reference to ‘authority’ now means either the FCA or the PRA.*

## Volume I

## Part 1 – Bank of England (BoE)

- **Deputy governors**
  - The Act specifies that, in addition to the Governor of the BoE, there should also be a deputy governor for financial stability, monetary policy and prudential regulation. In addition, there should be no more than nine non-executive directors (NEDs).
- **Bank’s financial stability objective**
  - The Act amends the stability objective for the BoE. Instead of ‘ *contribute to protecting and enhancing* ’ financial stability, the Bank is now tasked to ‘ *protect and enhance* ’ the financial stability in the UK.
- **Financial stability strategy and Financial Policy Committee (FPC)**
  - The BoE created a subcommittee (the Oversight Committee) consisting of the NEDs, with the purpose of keeping under review the Bank’s performance in relation to its objectives, the duty of the FPC and the Bank’s strategy. In addition, the Committee will monitor the extent to which the objectives have been met, keeping under review the internal financial controls of the Bank, with a view to securing the proper conduct of the Bank’s financial affairs.

## Part 2 – Amendments of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA)

- **The regulators (FCA/PRA)**
  - The FCA’s strategic objective is ensuring that the relevant markets function well. In addition, it also has a number of operational objectives which relate to consumer protection, integrity and competition.
  - In terms of its consumer protection, this means securing an ‘ *appropriate degree of protection for consumers* ’.
  - In terms of integrity, this refers to protecting and enhancing the integrity of the UK financial system.
  - The competition objective promotes effective competition in the interests of consumers in the markets for regulated financial services, and services provided by RIEs. The FCA must maintain arrangements to supervise, monitor and be able to take appropriate enforcement action against authorised persons. It must consult practitioners and consumers on the extent to which its general policies and practices are consistent with its general duties. In satisfying these

requirements, the FCA must also make arrangements to establish a practitioner panel, a smaller business practitioner panel, a markets practitioner panel and a consumer panel.

- The PRA’s general objective is promoting the safety and soundness of PRA-authorised persons. The PRA will seek to satisfy this objective by looking to ensure that the business of PRAauthorised persons is carried on in a way which avoids any adverse effect on the stability of the UK financial system and seeks to minimise the adverse effect that the failure of a PRA-authorised person could be expected to have on the stability of the UK financial system.
- The PRA also has an insurance objective, which is to contribute to the securing of an appropriate degree of protection for those who are or who may become policyholders. The PRA must make arrangements to establish a practitioner panel.
- **Extension of scope of regulation**

The Act added two new regulated activities:

1. Where a regulated activity is carried on by way of business.
2. Relating to information about a person’s financial standing and the setting of financial benchmarks.

This draws into regulated business the need for a person to be an approved person, and includes anyone who contributes to the setting of financial benchmarks. This change occurred because of the LIBOR (London Interbank Offered Rate) rate-setting issue.

- **Designation of activities requiring prudential regulation by the PRA**
  - HM Treasury can specify which regulated activities will be supervised by the PRA, and therefore become PRA-regulated activities, although the PRA can also provide for appropriate exceptions to be applied.
- **Permission to carry on regulated activities**
  - The Act highlights that regulatory permission to carry on one or more permitted regulated activities must be made to the appropriate regulator; for example, the PRA where the regulated activities consist of PRA-regulated activities or the applicant (firm) is a PRA-authorised person, and to the FCA in any other case. This could mean that an authorised firm could be dually authorised by the PRA and the FCA depending on the type of firm and the regulated activities which it undertakes. Other firms would be authorised by either the PRA or the FCA.
- **Prohibition orders** FSMA Section 56 is amended so that both the FCA and the PRA are able to make a prohibition order if the regulators can evidence that an individual is not a **fit and proper** person to perform their functions in relation to a regulated activity.
- **Rules and guidance (general rule-making powers of the FCA and PRA)** The Act provides the FCA and the PRA with powers allowing them to make rules applying to authorised persons, with respect to the carrying on by them of regulated activities or the carrying on by them of activities which are not regulated activities.
  - The FCA also has powers to set rules requiring authorised persons to take specified steps in connection with the setting of a financial benchmark by a specified person.
- **Powers in relation to RIEs and clearing houses** The Act identifies that the FCA is the appropriate regulator for RIEs, and the BoE is the appropriate regulator for RCHs.
- **Power to take disciplinary measures against recognised bodies**
  - The Act provides public censure powers to both the regulators to publish a statement if they consider that a recognised body has contravened a relevant requirement imposed on that body. Both regulators can impose a financial penalty upon that body, an amount of which the regulators may consider appropriate.
- **Discipline and enforcement**

The Act provides that, where HM Treasury considers it necessary, no public disclosure will be made in respect of a warning notice made by the regulators, in addition to the fact that the person receiving the warning notice must not publish any details concerning the notice issued.

- **Provisions about consumer protection and competition** The Act provides for a designated consumer body to make a complaint to the FCA if it believes a feature, or a combination of features appear to be significantly damaging the interest of consumers and/or the market for financial services. Consumer bodies are designated by HM Treasury.

## Part 4 – Collaboration between the HM Treasury and the BoE, FCA or PRA

- **Duty of Bank to notify the HM Treasury of the possible need for public funds** When it appears to the BoE that there is a material risk of the following happening, then it must notify HM Treasury:
  - when it is expected to provide financial assistance to or in respect of a financial institution
  - when there is an expectation that there will be an exercise of the respective powers provided under Parts 1 to 3 of the Banking Act 2009 and the costs associated with the exercise of these powers, and
  - when the manager of the FSCS might reasonably request a loan from the National Loans Fund or financial assistance from HM Treasury.
- **Duty of the HM Treasury, Bank and PRA to coordinate discharge of function**
  - The Act specifies that HM Treasury and the BoE (including the PRA) must arrange to coordinate the discharge of their respective functions so far as they relate to the stability of the UK financial system and affect public interest.
- **Memorandum of understanding (MoU): crisis management** HM Treasury, the BoE and the PRA must prepare and maintain a memorandum describing in general terms how they intend to comply with their duty to coordinate their supervisory activities. HM Treasury must lay before parliament a copy of this memorandum.
- **MoU: international organisations**
  - The Act stipulates that HM Treasury, the BoE and the FCA/PRA must prepare and maintain a memorandum describing how they intend to coordinate the exercise of their relevant functions so far as they relate to their engagement on and with international organisations.

## Part 5 – Inquiries and Investigations

- **Cases in which the HM Treasury may arrange independent inquiries**
  - HM Treasury may arrange separate inquiries which may relate to events occurring in respect of CISs, a person carrying on a regulated activity, listed securities or an issuer of listed securities, particularly when the events may pose a threat to the stability of the UK financial system. In addition, HM Treasury may arrange an inquiry in relation to an event which occurs in respect of an RCH, or a recognised interbank payment system, which poses or could pose a serious threat to the financial stability of, or confidence in, the UK financial system.
- **Power to appoint a person to hold an inquiry**
  - The Act provides the framework for HM Treasury in respect of the scope of any inquiry, the period, conduct, and the direction of the inquiry, including the making of any reports.

• **Powers of appointed person and procedure** The Act details the powers the person has who is appointed to hold an inquiry and the procedures to be followed, including obtaining such information as is required.

- **Duty of the FCA and the PRA to investigate and report on possible regulatory failure** The Act provides for instances where the regulators must undertake an investigation when an event has occurred in relation to a regulated person or a CIS which indicates a significant failure to secure an appropriate degree of protection for consumers, or which could have a significant effect on the integrity of the UK financial system.
- **Conduct of investigation**

The Act states that it is for the regulators to decide how best such investigations are to be carried out. HM Treasury may, however, direct the regulators in relation to the control of the scope and period over which the investigation is to be carried out, the conduct of the investigation and the making of the report.

- **Statements of policy**

The Act requires both regulators to prepare and issue a statement of their policy intentions in relation to the exercise of their functions under the duty to conduct an investigation.

## Part 6 – Investigation of Complaints against Regulators

- **Arrangements for the investigation of complaints**

The Act requires both the FCA, and/or the PRA to make the necessary arrangements for the investigation of complaints arising in connection with the exercise of, or failure to exercise, any of their relevant regulatory functions, and in doing so, to appoint an independent person to be responsible and accountable for the conduct of investigations in accordance with the complaint’s regulators scheme.

- **Investigation of complaints**

The Act provides that the regulators are not necessarily obliged to investigate a complaint, in accordance with the complaints scheme, particularly if the regulators reasonably consider it would be more appropriate for the complaint to be dealt with in another way (ie, referring the complaint to the Upper Tribunal or by the issuing of other legal proceedings).

## 1.16 Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013

The **Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013** implemented some of the recommendations provided by the Independent Commission on Banking, chaired by Sir John Vickers, in relation to the ring-fencing requirements for the banking sector. Other reforms include the closer regulation of banking executives and the creation of individual accountability by establishing the **Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR)** , and the creation of a new criminal offence in relation to mismanagement leading to the failure of a bank. The Act also introduced a bail-in **stabilisation** option which forms part of the special resolution regime (SRR), the introduction of a payment systems regulator, and further powers which allow the FCA to take measures to limit the special resolution regime, and further allow for the introduction of the payment systems regulator.

It also implemented the key recommendations of the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS), which tackled and provided an urgent review of the professional standards and culture in the banking industry following the revelations of the LIBOR rate manipulation.

The Banking Reform Act is made up of eight parts. These are as follows:

- **Part 1** – Ring-fencing.
- **Part 2** – Depositor and the FSCS.
- **Part 3** – Bail-in stabilisation option.
- **Part 4** – Conduct of persons working in financial services sector.
- **Part 5** – Regulation of payment systems.
- **Part 6** – Special administration for operators of certain infrastructure systems.
- **Part 7** – Miscellaneous.
- **Part 8** – Final provisions.

The most relevant parts of the Act are as follows.

## Part 1 – Ring-Fencing

The underlying rationale of the ring-fencing regime is to insulate critical banking services from shocks to the financial system. The ring-fencing regime separates core retail banking services, which are of most importance to private individuals as well as smaller companies, from the investment banking services.

Core activities presently consist of accepting deposits or other payments, facilities for withdrawing money and for overdrafts. A UK-incorporated institution, which carries on these activities (a ring-fenced bank) will be subject to the regime unless it is regarded as a type of entity which is exempted; for example, certain categories of deposit-taking institutions, including building societies, credit unions, or banks which hold deposits below £25 billion. UK branches of non-UK banks will not be subject to the ring-fencing regime requirements, although UK-incorporated bank subsidiaries of non-UK banking groups are within its scope. These provisions came into force in 2019.

HM Treasury proposed a review of the ‘ring-fencing’ requirements as part of its Edinburgh Reform Regime – refer to chapter 5, section 1.3 for more details.

## Part 4 – Conduct of a Person Working in the Financial Services Sector

This is known as ‘SM&CR’, which initially applied to banks, UK branches of foreign banks, and insurance companies, but has been extended to apply to most regulated firms.

This is discussed in greater detail in chapter 2, section 2.

## 1.17 Bank of England and Financial Services Act (BEFSA) 2016

The Act is the third major piece of legislation that the government made to fundamentally reform the financial sector.

Of relevance is Sections 21 to 26, as they relate to the conduct of a person working in the financial services sector:

- **Section 21** – extending to authorised persons provisions of Part 5 of the FSMA 2000 (performance of regulated activities) which applies only to relevant authorised persons.
- **Section 22** – provides powers to the FCA and the PRA to make transitional provisions.
- **Section 23** – inserts the PRA and the FCA as the administrators of the **Senior Managers Regime** .
- **Section 24** – rules of conduct (minor cosmetic changes).
- **Section 25** – misconduct (minor cosmetic changes).
- **Section 26** – decisions causing a financial institution to fail: meaning of insolvency.

## 1.18 UK Financial Services Act 2021

The 2019 Queen’s Speech background paper expressed a desire to bring forward, in the upcoming parliamentary session, legislation to:

*‘ensure that the UK maintains its world-leading regulatory standards and remains open to international markets after we leave the EU’* .

When introduced to the House of Commons, the Bill’s stated objectives were to: enhance the UK’s world-leading prudential standards and promote financial stability, promote openness between the UK and international markets, and maintain an effective financial services regulatory framework and sound capital markets. However, HM Treasury added a fourth objective in recognition of amendments made during the Bill’s passage through Parliament, which is to: *‘protect consumers who use a range of financial services’* .

In April 2021, the **Financial Services Act 2021** received Royal Assent and came into effect.

It was the UK’s first step, post-Brexit, in re-establishing control of financial services regulations and ensuring that the UK remains an ‘open and dynamic financial centre’.

Although most of the Act seeks to shape the regulatory framework for UK financial services, it also contains a range of measures unconnected to Brexit. These included the following:

- Part 4A Permissions
- Duty of care
- Refer to chapter 2, section 6 for further details on the FCA’s ‘ **Consumer Duty** ’ initiative and rules.
- • Money laundering
  - Offences, forfeiture of money and overseas trustees
- Debt respite scheme
- Help to save
- Buy now, pay later (BNPL) products
- Financial collateral
- Payment services and the provision of cash.

The post-Brexit reforms included the following:

- UK Prudential framework
  - Investment firm’s Prudential regime, ie, the FCA’s Investment Firms Prudential requirements for MiFID firms
  - Basel III and 3.1 standards
- Access to financial markets
  - Overseas funds regime
  - UK MiFIR equivalence
  - PRIIPS
  - UK EMIR
- Insider dealing and market abuse
  - Maintaining insider lists
  - Transactions by senior managers
  - Personal data
- ‘IBOR’
  - Providing the FCA with the necessary powers to manage the cessation of critical ‘LIBOR’ benchmarks in a manner to protect consumers and ensures market integrity.

Further details on ‘insider dealing and market abuse’ can be found in chapter 3, section 3.

With regards to ‘UK Prudential Framework’, the following is a summary of the Investment Firms Prudential Regime (IFPR).

The UK IFPR follows the EU’s Investment Firms Directive and Regulations, which came into effect in June 2021, given the UK was heavily involved with the development of the legislation.

However, following Brexit, the UK undertook some minor amendments to ensure that it is fit for purpose for the UK market.

The UK Regime came into effect on 1 January 2022.

The approach under the IFPR is to consider the harm an FCA investment firm can cause to others based on the activities that it carries out and ensures that the amount of capital and liquid assets it holds is sufficient to undertake an orderly wind-down.

The following are the outcomes that were sought:

- A prudential regime that is aligned to the way that investment firms run their business:
  - taking into account the different business models of FCA investment firms, and
  - better protecting consumers and markets from the harm these firms may pose.
- FCA investment firms are subject to meaningful and consistent prudential requirements.
- FCA investment firms spend less time on complex capital requirement calculations that do little to help them to manage risk:
  - allowing a firm’s management more time to focus on running the business, managing and mitigating any harm and risk, and
  - allowing the FCA to focus on how a firm is managing itself.
- Prudential rules that are understandable and accessible, with most rules brought into a new single prudential sourcebook (MIFIDPRU).

## 1.18.1 Sections of the FCA MIFIDPRU Sourcebook

## Application (MIFIDPRU1)

The FCA removed the current definitions of FCA investment firms in BIPRU, IFPRU and exempt-CAD.

Rather, there will be two broad categories of FCA investment firm – a ‘small and non-interconnected’ investment (SNI) firm or a ‘non-SNI’ firm.

The prudential requirements in the IFPR are designed to scale with the size and complexity of the firm.

## **Prudential Consolidation and the Group Capital Test (MIFIDPRU2)**

Prudential consolidation will apply where there is an investment form group that comprises of a UK parent undertaking and its subsidiaries.

## Own Funds Resources (MIFIDPRU3)

The types and treatment of own funds under the UK IFPR will follow the same requirements as under the UK CRR (with some minor amendments) and will comprise the following three classes of capital:

- Common Equity Tier 1 (or CET1) capital.
- Additional Tier 1 (or AT1) capital.
- Tier 2 (or T2) capital.

## Own Funds Requirements (MIFIDPRU4)

The own funds requirements of FCA investment firms are based on a permanent minimum capital requirement (‘PMR’) and requirements based on the activities and services that the firm undertakes.

There are a number of transitional provisions to assist firms to adjust to the capital requirements of the UK IFPR – especially where there is a relatively large increase from their previous capital requirements.

## Concentration Risk (MIFIDPRU5)

General provisions on monitoring and control of concentration risk apply to all firms, with limited exemptions.

Provisions on limits and own funds requirements for concentration risk (K-CON) only apply to firms with a trading book, ie, trading as principal.

## Liquidity (MIFIDPRU6)

The UK IFPR applies a basic liquid asset requirement for firms which must hold liquid assets.

The FCA has noted that core liquid assets to be used to meet the basic liquid assets requirement must be denominated in sterling – as it considers that firms liquidity needs are in sterling.

The basic liquidity requirements form part of the overall framework that a firm must adopt for assessing its individual liquidity needs – this framework is called the ‘Internal Capital and Risk Assessment (ICARA)’.

## Risk Management and Governance, ICARA and SREP (MIFIDPRU7)

The FCA approach is underpinned by the following principles:

- Firms must consider and account for the risk of harm they pose to consumers and markets.
- The ‘ICARA’ process is the centrepiece of a firm’s risk management processes.
- Senior management is responsible for ensuring the appropriateness of the firm’s governance and risk management.
- The FCA will intervene at given intervention points if firms fail to act appropriately, or their actions prove unsuccessful.
- The FCA expectations on firms are proportionate to the risk of harm posed.

## Overall Financial Adequacy Rule (OFAR)

Firms’ financial and non-financial resources must be appropriate to the regulated activities that they undertake.

The requirements provide a link between financial resources required under the OIFPR and the FCA’s Threshold Conditions for permissions.

Firms determine the OFAR through their ICARA process, which represents what is required to meet the OFAR, unless otherwise advised by the FCA.

## Internal Capital Adequacy and Risk Assessment (ICARA)

All firms will be required to undertake an ‘ICARA’, which should be monitored/updated on a continuous process.

## Disclosure and ESG (MIFIDPRU8)

The FCA sets out the disclosures that are required for both ‘SNI’ and ‘non-SNI’ firms. Disclosure will be required for:

- risk management
- own funds
- own funds requirements
- investment policy
- governance requirements, and
- remuneration.

## Regulatory Reporting (MIFIDPRU9)

The FCAs intention is to only collect data that will let it supervise firms against the requirements of the UK IFPR.

Firms will be required to complete financial reporting on a quarterly basis.

## 1.19 BREXIT – The Implications for the UK and Europe

Brexit is the term used to define the UK’s exit from the EU, although the UK left the EU on 31 January 2020, it  remained subject to EU laws/rules until 31 December 2020.

The European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 (‘EUWA’) gives the government powers to amend current legislation to ensure that it functions when the UK is no longer an EU member state. The intention is that the same rules and laws apply on the day after the exit as on the day before, as far as possible.

Following Brexit, UK firms lost their ability to access the EU single market on the basis of ‘ **passporting** ’; this means that UK firms are limited in their ability to provide financial services to EU clients, likewise for EU firms to UK clients.

UK firms may look for exemptions in local law that allow for access by firms from non-EU countries or apply for permission from the European Commission, or other EU bodies under the access regimes for third countries provided in EU legislation.

To explain what this means to existing EU regulations:

- With respect to MiFIR, from 1 January 2021, the onshored (retained) ‘UK MiFIR’ legislation applies to all UK regulated firms.
- UK MiFIR means the Regulation (EU) No 600/2014 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 15 May 2014 on markets in financial instruments and amending Regulation (EU) No 648/2012 as it forms part of domestic law of the UK by virtue of the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018.
- UK MiFIR, together with the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive 2014/65/EU, as implemented in the UK by the FCA in its FCA rules (UK MiFID) ensure UK firms are complying with the same legislation as EU firms (although, over time, regulatory divergence will have an impact).

However, there were some areas where the FCA expected firms to be compliant with the UK onshored version of EU Regulations from 31 December 2020, they were:

- MiFID II Transaction Reporting
- EMIR Reporting
- SFTR Reporting
- certain MAR requirements
- CASS rules, and
- market-making exemption under the Short Selling Rules (SSR).

## 1.19.1 European Union (Withdrawal) Act

The European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 (‘EUWA’) created the ability for EU Regulations that were in existence before the Brexit transitional date of 31 December 2020, to be retained as primary legislation in UK law – and can only be updated by an Act of Parliament or any other primary legislation.

The European Union (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020 is an Act of the Parliament of the UK that makes legal provision for both the ratification of the Brexit Withdrawal Agreement and implements it into British domestic law. The Withdrawal Agreement was the result of Brexit negotiations.

Onshoring, through the EUWA, provides a basis for directly applicable EU law to continue to be effective in the UK after Brexit. For financial services, this means that EMIR, MAR and MiFIR have been ‘onshored’ in the UK and are now part of UK law – they are referred to as UK regimes (‘UK EMIR’, ‘UK MiFIR’ and ‘UK MAR’).

With regards to financial services, for the time being, the UK and the EU have the same regulations that apply – however, as noted in chapter 5 of the workbook, the UK is undertaking a review of financial services regulation now that the UK is no longer a member of the EU.

## 1.20 Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA) 2023

As noted by HM Treasury, the FSMA 2023 does:

- implement the outcomes of the Future Regulatory Framework (FRF) Review
  - this is now called the Smarter Regulatory Framework (SRF)
- maintain the UK’s position as an open and global financial hub
- harness the opportunities of innovative technologies in financial services
- bolster the competitiveness of UK markets and promote the effective use of capital, and
- support the levelling up agenda, promote financial inclusion and consumer protection.
