## 1. UK Statutory Structure

## 1.1 Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA) 2000

The **Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA) 2000** is the UK Act of Parliament which created the **Financial Services Authority (FSA)** , which was subsequently split into the **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)** and the **Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)** .

Throughout this workbook, the term ‘FSMA’ is used, which means the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000, as amended.

Although the FSA has been replaced by the FCA and the PRA, the FSMA still exists and is still relevant to UK financial services legislation. Parts of FSMA were amended by the **Financial Services Act 2012** , which created the FCA and the PRA as the new regulators for financial services in the UK.


FSMA was enacted in 2000 and its purpose was to:

- establish a new single regulator (the Financial Services Authority (FSA))

- make provisions for the regulation of financial services and markets, and

- provide for the transfer of certain statutory functions relating to building societies, friendly societies, industrial and provident societies and certain other mutual societies, and for connected purposes.

FSMA imposed four statutory objectives on the then regulator (the FSA) which are still in place:

- **Market confidence** – maintaining confidence in the financial system.

- **Public awareness** – promoting public understanding of the financial system.

- **Consumer protection** – securing the appropriate degree of protection for consumers.

- **Reduction of financial crime** – reducing the extent to which it is possible for a business carried on by a regulated person to be used for a purpose connected with financial crime.

Refer to chapter 2 for the statutory objectives and organisational requirements of the PRA and the FCA.

The statutory objectives are supported by a set of Principles of Good Regulation to be considered by the FCA and the PRA when discharging their functions (these are explained in more detail in chapter 2).

These are:

- efficiency and economy

- the role of management

- proportionality

- innovation

- international character

- competition, and

- public awareness.

## 1.2 Regulated and Prohibited Activities (Part II)

FSMA is subdivided into 30 parts. Part II, Regulated and Prohibited Activities, includes the general prohibition, which simply states that no person can carry out a **regulated activity** in the UK, or purport to do so, unless they are either authorised or exempt. It is left to a separate statutory instrument, known as the **Regulated Activities Order 2001 (RAO)** , to clarify precisely what these regulated activities are.

The most relevant parts of the legislation are summarised over the next few pages.

For a person to become authorised, that person must first apply to the regulator(s) (depending on whether the firm is single or dual regulated) for permission to perform particular regulated activities. If that person satisfies the **threshold conditions** , they will be granted permission.

Note that the term person here means the trading entity or firm, which could be incorporated as a company, or an unincorporated entity such as a sole trader or partnership or an individual.

Breaches occurring within the regulatory ‘perimeter’ are investigated by the FCA.


## 1.2.1 The General Prohibition

Section 23 of FSMA makes it a criminal offence to carry on regulated activities in breach of the general prohibition – in other words, it is a criminal offence to carry on regulated activities without first being authorised or being subject to one of the exemptions. The offence is punishable by a maximum sentence of two years in prison and/or an unlimited fine. It may be a defence to show that the individual had taken all reasonable precautions and exercised all due diligence to avoid committing the offence.

FSMA gives the FCA the power to make an order ( **prohibition order** ) barring an individual from performing a specified function. The prohibition order may relate to a specified regulated activity, any regulated activity falling within a specified description, all regulated activities by authorised persons generally, or to any person within a specified class of authorised person. An authorised person must take reasonable care to ensure that no function, in relation to the carrying on of a regulated activity, is performed by a person who is prohibited from performing that function by a prohibition order.

Any agreement made by a person in contravention of the general prohibition is unenforceable by that person against the other party. This is also the case for agreements made as a result of the activities of someone who was contravening the general prohibition, even if that person is not a party to the agreement. The other party is entitled to recover any money or property transferred under the agreement, and to be compensated for any loss suffered.

In order to understand whether someone is in breach of the FSMA general prohibition rules, it is necessary to understand what constitutes regulated activities. FSMA provides that these will be defined by reference to two sets of criteria:

1. A **range of investments** (including assets which we might typically think of as investments, for example, shares and bonds, but also other assets such as deposits and **contracts of insurance** ), and

2. A **range of activities** which may be carried on in connection with those investments, for example, dealing, managing or advising on investments, accepting deposits and effecting contracts of insurance. Not all of the activities can be related to all the investments – some are specific to just one type, eg, effecting a contract of insurance relates only to contracts of insurance.

If a person is performing one (or more) of these specified activities in relation to one (or more) of the specified investments, then that person is performing a regulated activity and requires either **authorisation** or an exemption. It is the combination of carrying on a specified activity in relation to a specified investment which gives rise to regulated activity.

The investments and activities are detailed in secondary legislation issued under FSMA – principally the RAO 2001, as amended in following years.


## 1.2.2 Specified Investments

The following are defined as specified investments within the RAO (as contained in the PERG sourcebook – PERG 2.6):

1. **Deposits** – that is, money paid by one person to another, with or without interest being earned on it, and on terms that it will be repaid when a specified event occurs (eg, when a demand is made). The obvious example is deposits held with banks and building societies. For clarity, the RAO sets out certain exclusions – eg, electronic money (covered separately below), money paid in advance for the provision of goods or services and money paid as a security deposit.

2. **Electronic money** – that is, monetary value (as represented by a claim on the e-money issuer) which is stored on an electronic device, issued on receipt of funds and accepted as a means of payment by third parties. In effect, it is an electronic substitute for notes and coins.

3. **Rights under contracts of insurance** – which includes both long-term insurance contracts (eg, life assurance and endowment policies) and general insurance (eg, motor and building insurance). The FCA provides guidance on identifying a contract of insurance in their Perimeter Guidance Sourcebook (PERG), since the identification process is not always as straightforward as you might think.

4. **Shares** – defined widely as shares or stock in any company (wherever incorporated) or in any unincorporated body formed outside the UK. The RAO definition excludes shares in open-ended investment companies (OEICs), since an OEIC is a **collective investment scheme (CIS)** and is captured under a separate definition. It also excludes some building society shares, since these can behave like, and are therefore, captured under the definition of, deposits.

5. **Instruments creating or acknowledging indebtedness** – this includes debentures, debenture stock and loan stock and, as a ‘mopping-up’ clause, specifies ‘ _any other instrument creating or acknowledging debt_ ’. Again, the definition is wide, so the RAO provides for some exclusions, eg, trade bills, cheques and other bills of exchange, and (because they are separately captured) contracts of insurance and government and public securities.

6. **Government and public securities (debt instruments)** – eg, gilts and US treasuries, and local authority/municipal loan stocks. Again, certain instruments are excluded, for example, trade bills issued by government bodies, and National Savings & Investments (NS&I) deposits and products.

7. **Alternative finance, investment bonds/alternative debentures** – a form of Shariah-compliant bond or _sukuk_ . Shariah is defined as ‘ _the moral code and religious law of a prophetic religion_ ’ (Islam).

8. **Instruments giving entitlements to investments** – warrants and similar instruments entitling the holder to subscribe in shares, debentures, government and public securities at a set price, and on or between set date(s) in the future.

9. **Certificates representing certain securities** – certificates and the like which confer rights in other instruments such as shares, debentures, gilts and warrants. It would include, for example, American depositary receipts (ADRs), which typically give the holder rights over a certain number of a UK company’s shares. These ADRs are designed to offer the investor (typically US-based) a more convenient way to invest in UK shares, because the shares are dealt in, and pay dividends in, US dollars. Also covered here are other depositary receipts, for example, global depositary receipts (GDRs).

10. **Units in a collective investment scheme** – covers holdings in any CIS, whether it is an authorised scheme or an unregulated scheme – for example, units in an authorised **unit trust** or shares in an OEIC, which may also be described as an investment company with variable capital (ICVC). This is why OEICs are specifically excluded from the heading of ‘shares’ (in point 4). Unregulated schemes can also take other legal forms, for example, limited partnerships; therefore, rights in such partnerships fall within the scope of units in a CIS.


11. **Rights under a pension scheme (Stakeholder and Personal)** – stakeholder pensions are pension schemes set up under the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999 which have to meet certain criteria and be run in a particular way. Personal Pension Schemes are designed for individuals who do not belong to a company scheme and/or who wish to take control of their own investment decisions for their pension provisions, for example, self-invested personal pensions (SIPPs). A wide range of investments may be held within a personal pension scheme.

12. **Options** – are the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell a fixed quantity of an underlying asset for a fixed price on or between fixed dates. **Options** are only covered if they relate to:

   - securities or contractually-based investments (eg, stocks, shares, bonds, or **futures** on similar instruments)

   - currencies

   - certain precious metals, including gold and silver, or

   - options on futures contracts and other **contracts for difference (CFDs)** ; see point 14 below.

13. **Futures** – contracts for the sale, or purchase of an asset if delivery and settlement will be made at a future date, at a price agreed when the contract is made. The RAO excludes futures agreed for commercial, as opposed to investment/speculative purposes; so, a contract to buy cocoa at an agreed price at some future date will not be caught if it is carried out by a chocolate maker to help them secure a certain price for the raw materials needed.

14. **Contracts for differences (CFDs)** – for example, spread bets and interest rate swaps. These are contracts where the investor’s aim is to secure a profit (or avoid a loss) by making money by reference to fluctuations in the value of an index, or from the price of some other underlying property. The RAO excludes futures and options since these are separately caught under the provisions already detailed.

15. **Lloyd’s syndicate capacity and syndicate membership** – relates in the main to the activities of the Lloyd’s members’ agents and managing agents.

16. **Rights under a funeral plan contract** – ie, certain plans under which the customer pays for benefits which will pay for their, or someone else’s, funeral upon their death.

17. **Rights under a regulated mortgage contract*** – ie, mortgage loans secured by a first legal charge on the property, where at least 40% of the property is to be used for the borrower’s, or some related party’s, dwelling. This specified investment also includes lifetime mortgages, a type of equity release transaction.

18. **Rights under a home reversion plan*** – another type of equity release transaction, whereby the customer sells part or all of their home to the plan provider in return for a lump sum or series of payments. They retain the right to stay in their home until they die or move into residential care.

19. **Rights under a home purchase plan*** – are alternatives to mortgages, which allow people to buy their homes while complying with Islamic principles, because financing via an interest-bearing mortgage is not permitted (under the strict interpretation of these Islamic principles).

20. **Rights under a regulated sale and rent back agreement*** – whereby a person sells all or part of their qualifying interest in land, or property, but remains in occupation of at least 40% of the land or property.

21. **Credit agreement (including Article 36H agreement)** – with respect to the regulated activities of entering into a regulated credit agreement as lender and exercising (or having the right to exercise) the lender’s rights and duties under a regulated credit agreement. This is sub-divided into four different types.

22. **Consumer hire agreement** – an agreement between a person (the owner) and an individual. This does not include a hire-purchase agreement.

23. **Greenhouse gas emission allowance** – there are two specified investments relating to the scheme for greenhouse gas emission allowance trading within the EU: the first kind comprises emission allowances that are auctioned as financial instruments or two-day emission spots; the second kind is an emission allowance itself.


24. **Rights to or interests in investments** – rights in anything that is a specified investment listed, excluding rights in home finance transactions, are themselves a specified investment.

25. **Emission allowances** – an allowance consisting of units recognised for compliance with an ‘Emission Trading Scheme.

The investments marked * are collectively known as ‘home finance transactions’.

## 1.2.3 Specified Activities

These include both classes of activity and categories of investment. An activity is a regulated activity for the purposes of FSMA if it is an activity of a specified kind which is carried on by way of business and relates to an investment of a specified kind. Investment includes any asset, right or interest, and ‘specified’ means laid down and identified in an order made by **HM Treasury** .

The RAO defines regulated activities by reference first to the range of specified investments, and secondly, the activities a firm may carry on in relation to those investments. An activity which is listed, but which is carried out in relation to an asset which is not a specified investment, is not a regulated activity. We also know that if a business is carrying on a regulated activity, then according to the General Prohibition (Article 19, Part II of FSMA), they must be either authorised or exempt.

The regulated activities themselves are as follows (as contained in the PERG sourcebook – PERG 2.7, and in The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Regulated Activities) Order 2001:

1. **Accepting deposits** – mainly the preserve of banks and building societies, but other firms may find themselves caught under this activity.

2. **Issuing e-money** – ie, acting as the issuer of e-money, as has already been described in point 2 of section 1.2.2.

3. **Effecting or carrying out contracts of insurance as principal** – this essentially applies to insurers.

4. **Insurance risk transformation** – the activity of assuming risk from an undertaking, under a contract of insurance.

5. **Dealing in investments as principal or agent** – this applies only to certain specified investments. ‘Dealing’ means buying, selling, subscribing for or underwriting the investments concerned. When the firm deals as principal (eg, on its own account), it applies only to those investments which are:

   - **securities** – shares, debentures and warrants, or

   - **contractually-based investments** , such as options, futures, CFDs and life policies.

   - When the firm deals as an agent (ie, on behalf of someone else), it applies to **securities** (in the same way as that for dealing as principal) and **relevant investments** . Relevant investments include contractually-based investments (again, in the same way as that for dealing as principal), and/or rights under pure protection and general insurance contracts.

6. **Bidding in emission auctions** – the bidding for emission allowances on an auction platform.

7. **Arranging deals in investments** – this covers:

   - bringing about deals in investments – that is, the involvement of the person is essential to bringing about or concluding the contract, and also

   - making arrangements with a view to transacting in investments, which may be quite widely interpreted as any arrangement pursuant to transactions in investments, for example making introductions.


   - The ‘arranging’ activities relate only to specified investments which:

   - are securities (eg, shares, debentures or warrants)

   - are relevant investments (eg, options, futures, CFDs and rights under insurance contracts)

   - have the underwriting capacity of a Lloyd’s syndicate or membership of a Lloyd’s syndicate, and

   - have rights to or interests in any of the above.

   - A typical example might be a broker making arrangements for its **client** to enter into a specific insurance contract.

8. **Arranging home finance transactions** – this covers the arranging and making of arrangements in relation to a regulated mortgage contract, a lifetime mortgage contract, a home reversion, a home purchase plan and regulated sale and rent back agreements. These home finance transactions are captured in the same way as those for arranging deals in investments.

9. **Operating a multilateral trading facility (MTF)** – an MTF is any system run by an investment firm or a market operator which brings together multiple third-party buying and selling interests in financial instruments, through the system and in accordance with non-discretionary rules. MTFs can be assimilated to alternative trading exchanges, providing additional pools of liquidity to their members (usually banks, major mutual funds and large insurance companies).

10. **Operating an organised trading facility (OTF)** – an OTF is a new type of regulated trading venue that came into force following the implementation of the **Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID II)** . OTFs permit the trading of derivatives and other non-equity financial instruments. Equity-like financial instruments are not permitted to be traded on an OTF.

11. **Credit broking activities relating to entering forms of consumer credit as lender –** rights under any contract under which one person provides another with credit and contracts for hire of goods, rights under a contract for the bailment or hiring of goods to a person other than a body corporate.

12. **Operating an electronic system in relation to lending activities relating to consumer credit (credit agreement and/or consumer hire agreement)** – entering into a regulated consumer hire agreement as owner; exercising, or having the right to exercise, the owner’s rights and duties under a regulated consumer hire agreement; credit broking; operating an electronic system in relation to lending; debt adjusting; debt counselling; debt collecting and debt administration.

13. **Managing investments** – applies in respect of investments belonging to someone other than the investment manager, for example, the customer, and/or when the investment manager exercises discretion over the management of the portfolio, on the customer’s behalf. The investment portfolio must include, either securities or contractually-based investments, or a mixture of both. A typical example is a portfolio manager. Non-discretionary management (where the investment firm does not take the final investment decision on a portfolio) is not covered under this heading. Nondiscretionary management is captured under the separately defined regulated activities of dealing in investments and advising on investments.

14. **Assisting in the administration and performance of a contract of insurance** – the activities carried on by intermediaries after the conclusion of a contract of insurance and for/or behalf of policyholders, in particular in the event of a claim.

15. **Activities in relation to debt (consumer credit)**

   - i. adjusting – negotiating with the lender of the owner, on behalf of the borrower or hirer; taking over, in return for payments by the borrower or hirer and any other activity concerned with the liquidation of a debt

   - ii. counselling – providing advice to a borrower about liquidation of a debt due under a credit agreement

   - iii. debt counselling

   - iv. debt collecting, and

   - v. debt administration.


16. **Safeguarding and administering investments** – this applies in the context of securities (eg, shares and debentures) and contractually-based investments (eg, options, futures, CFDs, and other qualifying insurance contracts). This section is subdivided into safeguarding and the administration of assets, and arranging, safeguarding and the administration of assets. In addition, the firm must also be holding the assets for someone else, and must be both safeguarding and administering the assets, categorised under this heading. A typical example could be a custodian bank, which might hold title documents to investments, hold dematerialised investments in its name, and administer the collection of interest, and/or dividends, or the application of corporate actions.

17. **Advising on investments** – this covers giving advice on securities and relevant investments. It does not extend to giving advice about deposits or generic advice (eg, ‘invest in the US, not in Europe’). Neither does it extend to giving information – facts, which are not tailored to constitute a recommendation – instead of advice. This does not apply to advice given in the course of carrying on the regulated activity of providing basic advice on stakeholder products. This includes advising on peer-to-peer (P2P) agreements and conversion or transfer of pension benefits. This is sub-divided into: advising on investments (except pension transfers and pension opt-outs) and advising on pension transfers and pension opt-outs.

18. **Sending dematerialised instructions and allowing dematerialised instructions to be sent** – this covers firms operating systems which allow for the electronic transfer of the title in certain investments (again, securities and contractually-based investments), and those which trigger instructions to be sent on those systems. An example of such a system is **CREST** .

19. **Managing Undertakings for Collective Investment in Transferable Securities (UCITS); acting as a trustee or depository of a UCITS; managing an alternative investment fund (AIF) (where the AIF is authorised/unauthorised); acting as a trustee or depository of an AIF (where the AIF is authorised/unauthorised) and establishing, operating and winding up a collective investment scheme** – this activity captures persons who set up, operate/administer and wind up any type of CIS, whether an authorised or an unregulated scheme. Acting as a trustee of an AUT (authorised unit trust), or as the depository or sole director of an OEIC are also separate regulated activities.

20. **Establishing, operating and winding up a pension scheme (stakeholder pension scheme and personal pension scheme)** – this activity captures those who set up, operate, or administer and wind-up stakeholder pension schemes, SIPPs and personal pension schemes. These activities may be carried out by the scheme trustees and/or the scheme administrators.

21. **Providing basic advice on stakeholder products** – this is a specialist-regulated activity, for those who advise on stakeholder products only. Stakeholder products conform to certain specific criteria for cost and accessibility.

22. **Advising on the conversion or transfer of pension benefits** – this covers advising people on the transfer of funds from occupational pension schemes. Note that advising on transfers from personal or stakeholder pension schemes would be covered under other categories.

23. **Lloyd’s market activities** – advising on syndicate participation at Lloyd’s; arranging (bringing about) deals in investments; making arrangements with a view to transactions in investments; managing the underwriting capacity of a Lloyd’s syndicate as a managing agent at Lloyd’s and the activity of arranging deals in contracts of insurance written at Lloyd’s.

24. **Entering into a funeral plan contract** – the ‘provider’ is the person to whom the prepayments are made and who undertakes to provide, or secure the provision of, the funeral at some future point, for example, the funeral director or a third party who arranges for another person to provide the funeral.

25. **Advising home finance transactions** – this covers advising on the merits of entering into, or varying the terms of, a regulated mortgage contract, a lifetime mortgage contract, a home reversion plan, a home purchase plan, or a regulated sale, and rent back agreement and regulated credit


agreements for the acquisition of land. All of these home finance transactions constitute regulated activity.

26. **Agreeing to carry on specified kinds of activity** – entering into a legally binding agreement to provide the service that it concerns

27. **Activities of reclaim funds** – ‘dormant’ account funds.

28. **Specified benchmarks** – the activity of operating and managing a benchmark.

Advising, dealing and arranging activities, when carried on in connection with a contract of insurance, and the activity of assisting in the administration and performance of a contract of insurance, are collectively known as ‘insurance mediation activities’ and are subject to the provisions of the Insurance Mediation Directive (IMD).

The following is a link to an FCA website page that lists the financial activities that it regulates: https:// www.fca.org.uk/firms/authorisation/how-to-apply/activities.

The FCA’s Perimeter Guidance sourcebook also contains details on authorisation and regulated activities (chapter 2); this is located in the ‘ _Regulatory/Registry Guide_ ’ block of the Handbook.

## 1.2.4 Regulated Activities Order (RAO) – PRA

The RAO specifies, for the purposes of FSMA, whose regulated activities are PRA-regulated activities and are, therefore, the regulated activities which are subject to prudential regulation by the PRA, rather than by the FCA.

The activities are:

- a. the activity of accepting deposits as specified by Article 5 of the RAO

- b. the activity of effecting a contract of insurance as principal as specified by Article 10(1) of the RAO

- c. the activity of carrying out a contract of insurance as principal as specified by Article 10(2) of the RAO

- d. dealing in investments as principal when carried on by a person designated by the PRA under Article 3 of the Order

- e. the activity of managing the underwriting capacity of a Lloyd’s syndicate as a managing agent at Lloyd’s as specified by Article 57 of the RAO

- f. the activity of arranging, by the society incorporated by Lloyd’s Act 1871(b) by the name of Lloyd’s, of deals in contracts of insurance written at Lloyd’s as specified by Article 58 of the RAO

   - g. any other regulated activity carried on by that society in connection with, or for the purposes of, the activity specified in (f) above.

## 1.3 Authorisation and Exemption (Part III)

Firms wishing to carry on regulated activities in the UK need to be either authorised or exempt, unless they are subject to one of the exclusions outlined in section 1.3.2 below. The term ‘firm’ includes individuals, bodies corporate (companies), branches of companies, partnerships and unincorporated associations.

Authorisation is provided under Part IV of FSMA (as amended by the Financial Services Act 2012, now referred to as ‘Part 4A’ permission). **Part 4A permission** is given by the FCA and/or the PRA, and, once granted, the firm becomes an authorised person. As an authorised person, the firm can carry on


regulated activities of which it is authorised without breaching the general prohibition and committing a criminal offence.

However, permission is not normally granted for all regulated activities. The Part 4A permission specifies which regulated activities the firm can carry on, those investments to which the activities relate, and any further requirements or special conditions (for example, a requirement that the firm should report to the FCA/PRA more frequently than is normally the case).

The regulated activities which a firm is given permission to conduct can be limited. For example, it may be permitted to deal as principal, but only for a particular type of client.

## 1.3.1 Guidance for Authorisation

Under Section 23 of FSMA (Contravention of the general prohibition), a person commits a criminal offence if they carry on regulated activities in breach of the general prohibition set out in Section 19 of the Act (the General Prohibition). Although a person who commits the criminal offence can be subject to a maximum of two years’ imprisonment and an unlimited fine, it is a defence for a person to show that they took all reasonable precautions and exercised all due diligence to avoid committing the offence.

Another consequence of a breach of the general prohibition is that certain agreements entered into by such persons could be unenforceable (see Sections 26 to 29 of the Act). This applies to agreements entered into by persons who are in breach of the general prohibition, but also applies to any agreement entered into by an authorised person, if the agreement is made as a result of the activities of a person who is in breach of the general prohibition.

Under Section 22 of FSMA, for an activity to be regarded as a regulated activity, it must be carried on by way of business. Whether or not an activity is carried on by way of business is ultimately a question of fact taking into account several factors. These include the degree of continuity, the existence of a commercial element, the scale of the regulated activity, the proportion which the activity represents of other activities carried on by the same person, but which are not regulated. The nature of the particular regulated activity which is carried on will also be relevant to the factual analysis.

## 1.3.2 Exclusions

Activities that fall under these exclusions need not be submitted to the regulator for authorisation.

## Exclusions from Dealing as Principal – Absence of Holding Out

Dealing in investments as principal is a regulated activity, which (on the face of it) means that persons dealing for themselves in the hope of making profits are required to be authorised or exempt.

This regulated activity is, however, restricted to those persons who are holding themselves out as and acting as **market makers** . The result is that a person buying shares for only themselves does not need to be authorised or exempt, unless they hold themselves out to be a dealer in investments.


In other words:

- firms which are professional dealers, for example market makers, and hold themselves out as such, are carrying on a regulated activity, but

- individuals or companies who are not in the business of dealing in investments, and who invest only for themselves in the hope of making profit, are excluded.

This exclusion relates to both securities (shares and bonds) and contractually based investments (futures, options and CFDs), as long as they are entered into by an unauthorised person.

## Other Exclusions

There are other exclusions covering situations where dealing as principal is not classified as a regulated activity:

1. A bank providing finance to another person and accepting an instrument acknowledging the debt.

2. A company or other organisation issuing its own shares, warrants or debentures.

3. Using options, futures and CFDs for risk-management purposes, as long as the company’s business is mainly unregulated activities and the sole or main purpose of the deals is to limit identifiable risks.

4. Entering into transactions as principal for, or in connection with, the following:

   - the sale of goods or supply of services

   - the sale of a company

   - acting as a bare trustee (or in Scotland, as **nominee** )

   - an employee share scheme

   - an overseas person

   - an incoming electronic commerce provider.

## Exclusions for Advice in Newspapers

There is a specific exclusion in relation to newspapers and other media from the regulated activity of advising on investments. If a newspaper includes investment advice, and that advice is not the principle purpose of the newspaper, then the newspaper is excluded from the regulated activity of advising on investments.

The existence of money, financial and city pages, or subsections of such material within a newspaper does not make the principle purpose of the newspaper anything other than the provision of news, so there is no requirement for authorisation.

If the principal purpose of a publication is the provision of investment advice, with a view to encouraging investors or prospective investors to undertake investment activity, then authorisation is required. This is the case for periodicals which ‘tip’ certain investments and are often sold on a subscription basis. They are often referred to as tipsheets and include publications, for example, _Warrants Alert_ (highlighting those warrants which offer good value to the investor).


## Trustees, Nominees and Personal Representatives

There is an exclusion from the need for authorisation if the person carrying on the regulated activity is:

- acting as representative of another party

- not generally holding themselves out as carrying on regulated activities, or

- not receiving additional remuneration for providing these investment services.

This exclusion can apply to the following types of regulated activity:

- Dealing in investments as principal.

- Arranging deals in investments.

- Managing investments.

- Safeguarding and administering investments.

- Sending dematerialised instructions.

- Advising on investments.

- Assisting in the administration and performance of a contract of insurance.

- Advising on, entering into, or administering a home finance transaction.

## Employee Share Schemes

In order to encourage companies to set up schemes enabling their employees to hold shares in the company they work for, there are exclusions from the company’s need to be authorised to operate such schemes.

The exclusion covers four types of activity, which are:

- dealing in investments as principal

- dealing in investments as agent

- arranging deals in investments, and

- safeguarding and administering investments.

## Overseas Persons

There are a number of exclusions for overseas persons carrying on regulated activities, providing they do not do so from a permanent place of business in the UK. These exclusions apply only if the business is completed through an authorised, or exempt, UK person, or if the business is the result of a legitimate approach, such as a UK client approaching an overseas person in an unsolicited manner. The exclusions cover mainly the following types of activity:

- Dealing in investments as principal.

- Dealing in investments as agent.

- Arranging deals in investments.

- Advising on investments.

- Agreeing to carry on the regulated activities of managing investments, arranging deals in investments, and safeguarding and administering investments or sending dematerialised instructions.

- Operating an MTF or an OTF.

- Entering into, or administering, a home finance transaction.


## 1.3.3 Exemptions

Certain sections of FSMA, and a statutory instrument called the FSMA Exemption Order 2001, disapply the general prohibition for certain persons when they are carrying on particular regulated activities. This means that such persons are exempt from the need to be authorised, even though some of the activities they carry on are, in theory, regulated activities.

The extent to which any exemption may apply may also be limited to specified circumstances (for example, another person who is authorised and has the relevant regulatory permissions, has accepted responsibility for the regulated activities in question) or subject to specified conditions (for example, a requirement that the activity is not carried on for financial gain).

FSMA provides that **appointed representatives** , **recognised investment exchanges (RIEs)** and **recognised clearing houses (RCHs)** , and certain other persons exempt under the miscellaneous provisions, are **exempt persons** . Members of Lloyd’s and members of the other professions are not exempt persons as such, but the general prohibition in Section 19 of FSMA only applies to them under certain circumstances.

The distinction is significant in relation to various provisions (for example, those in the RAO) which apply only to transactions and other activities which involve exempt persons. An example of an exemption subject to certain conditions might be a requirement that the activity is not carried on to make a profit.

These exemptions can be split into two groups:

- Those described as exempt persons under FSMA, for example, RCHs and RIEs.

- Those not described as exempt persons, but who may nonetheless be exempt from the need to apply to the FCA for authorisation, for example, a member of a **designated professional body (DPB)** , carrying on the regulated activities under particular circumstances.

This distinction is significant. Certain legal provisions apply only to transactions involving exempt persons, and not to non-exempt persons, who are only free from the need to apply for authorisation because of the specific circumstances of their activity.

## Exempt Persons

Those exempt persons who are appointed representatives, or miscellaneous persons, cannot be both exempt in relation to some regulated activities and authorised in relation to others. If a firm is already authorised and wishes to perform additional regulated activities, which might otherwise have fallen under the appointed representative, or the miscellaneous persons exemptions, it cannot claim those exemptions; the firm must extend its authorisation to include all the regulated activities it carries on.

## Appointed Representatives (AR)

Section 39 of FSMA and the FSMA (Appointed Representatives) Regulations 2001 exempts Appointed Representatives (ARs) from the need to obtain authorisation in order to carry out certain activities. Under this exemption, the AR’s principal firm is given the overall responsibility for vetting and monitoring the


ARs, which the FCA would normally conduct itself. The Appointed Representative Regulations 2001 provides that exempt appointed representatives can only carry on the following activities:

- Arranging deals in investments.

- Advising on investments.

- Safeguarding and administering assets.

- Dealing in investments as agent in long-term insurance contracts meeting specified conditions.

- Advising on and arranging regulated mortgage contracts.

- Advising on and arranging regulated home revision and home purchase plans.

- Assisting in the administration and performance of a contract of insurance.

- Providing basic advice on stakeholder products.

In particular, appointed representatives are not permitted to deal in investments either as agent or principal, or manage investments.

Following the publication in 2021 of its consultation, the FCA published its Policy Statement (PS22/11) in 2022, with the requirements coming into effect in December 2022.

The aim of the FCA’s consultation was to reduce potential harm to consumers and markets. The FCA has been clear that its proposals used results of their thematic reviews undertaken on the general insurance sector in 2016 and the investment management sector in 2019.

While the FCA has broad powers in relation to setting the requirements that apply to principals, more fundamental reforms of the AR regime would require the government to propose amendments to the legislation – hence the purpose of HM’s Treasury call for evidence.

HM Treasury noted that if reform was required, four key elements to the regulatory approach which need to be changed to the legislation to be enacted:

- the overall scope of the Section 39 exemption, including the regulated activities which ARs are permitted to carry on

- the regulatory tools available to the FCA, should it be concluded that the FCA should be empowered to do more to prevent abuse of the AR regime

- whether more direct regulatory requirements should be placed on ARs in order to strengthen incentives for regulatory compliance and high standards of conduct, and

- the role of the **Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)** in relation to ARs and their principals where consumers have experienced detriment while dealing with an AR.

The following changes came into effect:

- Additional information on ARs and notification requirements for principals:

   - This will allow the FCA to easily identify potential risks within principals and ARs.

   - It will also help it to assess whether the principal has the expertise, systems and controls to effectively oversee its ARs.

   - the FCA will be able to target its supervisory interventions more effectively.

- Clarifying and strengthening the responsibilities and expectations of principals:

   - Providing additional guidance for principals on their responsibilities.

   - Providing FCA expectations on how they should act and oversee their ARs.


## Recognised Investment Exchanges (RIEs) and Recognised Clearing Houses (RCHs)

Trading in, and issuance of, securities is conducted through formal exchanges; for example, the **London Stock Exchange (LSE)** . There are often separate clearing systems connected to these formal exchanges which facilitate the settlement of the trades which take place. The firms operating these systems are referred to as clearing houses.

FSMA gives the FCA the responsibility of recognising, regulating and supervising these exchanges and clearing houses.

Once recognised, these exchanges are referred to as RIEs, and clearing houses are referred to as RCHs. RIEs and RCHs are classified as exempt persons in that they do not need to seek authorisation from the FCA in order to carry on their regulated activities; they are, instead, recognised.

## Miscellaneous Exempt Persons

HM Treasury has established certain exemptions from which certain persons do not need to be authorised. Some of these exemptions are restricted in that they only apply under certain circumstances. For example, supranational bodies of which the UK or another **European Economic Area (EEA)** member state is a member, and both the UK central bank or a central bank of an EEA member state, are exempted from the need to be authorised to carry on any regulated activity, apart from the need to effect, or carry out contracts of insurance.

In contrast, certain bodies are exempted from the need to be authorised for the sole regulated activity of accepting deposits; these include municipal banks, local authorities and charities. As stated in FSMA 2000 (Exemption) Order 2001, the main organisations that are exempt in respect of any regulated activity other than for insurance business, are:

- the **Bank of England (BoE)**

- the central banks of EEA member states

- the European Central Bank (ECB)

- the European Investment Bank (EIB)

- the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD)

- the International Monetary Fund (IMF), and

- the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD).

## Exemption from the General Prohibition

Certain persons are able to perform limited regulated activities without the need to be authorised or exempt. In effect, the general prohibition section within FSMA does not apply to them. Such persons include the members of Lloyd’s insurance market and certain DPBs.

## Members of Lloyd’s

Several activities carried on in connection with business at Lloyd’s are regulated activities. These include:

- advising on syndicate participation

- acting as a managing agent for one or more syndicates, and

- arranging deals in insurance contracts.


However, FSMA disapplies the general prohibition for members of Lloyd’s in relation to contracts of insurance written at Lloyd’s.

The reason why the general prohibition does not apply here is because the FCA expects the activities at Lloyd’s to be suitably supervised and executed by the Society of Lloyd’s, and therefore additional FCA authorisation of its members is unnecessary. The FCA does, however, have certain powers to impose additional rules on the members (or former members) of Lloyd’s if it is felt necessary.

## Members of the Professions

There are five professions where individual firms are permitted to carry on particular regulated activities without the need to apply to the FCA for regulated permission. Professional firms are required to apply to their relevant professional body for permission to conduct these regulated activities. The individual professions include:

- accountants

- solicitors

- actuaries

- chartered surveyors, and

- licensed conveyancers.

Designated Professional Bodies (DPBs) are the professional bodies which are able to grant permissions. They include the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales (ICAEW), the Law Society, the Institute and Faculty of Actuaries and the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS).

The DPB must operate a set of rules with which its members must comply, and the regulated activity must be incidental to the provision of its professional services. For example, a firm of accountants providing tax advice might give a client advice in relation to which investments might best be sold to avoid the accrual of a tax liability. There are other restrictions to the activities carried out by DPBs member firms. For example, such firms are allowed to receive a financial reward only from the client (ie, they should not receive commissions from the providers of products held or bought by the client); and certain regulated activities may not be carried out by the members, namely:

- accepting deposits

- dealing in investments as principal

- establishing, operating or winding up CISs or stakeholder pension schemes

- effecting or carrying out contracts of insurance (including Lloyd’s business)

- providing funeral plan contracts

- issuing electronic money (e-money)

- providing basic advice on stakeholder products, and

- establishing a pension scheme.


## 1.4 Permission to Carry on Regulated Activities (Part IV)

In granting the permission to carry on a regulated activity, the regulator must be satisfied that the applicant will satisfy, and continue to satisfy, the threshold conditions in relation to all of the regulated activities for which they will have permission. See chapter 2, section 8.

The regulator may incorporate in the description of the regulated activity such limitations (as to the circumstances in which the activity may, or may not, be carried on) as it considers appropriate, or specify a narrower or wider description of regulated activity than that to which the application relates. It could also give permission for the carrying on of a regulated activity which is not included among those to which the application relates.

The regulator may, on receipt of an application of an authorised person, vary the permission by:

- adding a regulated activity for which it gives permission

- removing a regulated activity from those for which it gives permission

- cancelling a requirement imposed under Section 43 (Imposition of Requirements),or

- varying such a requirement.

The regulator may exercise its powers in relation to an authorised person if it appears that:

- they are failing, or are likely to fail, to satisfy the threshold conditions, or

- they have failed, during a period of at least 12 months, to carry on a regulated activity for which they have a permission.

When control of an authorised person changes hands, if it believes that the effect of the acquisition is uncertain, the regulator may vary the authorised person’s permission by imposing a requirement or varying a requirement included in the authorised person’s permission.

## 1.5 Performance of Regulated Activities (Part V)

The regulators (ie, the PRA and/or FCA) may make an order (prohibition order) prohibiting an individual from performing a specified function, any function falling within a specified description, or simply any function.

A prohibition order may relate to a specified regulated activity, any regulated activity falling within a specified description or all regulated activities, to an authorised person generally.

The regulators must provide the individual concerned with a warning notice, informing them that they propose to make a prohibition order. If the regulators decide to make a prohibition order, they must give the individual concerned a decision notice. The individual concerned may refer the matter to the Upper Tribunal, which deals with appeals by firms and/or individuals against FCA decision notices or supervisory notices.

Authorised persons must take reasonable care to ensure that a person does not perform either a Senior Management Function (defined under the SM&CR – refer to chapter 2, section 2) or a Controlled Function (defined under the Approved Persons Regime (APER), refer to chapter 2, section 3), under an arrangement entered into by the authorised person (including a contractor of the authorised person) in


relation to the carrying on by the authorised person of a regulated activity, unless approved by the PRA and/or the FCA.

The regulators may impose a penalty if they are satisfied that a person has at any time performed the activity requiring approval where at the time the person knew, or could reasonably be expected to have known, that they were performing an activity without the required approval.

The regulators are very likely to take action against a person if it appears that they are guilty of misconduct and if the regulators are satisfied that it is appropriate in all circumstances to take action against the person involved.

## 1.6 Disciplinary Measures (Part XIV)

If the regulators consider that an authorised person has contravened a requirement imposed on them under FSMA or UK law (ie, EU regulation onshored under the European Union (Withdrawal Agreement) Act), then they may publish a statement to that effect.

In addition, FSMA also gives the regulators the power to impose a financial penalty in respect of a contravention, of whatever amount is appropriate.

FSMA also gives the regulators the power to suspend or impose limitations on the authorised person, for a period of time, when regulators consider that the individual has contravened a requirement imposed on them.

FSMA dictates that the authorities must provide a warning notice to the authorised person if the regulators are going to take, or are proposing to take, disciplinary action.

## 1.7 General Rule-Making Power (Part X)

FSMA provides the regulators (the PRA and the FCA) with general rule-making powers that apply to authorised persons carrying out all activities whether regulated or not.

In this respect, the regulators have the power to make rules regarding:

- the handling of client money

- the requirement for firms to have a remuneration policy

- recovery and resolution plans for banking institutions

- insurance business rules

- price-stabilising rules, including the manner in which they are permitted, the conditions, circumstances and times when, and/or the period during which they are permitted

- financial promotion rules

- **money laundering (ML)** rules

- control of information rules

- modification of waiver

- contravention of rules, including the evidential provisions, action for damages and the limits on effect of contravening rules, and

- procedural provisions, including the notification of rules to the Treasury, rule-making instruments, verification of rules and consultation and general supplementary powers.


## 1.8 Injunctions and Restitution (Part XXV) and Penalties for Market Abuse (Part VIII)

FSMA empowers the FCA to be able to take action against individuals, and/or firms for carrying out or conducting market abuse. Under these circumstances, the FCA is responsible for applying to the courts to seek an injunction against any individual, and/or firm conducting such market abuse.

FSMA provides the FCA with the power to impose penalties for market abuse, which also includes **inside information** and **insider dealing** .

Refer to chapter 3, section 3 for more details on the UK’s market abuse regime and rules.

## 1.9 Offences (Part XXVII)

FSMA provides the FCA with powers in relation to how the regulator should deal with individuals, and/ or authorised persons who have made **misleading statements** or undertaken unacceptable practices. It also gives the regulator the power to instigate proceedings for an offence under: Part V of the **Criminal Justice Act (CJA) 1993** (insider dealing); prescribed regulations relating to ML; and Schedule 7 of the Counter-Terrorism Act 2008 (terrorist financing or money laundering).

Refer to chapter 3, section 4 for more details on the UK’s money laundering regulation and rules.
