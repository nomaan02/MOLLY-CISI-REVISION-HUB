## 2. International Financial Services Structure, Operation and Legislation

This section of the chapter highlights the main EU legislation and laws (Regulations and **Directives** ) that are relevant to the UK Financial Services industry due to their application and scope being extraterritorial.

A single market in financial services has long been an objective of the EU. In a single market, financial institutions authorised to provide financial services in one member state are able to provide the same services throughout the EU, competing on a level playing field within a consistent regulatory environment.

From a financial services perspective, this should mean that, in terms of investor protection, it makes no difference to a customer whether they buy a financial product from a provider in their home, or from another member state.

## 2.1 European Financial Services Structure, Operation and Legislation

## 2.1.1 The European Commission (EC)

The European Commission is independent of national governments, it represents and upholds the interests of the EU as a whole (collective).

Its main tasks are:

- right of legislative initiative – all legislative decisions taken by the European Council have to be based on a proposal from the Commission. It acts as the engine of integration, drawing up proposals for the development of EU policy and building steps towards harmonisation
- guardian of the treaties
- executive authority for the implementation of EU policy, and
- enacting technical standards drafted by ESMA.

The EC is steered by a group of 27 Commissioners, one from each member state, known as ‘the College’. Together, they take or make decisions on the Commission’s political and strategic direction. A new College of Commissioners is appointed every five years.

## 2.1.2 The European Parliament (EP)

It is a multinational chamber Parliament, consisting of Members of European Parliament (MEPs) with seats allocated on the basis of population. The Parliament is supposed to express the democratic wishes of EU citizens and represents their interests in discussions with other EU institutions. MEPs sit in Europewide political groups rather than in national blocks.

The MEP’s principal roles are:

- examine and adopt European legislation, sharing this with the European Council (Council of the EU)
- approve the EU budget, and
- exercise democratic control over the other EU institutions by setting up committees of inquiry.

## 2.1.3 The Council of the EU (the European Council)

The European Council defines the EU’s overall political direction and priorities. It is not one of the EU’s legislating institutions, so does not negotiate or adopt EU laws (the Council of the EU and the EP are responsible for adopting laws proposed by the Commission). Each member state has a representative in the Council.

## 2.1.4 EU Legislation

Primary legislation consists of the various treaties which have been negotiated and ratified by the member states.

Secondary legislation consists of regulations, directives, decisions, recommendations and opinions. All are binding with the exception of recommendations and opinions. Decisions can be addressed to both member states and/or individuals and businesses. Regulations are directly applicable and binding throughout the EU. They do not need to be transposed into national law; rather, member states are directly bound by such measures once they are implemented into EU law.

A directive is binding on member states in relation to the objectives to be achieved but gives member states discretion in determining how the legal measure should be incorporated into national and/or domestic law.

## 2.2 Financial Supervision in Europe

As part of the overall response to the financial crisis of 2007–08, the EU Commission enhanced the legal framework in relation to the oversight of financial services in the EU. The below framework has been operational since 2010.

The Commission adopted an architecture that created the following:

- **European Systemic Risk Council or Board (ESRB)** , to assess the risks to the stability of the financial system as a whole and provide early warning of any systemic risks and, where necessary, recommendations for action.
- **European System of Financial Supervisors** , to supervise financial institutions, which consists of a network of national financial supervisors working with three **European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs)** :
  - banking (European Banking Authority – EBA)
  - securities (European Securities and Markets Authority –ESMA), and
  - insurance and occupational pensions sectors (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority – EIOPA.

The ESRB has three main objectives. They are:

- to develop a European macroprudential perspective to address the problem of fragmented individual risk analysis at a national level
- to enhance the effectiveness of early warning mechanisms by improving the interaction between microprudential analysis, and
- to allow for risk assessments to be translated into action by the relevant authorities.

The objectives of the ESAs are to contribute to:

- improving the functioning of the internal market, which includes a high, effective and consistent level of regulation and supervision
- protecting depositors, investors, policyholders and other beneficiaries
- ensuring the integrity, efficiency and orderly functioning of financial markets
- safeguarding the stability of the financial system, and
- strengthening international supervisory coordination.

## 2.2.1 Implementation and Enforcement

## Level 1

- Framework legislation is proposed by the Commission and adopted by the Council and the EP. The directive or regulation specifies whether legislative power is delegated to the Commission to adopt Level 2 measures.

## Level 2

- Secondary legislation is drafted and adopted by the Commission in line with the delegation of legislative competence in the Level 1 text.
- This is done by four specialist committees of finance ministry experts. These are the European Banking Committee (EBC), the European Securities Committee (ESC), the European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Committee (EIOPC) and the European Financial Conglomerates Committee (EFCC).
- The Commission may seek advice from the relevant ESA on implementing measures.
- The ESAs would advise on detailed Level 2 requirements.

## Level 3

- The ESAs are empowered to adopt guidance, and/or guidelines, which is treated on a ‘comply or explain’ basis by national supervisors. The ESAs are required to consult and undertake cost/benefit analysis on this ‘where appropriate’.
- These are commonly produced in the form of ‘Q&As’ or opinion letters/statements.

## Level 4 Enforcement

- The Commission is responsible for ensuring that directives are properly transposed and that EU legal requirements are then applied, undertaking enforcement action where necessary.

## 2.3 The European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs)

The main purpose of the ESAs is to create a single EU rulebook, by developing draft technical standards which will be adopted by the EU Commission.

A key change in the introduction of the ESAs is that they have much stronger powers, having the ability to bind the member states in ways that their predecessors did not, with the trend being towards greater EU control. In the frequently asked questions (FAQs) section of its website, ESMA states:

*‘ESMA’s work on securities legislation contributes to the development of a single rule book in Europe…’*

The FAQs also state:

*‘As part of its role in standard-setting and reducing the scope of regulatory arbitrage, ESMA strengthens international supervisory cooperation. Where requested in European law, ESMA undertakes the supervision of certain entities with pan-European reach* .’

The ESAs also issue guidance, and/or guidelines, including recommendations with which national supervisors and firms must make every effort to comply, or explain why they are not able to ensure compliance with the requirements.

The ESAs have the power to investigate a national supervisory authority if they believe that the national authority is either failing to apply EU law, or is doing so in a way which appears to be in breach of EU law (or not in the spirit of the guidance, and/or guidelines which the EU has provided). Following an investigation, the ESA may issue a recommendation to the national supervisory authority, as well as obtaining formal opinion from the Commission. It is at this stage that the ESA will take appropriate decisions, binding on firms or market participants to ensure they comply with EU law.

ESAs can temporarily ban certain financial activities.

## 2.3.1 European Banking Authority (EBA)

The European Banking Authority (EBA) is composed of high-level representatives from the banking supervisory authorities and central banks of the European Union. Their role is to:

- advise the EU Commission, either at the Commission’s request, within a time limit which the Commission may lay down according to the urgency of the matter, or on the Committee’s own initiative; in particular, with regards to the preparation of draft-implementing measures in the field of banking activities, and
- contribute to the consistent implementation of Community directives and to the convergence of member states’ supervisory practices throughout the Community, enhancing supervisory cooperation, including the exchange of information.

## 2.3.2 European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA)

The European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority (EIOPA) is an independent advisory body to the EP and the Council of the EU. EIOPA’s core responsibilities are to support the stability of the financial system, including the transparency of markets and financial products, as well as the protection of insurance policyholders, pension scheme members and beneficiaries.

## 2.3.3 European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA)

The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) is an independent authority which helps underpin the stability of the EU’s financial system by ensuring the integrity, transparency, efficiency and orderly functioning of securities markets, as well as enhancing investor protection. In particular, ESMA encourages supervisory convergence both among securities regulators, and across financial sectors by working with the other ESAs.

As noted on the ESMA website, it contributes to the development of a single rulebook in Europe. This:

- ensures the consistent treatment of investors across the EU, enabling an adequate level of protection, and
- promotes equal conditions for the competition of financial service providers, as well as ensuring the effectiveness and cost-efficiency of supervision for supervised companies.

ESMA also contributes to the financial stability of the EU, identifying potential risks and providing advice. ESMA is also responsible for coordinating the actions of securities supervisors (with each EEA member state) and adopting emergency measures in a crisis.

While ESMA is independent, it is fully accountable to the European Parliament, where it will appear before the relevant committee, known as the Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs (ECON), at its request, and for formal hearings. ESMA is also accountable to the European Council and the European Commission. ESMA reports on its activities regularly at meetings, but also through an annual report.

ESMA’s competencies and powers include:

- a supervisory role in relation to credit rating agencies
- drafting legally binding technical standards in EU member states
- launching a fast-track procedure to ensure consistent application of EU law
- participation in Colleges of Supervisors and on-site inspections
- resolving disagreements between national authorities
- consumer protection responsibilities, including the ability to prohibit, for a period of three months, financial products which in its view pose a threat to financial stability or the orderly functioning of financial markets
- entering into administrative arrangements with supervisory authorities, international organisations and the administrations of third countries, and
- monitoring systemic risk of cross-border financial institutions.

Level 1 directives and regulations continue to set out the high-level political objectives and goals in the area concerned by the legislation. ESMA now has a bigger role in Level 2 in relation to the drafting of regulatory technical standards (delegated acts and implementing acts). Delegated acts relate to the substantive content of the legislative requirement, ie, setting out what authorisation information firms must provide to competent authorities, while implementing acts give effect to the substantive requirements. This includes standard templates and procedures for communicating information between competent authorities.

ESMA will develop guidelines and recommendations at Level 3 to ensure a consistent and effective supervisory approach. These guidelines and recommendations are addressed to competent authorities and/or financial market participants. While they are not legally binding, competent authorities must explain if they do not intend to comply with such requirements, and regulated firms would need to be able to explain to their competent authorities why they are not complying with the Level 3 requirements.

At Level 4, ESMA can, by a national competent authority, the EP, the Council, the Commission or by a relevant stakeholder group, be requested to launch an inquiry, or issue a recommendation addressed to the national authority. The Commission can also follow its usual procedures for referring a case against a member state to the EU Court of Justice.

## 2.4 The European Central Bank (ECB)

The ECB is the central bank for Europe’s single currency, the ‘euro’. The ECB’s main task is to maintain the euro’s purchasing power, and thus price stability in the eurozone area. The euro area comprises of the 20 EU countries which have adopted the euro since 1999.

The ECB and the national central banks together constitute the Eurosystem, the central banking system of the eurozone area. The main objective of the Eurosystem is to maintain price stability, safeguarding the value of the euro. The ECB is committed to performing all central bank tasks entrusted to it effectively. In doing so, it strives for the highest level of integrity, competence, efficiency and transparency.

## 2.4.1 Why a System Instead of a Single Central Bank?

There are three main reasons for having a system of central banking in Europe:

1. To build on the existing competencies of the NCBs, their institutional set-up, infrastructure, expertise and excellent operational capabilities. Moreover, several central banks perform tasks which are in addition to those of the Eurosystem.
2. It is appropriate for credit institutions to have an access point to central banking facilities in each participating member state.
3. Given the multitude of nations, languages and cultures in the euro area, NCBs are best placed to serve as access points of the Eurosystem.

## 2.4.2 Objective of the ECB

The objective of the ECB is to maintain price stability, in doing so to protect the value of the euro.

The Eurosystem comprises the ECB and the national central banks of the other ‘euro areas’ member states, with responsibility for:

- defining and implementing monetary policy
- conducting foreign exchange operations
- holding and managing the euro area’s foreign currency reserves, and
- promoting the smooth operation of payment systems.

In addition, the ECB also undertakes additional tasks in the areas of banking supervisions, banknotes, statistics, macroprudential policy and financial stability, as well as international and European cooperation.

## 2.5 The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID II) and Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MiFIR)

MiFID provisions came into force in 2007. One of the key aims of MiFID was to provide investor protection rules across the EEA. Investor protection is ensured, *inter alia,* via the obligation to obtain the best possible result for the client, information disclosure requirements, client-specific rules on suitability and appropriateness and rules on inducements. As a general principle, MiFID placed significant importance on the fiduciary duties of firms.

This is why MiFID established a general obligation for firms to act in the client’s best interest. MiFID was designed to support two key policy goals of the EU, which at the time were:

- extending the scope of the passport rules within the EEA to include a wider range of services, and
- removing a major hurdle to cross-border business by the application of **home state** rules.

As noted below, MiFID was repealed and replaced by a new Directive (MiFID II) and a Regulation (MiFIR) in 2018.

Within the workbook, reference to ‘MiFID’ means MiFID II and MiFIR.

Further enhancements were made to MiFID. In 2024 the revised MiFID regime (MiFID 3 and MiFIR 2) was published, with MiFIR 2 coming into effect in 2024 (unless Level 2 measures are required to be developed by ESMA), while EU member states must transpose MiFID 3 into national law by the end of September 2025.

## 2.5.1 Activities that can be Passported under MiFID

## MiFID Activity

- Receipt and transmission of orders in relation to one or more financial instruments.
- Execution of orders on behalf of clients.
- Dealing on own account.
- Portfolio management.
- Investment advice.
- Underwriting of financial instruments and/or placing of financial instruments on a firm commitment basis.
- Placing of financial instruments without a firm commitment basis.
- Operation of MTFs.
- Operation of OTFs.

## Ancillary Activities

The following services cannot be passported in their own right; they can only be passported if they are being provided in conjunction with a core investment service from the above list.

## **MiFID Ancillary Activity**

- Safekeeping and administration of financial instruments for the accounts of clients, including custodianship. Also related services such as the management of cash and collateral.
- Granting credits or loans to an investor to allow them to carry out a transaction in one or more financial instruments when the firm granting the credit or loan is involved in the transaction.
- Advice to undertakings on capital structure, industrial strategy and related matters; also, advice, and/or services relating to mergers and the purchase of undertakings.
- Foreign exchange services (but only if these are connected with the provision of investment services).
- Investment research and financial analysis, or other forms of general recommendation in relation to transactions in financial instruments.
- Services in relation to underwriting.
- Investment services and activities, and ancillary services, related to the underlying assets of certain derivatives when these are connected to the provision of investment or ancillary services.

## 2.5.2 Financial Instruments Covered by MiFID

MiFID applies only to activities in relation to a specified list of financial instruments. These are:

- transferable securities
- money market instruments
- units in collective investment undertakings
- options, futures, swaps, forward rate agreements and any other derivative contracts relating to securities, currencies, interest rates or yields, emission allowances or other derivatives instruments, financial indices or financial measures which may be settled physically or in cash
- options, futures, swaps, forwards and any other derivative contracts relating to commodities that must be settled in cash or may be settled in cash at the option of one of the parties other than by reason of default or other termination event
- options, futures, swaps, and any other derivative contract relating to commodities that can be physically settled providing that they are traded on a regulated market, an MTF, or an OTF, except for wholesale energy products traded on an OTF that must be physically settled
- options, futures, swaps, forwards and any other derivative contracts relating to commodities that can be physically settled not otherwise mentioned in point 6 of this section and not being for commercial purposes, which have the characteristics of other derivative financial instruments
- derivative instruments for the transfer of credit risk
- financial contracts for differences (CFDs)
- options, futures, swaps, forward rate agreements and any other derivative contracts relating to climatic variables, freight rates or inflation rates or other official economic statistics that must be settled in cash or may be settled in cash at the option of one of the parties other than by reason of default or other termination event, as well as any other derivative contracts relating to assets, rights, obligations, indices and measures not otherwise mentioned in this section, which have the characteristics of other derivative financial instruments, having regard to whether, *inter alia* , they are traded on a regulated market, OTF, or an MTF, and
- emission allowances consisting of any units recognised for compliance with the requirements of Directive 2003/87/EC (Emissions Trading Scheme).

Financial instruments not covered by MiFID include the following:

- Foreign Exchange (FX spot and the conversion related to settlement of a security where the settlement date is greater than two business days).
- However, this would not include where it relates to the provision of an investment activity or service (ie, entering into an FX forward contract or an FX option or future).

## 2.5.3 MiFID – Home versus Host State Regulation

The concept of **passporting** under MiFID relies on the concept of home state and **host state** regulators. In essence, the home state is where the firm carrying out the activities is established; the host state is the state in which provides services as a guest.

MiFID tackles the hurdle to a single market in the following ways:

- It allows firms to carry out cross-border business from their home state, solely on the basis of their home country rules.
- It harmonises these home country rules so that they are all sufficiently similar, meaning that an investor is, in theory, just as well-protected under the rules of one EEA member state as they are under the rules of another. There are common standards of investor protection.
- Host state conduct of business rules apply where a passported MiFID branch of a firm conducts business with host state residents.
- Home state rules will apply where the services of the passported MiFID branch in the previous bullet point are provided from the host state to residents in another EEA member state.

## 2.5.4 MiFID II and MiFIR

Since MiFID came into force (2007), financial markets had changed substantially. New factors (for example, new types of trading venues) and products came onto the scene and technological developments, such as high-frequency trading (HFT), altered the landscape dramatically. All these revealed shortcomings in MiFID, which is why the European Commission undertook a review of MiFID, and it was replaced by ‘MiFID II’ and ‘MiFIR’.

Refer to chapter 5, section 2 for details on the EU’s revision of the MiFID framework, which was published in the EU Official Journal in March 2024.

The MiFID II framework contained two separate pieces of European legislation:

- **A revised Directive (MiFID II)** – which is an amendment and restatement of MiFID. This covered a number of areas, including market structure, the scope of exemptions from financial regulation, organisational and conduct of business requirements for investment firms and trading venues, powers of national authorities, sanctions, and rules for third-country (non-EEA) firms operating through a branch.
- **A Regulation (MiFIR)** – setting out requirements for trade transparency, the mandatory trading of derivatives on organised venues and the provision of services by third-country firms without a branch.

The key elements of the framework are:

## Scope

- Expansion of firms subject to authorisation – now captures certain commodity firms, data providers and third-country firms.
- Additional instruments brought into the scope of MiFID II, such as structured deposits and emissions allowances.
- Position limits for commodity derivatives – powers for regulators to require existing positions to be reduced.

## Electronic Trading

- Derivatives, which are sufficiently liquid and eligible for clearing to be traded on trading venues (ie, either MTFs of OTFs).
- A new category of trading venues – OTFs.

## Transparency and Transaction Reporting

- Transparency requirements extended, to include bonds and derivatives.
- Trade reports published in real time (one minute for equities and five minutes for non-equity financial instruments) through approved publication arrangement (APA) firms.
- Transaction reports capturing additional information, ie, personal information on the investment decision-maker and execution decision-maker within firms.

## Third-Country Firms

- Equivalence decisions to be made by the European Commission in respect of third countries before firms from these jurisdictions can request to provide services.
- As a minimum, third-country firms seeking to access the retail market required to establish branches.

## Investor Protection

- Receipt of monetary inducements by certain firms, such as portfolio managers and firms giving independent investment advice is banned.
- Advice must meet certain criteria in order to be classified as independent and additional information will need to be provided to clients.
- The definition of non-complex instruments is updated to remove structured UCITS, to prevent these funds from being sold without an assessment of their appropriateness for the client.

## Product Intervention

- National regulators granted powers to permanently ban products, in coordination with the ESMA, who will also be able to temporarily ban products.
- Introduction for position limits for commodity derivatives, including powers to require existing positions to be reduced.

## 2.6 UCITS Directive – Selling Cross-Border CISs

The family of UCITS Directives is aimed at securing a common set of regulatory standards for open-ended funds (commonly known as OEICs and unit trusts in the UK) or sociétés d’investissement à capital variable (SICAVs) with the aim of removing barriers to cross-border trade.

Put simply, if a collective fund is set up in accordance with the UCITS rules, it should be able to be sold across the EU, subject only to local tax and marketing laws. So, a UCITS scheme can gain a single authorisation from its home state regulator, and need not apply for further authorisation in other member states before being sold to the public there.

However, only a relatively limited range of scheme types could qualify as UCITS and, therefore, be freely marketed throughout the EU. The requirements which needed to be satisfied included the following:

- The scheme had to be solely invested in transferable securities.
- No more than 10% of the fund could be in the shares or bonds of a single issuer.
- No more than 5% of the assets of the scheme were allowed to be invested in other CISs.
- The scheme was only able to hold money in bank deposits as ancillary liquid assets and not as a major part of the investment strategy of the scheme.
- The scheme was only able to invest in, or utilise, financial derivatives for efficient portfolio management or hedging purposes.

Demand for a wider variety of funds marketable throughout the EU rendered these investment restrictions somewhat out of date. As a result, ‘new’ UCITS Directives were developed, a summary of which is noted below:

- **UCITS III**
  - Impacting the operators of funds (the **Management Directive** ).
  - One broadening the range of underlying investments and strategies that could qualify as UCITS (the **Products Directive** ).

The Management Directive deals mainly with the management companies operating UCITS funds; for example, the degree to which they can delegate activities, their capitalisation, internal administration and accounting requirements. It widens the previous investment powers of UCITS schemes, to enable them to invest in money market instruments, other CISs, deposits and financial derivatives. It also allows certain UCITS to use strategies designed to replicate the performance of stock market indices (index tracker funds).

The Products Directive permitted the following:

- Investment in money market instruments such as Treasury bills and certificates of deposit. This enables money market funds to be UCITS-compliant for the first time, as long as no more than 10% of the fund is invested in instruments from any single issuer.
- Up to 100% of a fund to be invested in other UCITS schemes, or up to 30% in a non-UCITS retail scheme which is a regulated scheme. This gives the ability for funds of funds (FOFs) to be UCITScompliant.
- Investment in bank deposits, providing that no more than 20% of the scheme’s assets are held with the same institution.
- Investment in financial derivatives (for example, warrants) as part of the scheme’s investment policy, rather than just for efficient portfolio management. There are conditions that look through the derivatives, requiring that the underlying instruments must be capable of being held directly by the scheme.
- Index tracker funds which are intended to replicate the performance of an index are, under UCITS III, permitted to invest up to 20% of their value in a single issue. This can be raised to a maximum of 35% if justified by exceptional market conditions.
- **UCITS IV**

UICTS IV introduced the following:

• **Notification procedure** – the previous process of cross-border fund notification took a minimum of two months, although it was not uncommon for some member states to ask for more information and so delay the registration process. The Commission proposed to clarify member state supervisory responsibilities and ensure that a duly authorised UCITS has the right to access the market of another member state. Notification is by a simple electronic regulator-to-regulator communication, which effectively means that a UCITS has a right to access the market of another member state without delay.

- **Fund mergers** – the average fund size in Europe is much lower than in the US, while fund charges are higher. The ability of a UCITS to merge and pool assets should bring economies of scale and specialisation.
- **Asset pooling (master/feeder structure)** – master/feeder structures enables fund managers to pool similar funds into a master fund while still preserving the different funds’ labels (as opposed to merging the funds). The structure should benefit investors with economies of scale when pooling assets of the feeder/master funds, centralising the core investment management activity and preserving greater tax-efficiency for the end investor.
  - **Simplified prospectus** – the previous simplified prospectus was too long and complex. The new proposal replaced this with a short and easily readable Key Investor Information Document (KIID).
  - **Management company passport** – this was the most controversial of the Commission’s proposals. This allows the activities performed by the management company to be undertaken in a member state other than where the funds are domiciled and/or registered. This permits fund managers, and outsourced fund administrators who perform the activities of the management company, to centralise the administration of fund ranges in one location within the EEA.
- **UCITS V**

UCITS V introduced the following:

A UCITS will need to appoint a single depositary which must be located in the same member state as the UCITS.

The requirements also bring in specific depositary duties, such as:

- safekeeping functions
- harmonised oversight functions
- cash flow monitoring
- delegation
- liability, and
- investors’ rights of actions against the depositaries.

UCITS V also introduced remuneration requirements for UCITS managers; in particular, governance arrangements, identified staff, remuneration structures and proportionate application.

## 2.7 Prospectus Directive – Selling Securities Cross-Border

The Prospectus Directive is another example of a directive aimed at creating common standards across the EU, this time with the aim of simplifying the issue of a prospectus throughout the EEA.

The Prospectus Directive sets out common standards in terms of the information that must be provided about the issuer and the securities being issued or admitted for listing. It can be thought of as a single passport for issuers. It means that, once a prospectus has been approved by a home state listing authority, it must be accepted for the purpose of listing or public offers throughout the EU. The purpose of the Prospective Directive is, therefore, to make it easier and cheaper for companies (issuers) to raise capital in Europe.

## 2.8 Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) and Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR)

The **Capital Requirements Directive (CRD)** requires firms to satisfy certain financial requirements. In particular, firms need to have financial resources in excess of their regulatory financial resource requirements. These requirements aim to minimise the risk of a firm’s collapsing by being unable to pay its debts and are generally referred to as prudential rules.

The aim of the CRD is to ensure the financial soundness of credit institutions (essentially, banks and building societies, but also investment firms). The CRD stipulates how much of their own financial resources such firms must have in order to cover their risks and protect their depositors.

The CRD is a major piece of legislation that introduces a modern prudential framework, relating capital levels more closely to risks.

## 2.8.1 The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS)

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) was established by various central banks. The BCBS has no legal powers but creates common standards and guidelines of best practice with the aim that individual states will implement these in their own law.

The Committee tries to reduce divergences in international supervisory standards.

The Basel Capital Accord was implemented in 1988, with the aim of introducing a standardised strategy for measuring certain risks, such as credit risk, for credit institutions to comply with.

In 1999, the Committee issued a proposal for a new capital adequacy framework to replace the 1988 Accord – it updated the existing requirements to reflect the changing nature of financial institutions. The revised Basel Capital Accord (referred to as Basel II) was implemented in the EU via the CRD.

## Basel II Overview

Basel II revised the existing prudential framework to make the framework more risk-sensitive and more representative of banks’ risk management practices.

The revised framework included a measure for operational risk and more risk-sensitive weightings against credit risk. Basel II provided incentives for firms to improve their risk management practices.

However, Basel II left the overall level of capital held by banks collectively broadly unchanged.

Following the banking and financial crisis that started in 2007, it was thought that the Basel II regime partly led to the problems, because it is a rule-driven system based on ‘letter rating’ of risks, and is arguably by its nature backward-looking. Following the banking crisis, the BCBS encouraged banks to boost capital reserves by making provisions for bad debts throughout the economic cycle.

## Basel II Implementation

Basel II (which only applies to internationally active banks) was implemented in the EU via the CRD for credit institutions and investment firms.

The CRD amended two existing EU directives: the Capital Adequacy Directive (CAD) and the Banking Consolidation Directive (BCD).

The CRD implements in the EU the revised Basel framework, which is based on three pillars:

- **Pillar 1** – minimum capital requirements for credit, market and operational risks.
- **Pillar 2** – supervisory review, establishing a constructive dialogue between a firm and the regulator on the risks within, the risk management of and the capital requirements of the firm.
- **Pillar 3** – market discipline, robust requirements on public disclosure intended to give the market a stronger role in ensuring that firms hold an appropriate level of capital.

## **Pillar 1 – Minimum Capital Requirements**

There is a two-stage process to assess a firm’s capital requirement, reflecting market, credit and operational risk.

**First stage** – to assess the category of a firm. It could be:

- an own-account dealer
- a broker/manager, or
- an adviser/arranger.

**Second stage** – to assess how to calculate the capital requirement, dependent on whether the firm is:

- a limited licence firm
- a limited activity firm, or
- a full-scope firm.

## **Pillar 2 – Supervisory Review**

Under Pillar 2, a firm must constantly assess the internal capital it considers adequate and appropriate to cover all the risks it is exposed to within the context of its overall risk management framework.

The regulators determine the overall supervisory assessment, including risk mitigation programmes and the giving of individual capital guidance.

## **Pillar 3 – Market Disclosure**

Pillar 3 is designed to change behaviour and encourage market discipline by the development of disclosure requirements which will allow firms to assess their capital, risk exposures and risk assessment processes. Disclosures are to be made on an annual basis, or when there are changes of a material nature, which could be considered as material if their omission or misstatement could change the assessment.

## Basel III (CRD IV)

Basel III is the update to the Basel Accords, designed to prevent a repeat of the banking financial crisis. Basel III introduced more stringent requirements on banks in major G20 financial centres.

This represents the Commission’s plan for implementing the Basel III regulations, making the global financial system more resilient in the event of a crisis such as that of the global banking crisis of 2007–08.

Requirements under Basel III include the following:

- A tighter definition of common equity – banks will need to hold 7%, which includes a 2.5% capital conservation buffer.
- Counter-cyclical capital buffers – banks with a capital ratio below 2.5% face restrictions on paying dividends, undertaking buy-backs and the payment of staff bonuses.
- Limiting counterparty credit risk.
- Firms to comply with short- and medium-term quantitative liquidity ratios.
- An internationally harmonised leverage ratio, acting as backstop to risk-based capital measures.
- In 2023, the following risk coverage events came into effect:
  - Revised Standardised Approach for Credit Risk
  - Revised IRB Framework
  - Revised CVA Framework
  - Revised Operational Risk Framework, and
  - Revised Market Risk Framework.
- From 2023, the risk coverage output floor starts at 50%; and by 2025 it will reach 72.5%.

## CRD Changes

The ‘CRD’ has been updated and amended over the years. The changes are to address lessons learnt from the financial crisis.

In particular, CRD II aimed to improve the quality of firms’ capital by establishing criteria for assessing the eligibility of hybrid capital that will be counted as part of firms’ overall capital. In addition, it also developed rules to enhance the management of firms’ large exposures, restricting lending beyond a set limit. Lastly, it improved a firm’s risk management of securitisation, by requiring the originator of an issue to retain an investment in the issue.

Changes implemented under CRD III included strengthening the capital requirements for the trading book, so that it reflects potential losses from adverse market movements, and imposing higher capital requirements for re-securitisations, to ensure appropriate accounting of risks of investing in complex financial products.

It also includes rules to tackle pay incentives by requiring firms to have sound remuneration policies, as well as practices that do not encourage or reward excessive risk-taking.

## CRD V

The global financial crisis of 2007–08 highlighted problems in risk management frameworks of banks. In 2013, CRD IV and CRR (EU legislation that implemented Basel III) were introduced to address these problems – in particular, the quantity and quality of banks’ capital and liquidity resources.

CRD V introduced further measures, implementing Basel III’s enhanced Pillar 2 approach to the management and control of interest rate risk in the banking book.

CRD V achieves the objectives by:

- clarifying the application of supervisory requirements and guidance under Pillar 2
- adjusting requirements applied to remuneration policies
- requiring the establishment of intermediate parent undertakings, and
- updating the governance requirements applied to firms.

## CRD VI/CRR III

In 2021, the European Commission published a review of EU banking rules (the CRR and the CRD), implementing the Basel 3.1 framework.

The Commission’s proposed changes will primarily implement certain Basel II standards within the EEA, as well as firms providing cross-border financial services within the EEA.

Of particular concern to the industry was the requirement to establish a branch in order to provide banking services within the EEA – this would prevent a wide range of financial institutions from providing services within the EEA on a cross-border basis which are permitted under EEA member states’ local laws (ie, reverse solicitation by existing client). Although, these proposals were slightly watered down in the published CRD6 due to the existence of certain exemptions provided to third country firms undertaking certain business in the EU.

Following the publication in the EU Official Journal, the revised Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR3) came into effect on 1 January 2025 and the revised Capital Requirements Directive (CRD6) will come into effect on 11 January 2026. Although the requirements relating to the provision of services in the EU by third country entities and the requirement to establish a branch in the EU do not come into effect until 12 months later (11 January 2027) – EU member states must transpose the requirements into national law by 11 January 2026.
