## 16. Supervision (SUP)

## 16.1 Applications to Vary and Cancel Part 4A Permissions (SUP 6)

Firms can apply to the PRA and/or the FCA to vary a Part 4A permission, including where the firm ceases to carry on any of the regulated activities for which it has Part 4A permission (or expects to do so in the short term, normally less than six months).

Under Section 20(1) of FSMA – Authorised persons acting without permission, a firm is prohibited from carrying on a regulated activity in the UK (or purporting to do so) otherwise than in accordance with its permission.

Firms wishing to change their Part 4A permission can apply to the PRA and/or the FCA under Section 44 of FSMA – Variation at request of the authorised person.

Firms intending to expand their business should assess, taking appropriate professional advice where necessary, whether they will need to apply to the PRA and/or the FCA to vary their Part 4A permission before making any changes. When a firm intends to transfer its business to a different legal entity (for example, the business is to be transferred from a sole trader to a partnership), it will need to apply to the PRA and/or the FCA for cancellation of its Part 4A permission, and the entity to which the business is to be transferred will need to apply for a new Part 4A permission.

A firm which is seeking to vary its Part 4A permission substantially or cancel its Part 4A permission should discuss its plans with its usual supervisory contact at the PRA and/or the FCA as early as possible before making its application, in order to comply with Principle 11 of the Principles for Businesses. This is to help the PRA and/or the FCA and the firm to agree the correct approach for the firm. When a firm intends to cease carrying on one or more regulated activities permanently, it should give prompt notice to the PRA and/or the FCA to comply with Principle 11. A firm should consider whether it needs to notify the PRA and/or the FCA before applying to vary or cancel its Part 4A permission.

Under Section 55H or 55L of FSMA (as enacted by the Financial Services Act 2012), a firm may apply to the PRA and/or the FCA to vary its Part 4A permission to:

1. allow it to carry on further regulated activities

2. reduce the number of regulated activities it is permitted to carry out

3. vary the PRA and/or the FCA's description of its regulated activities (including by the removal or variation of any limitations), or

4. cancel any requirement applied for by the firm or imposed by the PRA under Section 55M and/or by the FCA under Section 55L of FSMA – imposition of requirements, or

5. vary any such requirement.

A firm may apply to vary its Part 4A permission to add a new regulated activity and at the same time remove a regulated activity for which it currently has permission.

When a firm wishes to cease carrying on an activity for which it has Part 4A permission, it will usually apply to vary its Part 4A permission to remove that activity. If a firm wishes to cease carrying on an activity in relation to any specified investment, it will apply to vary its Part 4A permission to remove that specified investment from the relevant activity.

When a firm is requesting to amend its Part 4A permission for regulated activities that would cause a significant change in the firm's business or risk profile, then the regulator may require the firm to complete appropriate parts of the full application pack (see the FCA and/or the PRA website: How do I get authorised) as directed by the PRA regulators FCA.

Applications for variation involving significant changes may be processed by the firm's usual supervisory contact at the FCA and/or the PRA, in conjunction with the Permissions department. Examples of an application for variation of Part 4A permission which may represent a significant change include, but are not limited to, an application:

- to carry on new regulated activities such as accepting deposits

- to extend the insurance business of a firm which already has Part 4A permission, which includes carrying out or effecting contracts of insurance (or both), to new classes of specified investment, or

- to remove a requirement preventing a firm from holding or controlling client money, or

- which causes the firm to change prudential category by, for example, removing a requirement relating to prudential category.

## 16.1.1 When will the PRA/FCA Grant an Application for Variation of Permission?

The regulators are required by Section 41(2) of FSMA to ensure that a firm applying to vary its Part 4A permission satisfies and will continue to satisfy the threshold conditions in relation to all the regulated activities for which the firm has or will have Part 4A permission after the variation.

However, the regulators must take such steps as they consider necessary in relation to a particular firm, to meet any of their regulatory objectives. This may include granting a firm's application for variation of Part 4A permission when it wishes to wind down (run off) its business activities and cease to carry out new business as a result of no longer being able to satisfy the threshold conditions.

In addition, they may also refuse the application, if it appears that any of the regulators regulatory objectives would be adversely affected if the application were to be granted, and it is desirable in order to meet their regulatory objectives for the application to be refused.

## 16.1.2 Commencing New Regulated Activities

If the variation of the Part 4A permission is given, then the regulators will expect the firm to commence that regulated activity in accordance with its business plan (revised as necessary to take account of changes during the application process), or scheme of operations for an insurer.

Under Section 55L (FCA) and Section 55M (PRA) of FSMA, the PRA and/or the FCA can refuse an application from a firm to cancel its Part 4A permission if it appears that it is desirable for the application to be refused in order to meet any of the PRA and/or the FCA regulatory objectives.

A firm will be expected to demonstrate to the regulators that it has ceased carrying on regulated activities. As part of the application process, the regulator may require the firm to provide confirmation or verification from a professional adviser on certain matters to supplement the report, verifying that the activities have been ceased by the firm.

## 16.2 Waiver and Modification Rules (SUP 8)

Firms can apply for a waiver and modification of certain rules in the PRA and/or the FCA Handbook, although following the implementation of MiFID, the regulators have decreased the scope and limitation on the rules that can be waivered or modified for firms.

The PRA and/or the FCA is required by Section 148(6) of FSMA to publish a waiver unless it is satisfied that it is inappropriate or unnecessary to do so. If the regulator publishes a waiver, it will not publish details of why a waiver was required or any of the supporting information given in a waiver application.

Once the regulator has granted a waiver, it may vary it with the firm's consent, or on the firm's application. If a firm wishes the regulator to vary a waiver, it should give reasons for the application. If the waiver that has been varied has previously been published, the regulator will publish the variation, unless it is satisfied that it is inappropriate or unnecessary to do so, having regard to any representation made by the firm.

A waiver can be revoked at any time, but only if the conditions in Section 148(4) of FSMA are no longer satisfied and when the waiver is otherwise no longer appropriate.

A waiver can be revoked with immediate effect, if the regulator considers that this is necessary, for example, in order to prevent undue risk to consumers.

If the regulator proposes to revoke a waiver, or revokes a waiver with immediate effect, it will:

1. give the firm written notice either of its proposal or of its action, giving reasons

2. state in the notice a reasonable period (usually 28 days) within which the firm can make representations about the proposal or action. If a firm wants to make oral representations, it should inform the regulator as quickly as possible, specify who will make the representations and which matters will be covered: the regulator will inform the firm of the time and place for hearing the representations and may request a written summary, and

3. after considering any representations, in the case of a proposed revocation, give the firm written confirmation of its decision to revoke the waiver or not; or, in the case of a revocation that has already taken effect, either confirm the revocation or seek the firm's consent to a new waiver.

If the waiver that has been revoked has previously been published, the regulator will publish the revocation unless it is satisfied that it is inappropriate or unnecessary to do so, having regard to any representations made by the firm.

## 16.4 Notifications to the FCA (SUP 15)

A firm is required to provide the FCA with a wide range of information to enable them to meet their responsibilities for monitoring the firm's compliance with requirements imposed by or under FSMA. Some of this information is provided through regular reports, including reporting requirements and transaction reporting.

In addition, other chapters in the Handbook set out specific notification and reporting requirements. Principle 11 (Relations with regulators) includes a requirement for a firm to disclose appropriately anything relating to the firm of which the FCA would reasonably expect notice.

SUP 15 sets out:

1. Guidance on the type of event or change in condition which a firm should consider notifying in accordance with Principle 11. The purpose of the guidance is to set out examples and not to give comprehensive advice to firms on what they should notify in order to be in compliance with Principle 11.

2. Rules on events and changes in condition that a firm must notify the regulator of. These are the types of event that the regulator would expect to be informed, usually as soon as possible, if it is to be able to carry out its monitoring function effectively and react in good time to developments that may require a regulatory response.

3. Rules on core information that a firm must provide, for example, its name and address and the names of its other regulators, so that the regulator is able to maintain a relationship with the firm and with those regulators.

4. Rules requiring a firm to ensure that information provided is accurate and complete. Section 398 of FSMA makes it an offence to knowingly or recklessly provide information which is false or misleading in a material particular, in purported compliance with any requirement imposed by, or under FSMA, firms must also take due care to ensure the accuracy of information and ensure that information is complete.

5. To implement the provisions of UK MAR for the reporting of transactions about which there is reasonable suspicion of market abuse.

A firm must notify the regulators immediately it becomes aware, or has information which reasonably suggests, that any of the following has occurred, may have occurred or may occur in the foreseeable future:

1. the firm failing to satisfy one or more of the threshold conditions

2. any matter which could have a significant adverse impact on the firm's reputation

3. any matter which could affect the firm's ability to continue to provide adequate services to its customers and which could result in serious detriment to a customer of the firm, or

4. any matter in respect of the firm which could result in serious financial consequences to the UK financial system or to other firms.

## A firm must also notify the FCA of:

- a. a significant breach of a rule (which includes a principle) or Statement of Principle

- b. a breach of any requirement imposed by FSMA, or by regulations, or an order made under FSMA by HM Treasury (except if the breach is an offence, in which case (c) applies)

- c. the bringing of a prosecution for, or a conviction of, any offence under FSMA

- d. a breach of a directly applicable provision in UK MiFID

- e. a breach of any requirement in Regulation 4C(3) (or any successor provision) of FSMA 2000 (Markets in Financial Instruments) Regulations 2007, or

- f. a breach of a directly applicable provision in the auction regulation.

A firm must notify the regulators immediately if:

1. civil proceedings are brought against the firm and the amount of the claim is significant in relation to the firm's financial resources or its reputation

2. any action is brought against the firm under Section 71 of FSMA, actions for damages, or Section 150, actions for damage

3. disciplinary measures or sanctions have been imposed on the firm by any statutory or regulatory authority, professional organisation or trade body (other than the PRA and/or the FCA) or the firm becomes aware that one of those bodies has started an investigation into its affairs

4. the firm is prosecuted for, or convicted of, any offence involving fraud or dishonesty, or any penalties are imposed on it for tax evasion, or

5. it is an Occupational Pension Scheme (OPS) firm, which is a trustee, and is removed as trustee by a court order.

A firm must notify the regulators immediately if one of the following events arises and the event is significant:

1. It becomes aware that an employee may have committed a fraud against one of its customers.

2. It becomes aware that a person, whether or not employed by it, may have committed a fraud against it.

3. It considers that any person, whether or not employed by it, is acting with intent to commit a fraud against it.

4. It identifies irregularities in its accounting, or other records, whether or not there is evidence of fraud.

5. It suspects that one of its employees may be guilty of serious misconduct concerning their honesty or integrity, and which is connected with the firm's regulated activities, or ancillary activities.

A firm must notify the regulators immediately of any of the following events:

1. the calling of a meeting to consider a resolution for winding up the firm

2. an application to dissolve the firm, or to strike it off the Register of Companies

3. the presentation of a petition for the winding up of the firm

4. the making of, or any proposals for the making of, a composition, or arrangement with any one or more of its creditors

5. an application for the appointment of an administrator, or trustee in bankruptcy to the firm

6. the appointment of a receiver to the firm (whether an administrative receiver or a receiver appointed over particular property)

7. an application for an interim order against the firm under Section 252 of the Insolvency Act 1986 (or, in Northern Ireland, Section 227 of the Insolvency (Northern Ireland) Order 1989)

8. if the firm is a sole trader:

   - an application for a sequestration order on the firm, or

   - the presentation of a petition for bankruptcy, or

9. anything equivalent to (1) to (8) above occurring in respect of the firm in a jurisdiction outside the UK.

## Notifications of Suspicious Transactions

A firm which arranges or executes a transaction with or for a client in a qualifying investment admitted to trading on a prescribed market, and which has reasonable grounds to suspect that the transaction might constitute market abuse, must notify the FCA without delay.

Firms must decide on a case-by-case basis whether there are reasonable grounds for suspecting that a transaction involves market abuse, taking into account the elements constituting market abuse. If the firm becomes aware of a fact, or information that gives reasonable ground for suspicion concerning a transaction, it must make a notification to the FCA without delay.

## 16.5 Transaction Reporting (SUP 17A)

Transaction reporting requirements apply to a:

1. UK MiFID investment firm

2. third country investment firm

3. person who is the operator of an approved reporting mechanism or of a regulated market, or MTF that is used by a firm to report transactions to the FCA, and

4. firm acting in its capacity as a manager, or operator of:

   - a collective investment undertaking

   - a pension scheme

   - an OPS

   - a personal pension scheme, or

   - a stakeholder pension scheme.

A firm which executes a transaction in any of the following must report the details of the transaction to the FCA:

1. any financial instrument admitted to trading on a UK or EU regulated market, or a prescribed market (whether or not the transaction was carried out on such a market)

2. any OTC derivative, the value of which is derived from, or which is otherwise dependent upon, an equity- or debt-related financial instrument which is admitted to trading on a UK or EU regulated market, or on a prescribed market, or

3. a basket or index of instruments which includes one or more financial instruments admitted to/ traded on a UK or EU regulated or prescribed market.

Under MiFID II, both firms (where they are subject to the requirements) must make a report to the FCA. Firms must report the required details of the transaction to the FCA as quickly as possible, but no later than the close of the working day following the day upon which that transaction took place.

Up to 65 data fields have to be reported by firms to the FCA.

In November 2024 the FCA published a discussion paper titled _'Improving the UK transaction reporting regime'_ .

The FCA intends to publish a consultation paper in H1 2025, followed by a Policy Statement, with final rules, in late Q4 2025 or early in 2025.

Following an announcement by HM Treasury, the FCA is looking at evolving the transaction reporting and instrument reference data requirements. The Discussion Paper informs the FCA's position on the development of a new transaction reporting regime that will remove unnecessary burdens for firms while maintaining high regulatory standards for UK markets.

The FCA's approach to developing the transaction reporting regime is based on two main objectives:

- Improve the usefulness of transaction reporting data through better data quality.

- Support the competitiveness of UK markets by ensuring requirements remain proportionate for firms.

## 20. Regulatory Guides

In the regulatory guides block of the FCA Handbook, the Perimeter Guidance (PERG) Manual gives guidance about the circumstances in which authorisation is required, or where exempt person status is available, including guidance on the activities which are regulated under the Act, and the exclusions which are available. In addition, the FCA's EG, which is in the same block, sets out the FCA's approach to how it exercises the main enforcement powers it has, both under FSMA and under the Unfair Contract Terms regulations. It does this mainly through the activities of its Enforcement Division.

## 20.1 Perimeter Guidance Manual (PERG)

The purpose of the PERG Manual is to give guidance about the circumstances in which authorisation is required, or exempt person status is available, including guidance on the activities which are regulated under the Act, and the exclusions which are available.

PERG guidance is issued under Section 157 of FSMA. It represents the views of the FCA and/or the PRA and does not bind the courts. For example, it will not bind the courts in an action for damages brought by a private person for breach of a rule (see Section 150 of the Act, actions for damages), or in relation to the enforceability of a contract where there has been a breach of Section 19, the General Prohibition) or Section 21, restrictions on financial promotion, of the Act (see Sections 26 to 30 of the Act, enforceability of agreements).

Although the guidance does not bind the courts, it may be of persuasive effect for a court considering whether it is just and equitable to allow a contract to be enforced. Anyone reading this guidance should refer to FSMA and to the relevant secondary legislation to find out the precise scope and effect of any particular provision referred to in the guidance. If a person acts in line with the guidance in the circumstances mentioned by it, the regulators will proceed on the basis that the person has complied with the aspects of the requirement to which the guidance relates.

PERG 2 is titled _'Authorisation and regulated activities'_ , it is relevant and applies to firms who need to know what activities fall within scope of FSMA.

PERG 8 is titled _'Financial promotion and related activities'_ , it applies to firms who need to know whether their communications to clients (or potential clients) are subject to FSMA.
