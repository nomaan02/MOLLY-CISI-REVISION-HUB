## 1. Regulatory Structure in the UK

HM Treasury has overall responsibility for the UK's financial system, the institutional structure of financial regulation and the legislation that governs it.

The Financial Services Act 2012 enacted the split of the then regulator, the Financial Services Authority (FSA), replacing it with three regulatory authorities – the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) and the Financial Policy Committee (FPC).

- The **FPC** – part of the Bank of England (BoE), with responsibility for macroprudential regulation, or regulation of the stability and resilience of the financial system as a whole.

- • The **PRA** – regulating deposit takers (ie, banks), insurers and systemically important investment firms. This is also part of the BoE.

- The **FCA** – regulating conduct issues across the entire spectrum of financial services. It is responsible for market supervision, as well as the prudential supervision of firms not supervised by the PRA (ie, solo regulated firms). The FCA is accountable to HM Treasury.

## 1.1 The Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)

The PRA's role is to contribute to the promotion of the stability of the UK financial system through microprudential regulation of credit institutions (banks/deposit takers), credit unions, insurance companies and significantly important investment firms. It has an overall objective to promote the safety and soundness of regulated firms, it meets this objective primarily by seeking to minimise any adverse effects of firm failure on the UK financial system, and by ensuring that firms carry on their business in a way that avoids adverse effects on the system.

For insurance supervision, the PRA has two complementary objectives – to secure an appropriate degree of protection for policyholders and, as needed, to minimise the adverse impact that the failure of an insurer or the way it carries out its business could have on the stability of the system.

The PRA supervises around 1,500 banks, building societies, credit unions, insurers and major investment banks.

## 1.2 The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)

The FCA follows an approach of credible deterrence, pursuing enforcement cases to punish wrongdoing. Its market regulation continues to promote integrity and carry on the fight against market misconduct and insider dealing.

The FCA's strategic objective is to ensure that financial services markets function well.

To ensure that the relevant markets work well, the FCA focuses on delivering good market conduct. The FCA's key priorities in this respect are:

- a renewed focus on wholesale conduct – in particular inherent conflicts of interest

- trust in the integrity of markets, and

- preventing market abuse.

The National Audit Office is the statutory auditor of the FCA, with the power to carry out value-formoney reviews. HM Treasury is also able to carry out economic, efficiency and effectiveness reviews. The FCA is directly accountable to HM Treasury, but ultimately to Parliament.

The FCA is primarily responsible for undertaking investigations and enforcement action in relation to financial crime. The FCA sees 'credible deterrence' as using enforcement as a regulatory tool – selecting cases to enforce against to send a strong and clear message to other firms not to engage in wrongful activity.

Powers provided by the Financial Services Act 2012 mean that the FCA can publicly announce that it has begun disciplinary action against a firm or an individual – however, they have to date not used this power due to the complications and unintended consequences that this announcement could entail. The FCA is able to publish details of a warning notice proposing disciplinary action, to signal the start of formal enforcement proceedings. However, the FCA must consult the recipient of the warning notice before publishing.

Following the coming into effect of FSMA 2023, the PRA, along with the FCA, have a secondary objective to:

_'facilitate the international competitiveness of the UK economy and its medium-to long-term growth (subject to international standards)'._

## 1.3 FCA Strategic and Operational Objectives, Statutory Objectives, Structure, Powers and Activities

Sitting underneath the FCA's strategic objective are their operational objectives – which are to:

- protect consumers from bad conduct from regulated firms

- maintain market integrity (protecting the integrity of the UK financial system), and

- promote effective competition in the interest of consumers.

Underlining the operational objectives are the following broad outcomes – which are embedded into the Consumer Duty (refer to section 6 of this chapter):

- Consumers have access to fair products and services, which deliver what they promise.

- Consumers can be confident that firms treat them fairly and fix problems promptly.

- Competition contributes to improved consumer outcomes.

- Firms compete on clear costs and consumers have the information they need.

- Consumers can trust firms to be fit and proper and for financial markets to be clean.

- A respected regulatory system that enables firms to assess and measure their level of compliance.

The FCA carries out in-depth structured supervision work with those firms with the potential to cause the greatest risks to their objectives. This means fewer supervisors allocated to specific firms – but allows the greater flexibility to carry out more reviews on products and issues across a particular sector or market-thematic reviews. This new approach is underpinned by judgement-based supervision.

The FCA's integrity objective includes within it the 'soundness, stability and resilience' of the UK financial system. With regard to resilience, they expect firms to operate high standards in their risk management, having procedures in place to ensure continuity of critical services. Firms are required to comply with standards for resilience and recovery established in this area.

To ensure that the relevant markets work well, the FCA has increased its focus on delivering good market conduct outcomes.

The thinking behind 'outcomes focused' supervision is that, previously, there was too much focus and attention paid to 'box-ticking' and 'principles-based regulation' in supervision. The focus now is around good supervision, based on judgement and outcomes, the art of looking at a situation from several angles and seeking to identify weaknesses.

The FCA's approach to supervision is incorporated into its Supervision Sourcebook, in SUP1A.1.

The FCA will make supervisory judgements about a firm's business model and forward-looking strategy, and intervening when it sees unacceptable risks to the fair treatment of customers. It will look for firms to base their business model, their culture, and how they run their business on a foundation of the Consumer Duty and/or the Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC) (where applicable) in delivering desired outcomes for customers – retail and professional clients.

To make judgement-based supervision work, the FCA needs staff who have strong, relevant skills, and experience. From an FCA perspective, conduct regulation, which is essentially about how people behave, is different from prudential regulation which can be more of an analytical exercise. Therefore, this requires a different skill set.

This approach means that FCA supervisors will conduct in-depth and structured supervisory work on those firms with the greatest potential to cause risk to consumers, market integrity or competition. Some firms may experience a higher intensity of supervision than others.

The Financial Services Act 2012 gave the FCA powers to help it achieve its outcomes. One of the important powers is the ability to ban financial products that pose unacceptable risks to consumers, including publishing details of misleading financial promotions, and letting people know when the FCA is proposing to take disciplinary action against a firm.

## 1.3.1 Risk-Based Approach

The FCA takes a proportionate, judgement-based and outcomes approach. It assesses the risk a firm poses to its objectives, either individually or collectively, and then focuses its resources on managing the risk issue most likelihood to crystallise.

In summary, the FCA:

- identifies and assesses risks (both emerging and current) to consumers, firms and financial markets

- identifies the risk of market failures hindering effective competition

- develops a general understanding of the risks and issues in the financial markets to support its policy-making, authorisation, supervision and enforcement functions

- makes evidence-based policies to help change firm and market behaviour

- prioritises, manages and mitigates risk consistently, and uses a risk-based approach for making decisions

- establishes common standards and principles for measuring and assessing risk across the organisation, and

- puts in place the infrastructure, systems and tools to catalogue, analyse and assess risk.

## 1.3.2 Secure an Appropriate Degree of Protection for Consumers

The FCA expects firms to have their customers at the heart of how they do business, when they develop, and promote or sell products and services. Therefore, the FCA's aims and objectives are to:

- ensure that customers are treated in a way that is appropriate for their level of financial knowledge and understanding

- be more outward-looking, by engaging more with consumers and understanding more about their concerns and behaviour

- set clear expectations for firms and be clear about what they can expect from the FCA

- intervene early to tackle potential risks to consumers before they occur, and

- maintain a strategy of 'credible deterrence', including intervening earlier, and continuing to hold senior individuals to account.

## 1.3.3 Protect and Enhance the Integrity of the UK Financial System

The FCA seeks to develop and support a healthy and successful financial system, where financial markets are efficient and transparent, firms can thrive and consumers can place their trust in transparent and open markets. This means that the markets need to be supported by appropriate infrastructure, access and transparency to meet the needs of the consumers, corporates and other wholesale clients that use them.

To achieve this, they seek to ensure that:

- senior management are accountable for their firm's activities, including principal and agency responsibilities in the wholesale markets

- there is a positive culture of proactively identifying and managing conflicts of interest

- there is orderly resolution and return of client assets

- firms' business models, activities, controls and behaviours maintain trust in the integrity of markets and do not create or allow market abuse, systemic risk or financial crime

- market efficiency, cleanliness and resilience are delivered through transparency, surveillance and the supervision of infrastructures, as well as their principal users

- firms, acting as agents on behalf of their clients, put clients' best interests at the heart of their businesses, and

- early intervention in wholesale markets to mitigate the risk of harm being transmitted to retail consumers.

## 1.3.4 Promote Effective Competition in the Interests of Consumers

The FCA applies a holistic approach to assessing whether competition in markets is working well for consumers. In assessing whether intervention is needed, it considers a range of market failures including:

- unilateral market power

- barriers to entry and expansion

- coordinated conduct by firms

- vertical relationships

- weak customer response, and

- principal-agent problems.

As noted above, the FCA's aim and objective is to be a proactive force in enabling the right outcomes for consumers and market participants.

In addition to fulfilling its stated objectives, the FCA is responsible for:

- the conduct regulation of all firms, covering the range of their dealings with retail customers, through to their activities in wholesale markets; it regulates about 42,000 firms in total, including those prudentially supervised by the PRA

- the prudential supervision of all firms not prudentially supervised by the PRA (approximately 41,000)

- setting specific rules for around 17,000 firms

- supervision of trading infrastructure including the investment exchanges and over-the-counter (OTC) markets and monitoring firms' compliance with the market abuse regime

- investigating and prosecuting insider dealing through the use of its criminal powers

- acting as the UK Listing Authority, and

- overseeing the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) and the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS), jointly with the PRA.

The FCA has increased its focus on the conduct of firms. Firms' senior management set the culture of their organisation, so the FCA is looking to ensure that what is decided and agreed by senior management translates to good outcomes for consumers.

The Policy, Risk and Research Division within the FCA acts as the radar of the organisation. It combines research into what is happening in the market and to consumers with better analysis of the type of risks and where they appear.

In summary the division:

- identifies and assesses risks to consumers, firms and markets – both emerging and current

- creates a common view of the risks in financial markets to inform the FCA's authorisation, supervision and enforcement decisions, and

- uses the knowledge of these risks to make evidence-based policy that changes behaviour.

By bringing together the information gathering, analysis, research and policy-making into one place, the FCA is better placed to make timely and effective interventions where risks have been identified.

## 1.4 PRA Strategic and Operational Objectives, Structure, Powers and Activities

In addition to its statutory objectives, noted above in section 1.1, the PRA has eight fundamental rules that firms must comply with.

Therefore, firms regulated by the PRA must:

1. conduct its business with integrity

2. conduct its business with due skill, care and diligence

3. act in a prudent manner

4. maintain adequate financial resources at all times

5. have effective risk strategies and risk management systems

6. organise and control its affairs responsibly and effectively

7. deal with its regulators in an open and cooperative way, and must disclose to the PRA appropriately anything relating to the firm of which the PRA would reasonably expect notice, and

8. prepare for resolution so, if the need arises, it can be resolved in an orderly manner with a minimum disruption of critical services.

There is a key difference between the PRA's statutory objectives and its fundamental rules. The objectives are applied to the PRA, and they establish what the PRA is to achieve as a regulator. The fundamental rules are applied by the PRA, setting out the expected rules to be adhered to by firms regulated by it.

The PRA achieves its statutory objectives by:

- seeking to ensure that any firms that fail, do so in a way that avoids significant disruption to the supply of critical financial services

- emphasising resolution planning to permit orderly failure

- cooperating closely with the FPC and the FCA to ensure macro and microprudential regulation is aligned across the markets, and

- working with the FCA and others to ensure the UK authorities have a strong voice in global policy making.

The PRA's approach to supervision relies significantly on the judgement of its staff. The PRA supervises firms and assesses whether they are financially safe and sound, that they meet, and are likely to continue to meet, the threshold conditions. It looks not just at current risks, but also against those that could manifest in the future.

The PRA focuses on those issues and those firms that pose the greatest risk to the stability of the UK financial system. The frequency and intensity of the supervision experienced by firms increase in line with the risks that they pose to the stability of the financial services sector.

The PRA focuses on material issues when engaging with firms.

As the PRA's approach is based significantly on the judgement of its staff, it relies on experienced staff and detailed analysis.

The PRA utilises a risk framework, which captures three key elements:

- The potential impact that a firm could have on financial stability in the UK, both by the way it carries out its business and in the event of failure.

- How the external context in which a firm operates, and the business risks it faces (together, its risk context) might affect the viability of the firm.

- Mitigating factors including: a firm's management and governance and its risk management and controls (operational mitigation); its financial strength, specifically capital and liquidity (financial mitigation), and its resolvability (structural mitigation).

## 1.4.1 Enforcement Powers

The PRA's preference is to use its powers to secure up front remedial action, given its approach of intervening early to address emerging risks. However, it does have the authority and powers to impose financial penalties and publish censures in cases where sanctions are inappropriate. It also has powers when directions or restrictions imposed by the PRA are ignored by the firm.

The PRA deploys its disciplinary powers as follows:

- Reinforcing the PRA's objectives and priorities.

- Promoting high standards of regulatory behaviour.

- The need to send a clear signal to a firm (and to the regulated community), about the circumstances in which the PRA considers a firm's behaviour to be unacceptable.

- Deterring future misconduct.

## 1.4.2 BoE Enforcement Decision Making Committee (EDMC)

The Enforcement Decision Making Committee (EDMC) is a committee of the BoE. It helps the BoE to discharge its responsibilities and strengthen its enforcement process by ensuring that there is a functional separation between the investigation teams and the decision makers in contested enforcement cases.

Members of the EDMC will be independent of the BoE's executive team and will not be employees of the BoE. They will be appointed by the Court of the BoE and will serve for a term of three years. They will not be permitted to serve more than two three-year fixed terms.

The EDMC will meet as often as necessary to discharge its functions; a panel of three members will be required to convene to hear and resolve each contested enforcement case and will have one vote on the matter under consideration. If the votes are split, then the panel lead hearing the case will have the casting vote.

## Warning Notices

In contested enforcement cases, if the BoE considers that a warning notice is appropriate, then it will make a recommendation to the EDMC, who will make the decision and will also determine the wording of the warning notice.

## 1.4.3 Structure

The PRA's approach is advanced primarily by its frontline supervisors. Its judgements are grounded in analysis, supervisory experience and a strong understanding of the sectors it supervises, gained through direct exposure with the senior management of firms. The supervisors are supported by risk specialists.

The structure of the PRA is such that the chief executive officer (CEO) is supported by three business areas:

- Banking:

   - domestic UK banks

   - international UK banks

   - investment banks and overseas banks, and

   - risk specialists.

- Insurance:

   - general insurance, and

   - life insurance.

- Policy:

   - banking policy, and

   - insurance policy.

## 1.4.4 Approach to Supervision – PRA

The PRA's approach relies significantly on judgement. The PRA supervises firms to judge whether they are safe and sound; and whether they meet, and are likely to continue to meet, the threshold conditions. PRA supervisors therefore reach judgements on the risks that a firm poses to the PRA's objective, and how these can be addressed.

The PRA has a statutory objective to promote the safety and soundness of firms. It is required to pursue this primarily by seeking to avoid adverse effects on financial stability, and in particular by seeking to minimise adverse effects resulting from disruption to the continuity of financial services that can be caused by the way firms run their business, or upon their failure.

Therefore, the PRA's key objective is focusing on those issues and the firms that pose the greatest risk to the stability of the UK financial system. The frequency and intensity of supervision applied by the PRA to a particular firm will increase in line with the risk that they pose to its overarching objective.

The PRA's supervisory judgements are, therefore, based on evidence and analysis. It is inherent in a forward-looking system that at times the supervisor's judgement will be at odds with that of the firm. Furthermore, there will be occasions when events will show that the supervisor's judgement, in hindsight, was wrong.

The PRA's regulatory decision-making is rigorous and well-documented, consistent with public law. Its most significant supervisory judgements are taken by the Prudential Regulation Committee (PRC). The PRC board is involved in the most important decisions on general policy and individual cases.

Although, it should be remembered that both the PRA and the FCA do not operate a 'no-fail' regime with regards to the supervision of firms.

## 1.5 Approach to Supervision – FCA

The FCA defines 'supervision' as the continuing oversight of regulated firms and of individuals controlling these firms, to reduce actual and potential harm to consumers and markets.

The FCA adopts a 'pre-emptive' approach based on making forward-looking judgments about firms business models, product strategy and how they run their businesses, in order for the FCA to identify, determine and intervene earlier to prevent problems arising.

Therefore, the FCA's approach to the supervision of firms contributes to the delivery of the statutory objective to protect and enhance the integrity of the UK financial system.

Where the FCA has responsibilities for prudential supervision, its focus will be on the reduction of impacts to customers and the integrity of the financial system where firms fail. In addition, when consumer detriment occurs, they will seek redress for impacted consumers.

Underpinning the FCA's work for supervision are supervisory principles; these guide the FCA's work to protect consumers, enhance market integrity and promote competition and form the basis of its supervisory approach.

The supervisory principles are complementary to the **Principles for Businesses** which outline the FCA's expectations of firms.

-

## **Forward looking**

   - Pre-empt poor conduct so that the risk does not crystallise, or where it does happen that the impact does not cause significant harm to markets or consumers.

- **Focus on strategy and business models**

   - Assessment of firms' business models and strategies to identify potential emerging risk of harm.

   - Have a good understanding of a firm's business model, activities it undertakes and the risk to markets and consumers that this could pose.

- **Focus on culture and governance**

   - Assess the drivers of behaviour within firms.

- **Focus on individuals as well as firm accountability**

   - Hold to account senior individuals who manage and/or direct the firm with respect to its strategy.

-

## **Proportionate and risk-based**

   - Use knowledge and understanding of markets that the FCA regulates to identify and target firms whose misconduct could cause harm.

- **Two-way communication**

   - Engagement with regulated firms and consumers.

   - Clear on the behaviour expected of firms, the good and poor practice that is observed.

- **Coordinated**

   - Supervision teams work closely with other FCA departments.

   - Work with other UK and international regulatory bodies.

- **Put right systematic harm that has occurred and prevent it from happening again**

   - Move quickly to prevent harm happening to markets and/or consumers.

   - Where appropriate refer action/activities to the Enforcement division.

The FCA supervision model risk assessment process applies to all firms, although detailed requirements will vary between firms – some firms will have a highly intensive level of contact and others less so. This depends on the risk that they pose to the FCA's strategic and operational objectives.

The supervision model is based on three types of work ('pillars'):

- **Proactive**

   - preventative work through structured conduct assessment of firms

   - this includes model analysis and the drivers of culture

- **Reactive**

   - dealing with problems that are emerging or have crystallised, and securing customer redress or other remedial work (eg, to secure the integrity of the market) where necessary, and

- **Thematic**

   - looking at sectors of the market or products within a sector that are putting or may put consumers at risk

   - wider diagnostic or remedy work where there is actual or potential harm across a number of firms or a products/service offering.

## Supervision by Portfolio

The FCA supervises most firms as members of a 'portfolio' of firms that share a common business model. The FCA analyses each portfolio and agrees a strategy to take action on firms posing the greatest risk – communicating this with firms on their expectations, priorities and examples of good or poor practice.

## Dedicated Supervision Teams

The FCA has a dedicated supervision team that monitors and engages with firms with the most likely potential of harm that could have an impact on consumers and markets. This team has a view of the whole firm across all sectors it operates in; it assesses the potential harm that the firm may cause and agrees a strategy to reduce or prevent it before it crystallises.

## 1.5.1 FCA Prudential Supervision

Although the PRA has prudential responsibility for bank/deposit takers (credit institutions), insurers and significantly important investment banks, the FCA is the prudential supervisor for a wide range of firms across the financial services sector, such as investment firms, asset managers, independent financial advisers, mortgage and insurance brokers – all those not supervised by the PRA (FCA solo-regulated firms).

The FCA's approach aims to minimise the harm to consumers, wholesale market participants and market stability when firms experience financial stress or fail in a disorderly manner.

## Prudential Categories

Firms that are prudentially regulated by the FCA fall into three prudential categories: P1, P2 and P3. These categories reflect the impact that the disorderly failure of a firm could have on markets and consumers.

- **P1** – firms whose failure would cause lasting and widespread financial and reputational damage to their customers, client assets, the marketplace and beyond. These are subjected to periodic capital and, if applicable, liquidity assessment. The FCA's prudential specialists conduct these tests every 24 months.

- **P2** – firms whose disorderly failure would damage consumers and client assets but are more easily dealt with than the failure of a P1 firm. These are subjected to periodic capital and, if applicable, liquidity assessment. The FCA's prudential specialists conduct these tests every 48 months.

- **P3** – firms whose failure, even if disorderly, would be unlikely to cause significant harm to consumers or market integrity. The FCA supervises these firms on a reactive basis.

Like the conduct categories, the prudential categories determine the intensity of their prudential supervision for each firm. Through prudential activities the FCA seeks to ensure that:

- firms maintain adequate financial resources in line with legal requirements

- the FCA has an early warning system for financial issues that could drive behaviours that endanger a firm's compliance with conduct, financial crime, client assets and other core regulatory standards

- any wind-up of a firm could happen with little or no damage to markets and consumers.

## **Financial Resources Requirements (FRR)**

The **FCA Handbook** specifies minimum **Financial Resources Requirements (FRR)** for all firms. Firms must maintain sufficient resources to meet their financial obligations at all times, and must be able to show how they have determined what is sufficient. This is the starting point for any prudential supervisory review.

The specific scope and nature of FRR varies by firm type and permissions:

- P1 firms have a capital and (if applicable) liquidity assessment every two years.

- P2 firms have a capital and (if applicable) liquidity assessment every three to four years, by examining a firm's own assessments of financial requirements.

## Sources of Information on the FCA's and PRA's Supervisory Approach

The FCA has been placing a greater emphasis on providing 'informal guidance' to firms and individuals, via speeches, public statements and Dear CEO letters, in addition to formal consultations.

Therefore, firms and individuals within firms, must stay up to date with the methods of communication that the FCA is using to deliver its intentions and expectations of firms and/or individuals.

Both the PRA and the FCA publish a number of documents which should be reviewed and assessed by firms. These documents include the following:

- **Annual Report (FCA only)**

   - This provides an update on what the FCA have delivered and achieved in the past year (period in question).

- **Annual Business Plan (PRA & FCA)**

   - For the FCA, this explains how they see their future role and priorities, how they intend to deliver them and how they measure their performance.

   - For the PRA, the annual plan sets out the work plan for each of the PRA's strategic goals to support delivery of their strategy.

-

## **Dear CEO Letters (FCA only)**

   - An open letter sent to firms (providing specific services or performing specific activities) identifying concerns and issues that the FCA have identified, sometimes from thematic reviews of firm's activities or issues, that they wish the industry to take note of.

   - In some instances, the firm's CEO would be required to provide an attestation to verify/confirm that they have undertaken an assessment relating to the concerns that the FCA identify in the letter.

-

## **Thematic Reviews (FCA only)**

   - Used to assess a current or emerging risk regarding an issue or product across a number of firms in a market or sector.

   - Also undertaken following the implementation of new rules, to assess that they are delivering the intended aims and objectives of the new rules.

-

## **Discussion Papers and Consultation Papers (PRA & FCA)**

   - A publication that explores policy changes and/or an issue that needs addressing in the form of a consultation with the industry.

   - The response period is open for a specific period of time.

-

## **Policy Statements (FCA & PRA)**

   - This provides feedback on the consultation paper and approach adopted along with links to the final rules (statutory instrument).

-

## **Supervisory Statements (PRA only)**

   - The supervisory statement provides feedback and approach adopted by the PRA, along with links to the final rules (statutory instrument).

-

## **Final Notices (FCA only)**

   - A statutory notice issued when the FCA takes enforcement action against an individual and/or a regulated firm.

-

## **Enforcement Notices (FCA only)**

   - Published by the FCA to advise individuals of a course of action undertaken by the FCA against an individual and/or a legal person (firm).

   - All such documents are published by the FCA and PRA on their websites. These websites are a useful source of information and guidance for firms to assess how they might be affected and/or what might be relevant to them in terms of the regulated activities that they undertake.

-

## **Published Speeches and Websites (PRA & FCA)**

   - Speeches made by PRA and/or FCA staff at events where they are invited to speak/discuss a specific topic (ie, benchmarks and market abuse) and/or their general approach to issues.

-

## **Enforcement Notices (FCA & PRA)**

- Published by the PRA and the FCA to advise individuals of a course of action undertaken by them against an individual and/or a legal person (firm).

- All such documents are published on their websites.

- The websites are a useful source of information and guidance for firms to assess how they might be affected and/or what might be relevant to them in terms of the regulated activities that they undertake.

## **FCA Strategy 2025–30**

In March 2025, the FCA published its 'new' five-year strategy, which focuses on:

- deepening trust

- rebalancing risk

- supporting growth, and

- improving lives.

To achieve the strategy, the FCA will:

- be a smarter regulator:

   - efficient and effective regulator

   - proportionate and predictable – easier to engage and deal with

   - reform how they regulate

- support growth:

   - focus on the competitiveness of UK financial services

- help consumers:

   - product innovation and widened access

   - consumers get the support and information that they need

   - underpinning this will be the Consumer Duty

- fight crime:

   - work with firms that want to play their part in tackling crime (disrupt crime)

   - consumers have the tools they need to protect themselves.

The FCA highlighted five opportunities and challenges to meeting its five-year strategy, namely:

- technology

- growth

- global uncertainty

- challenging financial resilience, and

- demographic change.
