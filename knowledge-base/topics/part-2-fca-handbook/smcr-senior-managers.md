## 2. Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR)

Following the global financial bank crisis of 2007–08, the UK government created the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) to consider and report on professional standards and the culture of the UK banking sector. It took into account regulatory and competition investigations into the LIBOR rate-setting process and lessons learned about corporate governance, transparency, conflicts of interest, and their implications for regulation and for government policy. Initially it was called the Accountability Regime, but changed its name to the Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR). The essence of the 'new' regime was one of accountability for firms, as well as individuals who manage and direct the activities of the firm.

The recommendations of the PCBS were accepted and adopted by the government, and inserted into the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013.

The SM&CR came into effect for UK banks (deposit takers – credit institutions), branches of third country banks and insurance companies in 2016. It was phased in for other types of firms – notably Solvency II firms (including the Society of Lloyds, managing agents, incoming branches of non-UK firms and insurance special purpose vehicles), non-directive firms (insurers outside scope of Solvency II) and small run-off firms, FCA solo-regulated firms and claims management companies.

## **All PRA regulated firms are now subject to the SM&CR, as well as the majority of FCA-regulated firms – except for appointed representatives and other limited types of firms – who are still subject to the Approved Persons Regime (APER).**

There is no territorial limitation. The concept of an individual being captured by the Senior Managers Regime and/or the Certificate Regime is not decided on where the individual is based but rather the role that they perform in respect of the UK entity.

## 2.1 Senior Managers Regime (SMR)

The definition of a 'senior manager' is someone who is responsible for managing the firm on a dayto-day basis. Individuals are classed as an 'SMF' because they are undertaking a Senior Management Function.

The regime is intended to capture the very senior individuals within banks who make the decisions and determine the direction of the firm in the discharge of regulated activities.

This approach is slightly amended in respect of foreign branches, where the structure is different to UK banks. For foreign banks the regime will not catch overseas individuals who set the strategy or agree the strategy, rather it will capture those individuals who will implement that strategy in the UK branch. The regime only applies to 'branch activity'. However, an individual based overseas could be captured if they are involved in implementation of the strategy and day-to-day management of the branch.

The PRA and FCA have a list of key responsibilities that firms will be required to allocate among senior managers.

Where an individual performing a 'SMF' is going to be absent from work for a period greater than 12 weeks, but is expected to return and their position is being left open by the firm, then the firm does not have to apply to remove their authorisation.

It is only where the individual will not be returning to their role that requires authorisation (a 'SMF' role) that the firm must notify the PRA and/or the FCA.

## 2.1.1 Application to Banks and Deposit Takers

The PRA requires that all banks must have at least one individual performing as Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Chief Financial Officer (CFO) and Chairman SMF, as well as a head of key business area and group entity senior manager, and extends the Senior Managers Regime (SMR) to all board members not already caught by the PRA regime.

The FCA requires firms to have individuals in SMF roles of executive directors, significant responsibility senior manager, compliance oversight and money laundering reporting.

Between the PRA and the FCA there are 17 senior management functions (five for the FCA and 12 for the PRA) that can/have to be allocated: these can be jointly allocated (although both would be responsible for the role/responsibility).

For UK banks the FCA requires SMFs to be in the roles of executive directors, compliance oversight, **money laundering reporting officer (MLRO)** and other overall responsibility functions (previously called significant responsibility function).

The 'other overall responsibility' function is intended to cover individuals with overall responsibility for one or more key functions or identified risks, and who perform a function not caught by another SMF. This 'overall function' allows firms to designate senior executives who have ultimate responsibility for functions but would not otherwise be part of the SMF structure.

Non-executive directors (NEDs) are also subject to the Senior Managers Regime – six functions are identified (five by the PRA and one by FCA), as noted below:

1. Chairman.

2. Chair of the Risk Committee.

3. Chair of the Audit Committee.

4. Chair of the Remuneration Committee.

5. Senior Independent Director.

6. Chair of the Nomination Committee (FCA only).

Firms will have to undertake annual assessments to confirm that individuals performing a senior management function are fit and proper.

Underneath the SMFs, the regulators have developed a list of key responsibilities (known as 'prescribed responsibilities') that firms will be required to allocate among senior managers – some are shared between the FCA and the PRA.

## 2.1.2 Application to Insurers/Insolvency II Firms

The FCA approached identifying key management functions in insurers the same way as banks – the most senior in the firm, who control and direct the firm.

There may be cases where a senior manager applies to perform both a PRA and FCA Senior Management Function at the same time. In this case, the FCA would apply an 'Overlap Rule'. The Overlap Rule applies when a firm is seeking approval from the PRA for a candidate to perform a PRA-designated SMF and also intends this candidate to perform an FCA SMF, once the PRA gives its approval. The Overlap Rule applies to FCA governing functions only.

The FCA decided against introducing requirements for firms to have a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO), rather firms only need to have an MLRO if the **Money Laundering Regulations (MLRs)** apply to them. However, they must still have appropriate systems and controls in place to prevent financial crime.

Solvency II firms must ensure that one or more persons perform each of the following Senior Insurance Management Functions on its behalf:

- Chief Executive Function.

- Chief Finance Function.

- Chairman.

The FCA introduced the 'Conduct Risk Oversight Officer' function because of the size, scale and structurally-unique role of the Lloyd's market. This function is consistent with the PRA's current Underwriting Risk Oversight Function, which is also Lloyd's-only. The Conduct Risk Oversight Officer for Lloyd's is responsible for overseeing the conduct of business standards required by the Lloyd's market for the managing agents who write business at Lloyd's only. The senior manager is also accountable for monitoring and overseeing managing agents' compliance with the Lloyd's conduct of business standards.

The FCA applied the 'Overall Responsibility' requirement to all Solvency II firms (excluding small run-off firms and insurance special purpose vehicles (ISPVs)) and large non-directive firms (NDFs). Therefore, these firms must have a senior manager with overall responsibility for every activity, business area and management function in the firm.

The FCA broadened the Compliance Oversight Function (SMF16) to cover all insurers for all requirements of the regulatory system for which the FCA is responsible. Therefore, insurers have to comply with all FCA rule requirements, as well as with directly applicable UK or onshored (retained) EU legislation where the FCA has responsibility for ensuring compliance.

Individuals are allowed to cover for an approved person (SMF) without being approved, where the absence is temporary or cannot be reasonably foreseen, and the appointment is for less than 12 consecutive weeks.

The FCA extended this 12-week rule to apply to responsibilities under the 'Overall Responsibility' requirement.

SMFs functions 9 to 14 should be held by approved NEDs, rather than executives, The FCA adopted the same approach for NEDs as those that apply to banks  and deposit takers.

The approach for non-executive directors (NEDs) is the same as for banks, as noted above.

## 2.1.4 Application to Benchmark Administrators

The Senior Managers Regime has also been applied to benchmark administrators that do not undertake any other regulated activities.

The FCA decided that benchmark administrators should automatically be subject to the core SMR, on the basis that they provide critical market infrastructure and the pricing of many financial instruments and contracts and the financial services sector depends on the accuracy and integrity of these benchmarks.

Therefore, the FCA considers that these firms must have a healthy culture and high standards of personal conduct, avoiding and preventing harm to markets and consumers.

The rules and requirements apply to UK-based benchmark administrators.

Benchmark administrators that are classified as 'core' firms will be required to have/appoint individuals to the following SMFs:

- SMF 1 – Chief Executive.

- SMF 3 – Executive Director.

- SMF 9 – Chair.

- SMF 27 – Partner.

Because these firms are subject to UK BMR (onshored/retained EU legislation), enacted via the statutory instrument titled 'The Benchmarks (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2018', they will not be required to appoint individuals to the function of Compliance Oversight (SMF16) and Money Laundering Reporting Officer (SMF17).

Core benchmark administrators will be required to allocate three prescribed responsibilities:

- (a) Performance by the firm of its obligations under the SMR, including implementation and oversight.

- (b-1) Performance by the firm of its obligations in respect of notifications and training of the **Conduct Rules** .

- (d) Responsibility for the firm's policies and procedures for countering the risk that the firm might be used to further financial crime.

By default, all benchmark administrators are classified as core firms for the purpose of the SM&CR, but for some firms the 'Limited Scope Benchmarks Administrators' will be more suitable – where they are required to appoint just one individual as a Senior Manager – the SMF 29 function (Limited Scope Function). These firms must apply to the FCA for a waiver to be classified as a Limited Scope Benchmark Administrator.

The FCA did not apply the Certification Regime to benchmark administrators, where they do not undertake any other regulated activities. This is because the UK BMR places requirements on administrators to ensure employees are fit and proper – so this achieves the same result and outcome.

The Conduct Rules apply to employees in benchmark administrators that undertake regulated and unregulated financial services activities.

The FCA applied the Conduct Rules to firms subject to UK BMR for the regulated activity that they undertake.

## 2.1.5 Application to All Types of Firms

One of the most important elements of the regime is that each SMF is required to have a statement of responsibility and a responsibilities map (a responsibilities matrix) – a single document describing management and governance arrangements, which identify how responsibilities have been allocated to senior managers so that there are no gaps in accountability within the firm. For FCA solo-regulated firms, this applies to firms classified as 'enhanced firms'.

Firms must develop, and have in place, a responsibilities map, which clearly identifies the structure and individuals who are senior managers. These must be allocated to an SMF; sharing of responsibilities is permitted between individuals – although the PRA and the FCA see this being used in very limited circumstances. In these circumstances, both individuals would share the responsibility – which must be noted on the statement of responsibility.

Firms must explain in their responsibility map why a responsibility is allocated jointly.

## 2.1.6 Statements of Responsibilities

As part of the regulatory application process, firms must provide statements of responsibilities to the FCA/PRA for approval. These must be shown to the regulators as part of the application process – they MUST be approved by the regulators. The maps identify which parts of the firm's business/activities senior managers are personally responsible for – these will then be used by the regulators when there has been a breach of rules/regulations. These statements must be accepted by the individual – they will be required to sign them, taking ownership of them, including maintaining and ensuring that they are up to date).

## 2.1.7 Handover Material

Where a person is becoming an SMF or is an existing SMF but is taking on new responsibilities, there is a requirement that necessary information and material reasonably expected for them to perform their responsibilities effectively, is made available in accordance with relevant regulatory requirements.

Firms must have a policy about how they comply with this requirement. These handover documents must be practical and helpful, including an assessment of what issues should be prioritised, containing judgment and opinion, not just facts and figures.

## 2.1.8 Duty of Responsibility

In the original PCBS recommendations and the subsequent FCA and PRA consultations, upon the occurrence of a regulatory breach, the senior manager would be deemed to have breached their personal duties and be guilty of misconduct. The onus would have been on the senior manager to satisfy the regulators that they had taken 'reasonable steps' to prevent the breach from occurring. The FCA would have used the statement of responsibilities and responsibilities map to identify the relevant individual with accountability and responsibility.

However, HM Treasury changed their approach, and in the Bank of England and **Financial Services Act 2016** , the burden of proof was shifted back to the regulators, with the PRA and FCA now being required to demonstrate that the senior manager (SMF) did not take such steps as a person in their position could reasonably be expected to have taken to avoid the firm's contravention occurring.

Therefore, the regulators have a burden of proof hurdle before they can bring disciplinary proceedings against the senior manager (SMF).

This is called 'the duty of responsibility'.

## 2.1.9 Overall and Local Responsibility

One of the main purposes and aims behind the SM&CR was that one or more individuals from a firm have responsibility for each of the activities, business areas and management functions of their firm.

Both the FCA and the PRA expect that individuals to whom overall responsibility for a function has been allocated are the most senior individuals within the firm for managing and/or supervising that function.

They will be either a member of the governing body (management body or equivalent) or will report directly to the governing body (management body or equivalent).

The individual(s) must be sufficiently senior and credible and have sufficient resources and authority. The FCA is aware that smaller firms may allocate responsibility to a small group of individuals – but for all firms, the FCA expects that when allocated to individuals then those individuals should be able to perform their responsibilities effectively.

## 2.1.10 'Long-Term Absence' – Impacts to Senior Managers (SMFs)

In 2021 the FCA and the PRA published a clarification on their expectation for firms to notify them when an individual who is in a SMF role, takes temporary leave for more than 12 weeks.

In cases where an individual who is in a SMF position is temporarily absent from their role, and their role has been kept open/they will be returning to their role, the firm will not need to notify the FCA (and, where applicable, the PRA) that the individual's approval should be removed.

Firms need to ensure that for this role they obtain approval for another individual to perform that required function during the interim period.

This does not change the notification requirements for firms to notify the FCA when the individual will not be returning to their role.

## 2.1.11 Consumer Duty – Implications for Senior Managers

(Refer to section 6 of this chapter for further details on the Consumer Duty requirements.)

Following the application of the Consumer Duty, senior managers are tasked with the following where the Consumer Duty applies (ie, the firm is considered in scope of the Consumer Duty due them having a relationship with retail clients and/or the products or services that they provide can have an impact on the outcomes of retail clients):

- The Consumer Duty is reflected in firms' strategies, governance, leadership and people policies.

- Senior managers ensure that their firm has appropriate oversight of customer outcomes through their systems and controls:

   - senior managers will be held accountable for delivering 'good consumer outcomes' within their area of responsibility within the firm.

- Firms must appoint a Consumer Duty champion at board level (or equivalent governing body), where they, the CEO and the Chairman of the Board ensure that Consumer Duty is regulatory discussed and raised.

## 2.2 Certification Regime

Individuals subject to the Certification Regime are not approved by the regulators; rather firms make an assessment that they are deemed 'fit and proper' to perform their role and provide them with an annual 'certificate' that they can perform their role.

There are different approaches taken by the PRA and the FCA due to their statutory objectives. The PRA approach limits the scope to 'material risk takers' (MRTs) as defined in the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR) (ie, individuals who are subject to the Remuneration Code – Code staff) while the FCA approach includes individuals performing their function from within the UK or dealing with a client in the UK who:

- would previously have been performing a **significant influence function (SIF)** role under APER, but who now are outside the SMR – as they are not an SMF (eg, CASS oversight, benchmark submitter, or administrators, proprietary traders and previous CF29 significant manager function-holders)

- are performing a significant harm function, including engaged in wholesale activities on the basis of dealing as principle or agent (traders, including Algorithm traders) and bring about deals in investments (sales staff)

- are in customer-facing roles which are subject to qualification requirements

- supervise or manage a Certified Person, where they are not an SMF

- the individual is employed by a UK bank, and/or the UK branch of a third country bank

- for third country banks, the individual is carrying out 'branch activity' as defined by the FCA.

Therefore, the PRA and the FCA require the responsibility for assessing the 'fitness and propriety' of staff who perform and/or carry out 'significant harm' functions to firms.

Significant-harm functions include:

- CASS oversight

- benchmark submission and administration

- proprietary trader

- significant management

- functions requiring qualifications

- managers of certification employees

- material risk takers.

The term 'material risk taker' is defined in the PRA Remuneration Rulebook as an individual whose activities, carried out on behalf of the firm, could have a material impact on the firm's risk profile.

Firms must provide a 'certificate' to individuals annually which states that they (the firm) are satisfied that the individual is 'fit and proper' to perform the function, it will also set out their role and responsibilities.

Where individuals are performing multiple certification functions – fitness and propriety must be assessed for each certification function, however a single certificate can cover the multiple functions being performed.

Where no certificate is issued to an individual, because they are deemed not to meet the 'fitness and propriety' text, then the firm must give written notice explaining what steps (if any) they propose to take, as well as notifying the FCA of the circumstances.

The Certification Regime could impact overseas employees of UK firms. Firstly, to individuals deemed to be 'material risk takers' (or 'Code' staff) for the firm due to their role, no matter where they are located, and secondly, where an individual overseas is dealing with, or has contact with, UK clients. Where an individual is based overseas and does not deal with UK clients and is performing another of the Certification functions (other than material risk takers) then they will not be a certified person.

The term 'code staff' refers to employees whose activities have a material impact on the firm's risk profile.

## 2.3 Conduct Rules

The FCA approach to the Conduct Rules means that all UK employees (with the exception of staff carrying out purely ancillary functions, such as cleaners and catering staff) will be within the regulatory scope.

The PRA, on the other hand, applied the Conduct Rules to senior managers and individuals who fall within the PRA Certification regime (material risk-takers).

By applying the Conduct Rules to all levels within a firm, the FCA is trying to embed a grass roots understanding of what acceptable and unacceptable behaviour is. Conduct Rules appear to be the FCA's key tool in reinforcing the importance of appropriate values and behaviours at all levels within firms.

Therefore, firms are obliged to make all staff aware of their obligations and provide training on how the rules apply. Firms must notify the regulators when there has been a breach of the Conduct Rules.

The FCA applied the six individual Conduct Rules to standard NEDs and the additional 'Senior Manager' Conduct Rule.

## First Tier – Individual Conduct Rules – PRA and FCA

Rule 1 You must act with integrity. Rule 2 You must act with due skill, care and diligence. Rule 3 You must be open and cooperative with the FCA, the PRA and other regulators.

## Individual Conduct Rules – FCA only

Rule 4 You must pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly. Rule 5 You must observe proper standards of market conduct. Rule 6 You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers.

Individual Conduct Rule 6 will not apply to individuals and/or firms that are not subject to Consumer Duty, meaning that Individual Conduct Rule 4 will continue to apply.

## Second Tier – Senior Manager Conduct Rules – PRA and FCA

SC1 You must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business of the firm for which you are responsible is controlled effectively.

- SC2 You must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business of the firm for which you are responsible complies with relevant requirements and standards of the regulatory system.

- SC3 You must take reasonable steps to ensure that any delegation of your responsibilities is to an appropriate person and that you oversee the discharge of the delegated responsibility effectively.

- SC4 You must disclose appropriately any information of which the FCA or the PRA would reasonably expect notice.

## 2.4 Regulatory References

A firm subject to the SM&CR must obtain a regulatory reference before it appoints an individual to a senior management function (SMF) or to perform a role that would be subject to the Certification Regime.

Where a firm receives a request to provide a regulatory reference, due to an ex-employee moving to a new firm, they are obliged to provide such reference and confirm if they consider the individual to be 'fit and proper'. They are only required to disclose instances of conduct that occurred or existed for six years from the date of the reference request being received.

The FCA developed a template for firms to use when requesting a regulatory reference, which can be found in SYSC 22, Annex 1 (Template for regulatory references given by SM&CR firms and disclosure requirements)]

## 2.5 The Directory

The FCA Financial Services Register includes the 'Directory', providing a single central location for information on individuals subject to either the APER or the SM&CR. The register is updated by the FCA and/or the PRA for senior managers, whereas individuals subject to the Certification Regime would be managed by the firms themselves.

One of the objectives of the register is to reduce potential harm to consumers, strengthen market integrity and enhance competition.

The register makes:

- information public on additional individuals carrying out a wider range of roles (including those not approved such as financial advisers, traders, portfolio managers and additional directors)

- information public on the individuals and the senior managers the FCA continues to approve in a way that is more accessible and user friendly, and

- enables users to find information on these individuals in a single public location.

The information is available in an aggregated form that can be extracted free of charge. Customers, firms and other stakeholders are able to use the Directory to easily find and check the identity of relevant individuals.

The register allows customers to benefit from greater information to make informed decisions on who to do business with, using the following features:

- **Workplace location** – list of individuals active in specific areas.

- **Business qualified to carry on** – list of individuals who hold appropriate qualifications, for example, to provide mortgage advice.

- **Regulatory sanctions and prohibitions** – the facility to check whether individuals are prohibited by the FCA.

## 2.6 Review of the SM&CR

In 2020, the PRA published a report on its first review of the implementation and application of the SM&CR to PRA firms (ie, deposit taking banks and certain insurance companies) in the period 2019 to 2020.

The PRA identified three 'key' themes and follow up actions and recommendations for each of them:

- **Theme 1 – Holding individuals to account through the SM&CR**

   - **Conduct and regulatory references** – ensure regulatory references are used in an appropriate manner.

   - **Remuneration** – articulate the link between the SM&CR and remuneration adjustments.

   - **Senior manager expectations** – underline the responsibility of prescribed responsibilities allocated to SMFS and Certified individuals.

- **Theme 2 – Myth busting and clarifying expectations**

   - **Diversity** – reaffirm the appetite for diverse skills and experience among senior management.

   - **Collective accountability** – further analysis on board responsibilities and individual's accountability to see if they are mutually reinforcing.

   - **Interim appointments** – clarify (along with the FCA) expectations where a senior manager takes temporary leave for longer than 12 weeks.

- **Theme 3 – Application of the SM&CR to different business models**

   - **Allocation of responsibilities** – use of 'Head of Key Business Area'; the way in which designation of certain individuals works in conjunction with the SM&CR; further guidance on allocating prescribed responsibilities and the potential for smaller firms to submit SM&CR documentation less frequently.

## 2.7 HM Treasury Paper – Proposals for a 'SM&CR' for Financial Market Infrastructures

In 2021, HM Treasury published a consultation paper setting out its intentions to create, and apply, a SM&CR regime for Financial Market Infrastructures (FMI) which they define as Central Clearing Counterparties, Central Securities Depositories and Payment systems (recognised under the Banking Act 2009).

The key features of the proposed SM&CR for FMIs are intended to be similar to the existing SM&CR regime, as set out in Part 5 of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 ('FSMA'), but modified to recognise the fact that FMIs are not authorised persons within the FSMA meaning of the term.

The Bank of England (BoE) would be granted new powers to implement, supervise and enforce the following:

- A **Senior Managers Regime** – this would give the BoE the power to determine whether individuals who perform roles that pose a potential risk to financial stability or to the continuing functioning of the FMI have the appropriate competence, expertise and probity to carry out their roles.

- A **Certification Regime** – this would require firms to certify any individual who performs a 'specified function' that could cause significant harm to the FMI or its users as fit and proper, both at recruitment and annually thereafter.

- **Conduct Rules** for all employees, which set minimum, high-level requirements regarding the conduct of individuals where necessary or expedient for advancing the BoE's financial stability objective.

The proposed regime would strengthen the individual accountability of senior managers within FMIs and would provide an effective and proportionate means of ensuring high standards of conduct among all staff. This would ensure that the firms which underpin the proper functioning and overall stability of the UK's financial system are subject to the highest regulatory standards.

In June 2022, HM Treasury published an update on responses received to the consultation and the next steps. HM Treasury noted that they will proceed with an SM&CR for CCPs and CSDs with particular details to be set out in secondary legislation and the FCA rules.

The government intends to have the option to extend the SM&CR to certain other systemic financial services entities in the future (credit rating agencies and recognised investment exchanges) should it decide that it is appropriate to apply the SM&CR to them. Therefore, they will legislate to create a new SM&CR 'gateway', when parliamentary time allows, which will enable HM Treasury to lay statutory instruments to apply the SM&CR to CCPs, CSDs, and, in the future, potentially to other financial services entities.

The government also intends to legislate to implement the SM&CR for recognised payment systems and specified service providers. However, they noted that will be taken forward separately to the creation of the 'gateway' and will have a longer timeframe.

Please refer to chapter 5, section 3 for details on the SM&CR review, which was contained within the Edinburgh Reforms announced in December 2022.
