## 6. Principles for Businesses (PRIN)

The FCA Handbook includes 12 key Principles for Businesses, which authorised firms must observe. The principles apply with respect to the carrying out of regulated activities, activities that include dealing in investments as principal, ancillary activities in relation to designated investment business, home finance activity, insurance mediation activity, accepting deposits, and the communication and approval of financial promotions.

If a firm breaches any of the principles that apply to it, it will be liable to disciplinary sanctions. However, the FCA and the PRA must be able to prove that the firm has been at fault.

The FCA also pursues an initiative called Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC), to encourage firms to adopt a more ethical frame of mind within the industry, leading to more ethical behaviour at every stage of a firm's relationship with its customers. Firms and customers (ie, professional clients) that are not subject to the Consumer Duty requirements, as noted below, will continue to comply with and follow the FTOC initiative.

The principles are a general statement of the fundamental obligations of firms under the regulatory system.

To reiterate, the 12 Principles for Businesses are as follows:

1. **Integrity** – a firm must conduct its business with integrity.

2. **Skill, care and diligence** – a firm must conduct its business with due skill, care and diligence.

3. **Management and control** – a firm must take reasonable care to organise and control its affairs responsibly and effectively, with adequate risk management systems.

4. **Financial prudence** – a firm must maintain adequate financial resources.

5. **Market conduct** – a firm must observe proper standards of market conduct.

6. **Customer's interest** – a firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly.

7. **Communications with clients** – a firm must pay due regard to the information needs of its clients and communicate information to them in a way which is clear, fair and not misleading.

8. **Conflicts of interest** – a firm must manage conflicts of interest fairly, both between itself and its customers, and between a customer and another client.

9. **Customers: relationships of trust** – a firm must take reasonable care to ensure the suitability of its advice and discretionary decisions for any customer who is entitled to rely upon its judgement.

10. **Clients' assets** – a firm must arrange adequate protection for clients' assets when it is responsible for them.

11. **Relations with regulators** – a firm must deal with its regulators in an open and cooperative way, and must disclose to the FCA and/or the PRA appropriately anything relating to the firm of which the FCA/PRA would reasonably expect notice.

12. **Consumer Duty** – a firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers.

## 6.1 Principle 12

With the implementation of the Consumer Duty, in scope firms must comply with Principle 12, which means that Principles 6 and 7 will cease to apply. Therefore, firms not in scope of the Consumer Duty will continue to comply with Principles 6 and 7.

The Consumer Duty requirements state that a firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail consumers, with firms needing to consider the characteristics and objectives of their customer as well as being able to understand and evidence whether those outcomes are being met/have been met.

As noted in section 1.3 of this chapter, the FCA's outcomes-focused regulatory approach seeks to achieve the desired result for the consumer and markets. However, this requires active participation and support by firms' directors and employees at all levels to achieve effective corporate governance. Within firms, this is more prominent now that the SM&CR applies to most regulated firms.

In 2021, the FCA published two consultation papers setting out their aims for the Consumer Duty, an initiative aimed at ensuring that firms provide and offer good outcomes to retail customers. This is not the first time that the FCA (or its predecessor, the FSA) has developed an initiative aimed at retail customers/consumers – we have had the Treating Customers Fairly (TCF) and, currently, the Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC). What is different this time is that the FCA requirements on Consumer Duty include a new Principle and high-level rules.

One of the main differences between the Consumer Duty and the 'FTOC' is the focus by the FCA regarding the prevention of firms causing foreseeable harm to consumers.

## Aims of the Consumer Duty

The FCA's aim, and objective, is to ensure retail customers receive good outcomes when they purchase products or services. In addition to the introduction of the Consumer Duty Principle, there are crosscutting rules and 'good' outcomes rules and guidance that provide greater clarity to both retail customers (in terms of their expectations) and to firms in the scope of the requirements.

## Implementation Date

The rules came into effect in July 2023 for new and existing products or services that are capable of being sold, open or available for renewal. Where products or services are closed (ie, they are not available to be purchased or accept new investment), the rules, subject to a delayed implementation, came into effect on 31 July 2024.

## Scope

The Consumer Duty applies to products and services offered to retail customers (or consumers).

The Consumer Duty does not apply just to existing customers; it also applies to prospective customers. The finalised guidance, published alongside the rules, provides additional clarity that unless there are regulatory obligations or legal contracts that exist, a firm is only responsible for its own activities and actions – they would not be responsible for and have to oversee the actions of other firms in a 'distribution' chain.

Products or services that have not been developed (and targeted) for retail customers are not in the scope of the Consumer Duty, where they:

- are only marketed and approved for distribution to non-retail customers (ie, professional clients), and

- are not provided to another firm under an arrangement (ie, distribution agreement) between them as part of a distribution chain for a retail product or service.

Attempts by firms to incorrectly classify a product or service as non-retail with the aim of avoiding the Consumer Duty, allowing them to distribute to retail customers, would be in breach of the Consumer Duty.

The Consumer Duty introduced obligations on firms acting under the 'agent as client' rules, which these firms need to ascertain if retail customers are at the end of the chain and whether they can determine or materiality influence the outcomes for these retail customers. While it is easy to ascertain and understand where firms are providing a product or service to a retail customer who is a direct client of the firm, it is less clear how the Consumer Duty applies to the wholesale market and firms who operate without having any direct retail clients. The first question to ask is: are they a direct client of the firm, and if they are not, can the firm have the potential to impact retail customers by being involved in a distribution chain and their actions (when manufacturing products) determine or materially influence retail customer outcomes?

For example, it will apply when a wholesale firm could influence or determine material aspects of:

- the design or operation of retail products or services, including their price and value

- the distribution of retail products or services

- preparing and approving communications that are to be marketed to retail customers, and

- engaging in customer support for retail customers.

Where the Consumer Duty does apply, it would be applied on a proportionate basis where the firm has material influence over retail customer outcomes. This will mean that a firm that is remote from retail customers, with no direct client relationship, will have a limited obligation.

The following wholesale activities are excluded from the Consumer Duty, where a firm:

- Manufactures products or services only for wholesale purposes – subject to, meeting the conditions in the 'retail market business' definition including activities relating to non-retail financial instruments.

- Marketing materials for a financial instrument (including the prospectus) feature prominent and clear disclosures to the effect that the financial instrument is:

   - only being offered to investors eligible for categorisation as professional clients or eligible counterparties under the FCA's rules, and

   - not intended for retail customers.

- The issuer of a financial instrument or, in relation to secondary market offers, the distributor, has taken reasonable steps to ensure that the offer and any associated promotional communications are directed only to investors eligible for categorisation as professional clients or eligible counterparties.

- • A minimum denomination, or otherwise a minimum investment of £50,000 applies to the financial instrument:

   - this exclusion does not apply to the sale of investment funds where an amount greater than £50,000 is invested.

- Market activities for certain financial instruments meeting the criteria in the 'retail market business' definition.

- Activities relating to insurance contracts of large risks for commercial customers or where the risk is located outside the UK.

- Activities connected to the distribution of group insurance policies or the extension of these policies to new members.

- The regulated activity of administering a benchmark, any ancillary activity to that activity and any activities undertaken by a benchmark administrator for the purpose of complying with the Benchmarks Regulation.

The Consumer Duty will only apply to firms conducting regulated activity in the UK.

## 6.1.1 The Consumer Duty Principle

Principle 12 states that _'a firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers'_ , and where this Principle applies, firms will no longer be subject to Principle 6 (Customers interests) and Principle 7 (Communication with clients).

Therefore, firms not subject to the Consumer Duty will continue to ensure they comply with Principles 6 and 7 and be subject to the FTOC initiative.

Where a firm's activities and business model mean that they engage with clients that are both in and out of the scope of the Consumer Duty, then they must comply with the relevant requirements based on the services and products which they are providing to the different types of customers for each activity (product/service) undertaken.

This will present operational challenges for firms that provide products and services to retail customers and non-retail customers (ie, professional clients).

It would be up to firms if they wanted to apply the Consumer Duty standards to their business activities that are not in the scope to ensure that have a single operating model. This would be something for firms to decide based on operating different standards, procedures, and policies for the products and services that they provide to all customers.

## Cross-Cutting Rules

These rules, and the accompanying guidance, sit within the Principles for Businesses Sourcebook (PRIN) to give the Consumer Duty emphasis and teeth, providing firms with formal requirements to comply with.

The FCA expects firms to display the following key behaviours when providing products and/or services that are in the scope of the Consumer Duty:

- **Act in good faith towards retail customers**

   - Standard of conduct characterised by honest, fair and open dealings.

   - Acting consistently with the reasonable expectations of retail customers.

   - Firms should consider this rule at the product or service design stage.

- **Avoid foreseeable harm**

   - Firms must ensure that retail customers understand and accept the inherent risk in products.

   - Firms do not have a responsibility to protect customers from all foreseeable harm:

      - customers must understand and accept inherent risks associated with products – firms must be clear and transparent.

   - Firms must take proactive steps to avoid causing 'foreseeable harm' where it is within their control.

   - Firms do not exploit customers' vulnerabilities, behavioural biases or lack of knowledge (financial literacy):

      - this does not mean that customers must be protected from all bad outcomes.

- **Enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives**

   - Support customers in order that they have the relevant information and understanding to be responsible for making their own decisions:

      - they need to be (should be) empowered to make choices for themselves and remain ultimately responsible for their decisions, actions and the consequences of these.

   - Firms have a responsibility to establish an environment in which customers have trust and faith in the firm and their products and services that they are purchasing.

   - Firms can assume and rely on the information provided to them by the retail customer as part of that due diligence where advisory or discretionary services are provided:

      - for execution-only or non-advised services, the firm can assume that the financial objectives of retail customers are to purchase, use and enjoy the product being purchased.

For each of the cross-cutting rules, firms need to assess and take into account the financial understanding (knowledge and awareness) of the target market audience, ensuring they are providing clear and transparent support where necessary.

## The 'Good' Outcomes the FCA Expects to See

-

## **Products and services outcome**

- Products and services for customers are fit for purpose and designed to meet the needs and objectives of the target market audience (group).

- The Consumer Duty interacts with the requirements in the **Product Intervention and Product Governance Sourcebook (PROD)** with regards to manufacturers and distributors of products. (PROD is covered in greater detail in section 14.11 of this chapter).

- Product providers (manufactures) are:

   - responsible for ensuring that a product and/or service is designed to meet a specific need or objective of an identified target market audience, and

   - required to carry out regular reviews of how a product is performing.

- Product distributors would be:

   - responsible for distributing and/or marketing a product in a way that focuses on and reflects its identified target market audience, and

   - responsible for taking all reasonable steps to prevent a product being offered to customers outside the intended target market audience.

Where multiple firms are involved in the distribution chain, each firm is required to take responsibility for the outcome in a manner that reflects its ability to influence choices about product design, operation and distribution and the potential to materially affect retail customers receiving (achieving) good outcomes.

-

## **Price and value outcome**

   - The price of products represents 'fair' value for customers in the target market/group, ie, who the product was developed to be marketed/sold to).

   - While the FCA is not a price regulator, it expects firms to offer products and services that represent fair value.

   - The FCA has been very clear that fair value does not mean that all retail consumers will achieve the same outcomes; those that shop around will have greater confidence to do so knowing that products and services will meet their needs and will represent better value than an existing provider.

   - Firms do not have to price products the same for new and existing retail customers, but they will need to be able to demonstrate that any discounts for new retail customers must be communicated clearly to both groups of customers to ensure that both could and/or are receiving fair value.

   - Value is not just the price paid by the customer for a product or service sold by a firm:

      - firms must ensure that there is a relationship between the price paid and the benefit that the customer receives from it.

- **Customer services outcome**

   - Firms are expected to provide an appropriate level of service that meets customers' needs throughout their relationship.

   - Firms must ensure that communications are fair, clear and not misleading, so that customers can make informed decisions about products and services.

   - Firms must present information in a manner that will be understood by customers.

      - Firms need to understand the 'financial awareness' of the target market audience.

- **Consumer understanding outcome**

   - Firms must communicate with customers in a consistent manner, so that they are able to make informed decisions about purchasing financial products or services.

   - The FCA expects firms to provide the required information at the right time and presented in a way that is clear and easily understood, enabling customers to be able to understand and assess:

      - the features of products and services

      - the likely costs, associated risks and the benefits from specific products or services, and

      - whether products and services will meet their financial needs and objectives.

- Firms need to consider who the target market audience is and use this to design their marketing material and information:

   - firms should put themselves in the shoes of the retail customers and sense check that the information that they are providing is aimed at the right level in terms of details/information and understanding.

## Firms Ongoing Obligations

## **Monitoring and Governance**

A firm's board or equivalent management/governing body will be held accountable by the FCA for implementation of the Consumer Duty requirements. They will also be responsible for approving and agreeing where a firm's requirements are not in the scope of the Consumer Duty – ensuring documentation is sufficient to determine the rationale why they are out of the scope.

A firm's board (or equivalent) must be provided with an annual report that assesses whether the firm is delivering good outcomes for its retail customers – consistent with the requirements of the Consumer Duty.

## **Culture**

The aims and objectives of the Consumer Duty can only be achieved if, within firms, there are changes made to their culture, behaviour and processes – which start at the top.

The outcomes of the Consumer Duty should be reflected in firm's strategy, governance and leadership. It is the responsibility of a firm's board (or equivalent management or governing body) and senior management to embed a culture where good outcomes for retail customers are achieved.

## **Accountability**

As noted in section 2 (SM&CR) of this chapter, the FCA introduced a Conduct Rule for firms in the scope of the Duty. Conduct Rule 6 introduces the _'You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers'_ for such firms.

The FCA will hold senior managers accountable for implementing and embedding the Consumer Duty within the firm via the SM&CR – therefore, firms must make sure that senior managers are clear about what they are accountable and responsible for and how they ensure the firms comply with the requirements of the Consumer Duty on an ongoing basis.

The FCA expected a senior individual to be the firm's Consumer Duty 'champion' (which could be a nonexecutive director or a senior manager depending on the type and size of the firm). These requirements were removed from February 2025, with the FCA no longer requiring firms to have a Consumer Duty champion. They did note, however, that firms can retain this role if they wish to do so.

## **What does this mean to Retail Consumers?**

Where firms meet their obligations, then retail consumers should (will) be confident, and should (will) be able to trust, in the following:

- That the firm is acting in good faith, in line with their interests.

- They can choose from a range of products and services that are fit for purpose – designed to meet their needs (of the target market audience).

- Information provided is clear and understandable, enabling them (the customer) to assess which products and services are most likely to meet their needs and objectives.

- Products and services deliver the benefits that they (the customer) reasonably expect them to.

- The customer service standards applied by firms consistently meet their (the customers') needs and expectations.

- They will be offered/will obtain fair value from financial products and services.

However, the Consumer Duty does not remove retail customers' responsibility for making decisions or prevent them from making decisions that are not in their own interests. Therefore, from a retail customer's point of view, it does not:

- require firms to provide all retail customers with the same terms or outcomes from products or services

- impose an open-ended duty that goes beyond the scope of the firm's role and its ability to determine or influence consumer outcomes

- protect retail customers from all potential harms

- specify exactly how firms should act, and

- apply retrospectively to past business (ie, the rules and requirements applied from 31 July 2023).

The Consumer Duty does not require firms to provide any particular product or service on particular terms or to particular retail customers – that remains a commercial decision for firms.

Also, the Consumer Duty does not prevent a firm from withdrawing a product or service. However, where a firm is planning to alter or withdraw a product or service, then it must consider whether this could lead to foreseeable harm for their retail customers or specific groups of retail customers – taking steps to mitigate the impact of this potential harm to those who could be affected.

## 6.1.2 The FCA's Approach to the Consumer Duty

By embedding the Duty within their authorisation, supervision and enforcement operations, the FCA believes that the Duty will set a higher standard of care for retail customers, with firms needing to focus on delivering good outcomes for their retail customers.

The FCA expected firms to have robust implementation plans. A firm's board (or equivalent) is responsible for oversight of the implementation and the ongoing compliance with the requirements.

## 6.1.3 FCA Observations on Consumer Duty Implementation

In February 2024, the FCA published additional information/guidance for firms regarding implementation in terms of good practice and where they see areas for improvement. This built on the work undertaken by the FCA when the Consumer Duty came into effect, and follows the FCA's review of firms 'implementation plans' and 'fair value frameworks'.

## Culture, Governance and Monitoring

FCA expected outcomes:

_'consumers to have confidence in retail financial services markets, with healthy competition based on high standards and firms focused on delivering good customer outcomes'._

## **Good Practice**

- Firms alter their company purpose to signal to staff that their actions and behaviour should focus on customer outcomes.

- Firms accelerate business changes to deliver better customer outcomes.

   - for example, some firms have paid more attention to service level metrics for call abandonment rates, root-cause analysis of complaints and customer satisfaction surveys, taking action to improve standards.

- Firms ensure responsibility for good customer outcomes is understood and owned across the business – not just by risk and compliance teams but by those involved from product design through to post-sale support.

- Firms increase focus on the customer at Board level, with firms' senior leadership teams giving serious consideration to what the Consumer Duty means for them at a practical and cultural level.

- Firms update staff bonus structures to ensure that incentivisation is in line with the aims of the Consumer Duty.

- Firms develop new data and metrics to better understand their customers.

   - introduce appropriate governance so that action is taken where problems are identified.

## **Areas for Improvement**

- Firms need to ensure that the focus on good customer outcomes is understood at all levels, in their strategies, leadership, and people policies.

   - where the Consumer Duty is primarily driven by programme teams or risk and compliance colleagues, and isn't discussed at Board level.

- Firms wait to see if the FCA will intervene to address an issue – rather than tackling it themselves.

   - this is likely to cause firms more complexity in the long run, especially if consumer redress becomes due.

   - the Consumer Duty requires firms to proactively identify and address issues and risks of harm.

- Firms need better data and monitoring strategies, they should not be complacent and assume that they can just repackage existing data.

   - the FCA expects firms to think seriously about what information they need to really understand their customers' outcomes and issues they may be facing.

## Consumers in Vulnerable Circumstances

FCA expected outcomes:

_'vulnerable consumers to have outcomes as good as other consumers'._

## **Good Practice**

- Firms consider the needs of customers with characteristics of vulnerability as part of product and service design.

   - for example, some firms have found different ways of supporting those who are elderly, such as by using 'selfies' for identity verification to confirm they are not being scammed.

      - fully review their approach, systems and processes, centralising operations relating to vulnerability. This has resulted in improved and more consistent: handling of vulnerable customers by specialist staff

      - capturing of data and identification of trends

      - quality assurance and compliance monitoring to make processes more efficient, effective, and focused on customer outcomes

      - outcomes for individual customers due to better-quality interactions with firms.

- Firms review their communications and make changes to support vulnerable customers.

   - for example, offering literature in alternative formats (such as braille, audio or with enlarged text) and making typographical changes so literature is more engaging (such as by using different colours or fonts)

   - firms have considered whether information is appropriate to customer groups with lower financial literacy or where English is not their first language.

- Firms use customer data, such as problematic trading patterns, to identify potential vulnerabilities. This information is passed to staff trained to address sensitive issues, such as gambling addiction, and appropriate communications are sent to those customers.

- Firms are improving the way they capture and record information about customer vulnerabilities or expand support to better meet customer needs.

   - for example, introducing a data flag to identify and monitor outcomes for customers who have requested documents in an alternative format

   - for example, a mortgage firm now allows brokers to pass on details about customers who may need help managing their home loans.

- Firms turn off productivity targets for customer service staff if someone is identified as vulnerable.

   - this encourages staff to take time to provide a bespoke service catered to that customer's needs.

## **Areas for Improvement**

- Firms fail to address identified weaknesses in processes to track vulnerable customers across multiple product sets and gaps in data and servicing capabilities. Firms should be able to identify where particular groups of customers – especially those that are vulnerable – receive poorer outcomes than other customers and take action to address this.

- Firms operating in the investment market, do not prioritise identification of and support for vulnerable customers. In some cases, this does not even appear to have been considered.

   - for example, our recent wealth data survey showed 49% of portfolio managers and 69% of stockbrokers identified no vulnerable customers among their customer base

   - FCA recent 'Dear CEO letter' to wealth and stockbroking firms pointed out that 50% of us will be classed as vulnerable at some point

   - this means it is likely that every firm will at some point deal with customers in vulnerable circumstances. We are concerned that firms are not thinking widely enough on vulnerability.

- Firms automatically assess all consumers over a certain age as vulnerable. While age is relevant to vulnerability, a generalised approach risks firms not tailoring support to reflect individual needs.

- Firms ask consumers to identify themselves as vulnerable and then unnecessarily requesting evidence of this.

   - evidence of firms telling those who identify as vulnerable that it might affect their ability to receive the service. This is a barrier to identifying vulnerability and ensuring consumers get the support they need.

- Firms asking consumers to repeatedly disclose their additional needs or personal circumstances when passed between teams.

   - firms must avoid causing foreseeable harm

   - this includes the impact of being asked to disclose their personal circumstances on a consumer's mental wellbeing and ability to engage with their provider.

## Products and Services

FCA expected outcomes

_'consumers to be sold products and services that are designed to meet their needs, characteristics and objectives'._

The FCA noted that they had seen harm where products or services were poorly designed or were distributed widely to customers for who they were not designed (ie, not the target market audience).

## **Good Practice**

- Firms carefully and precisely define the target market for products and services that could cause harm if sold to the wrong consumers. Firms have then ensured those products and services are sold correctly.

   - for example, redesigning sales journeys to be clearer on the types of customers and needs products are appropriate for. Others have introduced new products to meet different customer needs.

- Firms redefine data and monitoring capabilities to track customers who may have bought a product or service despite falling outside of the target market.

   - this allows firms to contact them or take other measures to avoid consumer harm.

- Firms Adapt products and services to deliver additional benefits for their customers.

   - for example, by introducing controls to limit exposure to financial loss and avoid the risk of foreseeable harm to customers.

- Firms identify products and services that were only available to customers through limited sales channels, and make the product available more widely where they meet the needs of a broader range of their customer base.

- Firms make positive changes to their product development processes, with greater focus on how new products will meet the needs of a specified target market and deliver good outcomes for them.

   - for example, one firm introduced '11 golden rules' for designing new products linked to Consumer Duty requirements

   - some firms have increased emphasis on consumer research and testing to help ensure products work well.

- Firms reconsider their role in 'distribution' chains and take steps to support good outcomes for the customer even if they do not have direct relationships with consumers

   - an investment firm identified that it had a small number of institutional clients with retail customers in the chain and offered alternative investment options more suited to them.

- Firms simplify their product offering so there are not products with similar or overlapping features, where this benefits customers

   - this makes it quicker and easier for consumers to identify and access the products they need.

## **Areas for Improvement**

- Firms do not share information effectively across supply chains. The FCA expects firms in the same distribution chain to share relevant information with each other. This will help firms to quickly address issues to prevent consumer harm and deliver good outcomes.

   - for example, manufacturer firms need to inform distributors of the characteristics of a product or service, its target market and the value it is intended to provide to customers

   - firms support manufacturers reviewing a product or service, distributors need to provide relevant information to them

   - firms in a distribution chain should consider what information they need from each other, with the joint goal of delivering good outcomes for the customer.

- Firms do not pay close enough attention to ensuring that their distribution strategies are driving good customer outcomes. In general, firms are responsible only for their own activities and do not need to oversee the actions of other firms in the distribution chain.

   - however, in some situations, firms do need to consider actions by other parties in the distribution chain.

   - for example, a manufacturer firm must consider how it expects a product to be sold and regularly monitor the product and its distribution over time so that it can deliver good customer outcomes.

      - for instance, if a lender finds third-party brokers are not distributing its loans in line with its intended distribution strategy and target market, it must consider appropriate action.

   - while the lender is not responsible for the activities of third-party brokers, it may, for example, need to provide additional information to brokers to ensure the loans reach the right borrowers. Again, this type of situation is unlikely to arise where firms are working well together.

- Firms fail to grasp that they might have a role in a distribution chain, what that role is, and what it means for their responsibilities.

   - for example, where a firm has a material influence over retail customer outcomes but is in a distribution chain and does not have a direct relationship with the end retail customers, it will still be subject to the Consumer Duty.

## Price and Value

FCA expected outcomes:

_'consumers to get products and services which offer fair value'._

## **Good Practice**

- Firms examine whether the total cost to consumers of their products and services – including fees, charges and other costs – provides fair value relative to their benefits.

- Firms have made changes to improve their value proposition by reducing costs for consumers by:

   - updating pricing models for products and services

      - for example, reducing the rate of interest paid on certain credit products and/or for certain types of customers

   - reducing or removing charges for certain products or ongoing services where these were deemed too high relative to the benefits provided

   - putting controls in place for certain groups or customers, for whom charges over a certain amount do not offer fair value, to improve value and remove these charges

   - moving some investment clients away from more expensive bespoke models to simpler model portfolios, where they are better suited to the size of the customer's investment.

- Firms enhance the benefits of products and services to improve value, For example, by:

   - increasing the amount of interest paid for certain savings and investment products

   - improving the benefits offered by products and services

   - the FCA noted that they had seen some insurance firms enhance product cover to include new benefits, such as physiotherapy and virtual medical care, widen the definition of certain terms in policy wordings to cover more eventualities, or increase product cover limits at no additional cost.

- Firms cap fees for long-term clients and waive fees entirely where firms could not justify the product's cost.

- Firms consider whether the customer receives fair value across the distribution chain.

   - for example, firms no longer recommending an intermediary where the amount they charge is above a certain level or ending agreements with distributors where they are concerned about value to the customer

   - other firms have restricted the ability of distributors to mark-up pricing or have capped commission payments to them.

- Firms take steps to monitor or close products where their value proposition risks causing poor customer outcomes.

## **Areas for Improvement**

- Firms fail to show that products offer fair value to retail customers.

   - for example, some firms have relied solely on an assessment of similar product offerings in a market. This alone does not prove that the customer is getting a good deal

   - the FCA noted that they had seen statements being made about value, without any qualitative reasoning outlining why a firm considers that its product offers fair value.

- Firms unable to justify what benefits they provide for the remuneration they receive.

   - the FCA expect firms to be able to explain their remuneration practices and how they are proportionate to the work they do.

- Firms add fees along the distribution chain that might mean the overall cost to the end consumer does not represent fair value.

   - this is likely to be particularly relevant where there are long or complex distribution chains with multiple fees added by multiple parties.

- Firms charge customers for a service they are not benefiting from.

   - some firms are providing – and charging for – ongoing services to customers who do not need it

   - in the worst examples, some customers are paying for a service, such as an annual review, and not receiving it.

- Firms structure investment products and services in a way that benefits firms rather than focusing on value for the customer.

   - for example, some firms are failing to consider the amounts customers invest and the value they receive for the risks they are taking

   - the FCA noted that they had seen value assessments that are disproportionately firm focused without being viewed through the lens of the customer.

- Firms fail to share sufficient information to enable other firms in the distribution chain to properly assess value to the end retail customer or understand what the firm is doing where it considers a product does not provide fair value.

## Consumer Understanding

FCA expected outcomes:

_'consumers to understand the information they are given and make timely and informed decisions'._

## **Good Practice**

- Firms work with experts to improve their communications across different channels and increase customers' understanding of their products and services. For example by:

   - changing the layout and presentation of content to improve clarity and boost customer understanding

   - simplifying the language used, with one firm rolling out a 'jargon buster' library across its business to help simplify documents

   - improving the accessibility of websites, making it easier for customers to navigate to contact details for further support

   - a firm added a new email address to its 'contact us' webpage and now offers outbound calls in some cases, including to support vulnerable customers.

- Firms develop customer understanding frameworks to support good outcomes.

   - for example, one firm considered how the way it marketed its loans could exploit customers' behavioural biases. They then took steps to provide more balanced information and moderate its incentivisation to customers with higher risk profiles.

- Firms design customer journeys, focusing on how consumers behave in practice and risks of harm, to better support understanding. For example:

   - identifying where customers could be subject to fees and charges and adding 'in the moment' prompts and push notifications during the customer journey to advise of these costs

   - an insurance firm adapted its approach to highlight its policies' exclusions on its website before customers start their application, so that it is clear up front what the policy does not cover

   - a bank added a webpage with a clear list of what's included with its current account product, as well as an equally prominent list of what isn't.

- Firms update customer interaction points and materials, such as call centre scripts, and deliver training so that staff are better equipped to support customer understanding.

- Firms are more proactive with their communications to support better customer outcomes.

   - for example, the FCA have seen firms use data to identify customers not earning enough interest/cashback to cover product fees.

- Firms develop ways to test customer understanding such as surveys, experiments, and interviews.

   - for example, one funeral plan provider tested 14 documents across its customer journey with customer focus groups to make sure they understand their options to change or cancel their funeral plan. Others have created ways to monitor the impact communications have on behaviour.

## **Areas for Improvement**

- Firms undermine customers' trust by pushing products or services that are too high-risk or complex for them. For example:

   - the FCA noted they had seen investment portfolio managers take advantage of long-established relationships with clients to play down the risks of portfolios which are not aligned to their client's risk profile but benefit the firm.

- Firms being unclear with customers about what charges apply, and when. One way to improve customer understanding is to provide worked examples of product and service costs.

- Firms that approve financial promotions, including those approving promotions for qualifying cryptoassets, make little reference to the Consumer Duty in our interactions with them. Firms need to ensure that they are considering the Duty and its requirements when approving financial promotions .

## Consumer Support

FCA expected outcomes:

_'consumers to be provided with support that meets their needs'._

## **Good Practices**

- Firms make changes to ensure the same level of support offered to new customers is available to existing customers.

- Firms review customer journeys and remove negative obstacles and 'sludge' practices that make it more difficult for customers to act in their own interests.

- the FCA noted that they had seen a consumer credit firm removed certain incentives and changed the way consumers take out credit online, to support better and more active decision making.

- firms made changes to enable customers to exit products through a wider range of channels.

- • Firms introduce positive interventions to customer journeys.

   - for example, a consumer credit firm now directs online customers to speak with an agent to receive tailored support where there are indications they may struggle to keep up with repayments, avoiding customers overstretching and setting up unsustainable repayment amounts

   - other firms have introduced new, flexible repayment plans to support customers struggling to keep up with their credit repayments.

- Firms create additional engagement touchpoints with customers to prompt them to make better decisions.

   - one firm updated its terms and conditions to include a commitment to let customers know if there are savings accounts with a better interest rate.

- Firms that outsource their support offering take steps to ensure this meets the Consumer Duty standard.

   - for example, a firm switched its third-party consumer support provider due to service challenges.

- Firms put in place processes to monitor the support they provide and identify areas for improvement.

   - for example, one firm launched an online panel which enables customers to provide feedback. Customers who join get regular invitations to online surveys where their views shape product and service development

   - one firm identified that individuals who act as Power of Attorney couldn't service all products in the same way customers could; this firm made changes to ensure these individuals now have the same options as the account holder.

## **Area for Improvement**

- Firms do not train staff well enough in terms of having complex conversations with customers

   - the FCA expects firms to train their staff to an appropriate level so they can support good outcomes for their customers, for example by understanding their circumstances and finding appropriate and tailored solutions where needed.

- Firms use business practices akin to 'gamification' on online trading applications platforms.

   - these practices can cause consumer harm by encouraging risky short-term trading, including brokerage deals that may not deliver fair value for customers. Firms must design and deliver customer journeys that support good outcomes.

- Firms do not take the time to understand customer circumstances where they are in financial difficulty.

   - this may mean that customers do not receive a forbearance solution appropriate for their circumstances or advice about other relevant sources of help, including support for customers with characteristics of vulnerability

   - this can lead to non-financial costs, such as stress and unnecessary delays.

- Firms do not have sufficiently robust systems to protect and help consumers from loss of investments, savings or personal data due to fraud or cyber-attacks.

   - the FCA expects firms to have robust systems and controls to avoid causing foreseeable harm to customers, and to provide prompt and appropriate support for victims of fraud.

In April 2025, the FCA reviewed how firms support customers in vulnerable circumstances, focusing on how banks and building societies handled customer bereavement and power of attorney (PoA). The FCA were looking for the outcomes people received, based on the review of cases, firms' governance, management information (MI), staff training and outcome testing.

Overall, the FCA noted that firms in their sample had taken steps to refine their approach to the treatment of customers in vulnerable circumstances since the introduction of the Consumer Duty.

They noted that they saw evidence of good practice in the support provided to customers, and that the _Consumer Duty and Vulnerability Guidance_ has had a positive impact.
