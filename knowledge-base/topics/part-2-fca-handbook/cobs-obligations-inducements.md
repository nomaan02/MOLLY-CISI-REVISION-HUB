## 13. Business Standards: Introduction

The business standards section of the FCA Handbook contains the detailed requirements relating to firms' day-to-day business. It contains the following Sourcebooks:

- **COBS – Conduct of Business Sourcebook** – the conduct of business requirements applying to firms.

- **ICOBS, MCOB, BCOBS, CMCOB and FPCOB** – the requirements applying to insurance firms, firms with mortgage business customers, banking firms, funeral plan providers and claims management companies.

- **MAR – Market Conduct** .

- **CASS – Client Assets** – the requirements relating to holding client assets and client money.

- **PROD** – Product Intervention and Product Governance.

- **ESG – Environmental, Social and Governance** .

The following sections cover the most important of these business standards (COBS and CASS), and the **Disclosure and Transparency Rules (DTR)** , which is part of the 'Listing, Prospectus and Disclosure' section of the Handbook.

## 14. Conduct of Business Sourcebook (COBS), including Product Intervention and Product Governance (PROD)

## 14.1 Application (COBS 1)

Following Brexit, a body of law was created in the UK which is based on EU law, that applied to the UK prior to the ascension of the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018.

'UK MiFID' means 'UK MiFIR' (Markets in Financial Instrument Regulation) together with the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive 2014/65/EU, ('MiFID II') as implemented in the UK by the FCA into its handbook.

Therefore, any reference to 'MiFID' means UK MiFID and UK MiFIR.

The FCA Handbook only applies to UK regulated firms and third country firms undertaking regulated investment business in the UK – ie, carrying out designated investment business in the UK whilst being regulated.

The Conduct of Business Sourcebook (COBS) is contained within the 'Business Standards' section of the FCA Handbook.

The aim of 'COBS' is to move the regulatory approach towards a better focus on outcomes rather than compliance with detailed and prescriptive rules. It also implements the provisions of the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID) and the revised Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II).

The general application rule states that firms are subject to the COBS Sourcebook if they carry on any of a range of activities (designated investment business) from an establishment maintained by them, or their appointed representative, in the UK.

The activities that are covered are:

- designated investment business

- long-term insurance business in relation to life policies, and

- activities relating to the above.

## 14.1.1 Eligible Counterparties

Certain of the COBS rules are disapplied for specific types of activity.

For example, a range of COBS rules are disapplied, in certain cases, for firms carrying on **eligible counterparty (ECP)** business. These include: (details on the counterparties and activities are covered in section 14.3 of this chapter)

- parts of **COBS 2** – the conduct of business obligations (such as inducements, agent as client and reliance on others)

- much of **COBS 4** – communicating with clients (fair, clear and not misleading) – including financial promotions, direct offer promotions and information disclosure requirements before providing services

- **COBS 8** – information on costs and charges, compensation information and client agreements

- **COBS 10** – appropriateness requirements (non-advised services)

- parts of **COBS 11** – **best execution** , client order handling

- parts of **COBS 12** – the labelling of non-independent research, and

- **COBS 16** – reporting information to clients.

As explained above, COBS applies to a firm carrying on the following activities from an establishment maintained by it in the UK, or from an establishment maintained by its appointed representative in the UK. This is called the general application and it is modified depending on the firm's location and activities. For example:

- The rules in COBS that derive from MiFID apply to UK MiFID firms carrying on MiFID business from a UK establishment.

   - Following Brexit, this includes EEA firms operating from a branch in the UK.

- The rules in COBS that derive from MiFID apply to a non-UK MiFID investment firm (ie, a third country firm operating a branch).

## 14.2 Conduct of Business Obligations (COBS 2)

## 14.2.1 Acting Honestly, Fairly and Professionally – 'The Client's Best Interest Rule'

A firm must act honestly, fairly and professionally in accordance with the best interests of its client, at all times when undertaking designated investment business for a retail client, and in relation to MiFID, or equivalent third country business for any other client (ie, professional clients and ECPs).

For a management company, this rule applies in relation to any UCITS scheme or UCITS scheme, the firm manages.

## 14.2.2 Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC)

The requirements for firms subject to the Consumer Duty were discussed in section 6 of this chapter (Principles for Businesses).

The FTOC initiative applies to the provision of services or products to consumers and professional clients who are not subject to the Consumer Duty.

A general theme of overriding fair play runs through the Principles for Businesses. This is coupled with a recognition that there is often an information imbalance between the firm and its customers (since the firm is usually more expert in its products and services than its customers).

Fair treatment has always been one of the regulator's Principles for Businesses. It is embedded in Principle 6 (Customers Interests), and the FTOC initiative is really no more than a clearer way of focusing firms' attention on what really matters.

The regulator has identified six consumer outcomes to explain to firms what 'fair treatment of customers' should look like:

1. Consumers can be confident that they are dealing with firms where the fair treatment of customers is central to the corporate culture.

2. Products and services marketed and sold in the retail market are designed to meet the needs of identified consumer groups and are targeted accordingly.

3. Consumers are provided with clear information and are kept appropriately informed before, during, and after the point-of-sale.

4. When consumers receive advice, the advice is suitable and takes into account their circumstances.

5. Consumers are provided with products that perform as firms have led them to expect, and the associated service is both of an acceptable standard and as they have been led to expect.

6. Consumers do not face unreasonable post-sale barriers imposed by firms to change product, switch provider, submit a claim, or make a complaint.

Firms must have appropriate management information arrangements in place to enable them to monitor their own performance, and be able to demonstrate and show that they are (have been) treating their customers fairly at all stages of their relationship.

These efforts are underpinned in MiFID, where a general obligation that _:_

_'…all firms act honestly, fairly and professionally, and in accordance with the best interests of the clients'._

The MiFID requirements are implemented through its inclusion in the COBS sourcebook. It may not appear to differ markedly from the overall impact of the FCA's Principles, and indeed, if firms comply with all the Principles, they should not, in general, have much difficulty in complying with COBS. However, there are some differences in specific areas.

## 14.2.3 Requirement for Fair Agreements

The requirement for a 'client agreement' applies to designated investment business carried on for retail and professional clients in relation to MiFID, or equivalent third country business. It also applies to ancillary services, to MiFID business or equivalent third country business.

It does not apply to insurance firms issuing life policies in a principal capacity.

Firms must, in good time, before a retail client is bound by any agreement relating to designated investment business, or ancillary services, or before the provision of those services, whichever is earlier, provide that client with:

- the terms of any such agreement, and

- the information about the firm and its services relating to that agreement, or those services required by COBS 6 (information about a firm and its services), including authorised communications, conflicts of interest and the firm's authorised status.

Firms must provide the agreement and information in a durable medium, or via a website, where the website conditions are satisfied.

A firm may provide the agreement and the information immediately after the client is bound by any such agreement only in the following circumstances:

- The firm was unable to comply with the requirement to provide the agreement in good time prior to the carrying out of investment business for the client, due to the agreement concluding using a means of distance communication which prevented the firm from doing so.

- If the rule on voice telephone communications (COBS 5.1.12 – distance marketing disclosure rules) does not otherwise apply, the firm complies with that rule in relation to a service that the firm is providing to that client.

A firm must establish a record that includes the agreement between itself and a client which sets out the rights and obligations of the parties, and the other terms on which it will provide services to the client.

The records must be maintained for at least the longer of:

- five years

- the duration of the relationship with the client, or

- in the case of a record relating to a pension transfer, pension opt-outs or additional voluntary contributions to a private pension, or pension contract from an occupational pension scheme, indefinitely.

Firms must consider other COBS rules (such as fair, clear and not misleading, disclosure of information and distance communications) when considering their approach to client agreements.

## 14.2.4 Unfair Contract Terms Legislation

The FCA's _'Unfair Contract Terms and Consumer Notices Regulatory Guide_ ' provides a guide on how the FCA will use its powers under the Consumer Rights Act (CRA) 2015.

In conjunction with the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), the FCA will consider the fairness of financial services contracts and consumer notices that are specific in a memorandum of understanding between the FCA and the CMA.

This means that the FCA will consider the fairness contract terms and consumer notices issued by authorised firms or appointed representatives.

Under the CRA a contract term or notice is considered unfair if, contrary to the requirement of good faith, it causes a significant imbalance in the parties' rights and obligations to the detriment of the consumer.

The FCA may consider the fairness of a term or notice within the meaning of the CRA if it receives a complaint from a consumer. The FCA will consider and review the contracts and where necessary it could challenge the firm's use of that term or notice.

There are different ways that the FCA can deal with unfair terms or notices, especially where a firm has breached a principle, rule or a breach of the financial promotion rules. This means that the FCA can take action under FSMA and not the CRA.

The FCA does not have the powers to grant redress to consumers who have suffered a loss because of an unfair term or notice. However, where the use of an unfair term amounts to a breach of an FCA rule, the FCA can apply to court for restitution or require restitution.

## 14.2.5 Inducements

MiFID requires investment firms to take all appropriate steps to identify, prevent or manage conflicts of interest that arise in the course of providing any investment and ancillary services.

An investment firm must not pay/provide to or accept/receive from any party other than its client, or a party acting on behalf of its client, any fee, commission or non-monetary benefit in connection with the provision of any investment or ancillary service.

This prohibition does not apply to any fee, commission or non-monetary benefit that:

- is designed to enhance the quality of the relevant service to the client and does not impair compliance with a firm's duty to act honestly, fairly and professionally in the best interests of the client, or

- enables, or is necessary for, the provision of an investment service by the firm, such as custody costs, settlement and exchange fees, regulatory levies or legal fees and which, by its nature, cannot give rise to conflicts with the firm's duty to act honestly, fairly and professionally in the best interests of the client.

This prohibition also does not apply to any non-monetary benefit that can be categorised as a 'minor non-monetary benefit'.

Therefore, the inducements rules should be seen as a 'payment rule', as it prohibits any payment, unless expressly permitted. The rules on inducements apply to firms carrying on designated investment business which is MiFID business, as well as non-MiFID business. The rules only apply to professional clients and retail clients, therefore investment firms undertaking eligible counterparty business will not be subject to the detailed inducement provisions.

These requirements also cover where a firm provides independent advice, restricted advice or portfolio management services to a retail client, although minor non-monetary benefits are permitted to be received.

Firms providing independent advice or portfolio management services to retail clients located outside the UK or to professional clients are prohibited from receiving any fees, commissions or monetary benefits (other than designated minor non-monetary benefits), and where they do receive these from third parties they must be returned and the client must be advised.

A minor non-monetary benefit is one which:

- is clearly disclosed prior to the provision of the relevant service to the client

- is capable of enhancing the quality of service provided to the client

- is of a scale and nature that it could not be judged to impair the firm's compliance with its duty to act honestly, fairly and professionally in the best interests of the client

- is reasonable, proportionate and of a scale that is unlikely to influence the firm's behaviour in any way that is detrimental to the interests of the relevant client, and

- consists of:

   - information or documentation relating to a financial instrument or an investment service, that is generic in nature or personalised to reflect the circumstances of an individual client

   - written material from a third party that is commissioned and paid for by a corporate issuer or potential issuer to promote a new issuance by the company

   - participation in conferences, seminars and other training events on the benefits and features of a specific financial instrument or an investment service

   - hospitality of a reasonable de minimis value, such as food and drink during a business meeting or a conference, seminar or other relevant training event.

Firms must keep full records of such payments/benefits made to other firms, for all MiFID business. The MiFID inducements provisions were fully implemented for non-MiFID firms/business. In relation to third-party payments, these firms will only have to comply with the 'does not impair compliance' test; the other two tests (disclosure and enhancement) do not apply. Retail non-MiFID business for the sale of packaged products is subject to the disclosure requirements of the inducement provisions.

The extension of the provisions does not, however, mean that firms are expected to disclose details of reasonable non-monetary benefits for non-MiFID business.

## Inducements and Research

MiFID II brought in new rules around how firms consume and pay for research. This meant that the FCA had to delete its previous rules ('use of dealing commission').

Under MiFID II any UK-regulated firm which provides independent advice, restricted advice or portfolio management services to retail clients is prohibited from receiving research for free, rather they must pay for the research.

There are two options open for the payment of research, the firm can either pay it directly from its own resources, or via payment from a research payment account (RPA) controlled by the firm funded by a specific charge to their underlying clients.

However, these provisions do not apply to non-UK-regulated bodies that produce research and nonUK-regulated firms that consume research. Firms' communications shall be deemed to be 'research' if it comprises research material or services:

- relates to one or several financial instruments or other assets

- relates to the issuers or potential issuers of financial instruments, or

- is closely related to a specific industry or market such that it informs views on financial instruments, assets or issuers within that sector, and

- which explicitly or implicitly recommends or suggests an investment strategy and provides a substantiated opinion as to the present or future value or price of such instruments or assets, or otherwise contains analysis and original insights and reaches conclusions based on new or existing information that could be used to inform an investment strategy and be relevant and capable of adding value to a firm's decisions on behalf of their clients.

More importantly, research providers must implement appropriate controls to ensure that they do not provide unsolicited research free of charge, especially to an entity that has to pay for the receipt of research.

On implementing the MiFID II text into its handbook, the FCA developed an approach that went beyond the requirements in the Directive (a term commonly referred to as 'gold-plating'). It provided an exemption for alternative investment funds (AIFs) and collective investment schemes (CISs) whose core investment policy does not generally involve investing in MiFID financial instruments. This means that private equity business and funds investing in/acquiring companies or property (real estate) would not be captured by the prohibition on research.

The prohibitions on the receipt of research unless it is paid for does not apply to certain entities (such as corporate companies, central banks, and banks) and non-UK and EEA regulated bodies that produce research and non-UK and EEA regulated firms that consume research.

In early 2021 the FCA consulted on making amendments to MiFID provision of research provisions – this was part of the UK's 'MiFID quick-fix'. Following the publication of a policy statement in November 2021, the following came into effect in 2022:

- Equity research on small and mid-cap listed or unlisted companies (SMEs) who have a market capitalisation below £200 million are exempt from the inducement rules:

   - this means that research on firms below this threshold could be provided by brokers to asset managers on a bundled basis (where asset managers make a single commission payment to brokers covering execution and research) or for free and would not constitute an inducement under our rules.

- Third-party research on fixed income currencies and commodities (FICC) instruments is exempt from the inducement rules allowing it to be provided on a bundled basis and would not constitute an inducement:

   - the final rules relate to research on FICC financial instruments as a product

   - the FCA noted the rational for not including 'macro-economic' research is because it can often explicitly or implicitly suggest an investment strategy, as well as including other elements of types of research (ie, equities) that are not easily disentangled.

- 'Independent' research providers are exempt from the inducement rules where they do not provide execution services and are not part of a group that includes a firm offering execution services.

- 'Openly' available written research is not within the scope of the inducement rules:

   - provided that it is freely available on the internet without any barriers to obtain/download the material.

## How Should an Investment Firm Deal with Unrequested Research that is provided Free of Charge?

Firms must have in place policies and systems to assess the nature of any service, benefit or material paid or provided by any third party to determine whether they can provide or accept it.

## Firms Should not Accept Research for 'Free'

Where a firm does not want to accept research material (ie, they cannot receive it without having to pay for it), they must take reasonable steps to stop receiving it or avoid benefitting from its content, for example by automatically blocking or filtering certain senders/materials where practicable, and/or requesting the provider of the research material to stop providing research.

## Macroeconomic 'Research'

Macroeconomic research is a relatively broad term.

A recital in MiFID II provides an expansive interpretation of the term, noting that it is:

_'value-added analysis that can inform investment decisions across a variety of financial instruments and asset classes'._

In particular, the following two conditions need to be met:

1. The material concerns one or several financial instruments or other assets, or current or potential issuers of financial instruments, or closely related to a specific sector or market such that it informs views on financial instruments, assets or issuers within that sector or market.

2. The material or service explicitly or implicitly recommends or suggests an investment strategy and provides an opinion as to the present or future value or price of such instruments or assets, or contains analysis and original insights and reaches conclusions based on new or existing information that could be used to inform an investment strategy and be relevant and capable of adding value to the investment firm's decisions on behalf of clients being charged for that research.

The general view in the industry is that most macroeconomic analysis is likely to, explicitly or implicitly, suggest an investment strategy (eg, by providing views on inflation expectations, economic growth, the interest rate curve or currencies for certain countries or regions).

If macroeconomic analysis is classified as research, it has the advantage that such research material is then capable of being received (and paid for) by investment firms, including (independent) investment advisers and portfolio managers.

If macroeconomic analysis is not considered as research (ie, because it is vague/general by nature – it does not make reference to a specific financial instrument), it does not automatically classify as a minor non-monetary benefit. Rather, portfolio managers and independent advisors would need to make a commercial decision whether to pay for it from their own resources or stop receiving such benefits or services.

One exception would be where a provider makes its macroeconomic-related research openly available to all investment firms and the general public on a website. Material made available in this way could be justified as a minor non-monetary benefit – representing information relating to a financial instrument or investment service that is 'generic in nature' (as defined in under Article 12(3)(a) of the MiFID II Delegated Directive).

The industry view is that 'openly available' research in this context would mean that there are no conditions or barriers to accessing it, for example having to log-in or sign-up, or the submission of user information, in order to access the material.

In other words, an individual would be able to read or download the research on a website without having an online account, to log in to an account or sign up to a subscription service.

In its Policy Statement published in December 2021, the FCA noted that the ESMA Q&A (Question 8 in Part 7 of the Q&A on MiFID II and MiFIR Investor Protection and Intermediaries) provides a definition of when research material is considered openly available.

However, it states that there may be limited circumstances where openly available is subject to conditions on access that are necessary to comply with relevant regulatory requirements – in these circumstances a firm would be required to demonstrate what steps it took to meet the rules and how such measures are proportionate.

## Research Payment Account (RPA)

The RPA must be funded by a specific research charge to clients, agreed with clients, developed by the firm as its research budget for the purpose of establishing the amount needed for third-party research in respect of investment services provided to its clients.

Firms must set and regularly assess the research budget, as a way of establishing an RPA and agreeing the research charge with their clients.

Firms are responsible for the RPA and must regularly assess the quality of the research purchased, based on robust criteria – including the contribution to better investment decisions for their clients, who pay the research charge.

Firms must provide clients with the following information in respect of an RPA:

- Information about the budgeted amount for research and the amount of the estimated research charge for each client.

- Annual information on the total costs that each client has incurred for third-party research.

Firms must ensure that the research budget and the RPA are not used to fund in-house research.

The RPA must only be used to pay for research, and not goods and/or services that do not fall into the definition of research. The following is a non-exhaustive list of goods and services that the FCA does not regard as research:

- post-trade analytics

- price feeds or historical price data that have not been analysed or manipulated in order to present the firm with meaningful conclusions

- seminar fees

- corporate access services

- order and execution management services, and

- administration of an RPA.

There has been much debate over the use of RPA and fund managers charging clients for research. A large number of fund managers took the decision to pay for research themselves, taking steps to reduce the amount of research that they receive (and pay for) and focus on relevant and appropriate research which will add value to their investment decision making. This also means that research received can be used to benefit all clients of the firm. The operational administration, and burden, of an RPA process within firms was cited as a barrier to taking this approach.

## FCA Review of Research Unbundling Reforms

In 2019, the FCA published its findings following a review of the research unbundling requirements that came into effect with MiFID II. It undertook a survey of 40 buy-side firms and visited ten firms from both the buy-side and sell-side. It also met with independent research providers.

The FCA's review identified a number of changes in the asset management and research market since the implementation of MiFID II which indicated the new rules had some impact. However, it was noted that research valuation and pricing were still evolving.

Overall, they found:

- Most asset managers choose to absorb research costs themselves.

- Most asset managers were still able to access the research they need.

- There was no evidence of a material reduction in research coverage, including for listed small- and medium-enterprises (SMEs).

- Asset managers' research valuation models have different levels of sophistication, particularly in evaluating the quality of research.

- There is a wide range of sell-side research pricing levels, which can be attributed to an ongoing process of price discovery in the industry.

- Some firms were uncertain in how the new rules apply, such as when attending trade association events, marketing research services or making contributions to consensus forecasts.

It is not unknown for research providers to charge a standard fee to access their written research. There were a variety of models identified, with some using tiered service pricing levels, others offering 'pay as you go', while others price per type of interaction or product consumed.

The review identified that some sell-side research providers were reluctant to offer a price to buy-side firms, preferring the buyers of the research (asset managers) to value and set the price for the research that they purchase.

## Payment Optionality

Following on from the Independent Research Review, commissioned by the government following the announcement of the 'Edinburgh Reforms' (refer to chapter 5), in April 2024, the FCA published a Consultation Paper proposing changes to the existing rules governing how payments for investment research are made.

Following publication by the FCA of its Policy Statement and final rules, from August 2024 in scope UK buyside firms (including fund managers and asset managers) have been provided with greater flexibility on how they can purchase investment research. They are permitted to bundle payments for third-party research and execution services, provided that they meet relevant requirements around the execution and research costs.

However, this new option is only relevant to where commission can be split between 'execution' and 'research' costs, meaning just shares and not non-equity financial instruments (such as fixed income, currencies and commodities).

## 14.2.6 Agent as Client and Reliance on Others

## Agent as Client

If a firm is aware that a person (C1), with or for whom it is providing services is acting as agent for another person (C2) in relation to those services, C1, and not C2, is the client of the firm in respect of that business.

## Reliance on Others

If a firm carrying on UK MiFID business receives an instruction to provide investment, or ancillary services for a client through another firm, and that other firm is a UK MiFID firm, and subject to equivalent regulations, then the firm can rely on:

- information relayed about the client to it by the third-party firm, and

- recommendations that have been provided by the third-party firm.

If a firm relies on information provided by a third party, this information should be given in writing. In additional guidance, the FCA states that it will generally be reasonable for a firm to rely on information provided to it in writing by an unconnected authorised person, or a professional firm, unless it is aware, or ought reasonably to be aware, of anything that would give it reasonable grounds to question the accuracy of that information.

Any information that a rule in COBS or CASS requires to be sent to a client may be sent to another person on the instruction of the client, so long as the recipient is not connected to the firm.
