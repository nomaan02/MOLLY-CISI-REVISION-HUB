## 15. Client Assets Sourcebook (CASS)

The rules relating to the custody and safeguarding of client money and assets are contained in the FCA's 'CASS' Sourcebook. The CASS rules are designed to ensure that firms take adequate steps to protect those client assets for which they are responsible.

Within CASS, the requirement to segregate client money from a firm's own money is aimed at ensuring that, if the firm fails, client money will not be used to repay its creditors. Usually this is done by ensuring that it is placed promptly in a designated client money account with a bank and ensuring that the bank treats client money as separate from the firm's own money.

CASS, in general, applies to every firm, with some specific exemptions. It applies directly in respect of activities conducted with, or for, all categories of client, ie, retail clients, professional clients and eligible counterparties.

**CASS 6** contains the custody rules that apply when a firm holds financial instruments for a client in the course of MiFID business, and when it is safeguarding and administering investments in the course of non-MiFID business.

**CASS 7** contains the client money rules, applying to a UK MiFID investment firm either when:

- it holds client money in the course of its UK MiFID business

- in the course of investment business that is non-UK MiFID business in respect of any investment agreement entered into, or to be entered into with or for a client, or

- it holds money in respect of which **CASS 5** – Client money: insurance mediation activity applies, and the firm elects to apply the provisions of CASS 7.

Firms must, when holding safe custody assets belonging to clients, make adequate arrangements so as to safeguard clients' ownership rights, especially in the event of the firm's insolvency and to prevent the use of safe custody assets belonging to a client on the firm's own account, except with the client's express consent. Firms must also introduce adequate organisational arrangements to minimise the risk of loss or diminution of client's safe custody assets. When a firm accepts client money, it must take the necessary steps to ensure that the client money is deposited in accordance with the requirement of CASS 7.13.3 (depositing client money) in that it is held in an account or accounts identified separately from any accounts used to hold money belonging to the firm.

CASS 7.13.3 stipulates that a firm, on receiving any client money, must promptly place this money into one or more accounts opened with any of the following:

- a central bank

- a Banking Consolidation Directive (BCD) credit institution

- a bank authorised in a third country, or

- a qualifying money market fund.

There are a number of circumstances when the client money rules will not apply, for example, when money is held in connection with a delivery versus payment (DVP) transaction (unless the DVP does not occur by the close of business on the third business day following the date of payment of a delivery obligation), or when it becomes due and payable to the firm. Banks holding monies as deposits with themselves are also exempted.

Following the global financial crisis of 2007–08 a number of insolvency appointments resulted in changes to the CASS requirements. Although the UK client asset regime has performed relatively well in facilitating the early return of client assets and money (compared with some overseas jurisdictions), the failure of Lehman Brothers and the financial crisis in general highlighted a number of issues relating to existing market practices.

Changes made to the CASS rules included the following issues:

- **Increased reporting to clients** – daily reporting on client money and assets holdings to all prime brokerage clients. This means that clients know exactly what is happening to their assets, what transactions have been completed and, if relevant, which and how many of their assets have been re-hypothecated.

- **Holding client money with group banks** – restricting the placement of client money deposits held in client bank accounts within a group to 20%. This limits the amount by which a client is exposed to group credit risk. The rules apply at an entity level if it is a BCD credit institution, a bank authorised in a third-country, a qualified money market fund or the entity operating or managing a qualifying money market fund and a member of the same group as the firm.

- **Prohibiting the use of general liens in custodial agreements** .

- **Creating a new controlled function with specific responsibility for client money and assets** – a senior individual within the firm should be responsible for oversight and protection of client assets and money. Proportionate to the size of the firm, this should be one named individual who will be subject to the SM&CR Certification Regime (they would not normally be a Senior Manager under the SM&CR Regime).

- **Introducing a Client Money and Assets Return (CMAR)** – a monthly return for large firms and medium sized firms, providing the FCA with an overview of firm-specific CASS holdings – enabling the FCA to make regulated interventions on a firm-specific or thematic basis. There is no requirement for small sized firms to submit a CMAR return.

## 15.1 Due Diligence and the Establishment of Client Bank Accounts

Firms that do not deposit client money with a central bank must exercise all due skill, care and diligence in the selection, appointment and periodic review of the credit institution, bank or qualifying money market fund where the money is deposited, and in the arrangements for the holding of this money.

Firms must keep a record of the grounds upon which they satisfy themselves as to the appropriateness of their selection of a credit institution, a bank or a qualifying money market fund. The firm must make the record on the date it makes the selection and must keep it from the date of such selection until five years after the firm ceases to use the third party to hold client money.

## 15.2 Reconciliation of Client Assets

A firm must keep such records and accounts as necessary to enable it, at any time and without delay, to distinguish safe custody assets held for one client from safe custody assets held for any other client, and from the firm's own assets.

CASS 6.6 sets out the obligations on firms to perform internal and external reconciliations. Broadly, reconciliations should be undertaken, as often as necessary, to ensure the accuracy of a firm's records and accounts, between its internal accounts and those records of third parties who hold the assets in safe custody. If possible, they should be done by someone who has not been involved in the production or maintenance of the records that are being reconciled.

If the reconciliation shows a discrepancy, the firm must make good (or provide the equivalent of) any shortfall for which it is responsible. If another person is responsible, the firm should take reasonable steps to resolve the position with that person.

Firms are required to inform the FCA without delay of any failure to comply with the reconciliation requirements, including reconciliation discrepancies and making good any such differences.

## 15.3 Reconciliation of Client Money

The main purpose for firms to carry out reconciliations is to ensure that their internal records of client money held matches the actual amount of client money held.

CASS 7.15 sets out the obligations of firms to perform internal and external reconciliations.

## 15.3.1 Internal Client Money Reconciliations

As explained in CASS 7.12.1R, in order to comply with its obligations under CASS 7.15.5R (Records, accounts and reconciliations), and when relevant SYSC 4.1.1R (General requirements) and SYSC 6.1.1R (Compliance), firms must carry out internal reconciliations of records and accounts of client money the firm holds in client bank accounts and client transaction accounts.

The following methods of reconciliation are deemed to be appropriate for these purposes by the FCA (the standard method of internal client money reconciliation).

- Each business day, a firm that adopts the normal approach should check whether its client money resource, being the aggregate balance on the firm's client bank accounts, as at the close of business on the previous business day, was at least equal to the client money requirement as at the close of business on that day.

- Each business day, a firm that adopts the alternative approach should ensure that its client money resource, being the aggregate balance on the firm's client bank accounts, as at the close of business on that business day is at least equal to the client money requirement as at the close of business on the previous business day. No excess or shortfall should arise when adopting the alternative approach.

For the purposes of performing its reconciliations of records and accounts, a firm should use the values contained in its accounting records, for example its cash book, rather than values contained in statements received.

If a reconciliation shows a discrepancy, the firm must investigate to identify the reason for the discrepancy and ensure that either any shortfall is paid into the client bank account, or any excess is withdrawn from the client bank account by close of business on the day the reconciliation is performed.

## 15.3.2 External Client Money Reconciliations

This means cross-checking the internal client money accounts against the records of third parties (eg, banks) with whom client money is held. Firms must perform external reconciliations as often as is necessary, and as soon as reasonably practicable after the date to which the reconciliation relates.

If there is a discrepancy, the firm must investigate and correct it as soon as possible. If it cannot do so, and the firm should be holding a greater amount of client money, it must pay its own money into the client bank account pending resolution of the discrepancy, which it must correct as soon as possible.

If a firm has not complied with these requirements or is for some reason unable to comply in a material aspect with a particular requirement, it must inform the FCA in writing.

The FCA believes that an adequate method of reconciling client money balances with external records is as follows:

- a reconciliation of a client bank account as recorded by the firm with the statement issued by the bank (or other form of confirmation issued by the bank), and

- a reconciliation of the balance on each client transaction account as recorded by the firm, with the balance of that account as set out in the statement (or other form of confirmation) issued by the person with whom the account is held.

## 15.3.3 Exemption from CASS

CASS does not apply to, inter alia:

- ICVCs

- UCITS qualifying schemes

- a credit institution under the BCD, in relation to deposits held with itself (held as 'banker' not as 'trustee')

- coins held for the value of their metal

- money transferred under title transfer collateral arrangements

- money held in connection with a DVP transaction (unless payment does not occur after three business days)

- money due and payable to the firm

- if a firm carries on business in its name but on behalf of the client when that is required by the nature of the transaction and the client is in agreement, or

- the custody rules (CASS 6) do not apply if a client transfers full ownership of a safe custody asset to a firm for the purpose of securing or otherwise covering present or future, actual contingent or prospective obligations.

Specific rules within CASS may be disapplied depending on the nature of a firm's activities. The details are set out within the individual rules.

There are specific requirements that firms must comply with in order to cease to treat any unclaimed balances as client money or assets which is allocated to an individual client. Firms are able to pay away such balances to a registered charity, providing that the firm can demonstrate that it has taken reasonable steps to trace the clients concerned and return the balance, and has held the balance concerned for at least six years in respect of client money and 12 years for clients' assets.

If a firm wishes to make use of the new requirements, then the governing body must approve of this and unconditionally undertake to pay to the clients concerned a sum equal to the balance paid away to charity in the event of the client seeking to claim the balance in the future.

Reasonable steps that a firm must undertake in the course of being able to demonstrate it has taken 'reasonable steps' in respect of client money and client assets include:

- determining the correct contact details for the client, and

- writing to the client at the last known address either by post or by electronic mail (e-mail).

Firms may also use any available means to determine the correct contact details for the client, including telephoning the client, searching internal records, media advertising, searching public records, mortality screening, using credit reference agencies or tracing agencies.

## 15.3.4 CASS Audit

If a firm holds client money and assets, then it must have an annual CASS assurance audit performed. The audit assurance report is submitted to the FCA. The Supervision Manual (SUP) determines the type of report that must be completed.

Where a firm claims that it does not hold client assets, the external auditor will be required to complete a limited assurance client assets report. The auditor must state whether anything has come to its attention that causes it to believe that the firm held client money or custody assets during the period covered by the report.

The Financial Reporting Council (FRC) is the body responsible for setting the standard for CASS audit reviews. The FRC has developed a 'Client Asset Assurance Standard' (Standard) which establishes requirements and provides guidance for CASS auditors reporting to the FCA in accordance with its SUP rules in respect of engagements that involve evaluating and reporting on a regulated firm's (firm) compliance with the FCA's CASS rules and other rules relevant to the holding of client assets.

The Standard establishes requirements with respect to:

- a. the process for forming, and the expression of, reasonable assurance opinions

- b. the process for forming, and the expression of, limited assurance opinions

- c. the provision of reasonable assurance to the FCA with respect to a firm's proposed adoption of:

   1. the alternative approach to client money segregation

   2. a non-standard method of client money reconciliation, and

- d. CASS auditor confirmations in respect of non-statutory client money trusts.

The key objectives of the CASS Assurance Standard are to:

- a. improve the quality of CASS audits and other CASS assurance engagements

- b. adequately support and challenge CASS auditors when undertaking CASS assurance engagements and, in particular, to define the nature and extent of the work effort required for both reasonable assurance and limited assurance CASS assurance engagements without undermining the importance of the CASS auditor's judgement

- c. support the objectives of the FCA's client assets regime regarding the effective safekeeping of client assets and client monies and in particular to guard against systemic failure of the CASS regime

- d. manage the expectations of:

   1. the management of firms that hold client assets, and

   2. third-party administrators (when a CASS auditor is engaged to provide assurance to the FCA on client assets that it handles or accounts for)

- e. support the effective training of CASS auditors by both the accounting bodies and other training organisations

- f. help to establish realistic expectations regarding the integrity of the UK client assets regime with the beneficial owners of client assets, and

- g. underpin the effectiveness of the FRC's enforcement and disciplinary activities with respect to CASS assurance engagements.

Prior to starting an audit, the CASS auditor would need to obtain an understanding of the firm's business model and permissions it has received from the FCA.

The CASS auditor must gain an understanding of the major activities that the firm uses to monitor internal controls relevant to its compliance with the CASS rules and how the firm initiates remedial actions in response to deficiencies in its controls. Where the firm has an internal audit or compliance function, which covers those major activities intended to ensure compliance with the CASS rules, the CASS auditor shall perform or obtain, as appropriate, and document:

- an evaluation of whether the function's organisational status and relevant policies and procedures adequately support the objectivity of the internal auditors, or compliance function

- an understanding of the nature of the internal audit or compliance function's responsibilities with respect to client assets

- an assessment of whether the function applies a systematic and disciplined approach, including quality control, and

- the activities the function has performed or intends to perform with respect to client assets.

The CASS auditor shall consider how the design of control activities:

- enables the firm to identify where client assets may arise in the business

- seeks to ensure that client assets are segregated and safeguarded effectively

- addresses the performance of internal and external reconciliations as required by the CASS rules, and

- addresses the establishment and acknowledgement of trust status over client assets.

The CASS auditor shall further evaluate whether there is adequate segregation of duties and if the design of the system incorporates sufficiently robust controls over system changes. The auditor must give an opinion on the following, as well as stating/identifying where the applicable requirements have or have not been met:

- The firm has maintained systems adequate to enable it to comply with the custody rules, the collateral rules, the client money rules and the mandate rules throughout the period.

- The firm was in compliance with the custody rules, the collateral rules, the client money rules and the mandate rules at the date at which the report was made.

- In the case of an investment management firm, personal investment firm, a UCITS firm, securities and futures firm, a firm acting as trustee or depositary of an AIF, a firm acting as trustee or depositary of a UCITS or IFPRU (Prudential Sourcebook for Investment Firms) investment firm, or MIFIDPRU (Prudential Sourcebook for MiFID investment firms) firm, when a subsidiary of the firm is, during the period, a nominee company in whose name custody assets of the firm are registered, that nominee company has maintained throughout the period systems for the custody, identification and control of custody assets which adequately included reconciliations at appropriate intervals between the records maintained (whether by the firm or the nominee company) and statements or confirmations from custodians or from the person who maintained the record of legal entitlement.

- If there has been a secondary pooling event during the period, the firm has complied with the relevant client money distribution rules in relation to that pooling event.

The CASS auditor will provide a 'nil' return where there have been no breaches identified.

The client assets report is made up of two parts:

- Part 1 is the auditor's opinion, and

- Part 2 is a breaches schedule.

The FCA expects that the list of breaches will include every breach of a rule in CASS insofar as that rule is within the scope of the client assets report and is identified in the course of the auditor's review of the period covered by the report, whether identified by the auditor or disclosed to it by the firm, or by any third-party.

For the purpose of determining whether to qualify its opinion or express an adverse opinion, the FCA would expect an auditor to exercise its professional judgment as to the significance of a rule breach, as well as to its context, duration and incidence of repetition. The FCA would expect an auditor to consider the aggregate effect of any breaches when judging whether a firm has failed to comply with the requirements described above.

Under FSMA (Communications by Auditors) Regulations 2001, CASS auditors have duties in certain circumstances to make reports to the FCA, including reporting breaches of the FCA's rules that it reasonably believes may be of material significance to the FCA. Therefore, where a CASS auditor becomes aware of such breaches, it does not wait to report them to the FCA by means of the breaches schedule that it appends to its routine assurance reports to the FCA.

The consequence of regulatory failure is the potential to be censored by the FCA, and depending upon the nature and seriousness of the regulatory failure, the penalty could lead to a fine.

## Review of Auditors' Client Assets Reports

Firms are required to review the 'draft' client assets report and where necessary provide an explanation of the circumstances that gave rise to each of the breaches identified in the draft report, and any remedial actions that they have undertaken or plan to undertake to correct those breaches.

The FCA expects firms to use the client assets report as a tool to evaluate the effectiveness of the systems that they have in place for the purpose of complying with the CASS requirements.

The auditor must deliver to the firm the final report within four months of the end of the period covered.

## 15.3.5 Rights of Use

The 'rights of use' is an arrangement under which a client transfers full ownership of assets to a collateraltaker (ie, an investment firm) for the purpose of securing or otherwise covering the performance associated with relevant financial obligations.

As there is a transfer of full ownership of assets to a firm, they cease to be client assets and so are not subject to CASS requirements. The exception to this is if the money and assets belong to a retail client and the purpose of title transfer is to secure or cover that client's present or future, actual, contingent or prospective obligations under a **contract for difference (CFD)** or a contract that is a future and that is entered into with a firm acting as market maker.

Title transfer collateral arrangements (TTCAs) are normally used by firms that undertake derivatives business, where the firms enter into margined transactions with clients. These arrangements allow those firms to treat margin (the amount paid by the client as collateral to open the position or bet) as their own working capital, rather than client money (which must be segregated). Often, those firms subsequently use that money to fund their hedging arrangements.

## 15.3.6 Rehypothecation

Rehypothecation is an alternative name for repledging – in other words, a party that receives a pledge of collateral and then pledges the same collateral to a third party. In practice this is operated by brokers/ prime brokers for hedge fund clients, where typically the hedge funds will post collateral against loans that they have taken out and the collateral is used by the broker/prime broker for its own purposes. The benefit to the hedge fund is lower costs of financing.

In the repo and derivatives market, rehypothecation is often referred to as 'reuse'.

The collapse of both Lehman Brothers and MF Global highlights the pitfalls of clients allowing the firm to reuse their assets as its own. In both cases internal records and accounts were incomplete and it was difficult to identify which assets had been pledged to the firm by which clients and who was owed what. Given the issues that the collapse of Lehman Brothers raised, where firms' collateral was frozen on a global basis while the authorities tried to unwind/unpick who was owed what, it is surprising that in 2011 the same thing happened again to clients of MF Global – who had sold/repledged over £10 billion of customer collateral.

In both cases, clients had to fight to regain assets pledged, because they were treated as unsecured creditors. This also highlighted inadequate record keeping, systems and controls at the firms.

## 15.3.7 Bare Security Interests

If a client has lodged collateral with a firm and it is provided on the provision that the collateral is just pledged or a charge is placed on the assets, the firm is not permitted to reuse the assets.

The client remains the legal owner of those assets until the bare security interest is exercised or crystallises on the client's default.

## 15.3.8 CASS Resolution Pack

A CASS Resolution Pack will enable firms that hold client money and client assets to be able to maintain and retrieve certain documents and information relating to the assets and cash that they held on behalf of clients in the event of a crisis, such as insolvency. Firms must maintain their CASS Resolution Pack at all times.

Firms must be able to retrieve documents required under the CASS resolution pack as soon as practicable, no later than within 48 hours.

The purpose of the CASS resolution pack is to assist an insolvency practitioner in returning client money and custody assets to clients in a timely manner should the firms cease to trade. The insolvency of Lehman Brothers International (Europe) and MF Global UK highlighted difficulties in returning client assets in a timely manner and determining who had control over the clients' assets.

Firms subject to CASS 6 (Custody rules) and CASS 7 (Client money rules) will be required to produce and maintain a CASS resolution pack. Firms are not required to complete a pack if they only hold client money for insurance mediation business (under CASS 5) and if they only arrange safeguarding and administration of assets.

Firms will need adequate internal procedures and processes for monitoring the content of their CASS resolution pack. If there are changes to processes and procedures, these will need to be updated in the pack within five days of the change occurring.

The individual performing the CASS operational oversight function must report annually to the firm's board in respect of compliance with the CASS resolution pack requirements. If a firm is unable to comply with the CASS resolution pack requirements then it must notify the FCA immediately.

## Contents of a CASS Resolution Pack

The following documents and information must be immediately retrievable:

- **Institutions** – identification of the institution appointed to hold client money, to hold or control client money in a client transaction account or for the deposit of safe custody assets.

- **Individuals** – identifies each senior manager and director and any other individual and the nature of their responsibility within the firm who are critical/important to the performance of operational functions related to any regulatory obligations, and the individual to whom the firm has appointed the carrying out/performance of the CASS operational oversight function.

- **Agreement with institutions** – a copy of the executed agreement, including any side letters or other agreements used to clarify/modify the terms of the executed agreement, between the firm and institutions, that relate to the holding of client money or safe custody assets – including written notification or trust acknowledgement letters sent/received.

- **Custody assets** – the most recent internal and external reconciliation relating to safe custody assets.

- **Client money** – the most recent internal and external reconciliation relating to client money. The following documents and information must be retrievable as soon as possible, but within no later than 48 hours.

- **Master documents** – containing information sufficient to retrieve each document in the firm's CASS resolution pack.

- **Agents** – documents identifying each appointed representative, tied agent, field representative or other agent of the firm which received client money or safe custody assets in its capacity as the firm's agent.

- **Group structure** – documents identifying and providing certain other information with respect to each group member and/or third party (a 'third-party outsourcer') involved in the performance of operational aspects of the firm's obligations under the client money/client assets rules.

- **Service providers** – copies of each executed agreement, side letter and/or amendment agreement with any third-party outsourcer.

- **Access to service providers** – documents which describe how to:

   - a. gain access to relevant information held by, and

- b. effect a transfer of client money or safe custody assets held by, each third-party outsourcer.

- **Procedures** – copy of the firm manual in which procedures for the management, recording and transfer of client money and safe custody assets that the firm holds are found.

- **Due diligence** – records of the grounds upon which the firm satisfied itself of the appropriateness of its selection of each third party with which it deposits safe custody assets and client money.

- **Client agreements** – if safe custody assets have been used for securities financing transactions, a copy of the consent from clients to use the assets and the assets used (number/type).

- **Records and accounts** – documents which enable the firm to distinguish client money and safe custody assets of one client from that of another client or the firm's own money.

- **Reconciliation methods** – explanation of the methods of internal reconciliation of client money balances.

- **Client categorisation and agreements** – copies of all agreements with retail and professional clients, records of client categorisation and supporting documentation and relevant documentation relating to recategorising of a client.

## 15.3.9 Mandate

A mandate is required from the client when the client is giving the firm control over their assets and/ or liabilities. A mandate is required if a firm is undertaking designated investment business (including MiFID business), insurance mediation activity (except if it relates to a reinsurance contract) and debt management activity. In most instances, the mandate will be provided to the firm through the firm's terms of business.

Where a client has a mandate with a firm over the control of their assets held at a third party, the client money (CASS 7) and custody rules (CASS 6) will not apply. A mandate is any means that gives the firm the ability to control a client's assets or liabilities, which meet the conditions in (1) to (6) below:

1. They are held at a third party.

2. They are obtained by the firm from the client, and with the client's consent.

3. If those means are obtained in the course of, or in connection with, the firm's insurance mediation activity, they are in written form at the time they are obtained from the client.

4. They are retained by the firm.

5. They put the firm in a position where it is able to give any or all of the types of instructions described in (a) to (d):

   - a. instructions to another person in relation to the client's money that is credited to an account maintained by that other person for the client

   - b. instructions to another person in relation to any money to which the client has an entitlement, if that other person is responsible to the client for that entitlement (including if that other person is holding client money for the client)

   - c. instructions to another person in relation to an asset of the client, if that other person is responsible to the client for holding that asset (including if that other person is safeguarding and administering investments, acting as trustee or depositary of an AIF or acting as trustee or depositary of a UCITS), and

   - d. instructions to another person such that the client incurs a debt or other liability to that other person or any other person (other than the firm).

6. Their circumstances are such that the client's further involvement would not be necessary for the firm's instructions described in 5(a) to 4(d) to be given effect.

The FCA has defined a mandate as 'any means that give a firm the ability to control a client's assets or liabilities'.

The mandate rules require firms to establish and maintain records and internal controls to prevent any misuse of the mandate authority granted by the client.

A mandate can take any form and need not state that it is a mandate. It can be a stand-alone document containing certain information conferring authority to control a client's assets or liabilities on the firm, a specific provision within a document or agreement that also relates to other matters, or can be provided by a client verbally or be in paperless form.

A firm must establish and maintain adequate records and internal controls in respect of its use of the mandates. This would include:

- an up-to-date list of each mandate that the firm has obtained, including a record of any conditions placed by the client or the firm's management on the use of the mandate and, where a mandate was received in non-written form in the course of, or in connection with, its designated investment business, the details required under CASS 8.3.2C R

- a record of each transaction entered into by the firm for each mandate

- internal controls to ensure that each transaction entered into by the firm is carried out in accordance with any conditions established by the client or the firm

- details of the procedures and internal controls around the giving of instructions under the mandates that the firm has, and

- where the firm holds a passbook or similar document belonging to the client, internal controls for the safeguarding (including against loss, unauthorised destruction, theft, fraud or misuse) of any passbook or similar document belonging to the client held by the firm.

The required list of mandates that a firm must maintain should be in a durable medium that allows storage of information in a way accessible for future reference by the regulator or by an auditor preparing a client money report. The record must also be able to be updated and amended where necessary, with the ability to ascertain the details of the mandate list prior to changes/updates being made.

If the instruction/authority for the mandate was provided verbally by the client, then the entry on the mandate list must contain the following information:

- the nature of the mandate (eg, debit card details, direct debits)

- the purpose of the mandate (eg, collecting insurance premiums)

- how the mandate was obtained (eg, by telephone)

- the name of the relevant client, and

- the date on which the mandate was obtained.

Firms must retain records for each mandate for the following periods after they cease to have the mandate:

- for a minimum of five years if the relevant mandate was held by the firm in the course of, or in connection with, its UK MiFID business, and

- a minimum of one year for all other instances.
