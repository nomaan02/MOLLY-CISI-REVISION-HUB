## 8. Threshold Conditions (COND)

Threshold conditions represent the minimum conditions which a firm is required to satisfy, and continue to satisfy, in order to be given and to retain Part 4A permission. The PRA and/or the FCA will take into account the context of the size, nature, scale and complexity of the business which the firm carries on, or will carry out if the relevant application is granted.

The COND Sourcebook gives guidance on the threshold conditions set out in or under Schedule 6 of FSMA. Under Section 41(2) of FSMA, in giving or varying a Part 4A permission, or imposing, or varying any requirement, the PRA and/or the FCA must ensure that the firm concerned will satisfy, and continue to satisfy, the threshold conditions in relation to all of the regulated activities for which it has, or will have permission.

When firms first apply for authorisation, or want to vary their permissions, they will be assessed against the FCA threshold conditions, meaning that the FCA will be able to set standards that applicants should meet, and the FCA can challenge a firm about its business model and strategy. The FCA will only assess FCA-only regulated firms for conduct and prudential issues. It will assess dual-regulated firms for conduct only, and the PRA will assess prudential issues for dual-regulated firms. This is discussed in greater detail in section 8.1.1 of this chapter.

The Financial Services Act 2012 provided the PRA and the FCA with powers to make 'threshold condition codes', which elaborate on the conditions and how these will apply to different classes of firm. The threshold condition and codes seek to reflect the responsibilities and objectives of the PRA and FCA. An example of this is the PRA focusing on the capital and liquidity held by the firms it supervises.

## 8.1 General and FCA Conditions

- **Location of offices** – the head office and the place where the firm carries out business must be in the UK.

- **Effective supervision** – firms must be capable of being effectively supervised by the FCA and/or the PRA, having regard to the nature, complexity and the way in which the firm operates, and the regulated activities that it carries on, or seeks to carry out.

- **Appropriate resources** – resources must be adequate and appropriate in relation to the business activities carried out, or to be carried out.

- **Suitability** – firms must satisfy the FCA and/or the PRA that it is a fit and proper person, having regard to all the circumstances, including:

   - a. its connection with any person

   - b. the nature of any regulated activity that it carries out or seeks to carry out

   - c. the need to ensure that its affairs are conducted soundly and prudently.

- **Business model** – firms must be able to demonstrate the ability to put forward an appropriate, viable and sustainable business model, given the nature and scale of business that they intend to carry out. In addition, the FCA expects firms to be able to demonstrate adequate contingency planning in their business model application. A firm will need to demonstrate that their business model meets the needs of clients and customers, not placing them at undue risk, or placing at risk the integrity of the wider financial services system, for example, from financial crime. The FCA will also expect firms to provide clear information, with evidence of how they will meet this threshold condition. The FCA has stated that it will recommend refusal at an early stage if it is not satisfied that a firm meets, or will continue to meet, this minimum expected standard.

## 8.1.1 FCA Additional Conditions

When firms apply for authorisation, or want to vary their permissions, they will be assessed against the threshold conditions.

The FCA will only assess FCA-only regulated firms for conduct and prudential issues. For dual-regulated firms they will only assess conduct related issues, the PRA is responsible for assessing prudential issues.

The FCA will apply the following principles:

- Align the assessments of new applications to the risk they pose to the FCA's statutory objectives.

- Be open with all potential applicants as they go through the authorisations process, making sure that communication occurs early on so that firms understand what is required of them, and look closely at the proposed business model and the viability of the firm over a medium-term horizon.

- Processes will be structured to support the operational objectives.

- Refuse applications at an earlier stage if it does not think the proposed offering of products or services are in the interests of consumers or, more broadly, if it poses a significant risk to the FCA's objectives.

- Share any risks and underlying themes that are identified with supervision colleagues, so they can monitor and assess them on an ongoing basis once the firm (or individual) is authorised.

## 8.2 PRA Conditions

The Threshold Conditions (TC) are the minimum requirements that firms must meet at all times in order to be permitted to carry on the regulated activities in which they engage. Firms will need to meet both the PRA-specific and FCA-specific TCs. The PRA-specific TCs will apply to banks, building societies, credit unions and designated investment firms that are regulated by the PRA for prudential purposes.

Firms will be required to conduct their regulated activity in a prudent manner and hold appropriate financial and non-financial resources.

## 9. The Fit and Proper Test for Approved Persons (FIT)

This section relates to firms, and individuals, subject to the SM&CR and the APER regime.

Section 59 of FSMA requires persons fulfilling either a controlled function (under the APER) or a senior management function (under the SM&CR) to be approved by the FCA as fit and proper.

An individual may only be permitted to perform either a senior management function, or a control function, after they have been granted 'approved person status' by either the FCA and/or the PRA. They will only grant an application if they are satisfied that the candidate is a fit and proper person to perform the controlled function/senior management function stated in the application form. Responsibility lies with the firm making the application, to satisfy themselves that the individual is for and proper for the role.

During the application process, the FCA may discuss the assessment of the candidate's fitness and propriety with the firm and may retain notes of such discussions. In making its assessment, the FCA will consider the role/function that is to be fulfilled, the activities of the firm, and the permission which has been granted to the firm. If any information comes to light that suggests that the individual might not be fit and proper, the FCA and/or the PRA will consider how relevant and important this information is.

In assessing the fitness and propriety of a person, the FCA and/or the PRA will look at a number of factors against a set of criteria, of which the most important will be the person's:

- honesty, integrity and reputation

- competence and capability, and

- financial soundness.

The following criteria are among those which will be considered when assessing an individual's fitness and propriety. Note that they do not, however, constitute a definitive list of the matters which might be relevant.

## 9.1 Honesty, Integrity and Reputation

The FCA and/or the PRA will have regard to whether a person has been:

- convicted of a criminal offence with particular consideration given to offences of fraud, dishonesty and financial crime

- the subject of an adverse finding or settlement in a civil case, again with particular consideration given to cases involving financial businesses and fraud

- the subject of previous investigation or disciplinary proceedings by the FCA and/or the PRA, or another regulatory authority

- the subject of a justified complaint in relation to regulated activities

- refused a licence to trade, or had a licence, or registration revoked

- involved in an insolvent business

- disqualified as a director

- dismissed from a position of trust, and/or

- able to demonstrate a readiness and willingness to comply with the requirements and standards of the regulatory system.

The FCA, and/or the PRA, will treat each application on its merits, considering the seriousness and circumstances of any matters arising, as well as (in some cases) the length of time which has elapsed since the matter arose.

## 9.2 Competence and Capability

In assessing an applicant's competence and capability, the FCA will have particular regard to whether the person:

- satisfies the relevant requirements laid down in the FCA's Training and Competence Sourcebook

- has demonstrated the experience and training needed for them to fulfil the controlled function applied for.

The FCA will consider previous convictions, dismissals, or suspensions from employment for drugs, alcohol abuse or other abusive acts, only if they relate to the continuing ability of the person to perform the controlled function for which they are to be employed.

## 9.3 Financial Soundness

In assessing an applicant's financial soundness, the FCA will have particular regard to whether the person has:

- been subject to any judgement to repay a debt, or pay another award that remains outstanding, or was not satisfied within a reasonable period

- filed for bankruptcy, been adjudged bankrupt, had their assets sequestrated, or made arrangements with their creditors.

The FCA will not normally require a statement of a person's assets and liabilities. The fact that a person may be of limited financial means does not of itself impact their suitability to perform a controlled function/senior management function.

## 10. General Provisions (GEN)

The General Provisions Sourcebook covers approval by the FCA, emergencies, status disclosure, the FCA logo, and insurance against fines. Firms must clearly disclose that they are authorised and approved by the FCA in all correspondence that they undertake with clients in respect of designated investment business.

Firms are prohibited from using the FCA logo.

## 11. Financial Stability and Market Confidence (FINMAR)

The FINMAR Sourcebook includes provision of powers derived from the Financial Services Act 2010, which amended FSMA. It provides the following powers to the FCA to require financial stability information.

- Financial stability information power, ie, to require a person to provide information, or documents relevant to the stability of one or more aspects of the UK financial system (Section 165A).

- Overseas financial stability information power, ie, exercisable at the request of an overseas regulator to require a person to provide information or documents relevant to the stability of one, or more aspects of the financial system in the country, or territory of that regulator (Section 169A).

## 11.1 Short Selling Regulation (UK SSR)

## Overview

The EU Short Selling Regulation (SSR) 236/2012 applies directly to EU member states – to EU shares, sovereign debt, and sovereign credit default swaps (CDSs), that are listed for trading on EU trading venues (including MTFs – multilateral trading facilities). The location of the person entering into a short sale in the instruments does not matter, so firms in third countries undertaking short selling are covered by the regulation.

Following Brexit UK firms must comply with UK SSR.

## Key Elements of the UK SSR

- Transparency requirements for short positions in shares and sovereign debt.

- Restrictions on uncovered short sales in shares, sovereign debt, and sovereign CDSs.

- Central counterparty (CCP) procedures to deal with settlement failure (triggering a 'buy-in' of shares if delivery is delayed by four business days).

- Emergency measures that may be taken in exceptional circumstances by competent authorities and ESMA.

The requirements introduced by the SSR represented a major change from the previous UK disclosure regime that applied just to UK financial sector companies.

## Disclosure Requirements

The regulation requires the following disclosures:

- Short positions in equities to be disclosed to the regulator (competent authority) where they reach or fall below thresholds of 0.2% of the issued share capital of the company, or each 0.1% above that. Short positions in equities reaching, or falling below 0.5% of the issued share capital, or each 0.1% above 0.5%, to be disclosed to the market (ie, publicly).

- The amended threshold level came into effect on 5 February 2024.

- Short positions in sovereign (government) debt above thresholds to be disclosed to the regulator. Where the total amount of the outstanding issued sovereign debt is €500 billion or less, the thresholds are 0.1% and then each 0.05% above 0.1%. Where the total amount of the outstanding issued sovereign debt is above €500 billion, or where there is a liquid futures market for the sovereign debt, the thresholds are 0.5% and then each 0.25% above 0.5%.

- Following on from the announcement of the Edinburgh Reforms in December 2022 and the subsequent 'call for evidence' publication by HM Treasury in November 2023, HM Treasury confirmed they will be removing the application to short positions for CDS and sovereign debt under the UK SSR.

## Restrictions on Uncovered Short Sales

Uncovered (naked) short selling is the practice of selling a security without having that security or borrowing the security beforehand.

A person may enter into a short sale of a share or of sovereign debt only if the person has:

- pre-borrowed the security, or has made similar arrangements

- entered into an agreement to borrow the security, or has a claim with similar effect

- a third party that adequately ensures that the security will be available for settlement, or

- the market making exemption (which provides the firm with the requirement for disclosing and preborrowing or covering the short sale).

Uncovered short positions in sovereign CDSs are not permitted.

## Possible Measures Covering Exceptional Circumstances

The FCA can, when necessary, and within certain restrictions, impose transparency requirements and restrictions on short selling in exceptional circumstances where there is a serious threat to the UK's financial stability or market confidence.

They also have the power to temporarily restrict short selling of financial instruments when there is a significant price fall of the underlying company during the day.

## Regulatory Update

The HM Treasury statutory instrument, 'The Short Selling Regulations 2025', was made and published in January 2025. The FCA is due to publish a consultation paper, with draft rules and a draft statement of policy on the use of its emergency intervention powers, in Q3 2025.

Following consultation, the FCA is expected to publish a Policy Statement, with final rules, in Q3/4 H2 2026.

## 12. Training and Competence (TC)

Principle 3 (Management and Control) requires firms to take reasonable care to organise and control their affairs responsibly and effectively. To comply with this requirement, firms must ensure that any employee involved in a regulated activity achieves and maintains the competence needed for that particular role.

The principles are built on further in the section of the FCA Handbook that deals with senior management arrangements, systems and controls (SYSC). This incorporates a high-level competence requirement which applies to all UK-authorised firms (including wholesale firms).

This high-level approach is further supplemented, for firms carrying on activities with or for retail customers only and in some limited cases for customers and consumers, by the Training and Competence (TC) Sourcebook. The TC Sourcebook is more focused on the outcomes achieved by firms through their internal training and competence arrangements.

The TC Sourcebook states that firms must not allow their employees to carry on/undertake a specified activity (other than an overseeing activity) for which there is a qualification requirement without their first having passed the relevant regulatory module of an appropriate qualification. Specifically, firms must not allow their employees to do any of the following without first passing each module of an appropriate qualification:

- certain advising and dealing activities

- acting as a broker fund adviser

- advising on syndicate participation at Lloyd's, and/or

- acting as a pension transfer specialist.

## 12.1 Exemptions from the Requirements

If a firm is satisfied that an employee meets certain conditions, then the requirement to have passed each module of an appropriate qualification only applies if they are carrying on one of the following activities:

- advising retail clients on investments which are packaged products

- acting as a broker fund adviser

- advising on syndicate participation at Lloyd's, and

- acting as a pension transfer specialist.

The conditions are that the firm must be satisfied that the employee:

- has at least three years' up-to-date relevant experience in the activity, which they gained while employed outside the UK

- has not previously been required to comply fully with the relevant examination requirements, and

- has passed the relevant module of an appropriate examination.

## 12.2 Selecting an Appropriate Examination

Firms are required to select an appropriate qualification from the list of qualifications maintained by the FCA (TC App 4 – 'appropriate qualification tables'). If they do so, this will tend to demonstrate compliance with the qualification requirements. (https://www.handbook.fca.org.uk/handbook/TC/ App/4/?view=chapter)

## 12.3 Training Needs

The T&C Sourcebook states that firms must assess their employees' training needs at the outset, and again at regular intervals (including if their role changes). They should also review the quality and effectiveness of their training.

## 12.4 Maintaining Competence

Firms are also required to review their employees' competence regularly – and to take appropriate action, when needed, to ensure that they remain competent for their role. In doing so, they should take account of:

- the individual's technical knowledge and its application

- their skills and expertise

- changes in the market, products, and regulation.

## 12.5 Activities to which the T&C Rules Apply

Designated investment business carried out for a retail client:

- advising

- dealing

- managing, and

- overseeing on a day-to-day basis.

Regulated mortgage activity and reversion activity carried on for a customer:

- advising

- designing scripted questions for non-advised sales, and

- overseeing non-advised sales on a day-to-day basis.

Non-investment insurance business carried on for a consumer:

- advising.

## 12.6 Statements of Professional Standing (SPS)

As part of the Adviser Charging rules, retail investment advisers must be in possession of a valid SPS from an FCA-accredited body (such as the CISI).

SPSs confirm that an adviser has:

- completed a Qualification and Credit Framework (QCF) Level 4 qualification, that appears on the list of approved qualifications in the FCA Handbook

- completed an annual programme of 35 hours of continuing professional development (CPD) that meets the requirements laid out by the FCA

- acted in accordance with the FCA's APER or SM&CR requirements, and

- agreed to adhere to a code of ethics.

The FCA have noted that advisers must complete a minimum of 35 hours of relevant CPD each year, with at least 21 hours of this being structured learning.

The FCA published qualifications tables (TC App 4) contains the appropriate qualifications that should be attained by employees in order to carry out certain activities for retail clients. These activities are set out in Appendix 1.1.1R of the Training and Competence (TC) Sourcebook.

The qualification requirements apply to individuals carrying out one or more of the following regulated activities:

- advising on (but not dealing in) securities (which are not stakeholder pension schemes or broker funds)

- advising on (but not dealing in) derivatives

- advising on packaged products (which are not broker funds)

- advising on friendly society tax-exempt policies

- advising on, and dealing in securities (which are not stakeholder pension schemes or broker funds), and/or

- advising on and dealing with or for clients in derivatives.

Advisers will need to hold an SPS as evidence that they are meeting the standards. The SPS is valid for 12 months.

The SPS contains:

- the adviser's name

- the name and contact details of the accredited body and a named signatory

- the end date of verification (maximum of 12 months from date of verification)

- confirmation that the adviser's qualification(s) have been verified.

- confirmation that the adviser has signed an annual declaration that states that they:

   - have kept their knowledge up to date, and

   - adhere to standards of ethical behaviour

- the adviser's individual reference number as it appears on the FCA Register, and

- a recommendation that the reader should check that the adviser is on the FCA Register, and how to do so.
