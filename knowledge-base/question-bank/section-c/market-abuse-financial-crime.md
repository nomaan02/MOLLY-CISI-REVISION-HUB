## C10.

### Question

Outline the key enforcement powers available to the FCA in dealing with financial or economic crime.

### Answer

The question was concerned with the enforcement powers available to the FCA specifically in connection with financial and economic crime. In the best answers, candidates referred to both the general FCA powers of enforcement as well as the specific offences and remedies available in the financial and economic area.

Specific FCA enforcement powers include:

- **(i)** Fines;

- **(ii)** Private or public censure;

- **(iii)** Injunction;

- **(iv)** Varying or withdrawing approval;

- **(v)** Varying or cancelling permission;

- **(vi)** Information gathering;

- **(vii)** Investigations;

- **(viii)** Reports by skilled persons;

- **(ix)** Restitution;

- **(x)** Bankruptcy or insolvency.

Candidates could also then have referred to the particular statutory remedies available to the FCA in connection with:

- **(i)** Market Abuse (MAR);

- **(ii)** Market Manipulation (Part 7 FSA 2012);

- **(iii)** Insider Trading (CJA);

- **(iv)** Money Laundering (POCA and SOCPA, ML Regulations, SYSC and JMLSG); and

- **(v)** Counter terrorism (CTA).

Candidates could also have referred to the more aggressive 'proactive' and 'credible deterrence' approach to enforcement adopted by the FCA following the financial crisis.

## C13.

### Question

Analyse the challenges financial institutions face in applying a risk-based approach to Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs). How do the FCA's proposed amendments to FG17/6 help address these challenges, and what changes could firms make to enhance their PEP risk management frameworks?

### Answer

This question asked candidates to analyse the challenges that financial institutions face in applying a risk-based approach to Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) and to give their opinion on how the FCA's proposed amendments to FG17/6 help address these challenges, as well as the changes that firms could make to enhance their PEP risk management frameworks. This was an open-ended question in which a lot of material could be included.

Financial institutions face significant challenges in applying a risk-based approach to Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs). These challenges stem from the inherent risks associated with PEPs, the complexity of accurately assessing those risks, and the need to balance regulatory requirements with practical business considerations. The FCA's proposed amendments to FG17/6 aim to address some of these challenges by providing clearer guidance on the proportionality of risk management, particularly concerning domestic PEPs. However, there are additional measures that firms can implement to further enhance their PEP risk management frameworks.

One of the primary challenges is accurately identifying PEPs, including their close associates and family members. PEP status can change frequently, and maintaining up-to-date information is resource-intensive. Moreover, distinguishing between domestic and foreign PEPs, and applying the appropriate level of scrutiny, adds another layer of complexity. The risk associated with PEPs varies widely depending on factors such as the individual's role, the country of origin, and the nature of their transactions. Institutions must assess the risk on a case-by-case basis, which requires a deep understanding of the political and economic environment in which the PEP operates. This assessment can be subjective and may lead to inconsistencies in how PEPs are treated across the institution. Enhanced due diligence (EDD) is required for PEPs, but applying EDD uniformly to all PEPs can be overly burdensome and may not be proportionate to the actual risk. The challenge lies in balancing the need for thorough scrutiny with the principle of proportionality, ensuring that resources are focused on higher-risk PEPs without unnecessarily disrupting business operations or client relationships.

The FCA's proposed amendments to FG17/6 provide clearer guidance on the treatment of PEPs, particularly domestic PEPs, and emphasise a more proportionate risk-based approach. The amendments suggest that not all PEPs should be treated as high risk by default, especially domestic PEPs who may hold positions with limited or no influence over financial matters. This change helps institutions apply a more nuanced approach, reducing the burden on firms and allowing them to focus their resources on higher-risk PEPs. The amendments also encourage firms to consider the specific circumstances of each PEP, such as their role, the nature of their transactions, and the potential risk they pose. This guidance aligns with the broader principles of a risk-based approach, allowing firms to tailor their due diligence processes to the actual level of risk presented by each PEP.

To improve the identification and monitoring of PEPs, firms should invest in advanced screening technologies that utilise artificial intelligence and machine learning. These tools can help automate the identification of PEPs, track changes in their status, and provide real-time alerts for any potential risks. Firms could implement a process for regularly updating their risk assessments for PEPs. This includes staying informed about changes in the political landscape, understanding the evolving risks associated with different regions, and adjusting the level of due diligence accordingly. Ensuring that staff across the institution are well-trained in identifying and managing PEP risks is crucial. Regular training sessions could be conducted to keep employees up-to-date on the latest regulatory requirements and best practices for PEP risk management. Firms should also establish clear internal guidelines that differentiate between domestic and foreign PEPs, with a focus on applying proportionality in their risk-based approach. These guidelines should be regularly reviewed and updated to reflect the latest FCA guidance and the firm's own risk appetite. Robust internal controls and governance structures are essential for effective PEP risk management. Firms should ensure that there is a clear line of accountability for PEP-related decisions and that senior management is actively involved in overseeing PEP risk management processes.

While the FCA's proposed amendments to FG17/6 offer valuable direction for managing PEP risks, it's essential to strike a balance between prescriptive requirements and a holistic understanding of risk. Overly rigid approaches can lead to inefficiencies, while a more flexible, risk-based approach allows firms to focus on genuinely high-risk scenarios. Less prescriptive measures can empower institutions to use their judgement, leading to more effective and nuanced risk management. Ultimately, less may be more when it comes to regulatory guidance, enabling firms to better tailor their responses to the specific risks they face.

## C16.

### Question

In your role as compliance officer to an FCA authorised firm you are concerned that, in spite of your efforts to educate staff on the impact of Money Laundering Regulations, there is still some ignorance among staff. Prepare a memorandum to all staff giving practical guidance on when it is and is not necessary to verify the identity of UK institutional and corporate investors and how to do so.

### Answer

**Memorandum**

**To:** All staff

**From:** Compliance Officer

**Date:** [text unclear in source]

**Re:** Money Laundering Scope

Set out below is guidance on when it is necessary to verify the identity of institutional and corporate customers, and how verification procedures should be carried out.

**When procedures are required**

Whenever a business relationship is to be established, or a one-off transaction or series of linked transactions of Euro 15,000 or more is undertaken, the identification procedures must be followed. Once identification procedures have been satisfactorily completed, then as long as records are maintained in accordance with the guidance notes, no further evidence is needed when subsequent transactions are undertaken.

Exemptions are:

- Transactions with any UK or EU credit or financial institution.

- Introduction from another financial institution in the EU or one of the countries in the Financial Action Task Force or Jersey, Guernsey or the Isle of Man. This concession only applies to one-off transactions.

- Postal or electronic business from an account held in the applicant's name at a UK or EU credit institution. In such situations record need be made of only the account number and branch sort code, if there is no apparent inconsistency between the name on the cheque (if applicable) and the name in which the account is held.

Irrespective of the size and nature of the transactions and the exemptions set out above, identity must be verified in **all cases** where money laundering is known or suspected.

**Procedures required – UK registered companies and partnerships**

No further steps, over and above the existing business practice, to verify identity will normally be required where the applicant for business is:

- A company quoted on the London Stock Exchange or another recognised or designated investment exchange.

- Known to be a subsidiary of such a company.

- A private company or partnership, one or more of whose directors/partners are already known to the firm.

Where the applicant for business is an unquoted company or a partnership and none of the directors/partners is already known, the firm should identify one or more of the principal director/partners and/or shareholders as applicable. In addition the following documents should normally be obtained:

- Copy of the latest report and accounts (audited where applicable).

- Copy of the certificate of incorporation/certificate of trade or equivalent.

The firm may also:

- Make a credit reference agency search or take a bankers reference; or

- For smaller companies, or partnerships, verify the identity of one or more of the directors/partners in line with the requirement for personal customers referred to earlier.

## C20.

### Question

Analyse how the UK's Bribery Act 2010 mitigates the risk of bribery and outline the responsibilities of financial services firms under this legislation. Additionally, discuss the major challenges that global financial services organisations face in combating bribery.

### Answer

This question asked candidates to analyse how the UK's Bribery Act 2010 mitigates the risk of bribery and outline the responsibilities of financial services firms under this legislation. Candidates were additionally asked to discuss the major challenges that global financial services organisations face in combating bribery. Like all questions under this section, this was again an open-ended question in which a lot of material might be included.

The Bribery Act 2010 is considered one of the most stringent anti-bribery laws in the world. It was introduced to combat bribery and corruption, not just domestically but also on an international scale. It was enacted to provide a comprehensive framework for preventing bribery in both the public and private sectors. The Act criminalises several forms of bribery, including offering, promising, or giving a bribe; requesting, agreeing to receive, or accepting a bribe; bribing a foreign public official; and the failure of a commercial organisation to prevent bribery. The Act's extraterritorial reach means that UK companies, as well as individuals associated with them, can be prosecuted for bribery offences committed anywhere in the world.

One of the key ways in which the Act mitigates bribery risk is through the introduction of the corporate offence of failing to prevent bribery. This offence holds companies liable if an associated person (such as an employee, agent, or subsidiary) commits bribery on their behalf, unless the company can demonstrate that it had adequate procedures in place to prevent such conduct. This provision incentivises companies to implement robust anti-bribery measures, as the absence of such procedures can lead to significant penalties, including unlimited fines. Furthermore, the Act requires transparency in corporate dealings and promotes a culture of integrity by encouraging companies to develop and enforce strong compliance programmes. The threat of severe penalties and reputational damage serves as a powerful deterrent against engaging in or tolerating bribery.

Financial services firms have specific responsibilities under the Act to ensure that they do not engage in or facilitate bribery. Financial services firms must establish and maintain robust anti-bribery policies and procedures. The Ministry of Justice has provided guidance on what constitutes 'adequate procedures', including top-level commitment, risk assessments, due diligence, communication and training, monitoring and review, and enforcement of anti-bribery policies. Firms are required to conduct regular and thorough risk assessments to identify and evaluate the bribery risks they face. This includes understanding the specific risks associated with the markets in which they operate, the services they provide, and the third parties with whom they interact. Furthermore, financial services firms must perform due diligence on their business partners, agents, and intermediaries to ensure they are not engaging in corrupt practices. This due diligence extends to understanding the anti-bribery policies of third parties and ensuring that these policies are aligned with the firm's own standards. Firms have a responsibility to educate their employees about the Act and the firm's anti-bribery policies. Regular training sessions should be conducted to ensure that all employees, especially those in high-risk roles, are aware of their responsibilities and the potential consequences of engaging in bribery.

Despite the robust framework provided by the Bribery Act 2010, global financial services firms face several significant challenges in combating bribery. Firms often operate in multiple jurisdictions, each with its own anti-bribery laws and regulations. Navigating this complex regulatory environment can be challenging, particularly when the requirements in different countries may conflict or when local customs may tolerate practices that are considered bribery under the Bribery Act. Cultural norms regarding business practices vary widely across the world. What is considered a customary business gift in one country may be viewed as a bribe in another. Global financial services firms must balance respecting local customs with ensuring compliance with anti-bribery laws, which can be a delicate and challenging task. Firms often rely on a network of third parties, including agents, consultants, and intermediaries. Managing the bribery risks associated with these third parties is difficult, especially in regions with high levels of corruption. Firms must ensure that their due diligence processes are rigorous and that third parties are held to the same anti-bribery standards. The enforcement of anti-bribery laws has become more aggressive in recent years, with regulators in multiple jurisdictions increasingly cooperating to tackle bribery. Global financial services firms are at risk of facing significant penalties and reputational damage if they are found to be in violation of anti-bribery laws, even if the bribery occurred in a different country. Beyond legal and financial penalties, the reputational damage associated with bribery allegations can be severe. Global financial services firms must work to protect their reputation by maintaining high ethical standards and demonstrating a commitment to combating bribery in all forms.

## C31.

### Question

Outline the principal provisions that apply with regard to data protection in the UK and comment on the effectiveness of these measures in securing privacy rights.

### Answer

The question was concerned with the purpose and content of the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) 2016/679/EU and the UK implementing measures with UK Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018. The EU adopted the GDPR in 2016, and it came into effect on 25 May 2018. It replaced the Data Protection Directive (DPD) 95/46/EC which was implemented in the UK under the Data Protection Act 1998. The right to respect private and family life, and home and correspondence was earlier protected under Article 8 of the 1950 European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) and the seven principles agreed by the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) "Recommendations Concerning Guidelines Governing the Protection of Privacy and Trans-border Flows of Personal Data" in January 1999 (which covered notice, purpose, consent, security, disclosure, access and accountability). Controls on automatic processing were provided for under the 1981 Council of Europe Convention for the Protection of Individuals regarding Automatic Processing Personal Data.

The GDPR applies to personal data, which is information relating to a living individual which can be identified directly from the information or through matching with other information that is, or is likely to be, in the possession of the organisation or entity concerned. Obligations are imposed on data collectors and data processors. Public entities and firms that regularly and systematically process personal data must employ a Data Protection Officer (DPO). Data is generally to be protected by design and by default in systems processing (Article 25). The GDPR contains eight revised data protection principles based on fair and lawful processing, for legitimate purposes, with data being adequate, relevant and non-excessive, accurate and up-to-date, not kept for longer than necessary, processed in accordance with individual rights, secure and properly transferred. Data subjects have eight principal rights under the GDPR, consisting of the right:

- to be informed

- of access (Article 15)

- to rectification (data correction)

- to data erasure (Article 17)

- to restrict processing

- to data portability

- to object to the processing of their data in certain circumstances

- related to automated decision making (including profiling).

Firms in breach of the GDPR can be fined up to €20 million or 4% of annual global turnover. European Member States must establish an independent Supervisory Authority (SA), with activities being coordinated through a European Data Protection Board (EDPB). The GDPR is implemented following the UK's withdrawal from the EU under the Data Protection, Privacy and Electronic Communications (Amendments etc) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019/419.

The GDPR has extended the personal rights initially conferred under the DPA 1995 in a number of important respects. Firms must consider data subjects' interests in managing personal data. The GDPR does not apply with regard to national security, police or judicial use, statistical and scientific analysis and personal use. Security and judicial matters are dealt with under Parts 3 and 4 of the DPA 2018 in the UK, with equivalent GDPR measures being applied to other areas outside the regulation under Part 2. The Information Commissioner (IC) is established under Part 5 and enforcement dealt with in Part 6. It is an offence knowingly or recklessly to obtain or disclose personal data without the consent of the data controller, procuring such disclosure or retaining data without consent and for selling or offering to sell personal data (which implements the requirements set out in the Enforcement of Intellectual Property Rights Directive (IPRED) 2004/48/EC). The GDPR does not attempt to confirm the nature of data or personal data in law and the rights of companies and data subjects outside the terms of the regulation. Separate concerns have arisen regarding the nature of consent and the cost and burden of compliance. Cyber security has had to be dealt with separately under the Network Information Systems (NIS) Directive 2016/1148/EU, which came into effect in August 2016, and was implemented on 9 May 2018. The government is currently reviewing the UK's approach to data protection and relevant legislative and regulatory measures.

## C44.

### Question

Assess the effectiveness of the revised provisions on misleading statements and impressions introduced under the Financial Services Act 2012 and comment on their significance as part of the larger UK statutory regime to control market abuse and manipulation.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to comment on the significance of the new statutory provisions adopted on misleading statements and impressions under the FSMA as amended. The earlier provisions contained in s397 on misleading statements and practices have been replaced by the new offences of misleading statements and misleading impressions in ss89 and 90 Financial Services Act 2012 as well as the new provisions on misleading statements and impressions in relation to benchmarks in s91 FSA 2012.

s89 applies with regards to making statements known to be false or misleading in a material respect, being reckless as to whether the statement is false or misleading or dishonestly concealing any material facts in relation to the statement (s89 (1) and (2)). It is also an offence to make a statement or conceal facts to induce another person to enter or not enter into a relevant agreement or exercise rights under a relevant investment. Offences are available for price stabilisation, control of information and buyback programmes. s89 no longer refers to promises or forecasts nor to deceptive statements. Separate offences apply with regards to misleading impressions under s90 where a person intends to create a false or misleading impression with regards to the market in relevant investments or the price or value of the investments (s90 (1) FSA).

This is extended to apply to intent to acquire, dispose of or subscribe for or underwrite investments or exercise rights under the investment or not and where the impression may generate a gain or cause loss to another person (s90 (2) and (3)). Exemptions are provided under (ss89 (3) and 90 (9)).

A new offence is created of making false or misleading statements in connection with benchmark arrangements with the intention that the statements are used to set the benchmark knowing that or being reckless as to the statement being false or misleading (s91 (1)). A separate offence is created where a person acts or engages in any course of conduct which creates a false or misleading impression as to the price or value of an investment or the appropriate interest rate (s91 (2)). Equivalent exemptions again apply (s91 (3)). A person guilty of any offence under Part 7 is liable for up to ten years imprisonment on indictment or a fine or both (s92 (1)) – this penalty increasing with the passing of FSA 2021 from the original seven years.

Reference could also have been made to the civil provisions on market abuse contained in the Market Abuse Regulation and separate offences on insider dealing in the Criminal Justice Act 1993. A short overview of the relevant offences, defences and penalties should have been provided. Candidates should also have referred to the different burdens of proof with the civil market abuse and criminal misleading statements and impressions and insider dealing offences.

Market manipulation would also clearly be contrary to FCA PRIN 1 (integrity), PRA FR 1, COCON 1 and COCON 5 as well arguably as PRIN 5 (market conduct), 6 (customers' interests) or PRIN 12 (Consumer Duty), possibly 8 (conflicts of interest), 9 (trust) and possibly 10 (client assets).
