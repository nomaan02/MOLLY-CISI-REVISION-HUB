# Section C — SM&CR & Governance

> Each Question carries 15 marks in total.

---

## C4.

### Question

There have been a number of examples of disciplinary action being taken against compliance officers. Consider the duties and obligations of a compliance officer. What practical steps must be taken by compliance officers to reduce their exposure to disciplinary action?

### Answer

**Duties and obligations of compliance officers**

The FCA's High Level Standards require that the firm's management appoint a director or senior manager to take responsibility for:

- Oversight of the firm's compliance

- Reporting to the firm's governing body on compliance.

The term 'compliance', which is central to the whole regulatory structure, is nowhere formally defined. It is generally understood to mean the institution and enforcement of procedures which are designed to enable a firm to comply with its regulatory obligations.

Although the titular compliance officer within a firm has the task of establishing and monitoring systems of compliance, the prime responsibility for ensuring compliance with all the relevant regulatory requirements rests with the directors (or partners) of the firm, and in particular with the chief executive.

In order to fulfil their responsibilities, the compliance officer must ensure that the compliance function is so **organised** that:

- The structure of the compliance function and the lines of responsibility are clearly set out in writing.

- Compliance officers have access to, and are demonstrably supported by, the chief executive; and the senior compliance officer has access to any audit committee of the board and to the non-executive directors.

- Compliance officers are in a position to be independent and objective.

- Adequate resources (of staff, skills, computer equipment and training) are available for compliance work.

- Compliance officers have access to all records and paperwork, however confidential, within the firm.

- Compliance officers have appropriate status, authority and personality.

The compliance officer must ensure that the **range** of his activities includes:

- Adequate staff training

- Well defined operating procedures and maintenance of written records

- Appropriate in-house rules (about personal dealing, confidential information, conflicts of interest, etc)

- Efficient in-house advisory services

- Regular monitoring of conduct of business

- Correct handling of customer complaints

- Full and timely disclosure to the FCA of routine returns and ad hoc requests for information.

**Practical steps**

The following steps will assist the compliance officer in ensuring that his objectives are fulfilled:

- Identify where the firm lacks a culture of compliance; highlight these areas to senior management and, on implementation of improvements, assist the staff in understanding and adapting to the changes.

- Ensure that a clear training programme is established, with the objective of ensuring that everyone understands his or her obligations and the costs of non-compliance.

- Ensure that the compliance manual sets down clear policies on, for example: Chinese walls, money laundering, independence, inducements and complaints procedures.

- Highlight the key areas of risk to the business and use this analysis for monitoring purposes.

- Remember the role of independence: this will at times involve reporting certain matters to senior management and, on occasion, to the FCA.

## C36.

### Question

What conditions are required for an effective and transparent system of compliance within a financial firm and how should senior management encourage this?

### Answer

This question asked candidates to discuss the conditions required for an effective and transparent system of compliance within a financial firm, **and** how senior management should encourage this. This is an open-ended question in which a lot of material might be included. Whilst this was a popular question for candidates many candidates missed out on full marks by not addressing the role that senior management play in building and maintaining an effective and transparent system of compliance.

With regards to the conditions required for an effective and transparent system of compliance within a financial firm, within a strong answer we would expect the following:

- Firms should have written compliance policies and procedures outlining principles and processes for managing compliance risks, and these should be in place, and regularly reviewed.

- The firm should ensure that the compliance function is independent and has formal status within the organisation.

- Regular risk assessments should be performed to identify and evaluate compliance risks at least annually.

- Regular and risk-based compliance assurance and testing should be undertaken to ensure adherence to policies and procedures.

- Compliance reports and management information should be provided to relevant stakeholders on a regular basis, summarising risks, corrective measures, and actions taken.

- Clear and effective communication channels should be in place for compliance matters within the organisation.

- Regular compliance training and education should be provided to all employees to ensure they understand and adhere to policies and procedures.

- Compliance staff should have the necessary skills, knowledge, and expertise needed for their roles.

- Horizon scanning is in place to effectively identify and address relevant changes and developments in the regulatory requirement.

- Roles and responsibilities for risk identification and risk management are clearly articulated.

- Arrangements are in place for the segregation of duties within the firm to articulate, identify and manage conflicts of interest.

- Effective systems and controls are in place to counter financial crime, proportionate to the nature, scale, and complexity of the firm's activities.

- An internal audit function separate from other functions should be in place, responsible for evaluating the adequacy and effectiveness of systems, internal control mechanisms, and arrangements.

- Complaints handling processes are in place, and complaints are used as a relevant source of information and improvement.

Concerning how senior management should encourage this, a strong answer could note that senior management should:

- Lead by example by adhering to and promoting the firm's compliance policies and ethical behaviour.

- Actively review management information and compliance reports and ensure timely implementation of corrective measures.

- Promote a speak-up culture, and create an environment where staff feel comfortable reporting compliance concerns without fear of retaliation.

- Encourage continuous improvement in compliance processes and stay updated with regulatory changes.

- Ensure the compliance function is provided with adequate resources, including qualified and experienced staff.

- Embed compliance as part of the firm's culture and an integral part of business activities.

## C39.

### Question

Comment on the need for and extent to which high ethical standards can be promoted by the Banking Standards Review Council (BSRC), as recommended by Sir Richard Lambert in his review of banking standards, in the City of London.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to comment on the potential value of the BSRC in promoting high ethical standards. The BSRC is to be established following the review conducted by Sir Richard Lambert with a consultation document being issued in February 2014 and final report in May 2014. This had been referred to in the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) final report on Changing Banking for Good (12 June 2013) with higher professional standards to be promoted in the banking area including through the development of a new set of core standards with the BSRC not acting as a lobby body. The BSRC would be funded by the industry although act in an independent manner.

Sir Richard Lambert recommended that participating institutions commit to a programme of continuous improvement of culture, competence and customer outcomes with various recommendations being made with regard to the BSRC specifically. It should establish standards of good practice, report annually, meet with nonexecutive directors and risk or reputation committee chairs, work with industry and stakeholders to develop a new code of practice, identify and encourage good practice and learning, development and leadership, assist compliance and work with professional bodies to improve training and qualifications. Nine specific recommendations were made with regard to purpose, governance, funding and reporting, arrangements, engagement, institutional relations, regulatory cooperation, raising standards of conduct over time, stakeholder involvement, training and avoidance of duplication of function.

The principal advantage of the BSRC is that it will operate on a self-regulatory basis and benefit from industry experience, engagement, commitment, funding and voluntary enforcements which may promote public trust and confidence and placate media and political criticism. Difficulties nevertheless remain in terms of additional cost, regulatory overlap, standards conflict, consistency, weak enforcement and limited overall effectiveness.

The question specifically asked for comment on the ability of the BSRC to increase ethical standards with a substantial body of ethical provisions already having been provided for under FCA PRIN, PRA Fundamental Rules, SYSC and COND, COCON and FIT, COBS, BCOBS and MCOB as well as with the statutory prohibitions imposed on market abuse, misleading statements and impressions and insider trading and general criminal and civil law. Many of the regulatory obligations imposed are principally based on integrity which is concerned with complying with expected high standards of professional conduct in any particular situation. Separate codes of ethics and standards are also applied by, for example, the CISI, Chartered Financial Analyst (CFA) and Association for Financial Professionals (AFP) and other professional bodies.

While the PCBS had intended that ethical standards were applied by firms and individuals, it has been confirmed that the BSRC will only be involved with firm rather than individual standards. The establishment of separate standard in the banking as opposed to other financial areas will create significant inconsistency. The need for a separate standards body may also appear to be inconsistent with the specific policy of establishing the FCA as a conduct regulator. Transferring banking standards to a separate body appears to contradict the basis for the new regulatory division and regime established under the Financial Services Act 2012.

Unfortunately, neither the PCBS nor Banking Standards Review reports considered the meaning, nature, source and content of financial ethics and the relationship between the existing provisions currently applicable. The simple solution would have been for the FCA to prepare an Ethical sourcebook within its Handbook (possibly referred to as ETHIC) to clarify these essential points and to provide additional guidance on the application of these provisions in specific areas either in the new Ethics sourcebook or separately.

No specific answers were expected with candidates only having to outline the various issues and arguments involved.

## C41.

### Question

Discuss the extent to which the Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR) assists to ensure that key personnel within financial firms carry out their functions responsibly without any threat to the interests of their clients or the financial stability of the firms concerned.

### Answer

The creation of the new Senior Managers and Certification Regimes was recommended by the Parliamentary Commission for Banking Standards (PCBS) in "Changing Banking for Good" in June 2013. The new regime was provided for under Part 4 of the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013. This replaces the earlier approved persons regime for banks, building societies, credit unions and PRA-designated investment firms, although this would be extended to other firms subsequently. The new regime came into effect on 7 March 2016. The new offence of causing a financial institution to fail was also provided for under s 36 of the 2013 Act. The objective of the new senior managers regime is to ensure that senior managers are held accountable for any misconduct within their specific areas of responsibility.

Firms have to prepare "responsibility maps" to allocate all relevant Senior Management functions (SMFs), with each manager having a statement of responsibility (SOR) (s60(2A) FSMA). Authorised persons must vet candidates before putting them forward for approval to ensure that they are fit and proper in terms of relevant qualifications, training, competence and personal characteristics (s60A). Conditions or time limits may be attached to approvals (s61(2B)) and approval may be varied subsequently (s 63ZB). Dual regulated firms are assessed by the PRA initially, although the PRA and FCA must both be satisfied that any applicant complies with the standards set out in the FSMA. Approved persons were earlier considered in terms of whether they were fit and proper having regard to whether they acted with honesty, integrity and reputation, competency and capability and financial soundness.

The regime applies to persons approved under s59 FSMA to carry out a designated senior management function (SMF) as provided for under s 59ZA FSMA as amended. The PRA has inter alia designated the following senior management functions: Chief Executive (SMF 1); Chief Finance (SMF 2); Executive Director (SMF3); Chief Risk (SMF 4); Head of Internal Audit (SMF 5); Head of Key Business Area (SMF 6); Group Entity Senior Manager (SMF 7); Credit Union Senior Manager (SMF 8); Chairman (SMF 9); Chairman of Risk Committee (SMF 10); Chair of Audit Committee (SMF 11); Chair of Remuneration Committee (SMF 12); Chair of Nomination Committee (SMF 13); Senior Independent Director (SMF 14); Compliance Oversight (SMF 16); Money Laundering Reporting (SMF 17); Other Overall Responsibility (SMF 18); Head of Overseas Branch (incoming non-EEA branches only) (SMF 19); and Other Local Responsibility Function (incoming non-EEA branches only) (SMF 22) (PRA March 2016).

A separate certification regime for deposit taking firms (other than credit unions) is created for other personnel that may pose a risk of significant harm to the firm or a customer. Firms are to certify the fitness and propriety of staff every year (s 63E FSMA) without pre-approval as under the earlier approved persons regime. The same conditions generally apply regarding certification as approval, including qualifications, training, competence and characteristics (s 63F).

A new set of Conduct Rules (COCON) have been created (under s 64A FSMA), although these largely replicate the earlier APER rules. COCON contains five individual conduct (IC) rules covering integrity (COCON 2.1.1), skill, care and diligence (COCON 2.1.2), openness and co-operation with regulators (COCON 2.1.3 earlier APER 3), due regard to customers' interests (COCON 2.1.4) and observance of proper standards of market conduct (COCON 2.1.5 earlier APER 3). COCON also contains four senior manager (SM) conduct rules covering taking reasonable steps to ensure business is controlled effectively (COCON 2.2.1 earlier APER 5), reasonable steps to ensure business complies with relevant regulatory requirements (COCON 2.2.3 earlier APER 7), reasonable steps to ensure appropriate delegation and oversight (COCON 2.2.3) and disclosure of all appropriate information to the FCA or PRA (COCON 2.2.4). Firms must ensure that all persons are properly notified of the COCON rules and take all reasonable steps to ensure that they understand how the rules apply to them (COCON 2.3.1), including through suitable training (COCON 2.3.2). COCON contains additional detailed guidance on the meaning of each of the rules (COCON 4), which expands the earlier guidance provided in the APER of Conduct of Practice (APER 3 and 4).

Candidates could have commented on the potential value of the new regime in improving conduct standards. While this is intended to make senior managers more accountable, the senior manager pool is considerably smaller than under the approved persons regime. Certification also transfers responsibility for other approvals from the authorities to firms. International banks have argued that the additional personal liability will act as a significant disincentive to attracting the most appropriate talent in London. All senior managers have been advised to ensure that they are insured and fall within banks' directors' and officers' insurance, and possibly to ask for indemnities in connection with potential regulatory action against the firm. All other significant staff have to be certified annually, with employment histories having to be considered for up to 6 years. This may, again, act as a significant disincentive to attracting high-quality staff.

## C58.

### Question

Is a fine just the price of doing business for many firms? Discuss the cost of noncompliance for financial firms.

### Answer

There is a long-standing concern in all areas of regulation that enforcement actions may not always deter and limit the level of offending or breach of regulations. Fines are a particular area of concern with examples where fines may be seen as a cost of doing business in some regulated areas. In the responses, we would like to see a general discussion of the costs of non-compliance and the use of fines in the UK financial system. An initial point is that fines are one of many regulatory punishments. These punishments are diverse and influence the firms' or individuals' ability to work in the financial services industry.

Considering some of these punishments and other costs of non-compliance is useful. For example, it would be helpful to detail outcomes such as prohibition or variation of regulatory permissions. Similarly, the focus of punishment has also shifted towards individuals such as senior managers and compliance staff. This is undertaken to alter corporate viewpoints of non-compliance, from considering the cost of the fine to considering personal and career influencing outcomes of non-compliance.

The scale of financial regulation fines in the UK has also increased over time and reflects the concern that the cost of the offending and the probability of being caught for a breach of regulation should be matched by the scale of fines levied. This increase in the level of fines is aimed at enhancing what might be viewed as an effective deterrent. These fines are levied using a range of different criteria yet should reflect the profits or benefits of the offending firm/individual from the offending through a process of disgorgement. Such fines also reflect other contributory factors and the seriousness of the breach of regulation. There are also penalty discounts which may be applied, for example, for settling the dispute with the regulator in a timely manner and conceding liability. For example, in the associated text for the workbook it outlines the level of fines and includes the following level of fines in recent years. This reflects a movement in regulatory punishments:

- 2017 – £229,515,303 (13 fines)

- 2018 – £60,467,212 (15 fines)

- 2019 – £392,303,087 (21 fines)

- 2020 – £192,570,018 (11 fines)

- 2021 – £567,765,219 (10 fines)

## C61.

### Question

"The regulatory regime established under the FSMA is, to a significant extent, based on internal management accountability and liability rather than external supervision and sanction." Discuss.

### Answer

The main obligations imposed on internal management should be discussed and contrasted with the external powers of supervision available to the FCA under the FSMA and Handbook of Rules and Guidance.

The main obligations applicable with regard to management are set out in the Block 1 High Level Standards including the requirements with regard to principles for businesses (PRIN), systems and controls (SYSC), code of conduct (COCON) and fit and proper test for approved persons (FIT). Each of the relevant requirements should have been outlined and discussed. General issues of liability and sanction should have been referred to including potential personal liability (s138D).

The importance of internal management should then be contrasted with the external supervisory powers available to the FCA. The powers of the FCA should be referred to (including information gathering and investigatory powers, reports by skilled persons, authorisation and permission, redress, disciplinary action and private warnings, public statements of misconduct, fines, variation or cancellation of permission and approval). Relevant decision-taking procedures and requirements could also have been referred to.

There is no need to come to any specific conclusion but only to identify the relevant provisions that apply with regard to both internal management and external supervision and sanctions.

## C62.

### Question

Evaluate the role of Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) enforcement actions in deterring financial misconduct and maintaining market integrity. Have fines and regulatory interventions been effective in changing corporate behaviour? Are there areas where enforcement could be improved?

### Answer

This question asked candidates to evaluate the role of the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in using enforcement actions to deter financial misconduct and uphold market integrity. Candidates are expected to assess the effectiveness of fines and regulatory interventions in changing corporate behaviour, and to consider whether there are areas where the FCA's enforcement approach could be improved. Answers missed marks by failing to evaluate the effectiveness of enforcement critically; particularly the debate over whether fines alone change corporate culture or simply serve as a cost of doing business.

The FCA plays an important role in maintaining market integrity through enforcement actions. These include investigating misconduct, imposing fines, banning individuals from financial services, and taking criminal enforcement action when necessary. Such interventions are intended not only to punish wrongdoing but also to act as a deterrent to future misconduct by firms and individuals. There have been high-profile cases where enforcement has proven effective, such as significant fines issued for market manipulation, mis-selling of financial products, or AML failings. These outcomes signal that the FCA is willing to act decisively, reinforcing its commitment to protecting consumers and ensuring fair, transparent markets. Enforcement also provides redress where appropriate, which can help restore consumer confidence.

However, critics argue that for large firms, fines may be viewed as a cost of doing business. This raises questions about whether enforcement leads to genuine cultural change. The Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR) was introduced partly in response to this, aiming to improve personal accountability. But questions remain over whether senior individuals are routinely held accountable when systemic failings occur. The FCA has recognised the need to improve in this area. In 2024, it announced plans to increase the speed and transparency of enforcement cases. This includes streamlining investigation processes, publishing more updates on ongoing cases, and ensuring decisions are communicated clearly. These reforms are intended to bolster public trust and reinforce the credibility of enforcement actions.

A further proposal that has drawn public attention is the idea of 'naming and shaming' firms under investigation before a final outcome has been reached. While this proposal has been controversial and may not be in place when the exam is taken, it reflects broader concerns about how effective enforcement is in influencing firm behaviour. Should candidates raise this point, they should consider arguments on both sides, including its potential to improve transparency versus the risk of reputational damage prior to due process. FCA enforcement is a vital regulatory tool but is not without limitations. While it does deter some misconduct, enforcement alone may not bring about lasting cultural change unless supported by proactive supervision, clear accountability, and meaningful individual consequences.

Improvements in transparency and speed, alongside further debate around initiatives like naming and shaming, suggest enforcement is evolving to meet public and regulatory expectations.
