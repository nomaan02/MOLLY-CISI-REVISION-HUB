## C11.

### Question

Assess the extent to which the conflicting objectives of increased market competition and efficiency can be reconciled with the maintenance of high ethical standards in modern markets and proper financial regulation.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to consider the relationship between competition and efficiency with the maintenance of high ethical standards. This could have been answered in a number of ways. The simplest may have been to have referred to the regulators' basic statutory objectives and the supporting supervisory principles in FSMA. Competition and efficiency are specifically included within the statutory regulatory framework set up under the FSMA.

Competition is specifically dealt with regard to international character, distortion and intra-firm competition.

The regulatory objectives identified should then have been contrasted with the need to maintain high ethical standards at all times. The FSA October 2002 Discussion Paper 18 on Ethics may have been referred to. Of more importance was the need to refer to the structure and content of the high-level standards imposed on all regulated institutions under Block 1 of the FCA Handbook. More specific reference may have then been made to particular sections within Block 1 to the extent that time was available. This specifically includes PRIN, COCON and SYSC as well as FIT and COND. Reference should also have been made to other supporting codes of standards such as the Code of Conduct issued by the CISI.

## C17.

### Question

"The effectiveness of the Financial Promotions regime has been achieved at the expense of unacceptable complexity." Discuss.

### Answer

Certainly, there is some overlap in the sources of rules surrounding financial promotions. Ignoring general case law and the requirements of the Advertising Standards Authority there are three main sets of rules governing Financial Promotions.

In FSMA, s.21, there is a legal restriction on the issue of advertisements, making it illegal to issue a financial promotion without being authorised or having the advertisement approved by an authorised person.

The Financial Promotion Order then contains some exemptions to s.21 for example the annual report and accounts exemption and exemptions to articles by journalists and promotions aimed only at investment professionals.

The FCA's Conduct of Business Sourcebook then sets out various rules relating to financial promotions, covering such matters as the fair, clear and not misleading rule, special requirements for communicating with retail clients, rules on disclosure of information about the past, simulated past and future performance of investment products, and rules on direct offer financial promotions and cold calls.

There is certain logic to the structure of the regulations. There is little detail in FSMA on the form and content of promotions. This is due to the inherently time-consuming nature of lawmaking – it is much more flexible for the Treasury to update the Financial Promotions Order (FPO) on the types of promotions that are or are not covered by the regulations and for the FCA to adapt their rules on the restrictions and relaxations in COBS than it would be for parliament to debate and write amendments into law.

Additionally, there is little overlap in the two main sources of detail – if you need to see if a type of promotion is exempt you turn to the FPO. If the promotion is covered then you look to the COBS to find information on the specific form and content permitted for that type of promotion.

Are the rules complex? Undoubtedly yes. Could the rules be written in a less complex manner that remained equally effective? Perhaps not. The three sources of legislation are each of a manageable size and written within a structure that means they can be appropriately amended should the need arise.

## C18.

### Question

Discuss the extent to which remuneration and bonus packages raise ethical issues that have to be dealt with by regulated firms and their non-executive boards of directors.

### Answer

The Conservative Government under Liz Truss ended the controls over bank bonuses that had been put into place in the wake of the Great Financial Crisis, and as such, this is a highly relevant and current topic that you can expect to be tested from time to time over the years to come.

Regulatory concerns arise with regard to the possible risk distorting effects that unbalanced incentive packages can have on risk management with consequent effects on firm and market instability.

The question could have been dealt with in a number of ways. Reference could, for example, have been made to the FCA's statutory objectives and supervisory principles in FSMA as well as to the high-level standards contained in PRIN, SYSC and COCON, COND, FIT and the CISI Code of Conduct.

Specific provisions include PRIN 1 (integrity), 3 (management and control), 6 (customers' interest), 8 (conflicts of interest) and 11 (relations with regulators); SYSC 2 (senior management arrangements), 3 (systems and controls including remuneration policies), 7 (risk controls) and 10 (conflicts of interest);

COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence) and 3 (regulatory relations); FIT 1 (honesty, integrity and reputation) and 2 (competence and capability); and CISI 1 (honesty and fairness), 2 (integrity), 5 (conflicts of interest) and 8 (high personal standards).

Reference may also have been made to COBS 9 (suitability), 10 (appropriateness), 12 (conflicts of interest), 2.3 (inducements), 11.6 (dealing commissions), 11.2 (best execution) and possibly 11.7 (personal account dealing).

Reference could have been made to the evolution of the rules that Truss's government repealed, including the regulator's CEO letter concerning remuneration packages in banks and the draft Code issued in March 2009 and possibly to the recent additional issues that arose with the partial nationalisation of major UK financial institutions in 2008. The FCA policy would generally appear to be to leave this to be dealt with within specific financial institutions in particular at the board level or through existing management arrangements for other senior staff. Firms must nevertheless avoid increasing or aggravating firm risks and exposures which fall within their risk management capability.

Candidates should also have included discussion on relevant ethical rather than purely legal or regulatory aspects of the problem. Reference could have been made to the regulator's general discussion papers and general approach in this area. Reference may also have been to the risk of conflicts of interest or cross-referencing to relevant high-level standards.

## C23.

### Question

Discuss the extent to which financial ethics should be subject to further statutory and regulatory control, beyond the protections already provided for under the FCA Handbook of Rules and Guidance and the common law.

### Answer

The question was concerned with the extent to which financial ethics are sufficiently dealt with under the FCA Handbook or whether additional legislative or statutory requirements are necessary.

Candidates could initially have referred to the extent to which financial ethics are promoted under the current FCA Handbook and supporting guidance and other FSA/FCA papers. This includes the draft Code of Ethics in CP09/18, CP10/12 on Competence and Ethics and the Conduct Rules under COCON. Reference may also have been made to Hector Sants' speech on 'Regulatory Culture' in October 2010 and FSA discussion paper on ethics in October 2002.

Specific reference could have been made to particular FCA Handbook provisions or policies including PRIN, SYSC and COCON as well as the TCF, RDR and new product regulation rules.

A statutory basis for ethical conduct is also provided under the Approved Persons Requirement (s59 FSMA), Code of Conduct (s64 FSMA) and Action for Damages (s71 FSMA). As well as more general action for statutory damages for breach of an FCA rule under s138D FSMA, both sets of damages under ss71 and 138D are nevertheless narrowly defined and would limit more general discretionary recovery under a loose unethical behaviour principle.

A number of difficulties would then arise in attempting to formulate a statutory rule for ethical conduct. Specific difficulties would arise regarding definition, certainty, defences and exceptions, compliance and proof, penalties and enforcement. It is also arguable that a general unspecified requirement would limit firms' legitimate practices, stifle innovation and impose unnecessary regulatory and legal costs and burdens.

## C30.

### Question

Critically evaluate the role of organisational culture in influencing conduct within financial institutions. How does the FCA's approach to culture and governance aim to enhance market integrity, and what steps can firms take to align their culture with regulatory expectations?

### Answer

This question asked candidates to critically evaluate the role of organisational culture in influencing conduct within financial institutions. How does the FCA's approach to culture and governance aim to enhance market integrity, and what steps can firms take to align their culture with regulatory expectations? Again, this was an open-ended question in which a lot of material could be included.

Organisational culture plays an important role in influencing conduct and decision-making within financial institutions. Recognising this, the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) places significant emphasis on culture and governance as key factors in promoting market integrity and consumer protection. Organisational culture comprises the shared values, beliefs, and norms that guide behaviour within an institution. A positive culture encourages ethical behaviour, accountability, and a focus on customer outcomes, while a poor culture can lead to misconduct, systemic failures, and significant harm to consumers and markets.

Key ways in which culture influences conduct include:

- Tone from the Top – Leadership behaviour sets the standard for acceptable practices. Leaders who prioritise ethical behaviour and transparency foster a culture where misconduct is less likely to occur.

- Incentive Structures – Reward systems that align with long-term, customer-focused goals discourage short-termism and risky behaviours.

- Communication and Training – Effective communication and regular training reinforce the importance of compliance and ethical conduct.

- Accountability Mechanisms – Clear accountability ensures individuals are responsible for their actions, which deters misconduct.

Historically, cultural failures have been central to financial scandals, such as the LIBOR manipulation and the mis-selling of financial products, underscoring the critical need for strong cultural frameworks. The FCA does not prescribe a one-size-fits-all culture but focuses on outcomes, encouraging firms to cultivate environments that prioritise good conduct. The Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR) for example is intended to enhance individual accountability by requiring senior managers to have clearly defined responsibilities, promoting a culture of ownership and ethical leadership. The FCA has also noted that a firm's governance structures should effectively support its purpose and values, ensuring decisions align with desired cultural outcomes. Remuneration and incentive systems should be designed to ensure they do not inadvertently encourage misconduct.

Through regular supervisory interactions, the FCA assesses cultural indicators, such as employee feedback mechanisms, decision-making processes, and the handling of misconduct. The FCA regularly publishes speeches, discussion papers, and guidance that highlight the importance of culture, fostering industry-wide dialogue and awareness. By adopting this multifaceted approach, the regulator aims to encourage firms to embed cultures that naturally drive good conduct.

Firms should articulate a clear set of values that prioritise ethical behaviour and customer welfare, ensuring these are communicated effectively throughout the organisation. Senior management should exemplify the desired culture, demonstrating commitment through their actions and decisions. Remuneration and promotion criteria should reward behaviours that align with the firm's values and penalise misconduct. Regular training programmes should reinforce the importance of compliance, ethical decision-making, and understanding of regulatory expectations. Systems that allow employees to report concerns without fear of reprisal are vital. Firms should actively encourage and act upon such feedback. Cultural considerations should be embedded into all business processes, from product development to customer interactions. Using tools like employee surveys, conduct metrics, and independent audits, firms should continuously evaluate their cultural health. The FCA's approach, which emphasises accountability, governance, and outcome-based assessments, seeks to foster cultures that inherently discourage misconduct. However, it is important for firms to proactively cultivate and maintain cultures that align with both regulatory expectations and ethical standards. Striking a balance between prescriptive requirements and a holistic understanding of risk is essential – sometimes, less prescriptive measures can empower firms to use their own judgement, leading to more effective and nuanced risk management. Ultimately, less may indeed be more when it comes to regulatory guidance in relation to culture, enabling firms to better tailor their responses to the specific risks they face.

## C42.

### Question

Explain the nature of the FCA's approach to conduct risk and comment on the extent to which it has been able to create a clear and meaningful regulatory framework to contain this over time.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to explain the nature of the FCA's approach to conduct risk and comment on the extent to which it has been able to create a clear and meaningful regulatory framework to contain this over time. The question could have been dealt with by explaining the statutory functions of the FCA and possibly contrasting these with the PRA. Relevant papers concerning the FCA's approach to conduct risk could have been noted and the significant uncertainties that remain in this regard. Reference could specifically been made to the FCA's new policy risk model and relevant drivers as well as relevant priority areas of focus. Candidates could also have referred to other relevant high-level regulatory provisions on conduct risk contained in the FCA Handbook.

The FCA has generally been made responsible for conduct regulation with the PRA managing prudential regulation under the revised institutional structure established under the FSA 2012. The FCA is specifically responsible for conduct risk although this is a difficult issue to define precisely. The term conduct risk is not referred to in the FSMA as amended. Conduct risk is not separately defined in the FCA Glossary nor is the term risk separately defined. The objectives of the FCA are referred to under the FSMA in terms of acting as a financial function authority with the specific operational objectives of promoting consumer protection, system integrity and competition (s1B (2) and (3) FSMA). Conduct risk is not separately defined and it would generally appear that this refers to anything that is not prudential regulation.

The FCA has been developing its approach to conduct risk under its 2013 and 2014 Risk Outlook documents with a series of priority actions being set out in its Business Plans for 2013 and 2014. While the 2013 Risk Outlook expressly referred to 'conduct risk', this is simply referred to in terms of risk in the 2014 Risk Outlook and Risk Outlook included within the 2015 Business Plan. The Risk Outlook was incorporated into the FCA Business Plan 2015/16 released on 24 March 2015. The FCA explains its prudential responsibility and prudential risk in its 2014 Risk Outlook at 65-67.

The new policy risk model is generally made up of two parts with Drivers of Risk (Part A) consisting of Underlying drivers (inherent factors and Structures and business conduct features) and Environmental developments) and Evolving risk landscape (Part B) consisting of Cross-market pressures and related risks and Forward-looking areas of focus (2014 Risk Outlook P7). The key drivers are expanded in the circular diagram provided by the FCA in the 2013 and 2014 Risk Outlooks. This refers to Inherent risks (Information asymmetries, Biases (rules of thumb and mental shortcuts) and the growing importance of financial capability), Structures and business conduct (market structures, culture and incentives and conflicts of interest) and Environmental drivers (economic and market, technological and policy and regulatory) (FCA, Risk Outlook (2013) P10; and FCA, Risk Outlook (2014) P8.

Seven forward looking areas of focus were identified in 2014 (technological developments, poor cultures and controls, firms to act against customers' best interest, retirement income products, consumer credit growth, complex terms and conditions and house price growth) which were revised in the 2015 Business Plan (to include technology, poor culture and controls, large back-books and pensions as well as consumer credit affordability, unfair contract terms and systems and controls with regard to financial crime). A number of more specific areas of concern are referred to under these headings.

Candidates may also have referred to relevant high level regulatory provisions covering conduct risk including PRIN 1 (integrity), 5 (market conduct), 6 (customers' interest), 7 (client communication), 8 (conflicts of interest), 9 (trust) and 10 (asset protection) as well as COCON 1 (integrity) and 3 (market conduct), SYSC 10 (conflicts of interest) and 19 (remuneration) as well as COND and FIT more generally. Training requirements may also have been referred to under TC as well as Treating Customers Fairly (TCF) and the Retail Distribution Review (RDR).

This is a difficult area and no specific answer was expected.

## C52.

### Question

Discuss the extent to which the promotion of financial ethics within regulated firms has been impacted by crisis events such as the recent Coronavirus (Covid 19) pandemic in the UK and elsewhere.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to discuss the extent to which the promotion of ethical standards in the UK has been impacted by recent crisis including the Coronavirus crisis 2020. Candidates should have explained the background to PRIN and the Fundamental Rules (FRs) within the larger handbooks and listed each of these. Candidates might have noted that some of these requirements are more prudential or asset rather than conduct rule based with the requirements most principally concerned with ethics including integrity, skill, care and diligence, interest trust, conflicts and communication disclosure (PRIN 1, 2, 6, 7, 8 and 9 and FR 1 and 2).

The key requirement in the ethics area is with regard to integrity. While no separate guidance is provided on this by the FCA or PRA with regard to firms, additional information is provided under COCON with regard to the meaning of the main equivalent provisions in connection with individuals.

Rather than focus on ethics, the FCA has shifted the examination to firm culture. Tracey McDermott gave a speech on 'Wholesale Conduct Risk' before the British Bankers Association on 13 June 2015. Andrew Bailey, former FCA Chief Executive Officer between July 2016 and March 2020 and previously Chief Executive of the PRA, gave a speech on 'Culture in Financial Services – A Regulator's Perspective' at the City Week 2016 Conference on 9 May 2016. Andrew Bailey stressed the importance of culture with culture being of 'the utmost importance to financial regulators'. Culture arose from a range of contributory forces including management and governance, remuneration and incentive structure, management quality and effectiveness and personnel adoption and adherence. All of the recent major prudential or conduct scandals arose from cultural failures in governance, remuneration, risk management and leadership.

Andrew Bailey stated that the authorities could not 'determine the culture of firms', which would not work, and could not 'write a regulatory rule that settles culture' with this being the product of many factors that the regulators could influence including governance, remuneration and risk management. There was 'no magic bullet to change culture' although the new senior managers and certification regime would assist in embedding 'the notion of personal responsibility for the affairs of the firm at the level of senior management.' It was 'not the job of regulators to enforce culture and to change culture' with firm incentives determining culture with the authorities attempting 'to align those incentives with our public interest objectives as well as the private interest of the firm.' Andrew Bailey separately addressed the HKMA Annual Conference for Independent Non-Executive Directors in Hong Kong on 16 March 2017, the Transforming Culture in Financial Services Conference in London on 19 March 2018 and the Investment Association Culture Conference, Mansion House, London on 6 November 2018. The FCA expects financial leaders to manage the drivers of behaviour in their firms to create and maintain cultures which reduce the potential for harm (FCA, 'Culture and governance' (3 August 2020)). Culture is stated to be at 'the heart' of how the FCA authorises and supervises firms. Culture is referred to as 'the habitual behaviours and mind-sets that characterise an organisation' with the FCA focusing on four key drivers of purpose, leadership, rewards and management and governance. The FCA published a collection of 28 papers on 'Transforming Culture in Financial Services' in March 2018 (DP 18/2) which examined what good culture might look like, the role of regulation and regulators, how firms might promote incentives and how to change behaviour for the better. This was followed by a Transforming Culture Conference and publication of an 'Event summary and next steps' paper on 19 March 2018.

The Coronavirus crisis 2020 could influence culture in relation to how firms treat customers, other financial institutions and possibly their own staff. The FCA and PRA issued a number of statements on regulatory adjustments and concessions as a result of the crisis.

The FCA published an Insight article on 'Conduct, culture and covid-19' on 10 June 2020. This noted that the crisis had changed the world and the way that people work within our short time with people having to adapt to the changes. While this created challenges and risks, this was also an opportunity to move from a crisis management approach to considering how businesses should be run more effectively with a strong focus on culture. Firms that failed to do so would produce poor conduct, low staff morale and weak future performance. Those that succeeded would generate positive outcomes for customers, employees and investors. A number of key themes were identified including clear purpose and direction, leadership integrity, risk and opportunity identification, psychological safety, continued diversity and inclusion, support employees and think, plan, experiment and adapt.

The FCA Business Plan for 2020/21 highlighted the importance of the crisis. Five key priorities were listed for the following 1-3 years based on transforming how the FCA works and regulates, enabling effective consumer investment decisions, ensuring that credit markets work well, making payments safe and accessible and delivering fair value in a digital age (FCA Business Plan for 2020/21 (7 April 2020)). The FCA's crisis response had been intended to ensure that markets continue to function in an orderly manner during a major repricing event, issue emergency guidance to allow government schemes to operate, support consumers, assist public access to essential banking services and protect the most vulnerable within society. The FCA would continue to update its operations in response to unfolding events and inform stakeholders appropriately.

Megan Butler, FCA Executive Director of Supervision – Investment, Wholesale and Specialists, gave a speech on 'The FCA's response to Covid-19 and expectations for 2020' at the PIMFA's Virtual Festival on 4 June 2020. While advisers and wealth managers had responded well to the crisis, attention had to shift from 'incident response' to longer-term impact with the FCA focusing on operational resilience, financial resilience and acting with integrity. The FCA specifically criticised firms that had attempted to avoid customer liabilities by closing down companies and setting up new ones. Certain specific crisis response measures were identified based on ensuring a good level of operational resilience, understanding firms' financial resilience to allow them to fail in an orderly manner, ensuring that markets functioned effectively through price formation and orderly trading, treating customers fairly and ensuring that customers were aware of and protected from scams.

Candidates could also have referred to earlier material in this area. A number of general documents and speeches have been issued on ethics over time. The FSA's initial general approach to ethics was set out in FSA, An ethical framework for financial services (October 2002) with competence being examined in FSA, Competence and ethics (June 2010) CP 10/12. Ethics was considered as part of the Retail Distribution Review (RDR) with the FSA recommending the creation of an enforceable Code of Ethics for investment advisors in FSA, Retail Distribution Review (November 2008) Feedback Statement 08/06. This was referred to again in FSA, Distribution of retail investments: Delivering the RDR (June 2009) CP 09/18 with eight general principles proposed. These can be summarised in terms of acting honestly and fairly at all times, integrity, legal and regulatory compliance, market integrity standards, conflict and, professional competence, declining to act and upholding the highest personal and professional standards. FSA Chairman, Howard Davies, discussed ethics in 'Ethics in regulation' in 2001.

FSA CEO Hector Sants switched the focus from ethics to culture in 'Do regulators have a role to play in judging culture and ethics?' in June 2010 and 'Can culture be regulated?' in October 2012. Hector Sants noted in the first speech that when he joined the FSA, he was told by senior management that 'the FSA does not do ethics.' Howard Davies had earlier stated that the FSA could not 'seek to act as the conscience of the square mile.' Rather than focus on ethics, the 'better focus for regulators would be culture, which no doubt reflects the ethical framework of the organisation.' Sants considered that the work ethics was 'not precise enough' and carried 'too much baggage for regulators.' There was nevertheless 'already a consensus of what constitutes the right behaviour', which included through the codes produced by the CISI and Worshipful Company of International Bankers which had 'broad acceptance in the community.' Firms should have 'a culture which encourages individuals to make judgements and deliver the outcomes we want' and that 'at all times we want an undertaking to act with integrity.' The regulators had therefore to focus on 'what an unacceptable culture looks like and what outcomes that drives.'

A number of speeches were made by FCA Chief Executive, Martin Wheatley (such as 'Competing on integrity' (November 2013); 'Ethics and Economics' (March 2014); 'The fairness challenge' (October 2013); and 'Good conduct and market integrity' (June 2014)) and other FCA staff (including Clive Adamson, FCA Director of Supervision, 'The importance of culture in driving behaviours of firms and how the FCA will assess this' (April 2013); and 'Trust and confidence – ensuring firms' ethics are built around their customers' (November 2013); and later Mark Steward, 'Culture and governance' (November 2015)).

Culture was referred to in the FCA Business Plan 2015/16 as a key driver in behaviour with an effective culture being one that supports a business model and business practices that have the fair treatment of customers and clients at their core and support market integrity. Culture began at the top with the FCA ensuring that firms and boards dealt with conduct issues as a priority. Boards had to engage in conduct issues by specifically investigating products or business lines, understanding cross-selling product strategies, possible loss and suitable product sales. Customer focus had also to be translated into how business decisions were made, the treatment of consumer responses, individual behaviour, issue escalation and remuneration and incentive awards.

The FCA had published a separate statement on 'Culture in banking' following the decision not to proceed with the conduct of thematic review on culture in wholesale banking as announced in its 2015/16 Business Plan.

The emerging UK ethical framework is reflected in the more specific provisions contained within the High Level Standards and other more specific laws and regulations designed to ensure ethical outcomes. No separate ethical sourcebook has been prepared at this time although the issue of banking standards is to be considered by the new Banking Standards Board (BSB) set up following the Banking Standards Review (May 2014) by Sir Richard Lambert after the earlier recommendations for the establishment of a professional body by the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) final report on Changing banking for good (12 June 2013) Vols I and II.

Candidates could also have referred to the specific requirements imposed under FCA PRIN, SYSC, COCON (and PRA FR) as well as COND for firms and FIT for individuals. While a number of PRIN are more prudential based including 3 (management and control), and 4 (financial prudence), others are more ethical and conduct based including 1 (integrity), 6 (customers' interests), 7 (communication), 8 (conflicts of interest), 9 (trust) and asset protection (10) as well as skill, care and diligence within PRIN 2. This is also reflected in COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), and 5 (proper standards of market conduct). Conflicts of interest are dealt with under SYSC 10 and remuneration under SYSC 19A. Elements of this are also reflected under more specific COBS including 3 (categorisation), 4 (communication and financial promotions), 6 (disclosure), 8 (agreements), 9 (suitability), 10 (appropriateness), 11 (dealing and managing), 12 (investment research), 13 and 14 (product information), 15 (cancellation) and 16 (reporting).

Reference may also have been made to the business standards sourcebooks including Conduct of Business (COBS), Insurance: Conduct of Business (ICOBS), Mortgages and Home Finance: Conduct of Business (MCOB) and Banking: Conduct of Business (BCOBS).

A number of other statutory provisions can also be considered to include an ethical element such as the prohibition on market abuse under the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) EU/596/2014 and misleading statements and impressions in ss89-90 FSA 2012 as well as separate insider dealing offences in the CJA.

## C55.

### Question

Comment on the effectiveness of the work programme of the Banking Standards Board (BSB) to increasing ethical standards in the banking and financial area within the UK.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to comment on the effectiveness of the new Banking Standards Board (BSB) in increasing ethical standards in the banking and financial area within the UK following the production of its Annual Report for 2016-2017 and other documents.

The BSB assumed the functions of the Banking Standards Review Council (BSRC), which was set up following the review conducted by Sir Richard Lambert with a consultation document being issued in February 2014, and final report in May 2014. This had been referred to in the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) final report on Changing Banking for Good (12 June 2013) with higher professional standards to be promoted in the banking area including through the development of a new set of core standards. The BSRC was not to act as a lobby body and would be funded by the industry while acting in an independent manner.

Sir Richard Lambert recommended that participating institutions commit to a programme of continuous improvement of culture, competence and customer outcomes with various recommendations being made with regard to the BSRC (and later BSB) specifically. It should establish standards of good practice, report annually, meet with non-executive directors and risk or reputation committee chairs, work with industry and stakeholders to develop a new code of practice, identify and encourage good practice and learning, development and leadership, assist compliance and work with professional bodies to improve training and qualifications. Nine specific recommendations were made with regard to purpose, governance, funding and reporting, arrangements, engagement, institutional relations, regulatory cooperation, raising standards of conduct over time, stakeholder involvement, training and avoidance of duplication of function.

The principal advantage of the BSB is that it would operate on a self-regulatory basis and benefit from industry experience, engagement, commitment, funding and voluntary enforcements which may promote public trust and confidence and placate media and political criticism. Some concerns nevertheless arose in terms of additional cost, regulatory overlap, standards conflict, consistency, weak enforcement and limited overall effectiveness.

The BSB specifically operates through research, action proposals and the production of good practice guidelines in the form of Statements of Good Practice and supplementary Supporting Guidance. The BSB has released a Statement of Good Practice on fitness and propriety with further guidance to follow on Certification and Regulatory References.

The 2016-2017 was the first Annual Report produced by the BSB and was based on the responses received to the BSB 28,000 Employee Survey and other comments made. The Review confirms that a large number of good practices had been adopted and positive developments had occurred in raising standards of behaviour and competence although changes were required in attitudes and behaviour that were detrimental to the interests of customers and clients. Progress was required in the areas of responsiveness, accountability, personal resilience and openness. The BSB confirmed that the review was not a single event with annual reviews to be conducted to ensure that culture operated in the interests of consumers. A number of specific priority themes were identified, in particular, with regard to alignment between values and businesses, creating a culture of responsibility and accountability and promoting personal resilience and well-being among employees.

The BSB has separately provided reports to 22-member banks on their internal performances with the results being discussed with the specific firms. The firms were asked to demonstrate the extent to which they implemented the BSB's Assessment Framework, which was based on nine principles (consisting of honesty, respect, openness, accountability, competence, reliability, responsiveness, personal and organisational resilience and shared purpose). Assessments would be carried out annually.

The BSB delivered earlier provisional comments (report cards) for the seven founding members (Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds Banking Group, Royal Bank of Scotland, Santander, Standard Chartered and the Nationwide Building Society) and three other banks sitting on its board. These constituted provisional assessments of behaviour and culture within the major banks. The institutions were asked to explain their goals and ambitions and specifically the purpose, objectives, methods of achieving the objectives and means of assessing these achievements. The BSB has accordingly made an important provisional attempt to assess the extent to which the banks achieve their own internal objects rather to set separate single common standards of behaviour.

The question asked for comment on the ability of the BSB to increase ethical standards. A substantial body of ethical provisions already having been provided for under FCA PRIN, PRA Fundamental Rules, SYSC and COND, COCON and FIT, COBS, BCOBS and MCOB as well as with the statutory prohibitions imposed on market abuse, misleading statements and impressions and insider trading and general criminal and civil law. Many of the regulatory obligations imposed are principally based on integrity that is concerned with complying with expected high standards of professional conduct in any particular situation. Separate codes of ethics and standards are also applied by, for example, the CISI, Chartered Financial Analyst (CFA) and Association for Financial Professionals (AFP) and other professional bodies.

The FCA had earlier stated in its 2015 Business Plan that it would conduct an internal review into banking culture and specifically whether promotion and incentives have contributed to scandals within the UK and abroad. The FCA later announced on 31 December 2015 that this review would be abandoned with two separate reviews of incentive structures in financial products sales and the use of customer information by insurance companies. The FCA stated that a traditional thematic review would not achieve its desired outcomes with the FCA focusing on encouraging the delivery of cultural change within individual institutions. The Treasury denied involvement although the Government had appeared to have adopted a more supportive approach to the banking industry with the earlier reduction in the bank levy.

The development of a new ethical standards framework remains a difficult issue in the UK and elsewhere. A substantial amount of work has been carried out although it is arguable that a clear framework has yet emerged. The new BSB may be able to make a further more substantial contribution over time although it is too early to judge.

Candidates could specifically have commented on the extent which the BSB can supplement this other work and assist promote appropriate standards across the banking area. No specific answers were expected with candidates only having to outline the various issues and arguments involved.

## C60.

### Question

Discuss what is meant by the term 'a fit and proper person' in the context of financial regulation and how a firm should assess employees and potential employees. (In your answer reference for example SMCR, APER and COCON as appropriate).

### Answer

UK financial regulation has long focused on the requirement for a person acting in a regulated role to have the required competence and honesty and integrity to undertake the role appropriately.

For example, under the Approved Persons (APER) regime the financial regulator approves individuals to undertake specified controlled functions. Part of this form of regulation is a requirement to adhere to the FCA's Statements of Principle for Approved Persons and Code of Practice for Approved Persons.

For instance, the four general principles for approved persons expect an individual to:

1. act with integrity in carrying out his controlled function

2. act with due skill, care and diligence in carrying out his controlled function

3. observe proper standards of market conduct in carrying out his controlled function

4. deal with the FCA and other regulators in an open and cooperative way and disclose appropriately any information which the FCA would reasonably expect.

_**[Tutor note: The examiner's reference to APER is curious, to say the least, given the fact that APER is now obsolete for all but a handful of appointed representatives of authorised firms. APER has been replaced by SMCR for all authorised firms from 2019. Note that the question requirement did not require reference to APER, but suggested that you could have referred to APER if you so chose].**_

The Conduct rules (COCON) are expected levels of behaviour seen across the financial industry by all relevant employees. These five rules, termed first tier rules, are applied to all employees of firms regulated by the financial regulator. The first three rules also apply to firms regulated by the prudential regulator:

Rule 1 You must act with integrity.

Rule 2 You must act with due skill, care and diligence.

Rule 3 You must be open and cooperative with the FCA, the PRA and other regulators.

Rule 4 You must pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly.

Rule 5 You must observe proper standards of market conduct.

This requirement was established in the Approved Persons Regime and continued in the Senior Managers Regime. These requirements outline that a person undertaking a regulated or controlled function needs to demonstrate a range of personal criteria to be suitable and acceptable for such a role. A person with acceptable criteria would be fit and proper for the role. Such personal criteria would include but are not limited to:

- honesty, integrity and reputation

- competence and capability

- financial soundness

These characteristics can be wide-ranging. For instance, honesty, integrity, and reputation would include checks as to whether the person has past convictions, is subject to any past or ongoing complaints, has been or is insolvent or has breached legal or regulatory norms in the past. Competence and capability would relate to whether the person has appropriate qualifications, training, and work history to be allowed to undertake a regulated role. This would also include the personal suitability and the potential medical lifestyle concerns which may limit the competence of an individual to undertake a regulated role. Lastly financial soundness would indicate if someone is solvent or subject to a debt order or similar. These financial criteria are important as the financial condition of an individual can influence decision making in some circumstances and situations.

In assessing the utility of this criteria, it is useful to consider examples; for instance, Paul Flowers working as the chairman at the Cooperative Bank is a suitable example. This appointment breached fit and proper person requirements in terms of the level of training (which was insufficient) and also in terms of lifestyle whereby Mr Flowers had troublesome drug addiction issues which might have been expected to impair his decision making.

Discussion of the process and procedures through which fit and proper status may be attributed is also useful to discuss.
