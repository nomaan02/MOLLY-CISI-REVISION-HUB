# Section C — Regulatory Framework & Structure

> Each Question carries 15 marks in total.

## C1.

### Question

Explain the mechanisms through which the FCA is made accountable for its activities under the regulatory regime set up under the FSMA.

### Answer

The accountability of the FCA is secured through a combination of the following:

- HM Treasury appoints the Chairman and governing body of the FCA.

- HMT receives a formal annual report from the FCA.

- HMT reviews of the economy, efficiency and effectiveness of discharge of the FCA's functions.

- HMT inquiries into defined events (systems threat or listing failure) with power to appoint, obtain information and report.

- Internal corporate governance with non-executive directors and effective internal delegation, monitoring, complaints and record systems. The FCA is expected to meet generally applicable standards of corporate governance such as the UK Corporate Governance Code to the extent that it is applicable.

- Annual public meeting and meeting report.

- Internal complaints arrangements.

- Duty to establish and to consult various panels (FCA Practitioner, Smaller Business Practitioner, Markets Practitioner and Consumer) and to consider the panels' opinions.

- Appeal against FCA decisions is possible to the Tribunal.

- Use of policy statements, discussion papers and consultation papers.

- Judicial review under general law or misfeasance in public office.

Reference should also have been made to the FCA's statutory objectives and to the principles of good regulation.

## C2.

### Question

The purposes of FSMA would be equally well achieved by abolishing the conduct of business rules and retaining only authorisation and compensation rules. Discuss.

### Answer

The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000, as it was originally enacted, was in essence a piece of consumer protection legislation aimed principally at protecting the private investor. It does this by requiring that all persons wishing to engage in regulated activities as defined by the Act are authorised to do so and that, once authorised, they are obliged to follow certain conduct of business rules, which concern their dealings with customers. If an authorised firm goes out of business owing its customers money which cannot be recovered through the insolvency laws, there is a compensation scheme set up by the FCA that will compensate investors (up to certain limits).

The requirement to become authorised has certainly been effective over the years in putting out of business those firms that do not satisfy the threshold conditions (currently to be found at FSMA, Sch. 6, and COND). This is obviously a very fundamental protection for investors. Any unauthorised regulated activity (such as unauthorised investment business) is a criminal offence, and the perpetrator is liable to a maximum two-year jail sentence and a fine. In addition, any contracts entered into are unenforceable against the customer.

Whilst this is obviously very important, it is not good enough for a firm to demonstrate that it was once able to pass a threshold ('fit and proper') test. It is important that it continues to behave itself correctly and, as an adjunct to this, it is liable to punishment under the FCA disciplinary rules if it fails to achieve the required standard in the conduct of its business with customers. To this end, the FCA has established 11 Principles for Businesses (PRIN), other High Level Standards (SYSC, etc) and extensive, detailed Conduct of Business (COBS) Rules.

In addition to this, a firm is expected to reach a minimum standard of financial security. This is achieved through the capital adequacy (financial resources) rules (PRU), which ensure that all firms meet certain minimum capital requirements depending on the nature of their activities. Furthermore, the client money (and other assets) rules (CASS) require a firm to keep its clients' money separate from that of the firm at all times.

If, in spite of this, the firm becomes insolvent owing customers money (whether through management incompetence or fraudulent practice, or for some other reason), then the Financial Services Compensation Scheme comes into play for eligible claimants (as defined). This allows for payment to eligible claimants of (at least part of) their otherwise unrecoverable losses.

There are those who think that the costs of compliance are excessive and that compensation scheme payouts are inadequate. In consequence, it could be argued that it would be better to do away the conduct of business rules entirely and pump a proportion of the costs saved into a better compensation scheme.

What this argument misses, though, is the fact that a payout from the compensation scheme is only available when a firm has become insolvent and not when a customer is dissatisfied with a firm for any other reason. The compensation scheme does not protect investors who have suffered from mismanagement or poor advice at the hands of a firm.

Institutional investors may need only limited protection, but private investors would be likely to lose confidence in the integrity of the markets if the conduct of business rules were abolished. The consequence could be that fewer retail investors would wish to enter the market if there was no formal regulatory control over the way in which transactions were conducted.

## C3.

### Question

Outline the disciplinary powers of the FCA and comment on the policy behind the process, considering its fairness for firms and individuals as well as its perceived effectiveness, or otherwise, in the eyes of investors.

### Answer

**Disciplinary powers of the FCA**

In the event of a breach of the rules by an authorised firm or approved person, the following powers are available to the FCA:

- Private warning or public statement of misconduct

- Appropriate financial penalties

- Issue of restitution order

- Withdrawal of authorisation or approval.

In cases of market abuse the FCA's powers extend more widely to catch any market user (including, for example, retail investors).

Any penalty has to be preceded by a **warning notice**, which sets out the terms of any public statement or the amount of any financial penalty. Thereafter, any decision to impose a penalty will be accompanied by a **decision notice**.

**Restraints on the powers of the FCA**

The FCA's powers over authorised firms and approved persons are subject to the following restraints:

- The Upper Tribunal (Tax and Chancery Chamber) is available for those who wish to appeal against a decision by the FCA.

- The FCA is required to publish a statement of its policy on financial penalties; and a copy of this statement must be provided to the Treasury.

**Fairness and effectiveness**

In devising an effective disciplinary process, the FCA must seek to balance the need to be firm but fair towards authorised persons with the obligation to provide adequate protection for investors.

One basic element of fairness is the opportunity for those disciplined by the FCA to appeal if they are dissatisfied with a penalty.

As regards effectiveness, the FCA operates on the basis of civil standards of evidence (a case has to be proved "on the balance of probability") rather than criminal standards ("beyond all reasonable doubt"). This means that allegations of misconduct can be dealt with more quickly and efficiently than by recourse to the criminal courts (where the State has shown a reluctance to prosecute in certain areas – for example, insider dealing or market rigging – because of the difficulty of obtaining a conviction).

Unlike the courts, however, there is no obligation on the FCA to "follow precedent", which can give rise to concerns about inconsistency in the FCA's decisions.

**Conclusion**

The imposition of large fines imposed by the FCA on major financial institutions is likely to lead ultimately to a much more expensive marketplace in which investors buy products and services. The target is for the FCA to wield its disciplinary powers in an efficient manner in order to discourage wrongdoing by firms; but it must also be cost-effective in order to avoid running up costs that will ultimately be borne by consumers.

## C9.

### Question

Explain the principal changes brought into effect under the Financial Services Act 2021 and comment on the extent to which they will improve financial regulation in the UK.

### Answer

The Financial Services Act 2021 received Royal Assent on 29 April 2021. This enables the introduction by the FCA of a dedicated or tailored Investment Firms Prudential Regime (IFPR) due to the differences that arise between banks and investment firms, and the effects of this on prudential regulation. Investment firms do not accept deposits or grant traditional loans but provide investment services and carry out investment activities. Regulation can, therefore, be disproportionate, inappropriate and impose unnecessary administrative and compliance burdens in many cases.

The IFPR will apply to investment firms prudentially regulated by the FCA and their holding companies, with systemically important designated investment firms being subject to PRA oversight and CRD 4. This will allow the UK to maintain a more effective prudential regime for systemic and non-systemic investment firms. The IFPR will principally be established through FCA rules which will set firm level requirements. Specific areas to be covered are set out in the Act including capital, liquidity, exposure to concentration risk, reporting, public disclosure, governance arrangements and remuneration policies.

A separate enhanced accountability framework is set up for the FCA to reflect its additional responsibilities in this area. Specific important public policy considerations are to be taken into account including: relevant international standards, the relative standing of the UK as a place for internationally active investment firms to carry on activities, the net zero emissions target set under the Climate Change Act 2008; financial services equivalence and any other matters set out in the HM Treasury regulations.

The Act separately enables HM Treasury and the UK regulators to implement remaining Basel 3.5 implementation or the additional 'finalisation' standards agreed in 2017 to the extent not already given effect to with a parallel accountability framework set up for the PRA regarding these new powers. Further powers are conferred in relation to LIBOR transition arrangements and outstanding contracts.

This follows a recommendation for the adoption of a legislative solution by the Working Group on Sterling Risk-Free Rates in the 'Tough Legacy Report' (Working Group on Sterling Risk-Free Reference Rates, 'Paper on the Identification of Tough Legacy Issues' (May 2020)).

The Chancellor of the Exchequer confirmed in a written statement to Parliament in June 2020 that necessary amendments would be made to the EU Benchmarks Regulation (BMR) (Chancellor of the Exchequer, 'Financial Services Regulation', 23 June 2020). The FCA is given additional powers to manage an orderly wind-down of critical benchmarks, such as LIBOR, following a formal representativeness assessment and to prohibit supervised entities from using a specific benchmark. A new permanent market access regime (referred to as the Gibraltar Authorisation Regime (GAR)) was established to allow reciprocal market access between the UK and Gibraltar. A new Overseas Funds Regime (OFR) was set up to allow overseas collective investment schemes (CISs) to be marketed to UK investors, including retail investors, retail CISs and money market funds. The UK's MiFIR equivalence regime for third country investment firms has been amended. Technical revisions were made to the onshored European Market Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR) including to improve clearing services access. The Act contains other miscellaneous reforms including in relation to the Variation or Cancellation of Part 4A FSMA permissions, Consultation on the Introduction of a Duty of Care on Authorised Firms, Money Laundering, Help to Save, Buy-Now-Pay-Later Products, as well as financial collateral payment services and cash provision.

## C12.

### Question

Discuss the role of Threshold Conditions in the authorisation of firms by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA). How do these conditions ensure that only suitable firms are allowed to operate within the UK financial services market?

### Answer

This question asked candidates to discuss the role of Threshold Conditions in the authorisation of firms by the FCA and PRA; and to evaluate how these conditions ensure that only suitable firms are allowed to operate within the UK financial services market. This was an open-ended question in which a lot of material might be included. The first part of the response should consist of a discussion around the Threshold Conditions. The response should then address how, and to what extent, the Threshold Conditions ensure that only suitable firms are allowed to operate within the UK financial services market. Reference should have been made to the six revised General Principles and to the takeover timetable.

The Threshold Conditions (COND) set out the fundamental regulatory requirements firms must meet to gain and maintain authorisation from the FCA or PRA to operate in the UK financial services market. These conditions are designed to ensure that firms are suitable, financially sound, and capable of operating in a way that supports the stability and integrity of the financial system, while protecting consumers.

The Threshold Conditions form a gateway for firms seeking to operate within the UK's financial market. They set minimum standards in several key areas:

- **Location of Offices** (COND 2.2): Firms must have their head office and principal place of business in the UK if they carry on regulated activities in the UK. This ensures that regulators can exercise effective oversight. For a firm to be supervised adequately, it must be within reach of UK authorities, allowing them to monitor and enforce compliance easily.

- **Effective Supervision** (COND 2.3): Firms must be capable of being effectively supervised by the FCA or PRA. Firms must have systems in place that enable regulatory bodies to assess their compliance with the rules and respond promptly to any supervisory requests. This helps ensure transparency and cooperation between the firm and regulators, mitigating risks posed by inadequate oversight.

- **Appropriate Resources** (COND 2.4): Firms must have sufficient financial and non-financial resources to run their operations safely and effectively. This includes capital adequacy, systems, control functions, and competent personnel. By ensuring firms have the necessary resources to meet obligations, this condition helps prevent firms from becoming financially distressed, reducing risks to customers and the market as a whole.

- **Suitability** (COND 2.5): The firm and its senior management must be 'fit and proper', which includes having the necessary honesty, integrity, competence, and regulatory compliance history. This condition is designed to minimise the risk of misconduct and ensure that those running financial institutions are capable of managing the firm in a compliant and ethical manner.

- **Business Model** (COND 2.7): The firm must demonstrate that its business model is suitable for the regulated activities it seeks to carry out, and that it does not pose a risk to the FCA's objectives, which include protecting consumers and ensuring market integrity. The PRA will focus on whether the business model is sustainable in terms of financial soundness, particularly for firms like banks and insurers, where stability is crucial.

These conditions collectively seek to ensure that firms entering the UK market have the necessary foundations to operate in a manner that supports market stability, minimises risk, and protects consumers. While the Threshold Conditions provide a strong initial barrier to entry for unsuitable firms, there are questions about whether they fully ensure that only suitable firms operate in the market. One potential issue is that the conditions focus heavily on firms at the point of authorisation. While firms must continue to meet these conditions on an ongoing basis, enforcement tends to rely on the regulators identifying issues through supervisory processes. In some cases, firms may deteriorate after gaining authorisation, without immediate intervention from regulators, creating a window of risk.

Moreover, COND 2.3 (Effective Supervision) and COND 2.4 (Appropriate Resources) are somewhat subjective, and their interpretation can vary depending on the complexity of a firm's business model. For example, in fintech, where technology changes rapidly, ensuring effective supervision and that resources are 'appropriate' may be challenging due to the evolving nature of risks in areas such as cybersecurity and data privacy. Regulators may find it difficult to assess or respond to these risks quickly enough, potentially allowing unsuitable firms to slip through the cracks.

Additionally, while the Suitability (COND 2.5) condition aims to ensure that firms are managed by individuals with the necessary integrity and competence, past failures in governance, such as the collapses of financial institutions like BCCI or scandals at firms like Enron, suggest that even stringent fit-and-proper tests are not always foolproof. Dishonest or incompetent behaviour may not be apparent until a firm has been operational for some time, leading to consumer harm or market disruption.

The regulators have taken steps to address some of these issues by placing an emphasis on continuous monitoring and supervision as well as requiring firms to update regulators on significant changes that could affect their ongoing compliance with the Threshold Conditions. Additionally, SM&CR has been introduced to complement the Threshold Conditions by assigning personal accountability to senior managers, thus reinforcing individual responsibility for meeting regulatory standards.

However, there remains a question as to whether the current level of scrutiny and ongoing supervision is sufficient, particularly for firms in fast-moving sectors such as fintech. Stress-testing of business models and more dynamic risk assessments could be further developed to ensure that firms remain suitable as they grow or as market conditions change. While the Threshold Conditions serve as a gateway for ensuring firm suitability, they are not perfect. Firms that pass the initial authorisation may still pose risks over time if ongoing supervision is not sufficiently proactive. Continuous improvement in regulatory monitoring, particularly in sectors with rapid innovation, is essential to ensure that the Threshold Conditions fulfil their purpose in the long term.

## C32.

### Question

Assess the extent to which the regulatory regime brought into effect under the Financial Services Act 2012 assists in preventing future financial crises in the UK, or only imposes disproportionate new regulatory obligation and cost compared to the previous regime.

### Answer

_**[Tutorial Note:** The question asked for an assessment of the effectiveness of the regulatory regime brought into effect under the FSA 2012. The question could have been dealt with in a number of different ways with **no specific answer being expected**. Although these changes took place under the Coalition government some time ago, it is quite likely that the government will seek to make changes to this regime in order to adapt the regulatory regime to the changing macroeconomic and political environment, particularly since Brexit. **Therefore it is a good idea for candidates for this exam to be aware of the background to the current regime, so that in future exams they can comment on future changes to this regime in an authoritative way.]**_

The changes adopted under the FSA 2012 were principally institutional in nature with the establishment of the Financial Policy Committee (FPC), within the Bank of England to assume a new macro-prudential function, and with the replacement of the FSA by a separate bank subsidiary, the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA), and an independent Financial Conduct Authority (FCA).

The PRA is principally responsible for the prudential regulation of banks, insurance companies, larger designated investment firms, credit unions and building societies. The FCA is responsible for the prudential regulation of all other institutions and the conduct regulation of all institutions. FSMA 2000 has been amended to divide the statutory powers and functions of the two institutions with the FSA Handbook being split to reflect these changes. All of the necessary statutory amendments were brought into effect under the FSA 2012 which amended FSMA 2000 as well as include additional specific provisions on Treasury, Bank of England, FCA and PRA collaboration and crisis management (Part 4), inquiries and investigations (Part 5), regulatory complaints (Part 6), offences (Part 7) and Banking Act 2009 amendments (Part 8).

While the UK model has been referred to as a 'Twin Peaks' system, following the Australian regime set up after the Financial System (Wallis) Inquiry in Australia in June 1996, a number of inconsistencies remain, in particular, with the FCA being the prudential regulator of 23,000 and the PRA the prudential regulator of only 1,700 firms (above). The original Twin Peaks papers also did not consider the central bank involvement nor need for separate macroprudential oversight.

Although not referred to in the Treasury or other regulatory documents supporting the establishment of the new regime, this can principally be justified on the basis of involving the creation of a central bank based macroprudential model with the 'subsidiarisation' of systemic function within the central bank and delegation of non-systemic activities to an independent agency.

The advantages of the new regime can be summarised in terms of separate focus, priority or mandate, distinct objectives, separate needs and outcomes with dedicated and specialist staff. These have to be set against the disadvantages of substantially increased cost, significant increase in complexity, inevitable regulatory fragmentation (with the production of separate FCA and PRA Handbooks replacing the earlier FSA and later Combined Handbook), regulatory overlap and duplication, substantial increase in regulatory obligation within firms, substantial increase in management demand within firms, policy conflict and confusion and inconsistent enforcement and sanction.

The FSA 2012 regulatory regime that came into effect in 2013 is far more complex that before and imposes substantial additional regulatory burden and cost on financial firms. These costs will inevitably be passed on to business and household clients. The principal changes are institutional in nature although the crisis was not caused by institutional failings as such.

The principal advantage of the regime introduced in FSA 2012 is that it created a central bank based macro-prudential model. Ever since, however, arguments have raged as to whether the benefits of the associated institutional changes created outweigh the longer term costs and burdens imposed. Institutional reform and structural regulation inevitably increase regulatory costs and obligation with any further additional stability advantages being unclear as successive requirements are imposed. The continuing and additional regulatory burdens imposed on firms have to be balanced against the correspondingly limited marginal benefits that may follow in terms of increased financial stability. Difficult questions of proportion and balance arise.

You can expect such arguments to be tested from time to time in future exams in the years to come.

## C34.

### Question

Comment on the effectiveness of the regime set up under the FSMA (as amended) to prevent or contain any abuse of power or procedure by the regulatory authorities.

### Answer

Accountability is generally secured through the extension of the earlier accountability regime set up under the FSMA to apply to the new regulatory authorities under the amendments brought into effect under the FSA 2012. These are essentially concerned with HM Treasury powers (appointment, annual report, reviews and independent enquiries), annual general meetings, corporate governance requirements, revised complaints procedure, competition scrutiny, Practitioner Panel and Consumer Panel consultation with Upper Tribunal appeals and Judicial Review.

Regulators have to comply with the formal warning and decision notice procedure with notices being brought before the Regulatory Decisions Committee (RDC) with appeal before the Upper Tribunal. All of the regulators are also subject to questioning before the Parliamentary Treasury Committee.

FSA 2012 specifically requires the Bank, FCA and PRA to maintain arrangements for the investigation of complaints against them under a revised complaints scheme. Complaints relate to any expression of dissatisfaction concerning the manner in which the regulators have carried out, or failed to carry out, any relevant functions. Anyone directly affected by any of the regulators' actions or inactions may lodge a complaint. Complaints are investigated by the regulators either jointly or individually as appropriate with a complainant having a right to have the complaint referred to the Office of the Complaints Commissioner (OCC). The Bank, FCA and PRA issued a document on Complaints against the regulators that sets out the relevant procedures with forms being available on the regulators' websites.

In commenting on the effectiveness of the new regime, candidates could have noted that the new regulatory structure is even more concentrated with many regulatory powers specifically being exercised by the Bank directly or through the PRA indirectly. The Bank is responsible for monetary policy, macro-prudential policy, payments and infrastructure, bank resolution and market liquidity (including lender of last resort).

Under the earlier Tripartite Committee arrangements, the Bank, FSA and Treasury had equal status. Under the new arrangements, the position of the FCA has effectively been diluted with the Bank only reporting to the Treasury every six months and with the Treasury function effectively being limited to approving emergency support funds.

## C38.

### Question

Comment on the need for the establishment of separate financial conduct and prudential regulators and the relative effectiveness of the UK institutional regulatory framework set up under the Financial Services Act 2012.

### Answer

The question focused on the difference between prudential and conduct regulation. This reflects the earlier 'twin peaks' division which was accepted by the Financial System Inquiry (Wallis Inquiry) in Australia in June 1996 which led to the establishment of the Australian Prudential Regulation Commission (later Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA)) and Corporations and Financial Services Commission (later Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC)). This was reflected in the UK with the creation of the PRA and FCA under the Financial Services Act 2012 with effect from 1 April 2013.

The core objective is to split prudential and conduct regulation on the basis that they are considered inconsistent and better managed by separate authorities focusing on the separate functions involved. Prudential regulation was principally concerned with stability and focuses on capital, liquidity, systems, governance and resolution. Conduct focuses on behaviour and customer or consumer protection. This division can be justified on the basis of separate focus, priority or mandate, inconsistent objectives, different needs and outcomes, dedicated and focused treatment and different disciplinary and enforcement requirements. The disadvantages include cost, complexity, obligation, management demand, duplication, policy conflict and inconsistent enforcement and sanction. Regulatory separation leads to overall regulatory fragmentation which fails to reflect the increasingly integrated and interconnected nature of modern financial markets.

The UK model is also inconsistent in theoretical terms with the PRA only being responsible for the prudential regulation of banks, building societies, credit unions, insurance companies and designated investment firms with the FCA managing the prudential regulation of all other firms as well as the regulation of markets and exchanges in addition to other conduct matters.

If the objective of the UK institutional reform was simply to create more effective and focused prudential and conduct regulation, this could have been more easily achieved by establishing a separate Prudential Regulation Division (PRD) and Conduct Regulation Division (CRD) within the FSA. This would have avoided the policy operational limitations referred to as well as the considerably more complex content of the FSMA following all of the statutory amendments required to split the FCA and PRA functions. Many of the other reforms considered necessary following the crisis had already also been adopted by the FSA and outgoing Labour administration before the Coalition government came into office in May 2010.

While it is difficult to justify the split between the PRA and FCA in purely regulatory terms, the new UK model can be understood in light of the desire to establish a central bank based macro-prudential model with integrated systemic regulation. The decision was taken to replace the Financial Stability Committee (FSC) created under the Banking Act 2009 with the Financial Policy Committee established under the Financial Services Act 2012 and to transfer the larger prudential regulatory functions of the FSA to the PRA set up as a subsidiary of the Bank of England. While a strong macro-prudential unit could have been established within the Bank of England and closer operational relations created between the Bank and the FSA, including possibly separate FSA prudential and conduct divisions, the decision was taken to re-transfer the prudential regulation of depository institutions from the FSA to the Bank with insurance companies and larger securities firms.

This can then be explained in terms of a central bank based macro-prudential model with integrated systemic prudential regulation and the transfer of non-systemic functions to the independent FCA. One difficulty that had arisen was that the FSA board had become overloaded with non-prudential matters although this could have been dealt with separate prudential and conduct board committees with only final decisions being referred to the full board.

Conduct regulation has become of increasing importance following the crisis with the large number of significant revelations of misconduct including with regard to payment protection insurance (PPI), interest rate swaps, money laundering breaches, sanction breaches, LIBOR, foreign exchange and alleged gold market fixing. This requires the development of a clear and coherent conduct risk policy. Whether this needs a separate institutional base is nevertheless still unclear. Institutional reform of itself rarely creates better regulation which can only be achieved through the adoption of appropriate supervisory and regulatory approaches with necessary funding and training of all relevant staff.

No set answer was expected. These are difficult issues with many arguments for and against. It is also a subjective matter to form any conclusive opinion as to the effectiveness of the UK regulatory regime.

## C49.

### Question

Comment on the extent to which a separate Rulebook for the PRA and Handbook for the FCA increases or decreases the effectiveness of financial regulation and supervision in the UK.

### Answer

The question focused on the effects of the production of a separate PRA Rulebook and FCA Handbook. The background to this is that the PRA and FCA had applied the earlier FSA Handbook of Rules and Guidance on a combined basis from the establishment of the new regulatory regime under the Financial Services Act 2012 in April 2013 until the production of the PRA Rulebook in 2015. The Rulebook and Handbook are created through the production of 'rule-making instruments' by the PRA and FCA under s 138G of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 as amended with the FCA having an express rule-making power under s 137A and the PRA under s 137G.

The creation of a separate PRA Rulebook and FCA Handbook is considered to be a natural extension of the split between prudential and conduct regulatory functions under the current UK regime. This can be justified on the basis that prudential and conduct regulation are considered inconsistent and better managed by separate authorities focusing on the separate functions involved. Prudential regulation is principally concerned with stability and focuses on capital, liquidity, systems, governance and resolution. Conduct focuses on behaviour and customer or consumer protection.

This division can be justified based on separate focus, priority or mandate, inconsistent objectives, different needs and outcomes, dedicated and focused treatment and different disciplinary and enforcement requirements. The disadvantages include increased cost, complexity, obligation, management demand, duplication, policy conflict and inconsistent enforcement and sanction.

The other difficulty that arises is that regulatory separation may lead to more general regulatory fragmentation, which fails to reflect the increasingly integrated and interconnected nature of modern financial markets. This may specifically arise with regard to dual-regulated firms, which are subject to the PRA eight fundamental rules (FR) and twelve FCA principles for businesses (PRIN). While this could become a problem in practice where common provisions are interpreted differently, this may not create any difficulty if the PRA and FCA adopt a common interpretation.

Care also has to be taken in assessing the degree of possible fragmentation elsewhere within the separate substantive provisions applied. PRA-regulated firms are subject to the PRA provisions with FCA firms having to comply with the FCA's measures. The distinct sets of firms concerned are subject to separate prudential requirements. Distinct prudential requirements are therefore applied to dual regulated and non-dual regulated firms. Many of these are also now based on other prudential regulatory requirements, including the directly applicable CRR measures and equivalent Solvency II insurance rules, which further limit contradiction and inconsistency.

The principal difficulties may then only remain in non-prudential areas for dual-regulated firms, which correspond with the conduct risk scope of FCA jurisdiction. As noted, this may principally arise with regard to the overlapping FR and PRIN provisions although this may again not cause any substantial difficulties in practice providing the regulators adopt a common interpretation.

No set answer was expected. These are difficult issues with many arguments for and against – and due credit is given to candidates setting out and justifying their views on such arguments.

## C51.

### Question

Assess the effectiveness of the new joint Complaints Scheme set up under FSMA 2000 (as amended) to allow parties to make complaints against the conduct of the regulatory authorities.

### Answer

The FCA and PRA are required under Part 6 of the Financial Services Act 2012 to maintain a complaints scheme for the investigation of complaints arising in connection with the exercise of, or failure to exercise, any of their relevant functions and to appoint an independent person (referred to as the Complaints Commissioner) to be responsible for the conduct of investigations in accordance with the complaints scheme (the Scheme). The Scheme came into effect on 1 April 2013.

The Office of the Complaints Commissioner is headed by the Financial Regulators Complaints Commissioner. The Complaints Commissioner considers grievances against the FCA, PRA, the Payment System Regulator and the Bank of England's oversight of clearing houses and settlement systems.

Complaints can be made by anyone who is directly affected by the way in which the regulators have carried out their functions, or anyone acting directly on such a person's behalf, if the complaint meets the requirements of the Scheme. To be eligible to make a complaint under the Scheme, a person must be seeking a remedy (which for this purpose may include an apology) in respect of some inconvenience, distress or loss, which the person has suffered because of being directly affected by the regulators' actions or inaction. Complaints should be made within 12 months of the date on which the complainant first became aware of the circumstances giving rise to the complaint. Complaints made later than this will be investigated under the Scheme only if the complainant can show reasonable grounds for the delay.

The Complaints Commissioner publishes a response to each complaint which begin on 17 December 2013 and which are made available to the public with the regulator's response. The Complaints Commissioner also publishes Annual Reports.

Most complaints relate to the conduct of the FCA with 90% of complaints being settled without being referred to the Complaints Commissioner with the Commissioner upholding the FCA's action in the majority of instances. 275 complaints were dealt with in 2019/20. 63 complaints substantive responses were issued with 49 regulatory decisions upheld and seven not upheld with five been partially upheld. 26 recommendations were made for corrective action, 14 for compensation and five for an apology with 33 of these were accepted by the FCA and three rejected (Complaints Commissioner, Reviewing how the financial services regulators consider complaints Annual Report (2019/20) 7 and 8). Only two complaints were considered against the PRA and one against the Bank of England. Matters considered included the accuracy of registers, firm supervision and delays in regulatory processes and lack of candour on failings and impact.

The Commissioner has stated that the scheme is not considered to have been operating satisfactorily in all respects (Annual Report (2019/20) 4). The FCA complaints' function was not able to cope with the volume and complexity of work, significant regulatory issues had not been identified despite investigations with recommendations for improvement had been adopted especially in terms of compensation and prioritising complainants' needs. Work was being undertaken to correct the omissions.

There had also been no effective prompt and public system to ensure quality assurance. The Commissioner recommended making the scheme more readily understandable to complainants and clarifying compensation policy, providing the Commissioner with a wider role in ensuring quality assurance and publication of the work undertaken by the FCA to review its functions and deal with concerns.

Complaints about financial firms rather than one of the regulators should be referred to the company concerned and then the Financial Ombudsman Service. The purpose of the Ombudsman scheme under the FSMA is to provide for the resolution of 'certain disputes' quickly and with minimum formality by an independent person (s 225(1)). The scheme is to be administered by a body corporate with further direction being provided with regard to jurisdiction and operation in Part XVI and Schedule 17 FSMA.

Complainants should generally use the financial institution's internal scheme first before making a separate reference to the Ombudsman. The Ombudsman may direct financial institutions to take any necessary action or make financial awards up to £355,000. The Ombudsman scheme is generally dealt with under the Dispute Resolution: Complaints sourcebook (DISP) with compensation under COMP within the Redress block of the FCA Handbook.

## C56.

### Question

Explain the principal changes brought into effect under the Bank of England and Financial Services Act 2016 commenting on their need and the extent to which they may improve financial regulation in the UK.

### Answer

Candidates were required to explain the principal changes brought into effect under the Bank of England and Financial Services Act (BEFSA) 2016 and comment on their need and value in improving financial regulation. The purpose of the BEFSA 2016 Act was generally to strengthen governance and accountability, update resolution planning and crisis management and extend the new Senior Managers and Certification Regimes to all authorised persons within the financial services area. A number of specific amendments were adopted in this regard.

The Treasury is given specific power to alter the title or add or remove Deputy Governors of the Bank under s1A Bank of England Act 1998 (s1). A new Deputy Governor for Banking and Markets is created as a member of the Court and of the FPC. The term of office of non-executive directors may be extended by up to six months (s2) with the Oversight Committee being abolished (s3) and with oversight functions being transferred to the Court. A new Prudential Regulation Committee (PRC) is created within the Bank and the status of the PRA as a subsidiary of the Bank abolished (s12). The Comptroller and Auditor General can carry out examinations of the economy, efficiency and effectiveness with which the Bank uses its resources (s9). The FPC is made a committee of the Bank rather than a sub-committee of the Court with the number of annual MPC meetings being reduced to eight.

FCA Chief Executives are required to appear before the Treasury Select Committee before appointment (s18). The Treasury may make recommendations to the FCA concerning aspects of economic policy that the FCA should consider in implementing its objectives (s19). The regulatory principles are amended to include a reference to diversity (s20). Provision is made in Schedule 4 to extend the Senior Managers and Certification Regimes to all authorised persons under Part 5 FSMA (s21). The definition of misconduct is revised to require that the regulators prove that the senior manager has not taken reasonable steps reversing the earlier burden of proof (s25). Provisions governing the enforceability of credit agreements are amended (ss27-28) with the Treasury being given power to provide financial assistance to take action against illegal money laundering with the cost being covered by a new industry levy and other money laundering disclosures to be made (ss29-30).

The Treasury may make regulations relating to transformer vehicles that are used in connection with insurance linked securities (s31). The Pension Wise service is extended to include the sale of annuities with the FCA issuing new rules on transfers (ss32-35). The Bank is to inform the Treasury where firms' resolution strategies have been developed or updated with the Treasury being able to request information on the Bank's assessment of public fund risks with regard to potential firm failure (36). The Treasury may make regulations with the consent of the Bank allowing banks within the same group as an existing issuer to issue banknotes in Scotland and Northern Ireland (s38).

While a number of these revisions are more technical, the most significant change was to confirm the PRA as an internal division within the Bank of England rather than as a separate subsidiary. This is supported by the creation of a Prudential Regulation Committee (PRC) within the Bank and a Deputy Governor for Banking and Markets. The subsidiary PRA's status was always anomalous and possibly based on the need to transfer its functions again if the institutional structure adopted under the FSA 2012 did not operate successfully. As the PRA has been effectively absorbed into the Bank's other operations, there was no need to continue its separate subsidiary position. The other most significant change was to extend the Senior Managers and Certification Regimes to all authorised persons that would otherwise have created an unlevel playing field between banks and other financial institutions.
