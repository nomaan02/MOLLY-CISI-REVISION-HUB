# Section C — International Regulation & Prudential Oversight

> Each Question carries 15 marks in total.

---

## C21.

### Question

Assess the effectiveness of the range of enforcement powers available to the authorities in the UK commenting specifically on any changes introduced following the UK, European and international financial crises or shocks.

### Answer

The question asked for comment on the effectiveness of the regulatory enforcement powers available to the authorities with specific reference to new measures. Candidates could initially have referred to earlier general powers including financial penalties (s206 FSMA), variation of permission (s45 FSMA), cancellation of Part 4 permission (s54 FSMA), withdrawal of authorisation (s33 FSMA), public censure (s205 FSMA), injunctions (s380 FSMA) and restitution (ss382 and 384 FSMA) and skilled persons' reports (s166 FSMA).

Candidates should have specifically referred to the revised or extended powers made available following the UK financial crisis. These include suspension of permission (s206A FSMA and s9 FSA 2010), removal of the restriction on imposing a financial penalty and cancelling authorisation (s10 FSA 2010), penalties for performing a controlled function without approval (s63A FSMA and s11 FSA 2010), suspending or restricting an approved person (s66 FSMA and s12 FSA 2010), publication of decision notices (s391 FSMA and s13 FSA 2010), requests for information (s165 FSMA and Schedule 12 FSA 2012), administration, receivership, winding-up or bankruptcy (Part 24 FSMA, Banking Act 2009 and Schedule 14 FSA 2012), complaint by a consumer body (super-complaint under s234C FSMA and s43 FSA 2012), requiring firms to comply with a consumer redress scheme (s404 FSMA and s14 FSA 2010), requiring information in relation to financial stability (s165A FSMA and s18 FSA 2010), suspension of a financial instrument from trading (s36 FSA 2012), regulated person references (s234D FSMA), OFT investigation (s234H FSMA) and product intervention rules (s137D FSMA and s24 FSA 2012).

Reference may also have been made to the new powers for the Treasury to arrange independent inquiries (s68 FSA 2012), duties of the FCA and PRA to investigate and report on possible regulatory failure (ss73 and 74 FSA 2012), publication of inquiry and investigation reports (s82 FSA 2012) and revised offences on misleading statements and impressions with regard to benchmarks (ss89-91 FSA 2012). Separate new provisions were also introduced in relation to complaints against the regulators (ss84-88 and Part 6 FSA 2012).

Separate reference may also have been made to the new requirements imposed on authorised firms in connection with recovery and resolution plans (RRPs) (s139B FSMA and s7 FSA 2010) and power to prohibit or require disclosure of short selling (s131B FSMA and s8 FSA 2010).

Reference may have been made to each of the additional statutory provisions adopted following the global financial crisis including the Financial Services Act 2010, Financial Services Act 2012, Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013 and the Bank of England and Financial Services Act 2016.

Candidates may also have referred to other European or international measures adopted following the crisis although the question strictly referred to UK powers adopted following the crisis. Candidates should have specifically commented on the effectiveness of the powers. This may have included discussing the Supervisory Enhancement Programme (SEP) adopted by the FSA and 'credible deterrence' enforcement policy as well as increased financial fine policy adopted by the FCA in recent years and the overall effectiveness of these measures.

## C22.

### Question

Explain the role and function of the principal international regulatory committees that operate in the financial area and comment on their value and effectiveness.

### Answer

Candidates had the opportunity to identify as many as possible of the principal international regulatory committees currently in operation and to describe their key role and function. Relevant committees include:

- a) Basel Committee on Banking Supervision;

- b) International Organisation of Securities Commissions (IOSCO);

- c) International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS);

- d) Committee on Payment and Settlement Systems (CPSS);

- e) Committee on the Global Financial System (CGFS);

- f) Joint Forum of Financial Conglomerates;

- g) Financial Stability Board (FSB);

- h) Financial Action Task Force (FATF).

As much information as possible should have been provided on the work and importance of these committees including any recent papers, such as the Basel Committee Basel III capital adequacy provisions following the recent financial crisis.

Reference could also have been made to other international accounting organisations such as the International Accounts Standards Committee (IASC), International Accounting Education Standards Board (IAESB) and International Public Sector Accounting Standards Board (IPSASB) and the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB).

While not strictly committees, candidates could have also have referred to other international financial institutions (IFIs) such as the International Monetary Fund, World Bank, Bank for International Settlements (BIS), Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) and other development banks (such as the Asia Development Bank (ADB), African Development Bank (AfDB), Latin American Development Bank and Inter-American Development Bank).

## C26.

### Question

Comment on the value and effectiveness of the strengthened enforcement policy adopted by the FCA in recent years and the extent to which this has improved behaviour in markets or simply generated additional penalty fines income for the FCA.

### Answer

The FCA has adopted a more aggressive 'proactive' and 'credible deterrence' approach to enforcement following the financial crisis. This was highlighted by a speech by Hector Sants in March 2009. Candidates could have reviewed all of the enforcement powers available to the FCA and additional matters dealt with under the Financial Services Act 2010 especially in terms of suspension, non-approved persons and short selling penalty powers. Reference may also have been made to the FCA's five-step process (based on disgorgement, discipline, mitigation, deterrence and discount). The high recent levels of fine, prohibition and conviction may also have been noted with reference to some of the specific penalties imposed in individual cases.

Candidates could then have specifically commented on the effectiveness of this policy in terms of deterrence and the ability of the FCA to secure its statutory objectives. Relevant issues include possible retroactive effect, lack of due process, limited crisis relevance and longer-term ineffectiveness in terms of continuing regulatory compliance and supervision. Higher retrospective penalties cannot compensate for ineffective ongoing supervision and regulation by the authorities. Reference may also, for example, have been made to the FSA Northern Rock Report and separate NAO reviews as well as the most recent RBS Report in December 2011.

## C35.

### Question

Assess the effectiveness of the macro-prudential oversight regime set up under the Financial Services Act 2012, commenting specifically on the role and function of the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to comment on the effectiveness of the macro-prudential regime set up under FSA 2012. This has been raised many times in previous examinations. Candidates should have referred to the establishment of the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England which has specific responsibility for macro-prudential oversight. Similar macro-prudential bodies have been set up in the US with the Financial Services Oversight Council (FSOC) and with the EU European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB).

The Bank of England Act 1998 was amended (under s2 FSA 2012) to provide that the Bank's Financial Stability Objective would be to protect and enhance the financial stability of the UK financial system with the Bank's Court of Directors to determine the Bank's strategy on Financial Stability (s9A). The amendments also provide for the establishment of the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) (s9B BEA 1998) although the FPC had operated on an interim basis since February 2011. The FPC replaced the earlier Financial Stability Committee (FSC) set up under the Banking Act 2009 (s238 BA 2009 inserting a new s2B BEA 1998).

The purpose of macro-prudential oversight is to monitor the potential build-up of any risk or exposure across the financial system as a whole. This was understood to be one of the principal failings leading to the UK and global financial crisis in 2008-2009. The functions of the FPC are to monitor the stability of the UK financial system to identify and assess systemic risk, give directions, make recommendations and prepare Financial Stability Reports or carry out any other functions determined by the Bank's Court. The FPC may give directions to the FCA or PRA in the exercise of their functions. The FPC may make recommendations including on the provision of financial assistance to financial institutions. The FPC is to continue to publish the Bank's Financial Stability Reports.

The FPC is being given a number of macro-prudential tools. The Bank and FSA issued a discussion paper in December 2011 which referred to a number of possible balance sheet, lending and market structure options. A first Macro-prudential Measures Order was issued in March 2013 to allow the FPC to impose additional own funds to cover residential and commercial property exposures or financial sector exposures.

Candidates did not need to refer to any of the specific statutory provisions although they should have explained the general purpose of the FPC and the nature of the powers to be exercised.

## C37.

### Question

Assess the extent to which recent financial reforms at the national, European or international levels have been able to secure an appropriate balance between financial regulation and market function or have only imposed unnecessary burdens and costs to the ultimate disadvantage of consumers and other recipients of financial services.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to comment on the extent to which a correct regulatory balance had been achieved at the national, European and international levels. The question strictly referred to national, European 'or' rather than 'and' international levels with candidates being able to comment on one, two or all three perspectives. To score lots of marks, it might have been easier to review regulatory developments in recent years at all three levels.

The objective was to assess the relative advantages of improved market regulation and function as against excessive burden and cost. The question did not specifically refer to the global, European or UK financial crises as such although candidates could have referred to this by way of useful background information.

Candidates should then have reviewed the more significant developments at each level and then comment on the extent to which these could be considered to be necessary or proportionate. This could have been dealt with in a number of different ways with no specific answer being expected. Necessary revisions following the crisis clearly include the strengthening of capital and liquidity, tightening of leverage and debt levels (under Basel III and EU CRD IV), strengthening governance, improving supervision, introducing new forms of bank resolution (such as under the UK Banking Act 2009 or proposed EU Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD)), extended forms of market or lender of last resort (LLR) and new full system macro-prudential oversight mechanisms. The new focus on promoting higher ethical standards has also to be fully supported especially through the enhanced enforcement of existing provisions, in particular, within PRIN, SYSC, COCON and now PRA FR 1-4 (for CRR, non-CRR and SII and non-SII firms). These deal with the core conditions that arose especially in terms of weak regulation, supervision, resolution, support and oversight and conduct.

Whether many of the other more specific institutional and regulatory changes adopted have also been necessary is less clear. The institutional reforms undertaken in the UK with the replacement of the FSA by the FCA and PRA cannot be justified on the basis of the supposed 'twin peaks' moral (due to the substantial inconsistencies involved) although it is understandable in terms of the desire to create a new central bank based macro-prudential model with the delegation of non-systemic prudential and conduct functions to an independent agency. Other more specific reforms are also less clearly justifiable including excessive restrictions on remuneration packages (beyond risk insulation), structural regulation and ring-fencing (under the Independent Commission on Banking (ICB), EU Liikanen Report and US Volcker Rule and proposed UK banking standards in addition to the existing principles for businesses. Other more specific detailed provisions within CRD IV or the US Dodd Frank Act 2010 for non-prudential nature could also have been referred to.

It is inevitable following a severe crises and substantial loss that there are significant political, public and media calls for reform. Such reforms are necessary provided that they are targeted and proportionate. Unnecessary, irrelevant and disproportionate amendments will simply undermine market function and innovation and increase cost and limit choice to the substantial disadvantage of customers, market counterparties and society as a whole.

No specific answer was expected provided some of these issues were discussed and informed opinions expressed.

## C40.

### Question

Discuss how financial regulation in the USA is organised compared with the UK and the relative merits of these systems.

### Answer

This question asked candidates to discuss how financial regulation in the USA is organised compared with the UK, and the relative merits of these systems. This is an open-ended question in which a lot of material might be included. Within a strong answer we would expect the following:

The financial regulatory system in the USA is characterised by its complexity and multiplicity of regulators, each with overlapping responsibilities.

Key regulatory bodies and their areas of responsibility include:

- The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), which oversees the securities markets, protecting investors, maintaining fair and efficient markets, and facilitating capital formation.

- The Federal Reserve (Fed), which regulates and supervises bank holding companies, state-chartered banks that are members of the Federal Reserve System, and other institutions.

- The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), which is responsible for the regulation and supervision of national banks and federal savings associations.

- The US Treasury, an executive agency responsible for promoting economic prosperity and ensuring the financial security of the US.

- Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA), which is the largest independent regulator for all securities firms doing business in the US.

- The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), which is responsible for the protection of consumers in the financial sector, overseeing banks, credit unions, securities firms, payday lenders, mortgage-servicing operations, foreclosure relief services, debt collectors, and other financial companies.

- The Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC), which regulates commodity futures and options markets.

This fragmented approach allows for a diverse and sometimes innovative regulatory environment, but it also leads to challenges such as regulatory gaps, overlapping jurisdiction, and increased complexity for financial institutions operating across state lines and different financial products.

The second element of the answer should cover how financial services regulation is structured in the UK, noting that the UK's financial regulatory system is more streamlined with fewer but more centralised regulators.

The Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA) 2000 is the cornerstone of financial regulation in the UK. The Act ensures a more coherent regulatory framework with clear demarcation of responsibilities and streamlined processes for regulatory oversight.

Key regulatory bodies and their areas of responsibility should be mentioned, including:

- The FCA, which regulates the conduct of financial firms to ensure markets function well, consumer protection, and competition.

- The PRA, which is responsible for the prudential regulation and supervision of banks, building societies, credit unions, insurers, and major investment firms.

- The Bank of England, which in addition to its monetary policy role, also oversees financial stability and macroprudential regulation through the Financial Policy Committee (FPC).

- The Payment Systems Regulator (PSR), which has been tasked to ensure that payment systems are operated and developed in a way that considers and promotes the interests of all the businesses and consumers that use them.

With regards to the relative merits of the US and UK system, it could be noted that:

The USA system, with multiple regulators, allows for specialised oversight tailored to different financial sectors (e.g., SEC for securities, CFTC for commodities). The complexity and gaps within the system can sometimes allow for innovative financial products and practices. However, overlapping responsibilities of regulators can lead to inefficiencies, regulatory arbitrage, and increased compliance costs for financial institutions. Differing regulatory standards and enforcement practices can also result in inconsistencies across states and financial sectors.

The UK system is more centralised and having fewer regulators reduces complexity and makes regulatory processes more transparent and easier to navigate. It provides uniform standards and regulations across the financial sector, ensuring consistent enforcement and compliance. Clear division of roles between the FCA, PRA, and BoE ensures efficient regulation and oversight without significant overlap. However, the centralisation of regulatory power can sometimes lead to slower responses to emerging financial innovations and crises.

There should be an acknowledgment that each system has its unique strengths and weaknesses, shaped by their respective regulatory philosophies and market needs. Reference could be made to The Edinburgh Reforms, which aim to make the UK's regulatory framework more agile, competitive, and responsive to new challenges.

## C48.

### Question

Outline the general purpose and content of the Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) IV package of measures and comment on their possible effectiveness in preventing future financial instability and crisis within the UK and Europe.

### Answer

The question was concerned with the character and content of the EU Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) IV package of measures. CRD 4 consists of the CRD 2013/36/EU and Regulation EU575/2013. These generally implement the Basel III capital, liquidity and leverage revisions within the EU and which will, in turn, be implemented within all of the EU member states. This directive was 'onshored' into UK law through the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018.

Basel 3 was finalised in 2010 and contains the new Basel Committee provisions governing capital, liquidity and leverage. Capital adequacy is generally increased from 2% to 4.5% with additional capital buffers been provided for including a conservation buffer (2.5%), countercyclical buffer (0–2.5%) and systemic risk buffer (1–3.5%). The new liquidity requirements include a short Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) and longer-term Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR). The leverage ratio consists of a 3% of own funds limit on total debt exposure.

This followed earlier Basel 1 in 1988, which established the minimum 8% ratio of capital to risk-adjusted assets for all internationally active banks, and Basel 2, which established the three pillars of minimum capital requirements (including credit risk (standardised, foundation and advanced internal ratings-based (IRB) approaches), market risk (standardised and models) and operational risk), supervisory review and market disclosure. These were revised following the global financial crisis to create the Basel 3 amendments.

These are implemented under the CRD and CRR with provisions involving national implementation being set out in the CRD and the more detailed common provisions which require no domestic adjustment contained in the CRR. These will be expanded through European Banking Authority (EBA) implementing measures. The CRD measures require separate UK implementation under the PRA Rulebook (with separate measures for CRR and non-CRR firms) and FCA Handbook (BIPRU) with the CRR being directly applicable as UK law and simply implemented on a copy out basis.

The CRD and CRR will form a key part of the European Banking Union (EBU) and Capital Markets Union (CMU). A number of steps have been taken to establish the EBU following the European financial crisis in 2009–2010 with this generally being based on the creation of a single Banking Rule Book (EBR) supported by a Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM) and Single Resolution Mechanism (SRM), which includes the Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD) 2014/59/EU, supported by the revisions to the Deposit Guarantee Schemes Directive (DGSD) 94/19/EC. The EBR essentially consists of the CRD and CRR and relevant implementing EBA technical standards.

Candidates could also have commented on the value of the new increased capital, liquidity and leverage measures. The purpose of capital is to provide a means of loss absorption for firms to prevent them from becoming insolvent. Liquidity ensures that firms maintain sufficient cash or readily realisable assets on an ongoing basis to cover ongoing liabilities. Leverage limits total debt exposure, which was a significant factor in the global financial crisis. The effect of these requirements is substantially to improve the stability of financial firms and prevent contagion. This regulatory adjustment is supported by enhanced supervision with new resolution procedures, emergency support facilities and wider macro prudential oversight. All of this should limit the possibility of a future crisis arising or, at minimum, the more stable and effective management of the crisis.
