## C6.

### Question

Compare and contrast a screen-based market making system with an open outcry market and discuss the merits and demerits of each from the point of view of the investor. Also, consider the pros and cons of an order-driven as opposed to a quote-driven market for trading securities.

### Answer

**a) Screen-based vs. open outcry trading**

**Introduction**

Of the London securities and derivatives markets, the LSE, LIFFE and ISDX all now use screen-based trading. Certain other international exchanges (particularly commodity exchanges) still make some use of open outcry trading.

**The case for open outcry trading**

1. A pit trade can be done instantly; this means that customer orders can be completed in a timely and efficient manner.

2. It is argued that prices are determined more efficiently, since all market participants are in physical proximity to one another. However, retail customers may feel that this method of price determination is too esoteric.

3. In view of the speed at which transactions can be executed, there is increased liquidity in the market.

4. There is clear transparency about the prices at which deals are done.

5. No technological risk.

**The case for screen-based trading**

1. Participants do not need to be in physical proximity; thus, anyone can participate irrespective of their geographical location.

2. Less physical infrastructure is required – there is no need for a trading floor, and this may reduce costs.

3. There are no restrictions on the number of participants or on the market hours.

4. The above factors must be balanced against the risk of catastrophe in the event of: (i) loss of telephone or electronic systems; or (ii) unauthorised access to the system by a firm's employee entering (accidentally or by design) 'rogue' transactions, which could lead to either massive losses or the collapse of the system.

**b) Order-driven vs. quote-driven trading**

**Introduction**

In London, in general, the most liquid stocks are dealt with on an order-driven system (SETS) while less liquid stocks are dealt on a combined (hybrid) order-driven and quote-driven system (SETSqx).

**The case for order-driven trading**

1. Participants can clearly see the price at which deals are done, thus assisting transparency. However, this is tempered by the fact that not all deals in SETS stocks are done via SETS.

2. The non-involvement of market makers cuts out the middleman and this results in reduced dealing costs, which can be passed on to the customer.

3. The minimum order size is one share, which ensures that deals in any size can be done at minimal cost without the need to phone round for prices, etc.

4. The system is the only practical system for illiquid securities in which market makers are not prepared to make firm two-way prices.

**The case for quote-driven trading**

1. Market makers are obliged to quote firm two-way prices during the mandatory period (8.00am to 4.30pm). This helps to ensure liquidity.

2. The publication of market makers' prices enables all market participants to determine the current prices of securities at all times.

3. Competitiveness is encouraged, particularly in those stocks that are the most liquid. Thus, spreads are narrowed, minimising costs for the investor.

4. Where deals are not done via a market maker, the firm must improve on the best market makers' prices. Again, this can only operate to the advantage of the investor.

## C53.

### Question

Discuss the general purpose and content of the MiFID II and associated regulation (MiFIR) measures and comment on their possible value in improving the efficiency and stability of securities and capital markets, both in the UK and across Europe.

### Answer

MiFID I was adopted in April 2004 and came into effect in November 2007. It created a common regulatory regime across the EU in investment services with the specific objectives of increasing competition and consumer protection replacing the earlier Investment Services Directive (ISD) 1993.

MiFID I distinguished between core investment services and activities and non-core ancillary services. The directive contained a number of provisions governing authorisation, regulation and passporting, client classification, client order handling, pre-trade and post-trade transparency, best execution and systemic internalisation.

The European Commission published a consultation document on MiFID II in September 2010 with a draft directive being produced in October 2011 for an MiFID II Directive and Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MiFIR) to amend the European Market Infrastructure Regulation (EMIR) which came into effect in August 2012. EMIR contains specific provisions imposing reporting obligations on OTC derivatives, clearing obligations for eligible OTC derivatives and common rules for central counterparties (CCPs) and trade repositories (TRs).

The European Parliament adopted MiFID II (2014/65/EU) in April 2014 that would come into effect in January 2017 with the MiFIR (EU/600/2014).

The FCA issued a Discussion Paper and Consultation Paper in May 2014 covering such issues as authorisation, passporting and best execution, equity transparency and equities trading, non-equity transparency and derivatives trading, high frequency and algorithmic trading and other market microstructural issues, data reporting service providers and data dissemination, access to CCPs, trading venues (including Regulated Markets (RMs), Multilateral Trading Facilities (MTFs), and Organised Trading Facilities (OTFs) as well as Systematic Internalisers (SIs)) and benchmarks, commodities and transaction reporting and retention of order data.

The FCA has summarised the principal changes in terms of scope and commodity derivatives, transparency, high frequency trading, market structure, organisational requirements, trade reporting, conduct of business rules revision and transaction reporting. Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Markets in Financial Instruments) Regulations (MiFIR) were published in 2017 (SI 2017/701).

The general objectives of MiFID II were to make financial markets safer, more transparent and more efficient with increased investor confidence and competition. It specifically extends earlier provisions to deal with new market structures, transparency, research and inducements and governance with additional price transparency, volume limits on dark pools, separation of analysis and commission payments and higher standards on investment products. Algorithmic and high frequency trading must be registered and the use of circuit breakers with capital adequacy being extended to commodity traders. MiFID II can be considered to have been effective to the extent that it introduces new requirements in these areas. Against this, it imposed substantial new obligations and costs on firms and has arguably fragmented rather than integrated markets across the EU. Trading will be removed from telephone to electronic markets with less analysis being produced. Data production and data transfer and storage costs have also been increased substantially.

Candidates should have commented on the value and purpose of the revisions made under MiFID II and the MiFIR as referred to in the question. No specific answer is expected here.

## C54.

### Question

Assess the effectiveness of the recent changes announced in connection with the strengthening of capital and liquidity standards in national and international markets, in maintaining financial stability.

### Answer

Candidates had to assess the effectiveness of the recent changes brought into place in connection with strengthening capital and liquidity standards. Higher capital, liquidity and leverage standards were imposed at the international level under the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision's Basel III documentation with these being implemented within the EU under the Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) IV package of measures which consists of the Capital Requirements Directive 2013/36/EU and Regulation EU575/2013.

Basel III was finalised in 2010 with capital adequacy generally being increased from 2% to 4.5% and with additional capital buffers been provided for including a conservation buffer (2.5%), countercyclical buffer (0-2.5%) and systemic risk buffer (1-3.5%). The new liquidity requirements include a short Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) and longer-term Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR). A separate leverage ratio was imposed made up of a 3% of own funds limit on total debt exposure.

Basel III followed the earlier Basel 1 standards in 1988, which established the minimum 8% ratio of capital to risk-adjusted assets for all internationally active banks, and Basel 2, which established the three pillars of minimum capital requirements (including credit risk (standardised, foundation and advanced internal ratings-based (IRB) approaches), market risk (standardised and models) and operational risk), supervisory review and market disclosure. These were revised following the global financial crisis to create the Basel III amendments.

Basel III is implemented in the EU under the CRD and CRR with provisions involving national implementation being set out in the CRD and the more detailed common provisions, which require no domestic adjustment, contained in the CRR. These are being expanded through European Banking Authority (EBA) implementing measures. The CRD measures require separate UK implementation under the PRA Rulebook (with separate measures for CRR and non-CRR firms) and FCA Handbook (BIPRU) with the CRR being directly applicable as UK law and simply implemented on a copy out basis.

Candidates could also have commented on the value of the new increased capital and liquidity standards and separately referred to the new supporting leverage measures. The purpose of capital is to provide a means of loss absorption for firms to prevent them from becoming insolvent. Liquidity ensures that firms maintain sufficient cash or readily realisable assets on an ongoing basis to cover ongoing liabilities. Leverage limits total debt exposure, which was a significant factor in the global financial crisis. The effect of these requirements is substantially to improve the stability of financial firms and prevent contagion. This regulatory adjustment is supported by enhanced supervision with new resolution procedures, emergency support facilities and wider macro prudential oversight. All of this should limit the possibility of a future crisis arising or, at minimum, the more stable and effective management of the crisis.

## C57.

### Question

Recent policy announcements have indicated that ring-fencing for UK-based banking may be phased out. Explain what the 'ring fence' is in a prudential regulatory sense and why this measure was introduced. Discuss the implications of the removal of ring-fencing.

### Answer

The ring-fencing requirement arose from the great financial crisis (GFC) of 2007–08 and as a response to the threat of contagion in the UK and internationally. Concerns had arisen in the GFC that risk could easily spread from institution to institution and between different financial sectors and national financial sectors. Furthermore, there were concerns before the GFC that many financial institutions were allowed to undertake a very wide range of financial activities rather than being restricted to undertake a number of restricted activities associated with a particular type of institution (banking, insurance, trading etc.): i.e., many institutions were allowed to undertake a wide range of different activities some of which may have increased the individual risks to institutions. To limit this contagion after the GFC it was proposed to split the retail operations including taking deposits away from trading activities in many banks. This was termed ring fencing in the UK whereby different activities could exist in the same institution yet would be ring fenced from each other. Different approaches to address this problem were also seen in the USA and the EU and were often further reaching than ring fencing seen in the UK. In the UK this policy was forwarded by the Independent Commission on Banking (Vickers report) and implemented by the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013.

Removing the ring fence can have many outcomes. It will allow banks to reduce the expense of complying with these regulations. There may also be considerable commercial benefits of undertaking trading activities and retail activities in tandem. Of course, these benefits need to be tempered by the concern that ring fencing was introduced to reduce systemic risk seen in the UK and the world financial system. Removing ring fencing may increase the risk of individual financial institutions failing and the risks to the financial system. Lastly, removing the system of ring fencing or similar arrangements is only currently being discussed in the UK. This removal may make the UK relatively less regulated internationally, with both benefits and drawbacks to this move.

## C59.

### Question

Explain the function of the Takeover Panel. Discuss the importance of the Panel's role from a financial regulation perspective.

### Answer

The Takeover Panel was established in 1968 as a private and voluntary body to ensure takeovers, mergers and associated transactions occurring between firms from the UK and associated areas were undertaken in an appropriate manner. This voluntary panel developed issuing a code outlining the procedures and how such transactions should be undertaken. Critical in this role was the concern that only some shareholders may benefit from a takeover action. The Panel and the associated code were enacted to limit these concerns and enable all shareholders to have a say in the proposed transaction, be offered broadly equal treatment, and generally be treated fairly. The treatment of minority shareholders in takeover/merger transactions was a particular concern to be tackled by this code. Lastly, the code established procedures to ensure that such a takeover or merger transaction is undertaken following an appropriate administrative process and can be completed in a timely and legal manner. The panel can also signal if a particular transaction has wider economic and societal concern such as those related to competition. This would result in the transaction being referenced to the antitrust/competition regulator or another appropriate regulator.

Since the establishment of the panel its role has expanded and been formalised. To date, the actions and the role of the Panel have been formalised together with the expectations of the takeover code and have been provided with legal backing, for example in Chapter 1 of Part 28 of the Companies Act 2006. This role is also supported by the European Directive on Takeover Bids.

As detailed in the workbook text the role of the panel is to:

- provide advice and guidance on the application and interpretation of the relevant rules to offerors, offerees, advisers and, where relevant, market participants

- monitor the progress of an actual or contemplated takeover bid, for breaches of compliance with the relevant rules, and

- enforce the code, by reaching decisions on issues arising, or seeking enforcement through the courts.
