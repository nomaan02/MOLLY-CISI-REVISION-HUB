## C5.

### Question

The regulators are coming to grips with the development of the internet as a global financial services marketplace. In what way does the current regulatory regime apply to internet advertisements and how far are the regulators capable of enforcing the rules? Consider what other opportunities may arise for electronic delivery of financial services and how should the regulators be planning to protect investors?

### Answer

Any material circulating on the internet or otherwise which constitutes a financial promotion (as defined by FSMA) may only be issued or approved for issue in the UK by an authorised person. A financial promotion (e.g. an investment advertisement) on a firm's internet site, even if entered on the site from an overseas branch, will be considered to have been issued to persons in the UK if it is accessible via a computer screen in the UK.

Under the Financial Promotions Order, communications via the internet are regarded as non-real time communications. Therefore, firms must ensure that they comply with the detailed COBS requirements regarding clarity, fairness and expertise.

Firms must, for example, ensure that:

- The promotional purpose is not disguised or misrepresented.

- The facts on which comparisons are made are verified.

- The accuracy of all material statements of fact can be substantiated.

The detailed rules on advertisement contents do not apply to financial promotions circulated abroad which clearly state that the advertisement (and the investment product or service it advertises) is directed only at persons abroad. However, unless a firm can restrict access to its website (e.g., by issuing passwords) such that only non-UK investors can have access, the advertisement will have to comply fully with the detailed rules.

**Enforcement**

In determining whether a regulatory issue arises the FCA must consider the following:

- The extent to which UK investors can access the site

- Whether the site contains appropriate disclaimers and warnings

- Whether the content of the site makes it clear (if that is the case) that it is not directed at UK investors

- Whether the site has been notified as a UK site to search engines

- Whether the existence of the site has been advertised in the UK.

The FCA still has to deal with the difficulty of policing extra-territorial business where the investment company is not UK-based. Here, the only practical way forward is to secure the co-operation of any relevant overseas regulatory authority.

**Further opportunities**

There is no doubt that the electronic delivery of financial services will expand and diversify rapidly. With this will come increased use of the medium for document delivery (with attendant risks for security) and the gradual removal of humans from the advisory and execution-only share dealing business (possibly even discretionary transactions).

In view of such significant future developments the FCA needs to consider their implications seriously. The FCA needs to ensure that it has the framework of regulation, the resources and the expertise to be able to monitor software as it is developed. In addition, there has never been a stronger need for international co-operation between regulators: one possible development might be a 'kitemark' issued by each national regulator to identify and endorse those investment products which it considers to meet basic minimum international standards.

Above all, however, there is an overriding need to educate the public in how to recognise and select appropriate products and avoid the rogues. Such public education should be a central part of the FCA's overall programme to promote open regulation.

## C14.

### Question

Outline the principal provisions that apply with regard to maintaining business continuity and operational resilience in the UK and comment on the effectiveness of these measures in securing financial stability, in particular, in the event of a major market disruption including military intervention.

### Answer

Candidates were asked to assess the extent to which financial firms within the UK may be able to manage and contain future global market shocks and maintain the stability of the UK financial system or further measures and what revisions are required. This may include some form of military action or disruption. Firms have to ensure that they have the necessary systems, controls as well as other continuity and business disruption arrangements and programmes. The COVID-19 crisis of 2020-2021 highlighted the need for firms to be able to withstand any form of shock. Many of the same requirements and systems' conditions will apply.

Firms must ensure that they can operate on a continuing basis in the event of any major market or social disruption arising. This is concerned with maintaining appropriate systems and controls with business continuity dealt with under SYSC 3.2.19 and operational risk dealt with under SYSC 13 including external events and other changes in SYSC 13.8. Firms should have in place appropriate arrangements, having regard to the nature, scale and complexity of the business, to ensure that they can continue to function and meet the regulatory obligations in the event of unforeseen interruption with arrangements being regularly updated and tested to ensure effectiveness (SYSC 3.2.19).

Firms must maintain appropriate Business Continuity Planning (BCP) arrangements which allow them to continue operating, including through remote staff working, as well as ensure continued compliance with all relevant regulatory obligations (SYSC 13.8). Firms should consider the likelihood and impact of a disruption on the continuity of operations from unexpected events. This may include disruptions arising through the loss or failure of internal and external resources (including people, systems and assets), information loss or corruption and other external events (including vandalism, war and acts of God) (SYSC 13.8.5).

Firms should adopt the appropriate arrangements to maintain the continuity of their operations and reduce the likelihood of disruption (including through succession planning, systems resilience and dual processing) and the impact of the disruption (including through contingency arrangements and insurance) (SYSC 13.8.6). The strategy for maintaining operational continuity and plans for communicating and testing the strategy should be properly documented. This would include formal business continuity plans to deal with short-, medium- and long-term disruptions (including resource requirements, recovery priorities and communication arrangements), escalation and invocation plans (including implementation processes and contact information), information integrity validation and appropriate review and update processes with regard to the firm's operations and risk profile (SYSC 13.8.7).

Firms should assess the appropriateness of any alternative sites used including with regard to the location, speed of recovery, adequacy resources and any issues that arise with regard to shared use (SYSC 13.8.8). Separate provisions apply with regard to outsourcing (SYSC).

Other continuity arrangements may apply. The Bank of England monitors business continuity and operational resilience within the UK's financial sector with the FCA and Treasury, with responses coordinated through the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) and National Crime Agency (NCA). The objectives are to ensure that retail and wholesale markets remain open and functioning, with there being an orderly and early return to trading in the event of closure, involve relevant infrastructure providers and market participants in decision-taking and be ready to facilitate other market initiatives. The UK has also adopted the Civil Contingencies Act 2004 which replaced the Civil Defence Act 1948.

Firms will have to comply with any sanctions imposed by the government or other regulatory restrictions imposed on countries or firms from identified countries. A number of other regulatory adjustments and concessions may be announced by the authorities which have to be adhered to. Firms must maintain effective monitoring systems to follow all directions and the necessary systems and controls to give effect to these in practice. Candidates could also have noted that the measures adopted in response to the global financial crisis beginning in 2008-2009 ensured that financial markets and firms were more stable and resilient, and capable of withstanding such shocks as the COVID-19 crisis. Other market and regulatory initiatives have been adopted in response to COVID-19 specifically while other existing systems, controls and continuity planning arrangements allowed firms to continue to operate. Candidates could have referred to any of the above.

## C15.

### Question

"The recent explosive growth of sovereign wealth fund investment presents no significant new regulatory obstacles for the FCA." Discuss.

### Answer

The question was concerned with Sovereign Wealth Funds (SWFs). SWFs are among the largest single investors currently and, despite the downturn in many aspects of legal work, are still generating a substantial amount of transaction activity within the major law firms.

There is no standard definition of 'Sovereign Wealth Fund' at this time although reference may be made to any of the major funds including in the Middle East as well as Scandinavia and China (each of which generally has its own website). Reference may also have been made to the current work being undertaken in this area by various organisations and committees including ESMA (the European Securities and Markets Authority) and the International Organisation of Securities Commissions (IOSCO) as well as the Basel Committee and the European Commission. The issue has also been discussed within the US Congress.

The specific regulatory issues that arise include the following:

- 1) Disclosure and transparency;

- 2) Corporate governance;

- 3) Management autonomy within target firms;

- 4) National security (although specific definition issues arise in defining relevant interests with this possibly extending to include energy companies, transport and freight (such as the New York Port Authority) in addition to military and armaments interests);

- 5) Money laundering;

- 6) Possible market distortion or disruption;

- 7) Moral hazard (with the implied sovereign support or guarantee provided); and

- 8) Potential corruption and political interference in certain countries.

Reference could also, for example, specifically have been made to the FCA's statutory objectives and the extent to which SWFs could cause specific difficulties for the FCA in regulatory terms.

## C24.

### Question

Comment on the extent to which the FCA has been able to respond to the challenges created by the emergence of electronic commerce and electronic money activities in recent years, both in terms of allowing innovation but constraining risk and exposure.

### Answer

The question raises the issue of the delivery of financial services through electronic means and the FCA's response in this regard. This then raises general issues with regard to financial innovation but also specific FCA policy in implementing the EU Electronic Commerce Directive (ECA) and the Electronic Money Directive (and new E-Money Directive) and the Electronic Money Regulations.

Issuing e-money is a regulated activity under the RAO with authorised firms being referred to as Electronic Money Institutions (EMIs) or registered small EMIs.

The Electronic Commerce Directive applies to Electronic Commerce Activities (ECAs) which are defined in terms of 'information society services'. These apply to services provided electronically, at a distance, for remuneration and at the request of the individual.

These were given effect to by the FCA under the Electronic Money Sourcebook (ELM) and the Electronic Commerce Directive (ECO) sourcebook with ELM being disapplied from April 2011 and ECO from October 2007. The second E-Money Directive (2EMD) came into effect in May 2011 with new Electronic Money Regulations (EMRs) in 2011. The FCA's approach to electronic money was set out in a paper issued in March 2011 with 2EMD to be implemented with the Payment Services Directive (PSD) under the EMRs.

As much relevant information as possible should have been provided with no specific answer being expected.

This explains the role of the Bank of England and PRA in relation to climate-related exposures and outlines the work undertaken on improving resiliency, climate related risks and supervisory approach adjustments. The PRA would embed climate change within its supervisory approach and incorporate this into supervisory expectations in dealing with firms. The Bank of England considers that climate change is relevant to its core mission of promoting the good of the people of the UK by maintaining monetary and financial stability.

Climate scenario analysis and stress testing was incorporated within the PRA's toolkit to assess the impact and exposures of climate pathways on the financial system. This was incorporated into the Climate Biennial Exploratory Scenario (CBES) which would assess physical and transition risks in relation to early, late and no additional action scenarios published by the Network for Greening the Financial System (NGFS). Firms have to be aware of all of these policies and initiatives and incorporate them within their own climate-related frameworks and wider systems and control compliance arrangements.

Candidates could have explained the background to PRIN and the Fundamental Rules (FRs) within the larger handbooks and listed each of these with the key requirement being to act with integrity. Firms would generally have to comply with all existing relevant provisions including PRIN 1 (integrity), PRIN 6 (customers' interests), PRIN 7 (communications), PRIN 8 (avoiding conflicts of interest) and PRIN 9 (trust) as well as possibly PRIN 10 (protecting client assets). This is a difficult and new area with no specific answer required. ## C28.

### Question

Discuss the principal options that may be available for UK financial firms to allow them to continue providing regulated activities across Europe following the withdrawal of the United Kingdom from the European Union in 2020 and comment briefly on their relative desirability.

### Answer

_**[Author's Note:** Obviously, Britain has now fully left the EU following the end of the Transitional Period. You would not be asked a question exactly like this one again. However, we have retained this 2021 question, and its post-exam report, within the pack to give you the ability to see how 'current issues' are tested, and how the Examiner would have expected candidates to tackle questions such as this one. The model answer also gives you some useful background knowledge.]_

The citizens of the UK voted in a National Referendum on 23 June 2016 to leave the EU on a 51.9% to leave the EU with the selected Article 50 departure date announced on 29 March 2017 being 29 March 2019. The British Government published a White Paper on the _Future Relationship Between the UK and EU_ (the Chequers Plan) on 12 July 2018. The Government negotiated a _Draft Withdrawal Agreement_ and _Outline Political Declaration_ with the EU with final terms being concluded on 14 November 2018. This would have included a transitional period until 31 December 2020 during which the UK remained a member of the European Economic Area (EEA), the Single Market and external EU Customs Union although it also contained a potentially limitless continuation (backstop) clause. The November Draft Agreement and Declaration were rejected by the House of Commons in a 'Meaningful Vote' on 15 January 2019 on a 432 to 202 vote against the proposed withdrawal arrangements with no clear alternative arrangements being agreed. Further compromise amendments were produced in Parliament on 29 January 2018 including a direction to the Government to renegotiate the backstop provision.

Financial firms principally enjoy passport rights to carry on activities in other EU Member States under specific financial Directives (including CRD4, MiFID 2 and Solvency 2) or under underlying Treaty rights as set out in the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) (originally the European Economic Community (EEC) Treaty 1958) which is supported by the Treaty on European Union (TEU) (initially adopted as the Maastricht Treaty in 1992). These rights are based on the underlying law of free movement and principally cover the right of branch establishment and services as well as the free movement of goods, workers and capital.

The following possible options can be distinguished for financial firms on the UK's withdrawal from the EU:

- 1) Existing establishment and services rights may be continued on directive passport or Treaty basis during the proposed 18 month or possibly extended transition period;

- 2) Establishment and services rights may then be continued further under a final UK EU international treaty trade agreement provided for under the Draft Withdrawal Agreement and Political Declaration;

- 3) Separate equivalence regimes may be exercised in the event of UK firms acquiring third country status although this is only available under specific directives such as MiFID, the Market Abuse Regulation, Solvency 2 Directive, Prospectus Directive and Transparency Directive;

- 4) Where no passport, Treaty or equivalence rights are available, UK firms can apply for new full original authorisation in another EU Member State which would then allow them to carry on activities (passport) into other Member States;

- 5) Firms with branches in other Member States may be able to apply for country specific authorisation under third country reciprocity arrangements;

- 6) Certain firms may be able to benefit from internal group business transfers where they have extended group structures that include separate banking, securities and insurance authorisation within the EU;

- 7) Some firms are considering setting up various types of 'back to bank' lending or other trading arrangements to allow them to copy the economic effects of business transfers within the UK and EU (although the ECB has indicated that limitations will be imposed on such activities by 2022);

- 8) Firms may also consider establishing representative offices within other Member States in the EU although this would only be for marketing and client development purposes with any authorised activities having either to be returned to the UK or only carried out through EU authorised institutions.

Candidates may have referred to the separate possible future Treaty arrangements that may be available although the specific financial options are as set out above. Any possible trade relations apart from a WTO opt-in would necessarily involve agreement on some form of _Draft Withdrawal Agreement_ and _Outline Political Declaration_. Future Treaty relations could include:

- a) Norway (or Norway Plus) EEA model including Single Market access and EU Customs Union Membership (with consultation rights and EEA Joint Committee involvement and the requirement for national domestic law implementation);

- b) Switzerland (European Free Trade Association (EFTA)) model based on bilateral trade agreements;

- c) Turkey (Andorra and San Marino) non-EEA and non-EFTA Customs Union model (excluding agricultural goods and services);

- d) Canada Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement (CETA) model for free trade including single market entry (but excluding certain food substances and services) or an EU Japan Economic Partnership Agreement (EPA) model;

- e) Singapore and Hong Kong unilateral free trade model;

- f) WTO default position in the absence of any other trade arrangement (with the EU being prevented from discriminating against UK firms and with border controls limited under the WTO Trade Facilitation Agreement) and with possible reliance on Article 24 which allows tariff free trade to be continued for exceptional reasons to allow parties to negotiate new trade relations.

## C29.

### Question

Explain the nature and function of the Recovery and Resolution Plans (RRPs) that financial firms are required to maintain in the event of future market instability or crisis.

### Answer

One of the most significant gaps in the earlier regulatory frameworks before the Global Financial Crisis was the ability of authorities to deal with domestic and Global Systemically Important Firms (GSIFs) in difficulty. Many authorities lacked necessary resolution as well as market support (lender of last resort) powers.

To add depth to your answer, you might have referred to some of the evolution of the regulations around these plans. New laws (under the Financial Services Act 2010 and ss 137J and K FSMA 2000 as amended) requiring Recovery and Resolution Plans (RRPs) were created as a result of this, with all major financial firms being required to maintain effective RRPs to manage both their pre- and post-crisis condition and either to protect their stability or facilitate a managed winding-down of their activities and an eventual winding-up. The Bank of England published its 'Approach to Resolution' in 2014 which refers to three separate phases of stabilisation, restructuring and exit. The PRA issued a consultation paper on this in 2014 (CP13/14) with a policy statement response in January 2015 (PS 13/14). The PRA's expectations on firm and group recovery plans were set out in Supervisory Statement 18/13 and resolution planning in Supervisory Statement 19/13 as amended.

Recovery Plans generally operate in a pre-crisis situation and allow the firm, for example, to raise additional liquidity or capital, dispose of assets or subsidiaries and transfer activities. Resolution Plans are intended to assist authorities manage the subsequent winding-up of the institution in the event of the failure of the Recovery Plan at the same time as protect the provision of critical functions and limit contagious effects and public costs. Resolution Plans generally operate with other special resolution procedures such as those set up under the Banking Act 2009 in the UK.

The PRA rulebook contains separate rules on Recovery Plans and Resolution Packs. Firms must prepare and maintain an appropriate resolution pack again submitted to the PRA online (PRA Resolution Pack rules 2.3 and 2.4). This should contain sufficient information and analysis to facilitate the planning for or taking of action in the event that circumstances arise that may lead to the failure of the business of the firm or the business of the firm's failure (rule 2.5). Equivalent provisions apply with regard to the preparation of group resolution packs by banks, building societies and designated investment firms (rule 3). The resolution packs must be kept up-to-date and the PRA notified of any material changes (rule 4). Firms must take into account the nature, scale and complexity of their businesses and establish and maintain appropriate internal processes to govern the resolution pack with an executive director being responsible for this. The firm's governing body must also assess, approve and oversee the production of the resolution pack with these arrangements being periodically reviewed by the audit committee (rule 5).

Candidates should have commented on the effectiveness of these arrangements. These should allow all major firms to manage any pre-crisis situation effectively with authorities being able to take necessary action in the event that the firm's internal RRPs fail to contain the crisis. Implementation of higher capital standards, new liquidity measures and leverage restrictions under Basel III with the new proposed RRP constitute the most important post-crisis responses and make many other measures redundant, with the other measures on risk management and balanced remuneration provision. Effective support (lender of last resort) arrangements are nevertheless still be necessary in the most extreme situations with effective macro-prudential oversight being required at all times.

## C33.

### Question

Discuss the extent to which financial firms must consider the impact of future exogenous (or external) shocks in designing or reviewing their internal ethical and cultural frameworks, with specific focus on major geopolitical events, such as the Russian entry into Ukraine in February 2022.

In this question, candidates were asked to assess to what extent they had to consider the impact of current and future exogenous (or external) shocks, including major geopolitical events, such as with the Russian entry into Ukraine in February 2022, in designing or reviewing their internal ethical and cultural frameworks. The FCA has issued specific guidance on the events in Ukraine and their impact on financial markets, noting the impact of the events and financial sanctions imposed. Firms engaged in securities activities were reminded of their obligations under the UK Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) and to disclose inside information as soon as possible, unless they have a valid reason to delay disclosure. The market was to be kept fully informed of any information or changes that are required to be disclosed under MAR.

Companies' disclosure obligations continued even where trading of securities had been suspended. Jon Cunliffe, Deputy Governor for Financial Stability at the Bank of England, gave a speech on 'Monetary and Financial Stability and the Invasion of Ukraine' at the European Economics & Financial Centre on 4 April 2022 in which he discussed the impact that the Russian invasion had on the UK economy and on UK financial stability.

The FCA issued separate guidance in relation to the COVID-19 crisis, with information also having been made available for consumers dealing with financial difficulties during the pandemic.

Candidates should have noted that firms have to ensure that they can continue to comply with all relevant regulatory obligations at all times and to maintain the necessary systems and controls and business continuity arrangements which can withstand any major disruption and shock. More obligations, such as those imposed under FCA PRIN and the PRA Fundamental Rules, become of even more importance at such times with financial ethical policies more generally.

Firms would generally have to comply with all existing relevant provisions including PRIN 1 (integrity), PRIN 6 (customers' interests), PRIN 7 (communications), PRIN 8 (avoiding conflicts of interest) and PRIN 9 (trust), as well as possibly PRIN 10 (protecting client assets). They must also continue to treat customers fairly at times. Parallel measures are imposed under the PRA Fundamental Rules. Individuals would be subject to equivalent provisions under COCON.

Candidates could have outlined the FCA's and the PRA's existing treatment of financial ethics in modern financial markets. They could have explained the background to PRIN and the Fundamental Rules within the larger handbooks and listed each of these, with the key requirement being to act with integrity. While no separate guidance is provided on this by the FCA or the PRA with regard to firms, additional information was provided under COCON 4, with regard to the meaning of the main equivalent provisions in connection with individuals.

Rather than focus on ethics, the FCA has shifted its examination to firms' culture. Andrew Bailey stressed the importance of culture, with it being of 'the utmost importance to financial regulators'. Culture is dependent on a range of contributory factors, including management and governance, remuneration and incentive structure, management quality and effectiveness, and personnel adoption and adherence. Many of the recent major prudential or conduct scandals arose from cultural failures in governance, remuneration, risk management and leadership.

The FCA expects financial leaders to manage the drivers of behaviour in their firms to create and maintain cultures which reduce the potential for harm. Culture is stated to be at "the heart" of how the FCA authorises and supervises firms. It is referred to as "the habitual behaviours and mindsets that characterise an organisation", with the FCA focusing on four key drivers of purpose, leadership, rewards, and management and governance. The FCA published a collection of 28 papers on 'Transforming Culture in Financial Services' in March 2018 (DP 18/2).

Candidates could have referred to any of the above.

## C45.

### Question

What are the major themes within the FCA's Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) strategy? Why do these themes exist and are they important for financial regulation?

### Answer

The FCA's Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) strategy was published in November 2021. This strategy was to build further trust and transparency in the area of ESG investing and other associated areas. The strategy is based on five core themes. For the workbook these are recorded as:

- Transparency – promoting transparency on climate change and wider sustainability along the value chain.

- Trust – building trust and integrity in ESG-labelled instruments, products, and the supporting ecosystem.

- Tools – working with others to enhance industry capabilities and support firms' management of climate-related and wider sustainability risks, opportunities, and impacts.

- Transition – supporting the role of finance in delivering a market-led transition to a more sustainable economy.

- Team – developing strategies, organisational structures, resources, and tools to support the integration of ESG into FCA activities.

These principles are important as they are part of the CISI Code of Conduct in Principle 7. This states a CISI member needs to "Respect Others and the Environment – to treat everyone fairly and with respect, supporting opportunity for all, embracing diversity and inclusion and ensuring that the environmental impact of your work is considered." More generally moving towards more sustainable and green methods of doing business reflects wider global challenges faced by businesses across the world. Despite this importance there are substantial concerns that 'green washing' can occur in the representation, development and marketing of financial assets linked to ESG objectives. This misrepresentation of the green credentials of financial assets is seen both in the UK and globally. This outcome has been attributed to a corrosive influence on this market for green and sustainable financial assets undermining trust and constraining growth in these markets.

It may be helpful to consider further regulatory publications in the areas such as the FCA discussion paper on sustainability disclosure requirements and investment labels 2021, and the 2021 FCA policy statement on enhanced climate-related disclosures for asset managers, life insurers and FCA-regulated pension providers. ## C46.

### Question

Explain the nature of financial technology (Fintech) and comment on the extent to which regulatory authorities can manage additional risks and exposures that may arise.

### Answer

FinTech refers to the use of new computing and digital technology to support and improve the structure and operation of banking and financial markets and the delivery of banking and financial services. This includes digital banking and payment, electronic securities trading (including algorithmic and high-frequency trading), digital insurance (including robo advice) as well as the increased use of automation and robotics (including chatbots), digital and cloud delivery, big data analytics, machine reading and learning and artificial intelligence (AI). This also includes distributed ledger technology (DLT) and blockchain with digital currencies such as Bitcoin. DLT is principally based on decentralisation and disintermediation, access cryptography and transaction hashing and verification mining and reconciliation based on computer code rather than traditional forms of law and regulation.

Regulatory technology (RegTech) refers to the use of technology for compliance purposes (including risk management and reporting) with authorities also using technology to improve supervisory contact and conduct (SupTech). The FCA launched Project Innovate October 2014 to promote the development of technological innovation within the financial services area. This includes supporting firms through its Innovation Hub, Advice Unit and Regulatory Sandbox (opened in May 2016) which allows firms to test innovative new products, services, business models and delivery mechanisms in real market conditions without the threat of regulatory sanction. Potential advantages arise through increased efficiency with lower costs, improved marketing, enhanced performance and fund management, innovative distribution, more efficient back office processing and improved customer service more generally.

A significant number of new challenges nevertheless arise from a technology, business model, consumer, user and stakeholder, markets, regulation and governance, infrastructure, central banking and monetary policy and financial stability perspective. These include technology risk and business and model failure, excessive outsourcing, technology dependence, network collapse, business dislocation and fragmentation, supervisory and regulatory dilution and defect and the possibility of increased speed and volume of loss transmission and overall financial instability and systemic collapse. More specific regulatory threats can be identified in terms of the impact of new technology on the FCA's consumer protection, integrity and competition objectives and the PRA's soundness, policyholder and competition objectives. The degree of disruption that may arise in the financial services industry cannot be predicted with certainty with the UK regulatory and supervisory framework having been set up in response to past non-technology related threats. It remains unclear to what extent the authorities would be able to respond to any future technology induced or aggravated crises that arise.

## C47.

### Question

Explain the current landscape for regulating crypto assets in the UK, including any consultations to date and proposed regulation in the future. In your answer, refer to the management of risks that arise from the trade in crypto assets.

### Answer

Currently the regulation of the trade in crypto assets is in a state of flux, with widespread consultation as to regulatory changes both in the UK and internationally. This market has raised major consumer protection concerns particularly in relation to the inappropriate sales and promotion of crypto assets. These activities have been linked with significant consumer detriment. The market is also poorly defined with crypto assets including a wide range of different and distinct products, which have changed frequently over time.

Moreover, there are concerns that some of the trade in some crypto assets may be associated with organised crime and could raise money laundering concerns. These are reflected in the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 where businesses carrying out certain crypto asset activities will need to comply with the regulations in relation to those activities and register with the FCA. There is also EU law in this area including the Sixth Money Laundering Directive (6MLD).

In January 2022, the FCA published a consultation paper on strengthening the financial promotion rules for high-risk investments, which include crypto assets, and in August 2022, the FCA published its final rules.

In its consultation, the FCA proposed to make changes in the following areas:

- Classification of high-risk investments

- Readily realisable securities

- Restricted mass market investments

- Non-mass market investments

- The consumer journey into high-risk investments

- Strengthen the role of firms approving and communicating financial promotions

- Applying the financial promotion rules to qualifying crypto-assets.

From 1 December 2022, the following requirements apply in relation to risk warnings for financial promotions of high-risk investments:

- Promotion of non-mass market investments (NMMI) is banned to retail investors

- Promotion of restricted mass market investments (RMMI) is subject to certain restrictions to retail investors

- Non-readily realisable securities and peer-to-peer agreements

- Crypto-assets were also envisaged being in this category, but this will follow at a later stage

- Enhanced requirements in terms of risk warnings (where the customer journey into high-risk investments begins)

- Risk warnings: stating that investors could lose all of their money invested

- Risk summaries: prescribed risk summaries – should take no more than two minutes to read

- Firm can diverge from this if there are valid reasons for doing so

- Banning incentives to invest: the ban of monetary or non-monetary benefits (ie refer a friend or new joiner bonuses)

- Cooling off period: minimum 24-hour cooling off period for first-time investors with a firm

- FCA would like to see firms applying higher standards than the minimum regulatory standards

- Personalised risk warnings: first-time investors with a firm must be provided with a personalised risk warning that must include the client's name

- Client categorisation: retail clients must self certify they meet the criteria to receive promotions on high-risk investments. Retail clients must provide evidence as to why they believe they meet the relevant criteria, ie, a statement on their income/assets.

The following Handbook guidance applied from 1 February 2023:

- Enhanced requirements for firms approving and communicating financial promotions

- Displaying the name of the approver and date of approval on the financial promotion

- Potential for use of firms' FRN if due to limits on space

- Approvers must have appropriate levels of competence and expertise in the type of investment/service being promoted

- Approvers must take an active role in ensuring that the promotion remains compliant while being used

- Approvers take reasonable steps to ensure compliance with appropriateness rules periodically through the life of the promotion

- Enhanced conflict of interest requirements for approvers

- Approvers must undertake preliminary suitability assessments before NMMIs are promoted to retail investors.

## C50.

### Question

Comment on the extent to which the UK regulatory and supervisory framework and measures adopted following the global financial crisis are capable of managing the additional risks and exposures that may arise with regard to external or exogenous shocks such as a biological pandemic or natural disaster.

### Answer

The Coronavirus crisis 2020 highlighted the importance of the need to manage endogenous (internal) as well as exogenous (external) shocks. These may include biological (pandemic) events, natural disasters (earthquakes and tsunami), climatic impacts (floods and droughts) as well as terrorism and cyber terrorism, warfare and nuclear threats. The question could either have been answered either by referring to the general responses adopted following the Global Financial Crisis or specific measures targeted at the coronavirus crisis.

More general earlier responses included the FSA's Supervisory Enhancement Programme (SEP) and credible deterrence enforcement initiative, enhanced capital, liquidity and leverage (under Basel 3 and EU CRD 4 and CRR), College Supervision, Resolution and Recovery Planning (RRPs) and Special Resolution Regimes (SRRs), management accountability and liability (including under the UK Senior Managers and Certification Regime) and new macro-prudential regimes (including with the UK Financial Policy Committee (FPC), European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) and US Financial Stability Oversight Committee (FSOC)). Reference could also have been made to the revision of the FSMA and establishment of the new institutional structure within the UK, with the creation of the FCA and PRA, as well as possibly with the introduction of structural regulation (ring fencing) although separate issues arise with this.

As much detail as possible could have been provided for the highest marks including reference to the specific legislative measures involved. It is arguable that all these measures substantially assisted ensure that financial markets and firms were more stable and resilient and capable of withstanding such shocks as the Coronavirus crisis. Other market and regulatory initiatives were adopted in response to the Coronavirus specifically while other existing systems and controls and continuity planning arrangements allowed firms to continue to operate.

While a number of regulatory adjustments and concessions were announced by the authorities, in particular, to allow the Government's market support measures to operate, markets and firms to ensure that they could continue to operate. The UK government announced a number of market support measures including the Corporate Financing Facility, Business Interruption Loan Scheme, Large Business Interruption Loan Scheme, Job Retention (Furlough) scheme, Term Funding Scheme, Business Rate Reductions, Small Business Grant Fund, retail, hospitality and leisure support, Business Support grants, Future Fund and Post-16 Provider Relief as well as 'Eat Out to Help Out' scheme. A series of regulatory initiatives were announced. These included maintaining earlier Systemic Risk Buffer (SRB) rates, modification of leverage ratios, reporting in disclosure amendments, governance concessions for senior managers including on furloughing functions and delayed implementation of Basel III.1 by one year. Other measures were announced by the FCA including in relation to consumer credit relief, listed company financing, pension and retirement income and client asset controls.

The Coronavirus crisis also highlighted the need for firms to ensure that they can operate on a continuing basis in the event of any market or social disruption arising. This is concerned with maintaining appropriate systems and controls with business continuity dealt with under SYSC 3.2.19 and operational risk dealt with under SYSC 13 including external events and other changes in SYSC 13.8. Firms should have in place appropriate arrangements, having regard to the nature, scale and complexity of the business, to ensure that they can continue to function and meet the regulatory obligations in the event of unforeseen interruption which arrangements are regularly updated and tested to ensure effectiveness (SYSC 3.2.19).

Firms must also maintain appropriate Business Continuity Planning (BCP) arrangements which allow them to continue operating, including through remote staff working, as well as ensure continued compliance with all relevant regulatory obligations (SYSC 13.8). Firms should consider the likelihood and impact of a disruption on the continuity of operations from unexpected events. This may include disruptions arising through the loss or failure of internal and external resources (including people, systems and assets), information loss or corruption and other external events (including vandalism, war and acts of God) (SYSC 13.8.5).

The firm should adopt appropriate arrangements to maintain the continuity of its operations and reduce the likelihood of disruption (including through succession planning, systems resilience and dual processing) and the impact of the disruption (including through contingency arrangements and insurance) (SYSC 13.8.6).

The strategy for maintaining operational continuity and plans for communicating and testing the strategy should be properly documented. This would include formal business continuity plans to deal with short, medium and long term disruptions (including resource requirements, recovery priorities and communication arrangements), escalation and invocation plans (including implementation processes and contact information), information integrity validation and appropriate review and update processes having regard to the firm's operations and risk profile (SYSC 13.8.7). Firms should assess the appropriateness of any alternative sites used including with regard to the location, speed of recovery and adequacy resources and any issues that arise with regard to shared use (SYSC 13.8.8). Separate provisions apply with regard to outsourcing (SYSC). The Bank of England monitors business continuity and operational resilience within the UK financial sector with the FCA and Treasury with responses coordinated through the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) and National Crime Agency (NCA). The objectives are to ensure that retail and wholesale markets remain open and functioning, there is an orderly and early return to trading in the event of closure, involve infrastructure providers and market participants and relevant decision taking and facilitate market initiatives. The UK has also adopted the Civil Contingencies Act 2004, which replaced the Civil Defence Act 1948.

## C63.

### Question

As Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) concerns continue to shape financial markets, UK regulators are increasing their focus on ESG. Discuss how financial firms are being impacted by ESG regulations and whether the current regulatory framework is effective in ensuring transparency, accountability, and long-term sustainability. What challenges do firms face in implementing ESG policies, and how might regulation evolve in the future?

### Answer

This question asked candidates to discuss how financial firms are being affected by the growing regulatory focus on Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) issues in the UK. It requires an evaluation of whether the current regulatory framework effectively promotes transparency, accountability, and long-term sustainability, as well as an analysis of the challenges firms face in implementing ESG policies. Candidates were also expected to consider how ESG regulation may develop in the future. While responses showed a good understanding of the growing importance of ESG in financial services, marks were missed by failing to reference specific regulatory initiatives such as TCFD, SDR, or the ISSB framework.

ESG considerations are playing an increasingly prominent role in the UK financial sector. Regulatory initiatives aim to improve transparency, accountability, and promote sustainable investing. The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and other regulators are introducing requirements for climate-related disclosures, enhanced governance standards, and rules around greenwashing. These changes are designed to help investors make informed decisions and to support the UK's transition to a more sustainable economy. Firms are being impacted through the need to update internal systems, gather reliable ESG data, and improve disclosures.

The FCA's requirements under Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) apply to many listed companies and asset managers, who must report on governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics related to climate risks. The introduction of the Sustainability Disclosure Requirements (SDR) will further raise the bar, introducing labels for sustainable investment products to prevent misleading claims. Challenges for firms include data availability, the cost of compliance, and aligning global ESG standards. Many firms struggle to obtain consistent and reliable ESG data from third-party providers, and there is a lack of standardisation across jurisdictions. Implementation requires investment in staff training, systems development, and integrating ESG factors into investment and risk management frameworks.

On the international stage, there is increasing momentum towards a harmonised global framework. At COP29, global leaders reaffirmed their commitment to the work of the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), which is working to develop consistent global sustainability disclosure standards. The UK has endorsed this direction, which may help reduce fragmentation and make ESG reporting more consistent across borders. However, the geopolitics of ESG remain complex. In the United States, there has been some backtracking on ESG commitments, particularly in politically conservative states where ESG investing is viewed with scepticism. This divergence can create uncertainty for multinational firms and underscores the importance of clear UK guidance and leadership in this space. Looking ahead, the UK's regulatory framework is expected to evolve in alignment with international standards, while continuing to address domestic priorities around climate risk, biodiversity, and social responsibility. Regulators will likely require more granular disclosures, and firms must demonstrate not just intent, but measurable ESG outcomes. A well-regulated ESG environment will improve investor confidence and help mobilise capital towards long-term, sustainable economic goals.
