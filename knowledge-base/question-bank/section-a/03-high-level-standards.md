**A3.5** Explain the “senior managers” regime. 

_(3 marks)_ 

Before undertaking a senior manager function on behalf of an authorised firm a person must first receive approval from the appropriate regulator to undertake that function. The firm seeks approval for the individual, and before granting approval the regulator must be satisfied that the candidate is fit and proper to perform the senior manager function to which the application relates. The regulator will have regard to a number of things when assessing the fitness and propriety of a person to perform a particular senior manager function. In particular these will include the person’s: 

- Honesty, integrity and reputation; 

- Competence and capability; 

- Financial soundness. 


Once approved an individual is subject to the provisions of COCON and the conduct rules (both Tier 1 and Tier 2) and is also subject to discipline by the regulator for failing to comply with relevant rules or principles. Penalties for this can include fines and withdrawal of approval. 

- **A3.6** Explain how the FCA ensures that those who seek to become authorised are Fit and Proper. _(4 marks)_ 

Schedule 6 of FSMA sets out threshold conditions which represent the minimum conditions which a firm is required to satisfy and continue to satisfy, in order to be given and retain Part 4A permission. There are five of these conditions as follows: 

- Location of offices 

- Effective supervision 

- Appropriate resources 

- Suitability 

- Business model 

The FCA will therefore consider where the firm is established, but also who has influence over running the business. The firm must demonstrate through its business plan that it will comply with the requirements of BIPRU and SYSC. The firm must demonstrate that its financial and other resources are appropriate for the likely risks that it will face. 

In assessing suitability the FCA must be satisfied by the firm that it is fit and proper person having regard to all of the circumstances, including connections to any person, the nature of the business and the need to ensure that its affairs are conducted soundly and prudently. 

The FCA will have regard to whether the firm will conduct its business with integrity and in compliance with proper standards, has competent and prudent management and can demonstrate that it conducts its affairs with due skill, care and diligence. 

In addition the FCA will look at whether the governing body of the firm is made up of individuals with an appropriate range of skills and experience to understand, operate and manage the firm’s regulated activities. Also that the firm has in place adequate systems of 


control to comply with requirements under SYSC. These should include areas such as recruitment of only employees who are honest and competent and committed to high standards of integrity. 

- **A3.7** Give FOUR examples of matters the FCA would have regard to when determining a person’s honesty, integrity and reputation. _(2 marks)_ 

   - Whether the person has been convicted of a criminal offence (particularly of fraud, dishonesty or otherwise relevant to financial services). 

   - Adverse findings or settlement in civil proceedings. 

   - Whether the person has been the subject of, or interviewed during an investigation by the FCA or other regulatory body. 

   - Previous contravention of any requirements and standards of a regulatory system.

- **A3.9** Explain briefly the general purpose and structure of the High Level Standards contained in the FCA’s Handbook of Rules and Guidance. _(4 marks)_ 

The High Level Standards are set out in Block 1 of the FCA Handbook of Rules and Guidance. The purpose is to establish the general minimum standards of conduct with which regulated firms and their key personnel (approved persons) must comply at all times. 

The High Level Standards consist of the: 

   - a) Principles for Businesses (PRIN); 

   - b) Senior Management Arrangements, Systems and Controls (SYSC); 

   - c) Code of Conduct (COCON) 

   - d) Threshold Conditions (COND); 

   - e) Statements of Principle and Code of Practice for Approved Persons (APER); 

   - f) Fit and Proper Test for Approved Persons (FIT); 

   - g) Financial Stability and Market Confidence (FINMAR); 

   - h) Training and Competence (TC); and 

   - i) General Provisions (GEN). 

- **A3.10** List three of the main requirements governing supervision of outsourcing relationships under SYSC. _(3 marks)_ 

Outsourcing is dealt with as an aspect of operational risk under SYSC 8. Firms must maintain robust governance arrangements and adequate internal control mechanisms over material and non-material outsourcing arrangements. Non-material outsourcing may be dealt with in a manner that is appropriate and proportionate to the firm's operations. 

The FCA may also object within one month where an MiFID investment firm intends to outsource portfolio management outside the EEA unless: 

- a) the other service provider is authorised or registered in its home country to provide the services covered and subject to effective supervision; and 

- b) an appropriate cooperation agreement has been entered into between the FCA and the overseas authority concerned.

**A3.12** Explain the different ways in which firms can manage conflicts of interest . 

_(3 marks)_ 

A firm must manage conflicts of interest fairly between itself and customers and between customers under FCA PRIN 8. Conflicts of interest can generally be dealt with either through declining to act, Chinese walls or maintaining an independence policy and disclosure as a last resort. 

Conflicts of interest are dealt with under SYSC 10 with firms being required to take all reasonable steps to identify conflicts between the firm and a client or between clients (SYSC 10.1.3R) with types of conflicts being set out in SYSC 10.1.4R. Firms must maintain and operate effective organisational and administrative arrangements to prevent conflicts creating a material risk of damage to clients (SYSC 10.1.7R) failing which the conflict must be disclosed to the client (SYSC 10.1.8R). 

Common platform firms and management companies must maintain an effective conflicts of interest policy (SYSC 10.1.10R) with specific provisions applying with regard to the content of the policy (SYSC 10.1.11R). Firewalls and Chinese walls are dealt with under (SYSC 10.2).

**A3.14** Explain the principal purpose of the Senior Managers Regime (SMR). 

_(3 marks)_ 

The objective of the **Senior Managers Regime (SMR)** is to ensure that senior managers are held accountable for any **misconduct** within their specific areas of responsibility. Firms have to prepare **Responsibility Maps** to allocate all relevant Senior Management functions (SMFs) with each manager adding a **Statement of Responsibility (SOR)** (s60(2A) FSMA). 

Authorised persons must vet candidates before putting them forward for approval to ensure that they are fit and proper in terms of relevant qualifications, training, competence and personal characteristics (s60A). Conditions such as time limits may be attached to approvals (s61(2B)) and approval can be varied subsequently (s 63ZB). The regime applies to persons approved under s59 FSMA to carry out a designated senior management function (SMF) as provided for under s 59ZA FSMA as amended. 

**A3.15** Explain the general purpose and content of the Financial Conduct Authority’s conduct risk control framework. _(3 marks)_ 

This question can be answered in a number of different ways. The FCA has generally been made responsible for conduct regulation with the PRA managing prudential regulation 


under the revised institutional structure established under the FSA 2012. The FCA is specifically responsible for conduct risk although this is a difficult issue to define precisely. The term conduct risk is not referred to in the FSMA as amended. Conduct risk is not separately defined in the FCA Glossary nor is the term risk separately defined. The objectives of the FCA are referred to under the FSMA in terms of acting as a financial function authority with the specific operational objectives of promoting consumer protection, system integrity and competition (s1B (2) and (3) FSMA). Conduct risk is not separately defined and it would generally appear that this refers to anything that is not of a prudential regulation nature in practice. 

The FCA has been developing its approach to conduct risk under its 2013 and 2014 Risk Outlook documents with a series of priority actions being set out in its Business Plans for 2013 and 2014. Reference may also have been made to various speeches. 

The new policy risk model is generally made up of two parts with ‘Drivers of Risk’ (Part A) consisting of Underlying drivers (inherent factors and Structures and business conduct features) and Environmental developments) and ‘Evolving risk landscape’ (Part B) consisting of Cross-market pressures and related risks and Forward-looking areas of focus (2014 Risk Outlook P7). 

Candidates may also have referred to relevant high level regulatory provisions covering conduct risk including PRIN 1 (integrity), 5 (market conduct), 6 (customers' interest), 7 (client communication), 8 (conflicts of interest), 9 (trust), 10 (asset protection) and 12 (consumer duty) as well as COCON 1 (integrity) and 5 (market conduct), SYSC 10 (conflicts of interest) and 19 (remuneration) as well as COND and FIT more generally. Training requirements may also have been referred to under TC as well as the Consumer Duty, Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC) and the Retail Distribution Review (RDR). 

- **A3.16** Explain the purpose and content of the offence of causing a financial institution to fail, imposed under section 36 of the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013. 

_(3 marks)_ 

s36 creates a new offence of causing a financial institution to fail. This applies where the person is a senior manager in relation to an institution and takes (or agrees to take) a 


decision (or fails to act) and was aware at the time of the decision that this could cause the failure of a group institution in circumstances where the conduct would fall below that which would be reasonably expected and the decision (or failure to act) led to the failure of the institution (s36(1)). 

The person is guilty on summary conviction for up to 12 months imprisonment or seven years on indictment or a fine or both (s36(2)). Relevant terms are defined in s37. 

**A3.17** Explain the principal purpose of the Certification Regime (CR) established under the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013. _(3 marks)_ 

The Certification regime was adopted with the Senior Managers regime following recommendation by the Parliamentary Commission for Banking Standards (PCBS) on ‘Changing Banking for Good’ in June 2013. The Certification regime was introduced for deposit taking firms (other than credit unions) and applies to personnel other than Senior Managers that may pose a risk of significant harm to the firm or a customer. Firms are to certify the fitness and propriety of staff every year (s 63E FSMA) rather than have preapproval by the authorities as under the earlier approved persons regime. The same conditions apply with regard to certification as approval including qualifications, training, competence and characteristics (s 63F). A new set of Conduct Rules (COCON) were also adopted (under s 64A FSMA) which replace the earlier APER rules.

**A3.20** Explain why it is important for a firm to keep accurate records of past business undertaken (SYSC 9). _(3 marks)_ 

SYSC 9 stipulates that keeping accurate records of past business is critical for effective regulation and compliance. Often it is required to keep records for seven years. Clear and accurate records allow a regulator to effectively monitor the firms’ behaviours, ensure they are compliant and have fulfilled all their obligations to their customers and clients. This involves record keeping both within the firm of internal procedures and of external actions in an accessible manner which may be stored for future reference by any appropriate regulatory authority. 

Critically, and to satisfy the demands of UK MiFID (as outlined in the workbook), the firm needs to ensure: 

1. The record can be reconstituted into each key stage of the processing of each transaction. 

2. It must be possible for any corrections or other amendments, and the contents of the records prior to such corrections and amendments, to be easily ascertained. 

3. It must not be possible for the records otherwise to be manipulated or altered.

**A3.24** Define and explain **one** of the cross-cutting obligations under the Consumer Duty. 

_(3 marks)_ 

• Acting in Good Faith Towards Retail Customers – Firms must act with honesty, fairness, and openness in all dealings with retail customers. Actions should align with what customers reasonably expect, and this principle should be embedded at the design stage of products and services to ensure fairness from the beginning. 

• Avoiding Foreseeable Harm – Firms are responsible for ensuring that retail customers understand and accept the inherent risks in products and services. They must take proactive 


steps to prevent foreseeable harm within their control and must not exploit customers' vulnerabilities, behavioural biases, or lack of financial literacy. 

• Enabling and Supporting Retail Customers to Pursue Their Financial Objectives – Firms should provide relevant information and understanding to support customers in making their own financial decisions. This approach empowers customers to take responsibility for their choices, fostering an environment of trust and confidence in the firm's products and services. 

**A3.25** What is the purpose of the Consumer Credit Sourcebook (CONC), and how does it protect consumers in the credit market? _(3 marks)_ 

The FCA first introduced the Consumer Credit Sourcebook (CONC) in April 2014. The purpose of CONC is to set out the detailed obligations that are specific to credit-related regulated activities and activities connected to those activities carried on by firms. 

CONC protects consumers by making sure that firms carry out affordability checks before offering credit, provide clear information about the costs and terms of loans, and don’t engage in aggressive or misleading sales practices. It also sets rules for dealing with customers in financial difficulty, such as offering appropriate repayment plans instead of taking immediate action like repossession. These rules help prevent consumers from taking on credit they can't afford and ensure that they are treated fairly if they face problems repaying a loan. 

**A3.26** What steps should firms take to achieve good outcomes for vulnerable customers? _(3 marks)_ 

To achieve good outcomes for vulnerable customers, firms should take appropriate action to understand the needs of their target market/customer base. Firms should make sure staff have the right skills and capability to recognise and respond to the needs of vulnerable customers. Additionally, firms should respond to customer needs throughout product design, flexible customer service provision and communications; and monitor and assess whether they are meeting and responding to the needs of customers with characteristics of vulnerability, and make improvements where this is not happening.
