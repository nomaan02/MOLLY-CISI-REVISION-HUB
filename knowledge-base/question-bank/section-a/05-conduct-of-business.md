- **A5.1** Explain the purpose and content of the aggregation and allocation rule under the Conduct of Business (COBS) section of the FCA Handbook of Rules and Guidance. _(4 marks)_ 

Aggregation is concerned with the grouping together of customers’ orders for execution either with those of other clients or with the firm’s.  A firm may not aggregate orders unless there is likely to be no disadvantage to the customer and any possible disadvantage has been disclosed to the customer either orally or in writing. 

Fair allocation is concerned with ensuring that no unfair preference is given to any one by a firm in its dealings having regard to transaction price and size. Firms must, in particular, ensure that customer orders are prioritised unless the firm can show on reasonable grounds that the transaction could not otherwise have been effected. 

- **A5.2** Explain what is meant by the best execution obligation imposed on firms, and identify the execution factors. _(2 marks)_ 

When executing an order for a client, a firm must take all sufficient steps to obtain the best possible result for its client taking into account the execution factors. 

The execution factors are: the price, costs, speed, likelihood of execution and settlement, size, nature or any other consideration relevant to the execution of an order. 

**A5.3** What are the best execution criteria, and what are they for? _(2 marks)_ 

The best execution criteria are used for the purpose of determining the relative importance of the execution factors (such as price, costs, speed, etc). 

The best execution criteria are: 

- The characteristics of the client (including their categorisation as retail or professional); 

- The characteristics of the client order. 


      - The characteristics of financial instruments that are the subject of the client order. 

   - The characteristics of the execution venues to which the order can be directed. 

   - (Execution venues include regulated markets, MTFs, OTFs, systematic internalisers, market makers or other liquidity providers.) 

- **A5.4** What is the general rule with regard to the timing of executing client orders? In what circumstances may this rule be varied? _(2 marks)_ 

In general, the rule on client order handling requires a firm to have in place procedures for the prompt, fair and expeditious execution of client orders. Otherwise comparable client orders should be executed in chronological order of receipt by the firm. 

Where client orders are otherwise comparable, they should be carried out sequentially and promptly, unless: 

- The characteristics of the order or prevailing market conditions make this impossible; or 

- The interests of the client require otherwise. 

**A5.5** What is the “quantitative test” for elective professional client status? _(2 marks)_ 

The “quantitative test” (which applies only in relation to MiFID business) requires that at 

least two of the following criteria are satisfied: 

- The client has carried out significant transactions on the relevant market at an average frequency of 10 per quarter over the previous four quarters. 

- The size of the client’s portfolio exceeds €500,000 (this includes cash deposits as well as financial instruments). 

- The client works, or has worked, in the financial sector for at least one year in a professional position, which requires knowledge of the transactions or services envisaged.

## **A5.7** 

What are the general obligations of a firm under the rule on suitability? _(3 marks)_ 

A firm must take reasonable steps to ensure that a personal recommendation, or a decision to trade, is suitable for its client. 

In order to assess suitability, the firm must obtain appropriate information about the client’s: 

- Investment knowledge and experience; 

- Financial situation; and 

- Investment objectives. 

The firm must obtain this information from the client, and it must be sufficient to give the firm 

a reasonable basis for believing that a proposed transaction: 

- Meets the client’s investment objectives; 

- Is such that the client is able financially to bear any related investment risks consistent with his investment objectives; and 

- Is such that the client has the necessary experience and knowledge to understand those risks. 

The firm is entitled to rely on the information provided by the client (unless it is aware that the information is manifestly out of date, inaccurate or incomplete). 

- **A5.8** Explain the FCA’s requirements on inducements. _(2 marks)_ 

Principle 1 (Integrity), Principle 6 (Customers’ interests) and PRIN 12 (Consumer Duty) require a firm to conduct its business with integrity, to pay due regard to the interests of its customers and to treat them fairly. The purpose of the rule on inducements is to ensure that 


a firm does not conduct business under arrangements that might give rise to a conflict with its clients. 

A firm must not pay to, or accept from, a person other than a client any fee, commission or benefit, unless: 

- The fee, commission or benefit is designed to enhance the quality of the relevant service to the client and does not impair compliance with the firm’s duty to act in the best interests of the client; and 

- The firm clearly discloses to the client the existence, nature and amount of the fee, commission or benefit before providing the service. 

**A5.9** Explain the purpose of a Chinese wall. 

## _(2 marks)_ 

Chinese walls are used in circumstances where the FCA would consider it appropriate for a firm to withhold or not to use information that would otherwise be disclosed to, or used for the benefit of, a client. A Chinese wall provides an artificial barrier to the flow of information between the parts of a firm (provided that the arrangements remain effective and are adequately monitored). This assists the firm in complying with Principle 8 (Conflicts of interest) which requires a firm to manage a conflict of interest fairly both between itself and its clients and between one client and another. 

**A5.10** What is the ‘qualitative test’ for elective professional client status? _(2 marks)_ 

The ‘qualitative test’ requires the firm to undertake an adequate assessment of the expertise, experience and knowledge of the client that gives reasonable assurance, in light of the nature of the transactions or services envisaged, that the client is capable of making his own investment decisions and understands the risks involved. 


- **A5.11** Apart from the ‘qualitative test’, what procedures must a firm follow before it may treat a client as an elective professional client? _(2 marks)_ 

The procedures require that: 

- The client must state in writing that it wishes to be treated as a professional client (either generally or in respect of a particular service or transaction or type of transaction or product); 

- The firm must give the client a clear written warning of the protections and investor compensation rights the client may lose; and 

- The client must state in writing that it is aware of the consequences of losing such protections. 

**A5.12** What information must a firm provide to a client about itself, its services and its remuneration? _(4 marks)_ 

A firm must provide a retail client and (in the case of MiFID business) any client with prescribed information. This includes: 

- Information about the firm and its services: 

   - Name and address of the firm and appropriate contact details 

   - Statement that the firm is authorised (and the name of its competent authority) 

   - Whether the firm is acting through an appointed representative (or a tied agent) 

   - Details of occasional and periodical reporting 

   - Conflicts of interest policy 

- Information about safeguarding of designated investments and client money 

- Information about costs and associated charges 

This information must be provided in a durable medium (or via a compliant website), either before any service is provided or (if this is impossible, as where an agreement is concluded by distance communication) immediately after starting to provide the service. 

A firm carrying on MiFID business must also make available information to enable a client to identify any relevant investor compensation scheme. 


- **A5.13** What were the main changes brought in by the Retail Distribution Review (RDR) and when did these become effective? _(2 marks)_ 

The three main changes brought in by the RDR are as follows: 

- When dealing with retail clients, a firm is now required to describe its services as either ‘independent’ advice or ‘restricted’ advice; 

- For new advice to a retail client commission-based remuneration has been ended. Firms are now required to charge retail clients an agreed fee for the advice they give; 

- The professional standards of investment advisers have been increased (to QCF Level 4). 

The changes brought in by the RDR became effective on 31 December 2012. 

- **A5.14** What are the two circumstances, in terms of the rule on appropriateness, when a firm may be required to give a warning to its client? _(2 marks)_ 

A firm is required to give a warning to the client in two circumstances (these warnings may be provided in a standardised format): 

   - Where the firm considers, on the basis of the information received, that the product or service is NOT appropriate for the client, it must warn the client of that fact. 

   - Where the client does not provide information to enable the firm to assess appropriateness (or where the client provides insufficient information regarding his knowledge and experience), the firm must warn the client that it is UNABLE TO DETERMINE whether the product or service is appropriate for him. 

- **A5.15** For the purpose of the rule on appropriateness, what are non-complex financial instruments? _(2 marks)_ 

Non-complex financial instruments must satisfy the following criteria: 

- They are not derivatives (or similar products) 

- There are frequent opportunities to trade in them at independently determined publicly available prices 


- They do not involve a potential liability that exceeds their cost 

- There is publicly available information on their characteristics that is likely to be readily understood by the average retail client for the purpose of making an investment decision. 

**A5.16** Outline the main conduct of business rules in respect of a firm’s order execution policy. 

_(4 marks)_ 

In order to satisfy its obligation to obtain the best possible result for its clients, a firm is required to establish and operate an order execution policy. This policy must include, for each class of financial instrument, information about the different execution venues that the firm will use to execute client orders and the factors affecting the choice of execution venue. 

A firm must provide appropriate information to its clients about its order execution policy. In the case of retail client, the firm must provide the following details about its execution policy before executing any transaction: 

- An account of the relative importance the firm assigns to the execution factors; 

- A list of the main execution venues used by the firm; 

- A clear and prominent warning that any specific instructions from the client may prevent the firm from taking the steps that it would otherwise have taken under its order execution policy to obtain the best possible result for the client. 

Where the order execution policy provides for the possibility that client orders may be executed outside a regulated market, an MTF or an OTF, the firm must inform its clients about this possibility. 

A firm must obtain the prior consent of its clients to its order execution policy. 

A firm must obtain the prior express consent of its clients before executing their orders outside a regulated market, an MTF or an OTF. This may be by means of a general agreement or transaction-by-transaction. 


A firm must regularly monitor the effectiveness of its order execution arrangements and execution policy, and it must review them annually. 

A firm must be able to demonstrate to its clients, at their request, that it has executed their orders in accordance with its execution policy. 

- **A5.17** What are the requirements in respect of a client limit order that is not immediately executed? _(2 marks)_ 

Where a client places a limit order in respect of shares traded in a regulated market and the order is not immediately executed under prevailing market conditions, then, unless the client expressly instructs otherwise, the firm must make the order public with a view to having it executed as soon as possible. The firm can do this by transmitting the order to a regulated market, an MTF or an OTF that operates an order book trading system. 

This obligation to make public a limit order will not apply to a limit order that is large in scale compared with normal market size (large in scale for this purpose is a size greater than approximately 10 per cent of the share’s average daily turnover). 

**A5.18** What information must be included in a periodic statement provided by a firm to a retail client? _(3 marks)_ 

The information to be included in a periodic statement comprises: 

- Name of firm 

- Name of retail client’s account 

- Statement of the contents and the valuation of the portfolio, including: 

   - Market value (or fair value) of each investment 

   - Cash balance at the beginning and end of the reporting period 

   - Performance of the portfolio during the reporting period 

- Total fees and charges 

- Comparison of performance with benchmark (if any) 

- Total of dividends and interest received 


   - Information about corporate actions (e.g. bonus issues) 

   - Relevant trade confirmation information (if not provided on a transaction-bytransaction basis) 

- **A5.19** Identify four of the ways in which conflicts of interest can be dealt with under the Conduct of Business Rules (COBS) contained in the FCA’s Handbook. _(4 marks)_ 

A firm must manage conflicts of interest fairly between itself and customers and between customers under PRIN 8. 

Conflicts of interest can generally be dealt with either through Disclosure, Declining to act, Chinese Walls or maintaining an Independence policy. Special rules apply with regard to common platform firms under SYSC 10 and investment research under COBS 12. Disclosure is now only to be used as a measure of last resort under SYSC 10. 

- **A5.20** Explain the two different types of professional client covered by the Conduct of Business 

Rules (COBS) contained in the FCA’s Handbook of Rules and Guidance. 

_(2 marks)_ 

## A client may either a **per se professional client** or an **elective professional client** 

(under COBS 3.5.1 R). 

A **per se professional client** includes any of the following (unless it is an eligible counterparty or is given a different categorisation under the chapter): 

- 1) an entity required to be authorised or regulated to operate in the financial markets; 

- 2) in relation to MiFID or equivalent third country business a large undertaking meeting two of the following size requirements on a company basis: (a) balance sheet total of EUR 20,000,000; (b) net turnover of EUR 40,000,000; and (c) own funds of EUR 2,000,000; 

- 3) in relation to business that is not MiFID or equivalent third country business a large undertaking meeting certain conditions; 


- 4) a national or regional government, a public body that manages public debt, a central bank, an international or supranational institution (such as the World Bank, the IMF, the ECP, the EIB) or another similar international organisation; or 

- 5) another institutional investor whose main activity is to invest in financial instruments (in relation to the firm's MiFID or equivalent third country business) or designated investments (in relation to the firm's other business) including securitisation of assets or other financing transactions entities. 

## **Elective Professional Client** 

A firm may treat a client as an **elective professional client** if it falls under 1) and 3) or, where applicable, 2): 

- 1) the firm undertakes an adequate assessment of the expertise, experience and knowledge of the client that gives reasonable assurance, in light of the nature of the transactions or services envisaged, that the client is capable of making his own investment decisions and understanding the risks involved **(the ‘qualitative test’)** ; 

- 2) in relation to MiFID or equivalent third country business in the course of that assessment, at least two of the following criteria are satisfied: a) the client has carried out transactions, in significant size, on the relevant market at an average frequency of 10 per quarter over the previous four quarters; b) the size of the client's financial instrument portfolio, defined as including cash deposits and financial instruments, exceeds EUR 500,000; or c) the client works or has worked in the financial sector for at least one year in a professional position, which requires knowledge of the transactions or services envisaged **(the ‘quantitative test’)** ; or 

- 3) the following procedure is complied with: a) the client must state in writing to the firm that it wishes to be treated as a professional client either generally or in respect of a particular service or transaction or type of transaction or product; b) the firm must give the client a clear written warning of the protections and investor compensation rights the client may lose; and c) the client must state in writing, in a separate document from the contract, that it is aware of the consequences of losing such protections. 


- **A5.21** List four ways in which a transaction may be considered unsuitable for a client under COBS 9. _(2 marks)_ 

The following transactions would be considered unsuitable for a client: 

- a) A transaction may be unsuitable for a client because of the **risks** of the designated investments involved, the **type** of transaction, the **characteristics** of the order or the **frequency** of the trading. 

- b) In the case of managing investments, a transaction might also be unsuitable if it would result in an **unsuitable portfolio** (COBS 9.3.1). 

Reference may also be to the following to the extent necessary: 

   - a) **Churning and switching –** frequency and agreed investment strategy (COBS 9.3.2); 

   - b) **Income withdrawals and short-term annuities –** all the relevant circumstances (COBS 9.3.3); 

   - c) **Loans and mortgages** - suitability of the overall transaction (COBS 9.3.2). 

- **A5.22** List the three main types of information that must be obtained to comply with the suitability requirement imposed under COBS 9 of the FCA Handbook. _(3 marks)_ 

To comply with the suitability obligation, firms must obtain information with regard to: 

- a) Knowledge and experience in the investment field; 

- b) Investment objectives; 

- c) Financial situation.

**A5.24** State the three principal client categories under the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) Handbook and PRA Rulebook. _(3 marks)_

**A5.25** What information and content needs to be provided within a suitability report when providing a client with investment advice? _(3 marks)_ 

The suitability report is a central procedure in ensuring appropriate advice is provided to clients. Briefly this report should include details of the client’s investment objectives, knowledge, education, profession, experience of investing and the risk tolerance they can bear and be consistent with their investment objectives.

**A5.26** Outline three circumstances where a conflict of interest may arise in the provision of financial services. _(3 marks)_ 

Conflicts of interest in financial services have significant regulatory implications. The workbook defines such conflicts of interest and they include situations where a person: 

- is likely to make a financial gain, or avoid a financial loss, at the expense of the client, 

- has an interest in the outcome of a service provided to the client, 

- has a financial, or other incentive to favour the interest of another client(s) over the interest of the client, 

- carries on the same business as the client, 


• receives, or will receive, from a person other than the client, an inducement in relation to a service provided to the client. 

**A5.27** How should a firm charge for investment advice provided to retail customers? 

_(2 marks)_ 

• Advisers must charge consumers directly for the advice and services they provide. They cannot be remunerated by the product provider for making personal recommendations to consumers. The fee must be agreed upon between the adviser and the consumer prior to the service being provided. Firms must disclose in writing to retail clients the nature of the advice service, whether it is independent or restricted. If providing restricted advice, the firm must explain the nature of the restriction. This disclosure must be made in good time before providing personal recommendations or basic advice related to retail investment products. 

• The charging structure should improve the clarity of how firms describe their services to consumers, address potential distortions in consumer outcomes due to adviser remuneration, and increase the professional standards of advisers. This includes the total price, related fees, commissions, charges, expenses, and any taxes payable via the firm. If costs cannot be indicated at the time, the basis on which they will be calculated should be provided to the client so they can verify them. 

**A5.28** Explain what a ‘key investor information document’ (KIID) is and the information that must be included within it. _(3 marks)_ 

A key investor information document (KIID) needs to be issued for each UCITS or mutual fund (Undertakings for the Collective Investment in Transferable Securities) scheme managed by an authorised fund manager (AFM). These documents should include the essential characteristics of the UCITS scheme. These documents should ensure investors understand the risks of the investment product and are able to make investment decisions on an informed basis. This requires that the KIID is fair, clear, not misleading and consistent with relevant parts of the prospectus. The KIID must include: 

- identification of the UCITS scheme 

- a short description of its investment objectives and investment policy 

- past performance or, where relevant, performance scenarios 


- costs and associated charges, and the risk/reward profile of the investment 

- appropriate guidance in relation to the risks associated with investments in the scheme. 

**A5.29** What individual requirements must an investment adviser meet in order to be qualified to provide regulated investment advice to retail customers? _(4 marks)_ 

To provide advice to a retail customer, an investment adviser must possess a valid statement of professional standing (SPS) from an FCA-accredited body. These statements of professional standing confirm that an adviser: 

- has successfully completed a Level 4 (or above) qualification listed in the FCA Appropriate Qualification Tables; 

- has completed 35 hours of CPD annually; 

- be deemed to be ‘fit and proper’ in accordance with the FCA’s APER (Approved Persons in 

Appointed Representatives) and/or the SM&CR (Senior Management and Certification Regime); 

- and has agreed to adhere to a code of ethics.
