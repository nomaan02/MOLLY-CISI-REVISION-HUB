- **A8.1** List six supervisory tools set out in SUP within the FCA’s Handbook of Rules and Guidance. _(3 marks)_ 

The main supervisory tools set out in SUP include: 

- desk based reviews; 

- liaison with other agencies and regulators; 

- management meetings or representative meetings; 

- on-site inspections; 

- reviews and analysis of periodic returns and notifications; 

- past business reviews; 

- transaction monitoring; and 

- use of auditors and skilled persons. 

Specific risks may also be dealt with through a number of additional tools including: 

   - making recommendations for preventative or remedial action; 

   - providing individual guidance; 

   - imposing requirements; or 

   - varying a firm’s permission including cancellation.

- **A8.3** Name four types of administrative sanction against a firm available to the FCA under FSMA. _(2 marks)_ 

The FCA may exercise any of the following administrative sanctions against a firm: 

   - a) Fine; 

   - b) Public censure; 

   - c) Variation, refusal or restriction of permission; 

   - d) Restitution order. 

- **A8.4** Explain the purpose of a warning notice, a decision notice, a final notice and a supervisory notice under the Decision Procedure and Penalties Manual (DEPP). _(4 marks)_ 

The purpose of the notices is set out in DEPP: 

- a) Warning Notice – This provides the recipient with details about action the FCA/RDC proposes to take and the recipient’s right to make representations. 

- b) Decision Notice – This provides the recipient with details about action the FCA/RDC has decided to take subject to the RDC complying with its formal procedures. 

- c) Final Notice – This confirms the terms of the action that the FCA/RDC has decided to take and the date from which it takes effect. 

- d) Supervisory Notice – This provides the recipient with details about action the FCA/RDC has taken or proposes to take generally with immediate effect and before any formal procedures have been taken. 


**A8.5** Explain the role of the Regulatory Decisions Committee (RDC). 

## _(3 marks)_ 

When an investigation is launched by the FCA, the process is administered by the RDC. The RDC is accountable to the board of the FCA, but it is outside the FCA management structure. RDC members represent the public interest and are drawn from practitioners and non-practitioners. The only FCA employee is the chairman of the RDC. 

The FCA will normally refer more significant disciplinary matters to the RDC. Thus, the RDC will usually take the final decision on the following actions: 

   - To refuse, restrict or modify a Part 4A permission application; 

   - To refuse an application for approved person status; 

   - To make a prohibition order; 

   - To exercise the FCA’s powers to impose financial penalties or public censure; 

   - To seek a court order against any person.

**A8.7** Outline how appeals are conducted against regulatory decisions under the FSMA as amended. _(3 marks)_ 

The question may have been answered either in terms of appeals against individual decisions or appeals against the conduct of the regulatory authorities. 

Hearings and appeals against regulatory decisions are dealt with under Part 9 FSMA as amended under s 23 FSA 2012. Appeals are managed under the Upper Tribunal (under the transfer of Tribunal Functions Order 2010 SI 2010/22). This applies with regard to decisions by the regulators, the Bank of England or in respect of compensation orders under the Banking (Special Provisions) Act 2000 or Banking Act 2009. 

Part 9 FSMA contains specific provisions on proceedings, decisions and supervisory notices, offences as well as legal assistance and an appeal on a point of law (ss 132-137). 

Appeals against regulatory authorities are dealt with under Part 6 Financial Services Act 2012. The authorities are required to make arrangements for the investigation of complaints in connection with the exercise of relevant functions with an independent investigator being responsible for conducting the investigations under the complaints scheme (s 84 FSA 2012). The FSA 2012 contains further specific provisions on relevant functions, consultation and publication of the complaints scheme, the investigation of complaints, with the investigator being exempt from liability in damages (ss 84 FSA 2012). 

**A8.8** Explain the principal features of the FCA’s Firm Systematic Framework (FSF). 

_(3 marks)_ 

The FCA’s Firm Systematic Framework (FSF) is provided for under SUP1A.3.4 with the FCA Supervision Model being based on the three pillars of the FSF, Event Driven Work and Issues and Products. 


Purpose of the FSF is to assess whether a firm is, currently and prospectively, being run in a manner that results in the fair treatment of customers, minimises risks to market integrity and does not impede effective competition. The FCA asks whether the firm has the interests of customers and the integrity of the market at the core of the way its business is run. 

The FSF will identify and mitigate the key drivers of conduct behaviour and ensure that firms behave in a way that minimises the risk that they present to the FCA’s objectives. 

**A8.9** Describe and explain the appeals process following receipt of a decision notice by the FCA. _(3 marks)_ 

Any person receiving a decision or supervisory notice from the FCA has the right to appeal this decision. This involves a referral to the Upper Tribunal (or the Tax and Chancery Chamber of the Upper Tribunal). The Upper Tribunal is independent of the FCA and is appointed by the government’s Ministry. 

This appeal must be undertaken in the 28-day grace period provided by the regulator after the notice has been issued. During this period the FCA cannot take the action it has proposed. 

An appeal at the Upper Tribunal involves a rehearing of the FCA’s decision and can include new evidence that was not available to the FCA at the time. The Tribunal’s decision is binding on the FCA. 

**A8.10** Briefly describe the FCA’s approach to enforcement action. _(3 marks)_ 

The FCA follows a proportionate and risk-based approach to enforcement, which includes credible deterrence by ensuring firms and individuals comply with regulations through disciplinary action. It also emphasises restorative measures by encouraging remediation and cooperation from firms. In addition, the FCA promotes transparency by publicising enforcement actions to maintain market confidence.
