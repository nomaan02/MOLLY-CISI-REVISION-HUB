**A2.1** Identify eight activities that may be passported under the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID). _(4 marks)_ 

The following activities that may be passported under the Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID): 

- (a) Receiving and transmitting orders; 

- (b) Execution orders on behalf of clients; 

- (c) Dealing on own account; 

- (d) Managing portfolios on a discretionary basis; 

- (e) Investment advice; 

- (f) Underwriting financial instruments; 

- (g) Placing financial instruments; 

- (h) Operating a Multilateral Trading Facility (MTF). 

**A2.2** Give a brief description of MiFID. 

## _(2 marks)_ 

The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive replaced and extended the scope of the Investment Services Directive (ISD). Amongst other things MiFID provides the regime for ‘passporting’ of financial services firms authorised in one member state to be able to conduct business in other member states. MiFID also extended the scope of ISD core activities to include advising on investments and also included commodity derivatives as well as transferable securities in the enlarged scope.

- **A2.5** Explain the general purpose and content of the Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) IV requirements within the UK. _(3 marks)_ 

The general purpose of the CRD 4 is to restate the core European measures that apply with regard to banks and investment firms. This consists of both a Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) and Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR) and replaces all existing measures in this area. This is supported by the separate Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID II) for investment firms. 

The CRD contains all of the core provisions with regard to definitions, competent authorities, market access, initial capital, freedom of establishment and services, reciprocity, prudential supervision, disclosure and ancillary provisions. The CRR contains the principal measures governing own funds and capital adequacy for credit risk, market risk and operational risk as well as large exposures, liquidity and leverage. 

The specific objective of CRD 4 was to impose higher requirements with regard to credit risk, liquidity and leverage following the global financial crisis beginning in 2007-08. These 


provisions were implemented under the Basel 3 measures adopted by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) in 2010. This, in particular, increased Core Equity Tier 1 capital (CET 1) from 2% to 4 ½% and imposed a separate Conservation Buffer (2 ½%), Countercyclical Buffer (0-2 ½%) and Systemic Risk Buffer (0-2 ½%) with a short 1-month Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) and 12-month Net Stable Funding Requirement (NSFR) and 3% leverage ratio. 

**A2.6** Explain the general purpose and nature of the Basel III and revised capital requirements. _(4 marks)_ 

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision imposes basic minimum common, international requirements in relation to the capital adequacy position of banks. The Committee's first standards were issued in 1988 (Basel I) with revised provisions in 2004 (Basel II) and following the recent global financial crisis in 2010 (Basel III). 

Basel III builds on the three pillars introduced under Basel II with a minimum requirements (pillar one), supervisory review (pillar two) and market disclosure (pillar three). 

Basel III revises the basic minimum requirements and imposes two new liquidity ratios and a minimum leverage ratio for the first time. The minimum capital requirements increase core two year one capital (essentially paid-up share capital and retained earnings) from 2% to 4.5% with an additional conservation buffer (2.5%), countercyclical buffer (0-2.5%) and systemically important institution buffer (0-3%). 

Basel III was implemented in 2019. 

The cumulative effects of imposing these requirements will be to increase the stability of banks by allowing them to absorb additional losses in the event of a major crisis and to withstand short-term lending restrictions through the new liquidity rules with overall debt levels being limited through the new leverage ratio.

**A2.8** Explain the role and function of the Financial Stability Board (FSB) at the international level. _(4 marks)_ 

The Financial Stability Board (FSB) was established at the G20 London Summit in April 2009 following the global financial crisis to extend and replace the earlier Financial Stability Forum (FSF) which had been set up following the Asian financial crisis beginning in Thailand in July 1997. The FSB consists of representatives from national financial ministries, central banks and supervisory authorities as well as other international financial agencies. This includes inter alia the BCBS, the International Organisation of Securities Commissions (IOSC), International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS) and the Committee on Payments and Market Infrastructures (CPMI). 

The purpose of the FSB is to coordinate the work of national financial authorities and international standard agencies, and to promote the implementation of effective regulatory, supervisory and other financial sector policies. The FSB has produced a large number of important reports in various policy areas since the global financial crisis beginning in 20072008. The FSB has specifically produced an online or virtual Compendium of Standards which is effectively a global financial rulebook/ This includes a specific list of 16 Key Standards (https://www.fsb.org/work-of-the-sb/about-the-compendium-ofstandards/key_standards/). 


**A2.9** How did Brexit affect the ability of UK firms to provide financial services to EU clients? 

_(3 marks)_ 

Valid points include: an explanation of passporting, transition, the end of passporting, and postBrexit options for UK firms. 

The term passporting arose from the Investment Services Directive (ISD) 1993. In this directive passporting considers situations where a firm authorised to provide investment services in one member state, could provide this same service in other member states without additional authorisation. 

Following notice given by the UK government to the EU and various deadline extensions, the UK left the EU on 31 January 2020, but stayed in the EU due to transitional provisions that ended at 11pm on 31 December 2020. During the transitional time period, UK firms had to comply with EU legislation, including implementing new legislation. The government introduced legislation that converted existing directly applicable EU legislation (Regulations) into UK law and preserved existing UK laws which implement EU obligations. 

obligations. 

Following the end of the transitional period, UK firms lost their ability to access the EU single market on the basis of ‘passporting’; this means that UK firms are limited in their ability to provide financial services to EU clients, likewise for EU firms to UK clients. 

UK firms may look to exemptions in local law that allow for access by firms from non-EU countries or apply for permission from the European Commission, or other EU bodies under the access regimes for third countries provided in EU legislation.
