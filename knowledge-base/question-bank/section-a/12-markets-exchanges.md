- **A12.1** The FSMA includes provisions with regard to RIEs and RCHs. Provide three examples of 

an RIE and one example of an RCH. _(2 marks)_ 

The recognised investment exchanges (RIEs) are as follows:

- c) ICE Europe; 

- d) AQUIS Exchange; 

- e) Cboe Europe Equities. 

The Recognised Clearing Houses (RCHs) are:

- b) LCH.Clearnet (which incorporates the London Clearing House); 

- c) LME Clear; 

- d) ICE Clear Europe. 

**A12.2** Explain the nature of and principal activity carried out by a systematic internaliser. 

_(3 marks)_ 

A systematic internaliser is a financial firm that matches customer orders internally on his own book. It is effectively an internal market. Firms may go on to formal markets at the end of the day to settle outstanding positions. 

A systematic internaliser is formally recognised under MiFID and MiFID II and MiFIR as an investment firm which deals on its own account, on an organised, frequent, systematic and substantial basis, by executing client orders outside regulated markets, multilateral trading facilities (MTF) or organised trading facilities (OTF). 


**A12.3** Explain the role of a market maker in the context of Markets in Financial Instruments Directive ll (MiFID II). _(2 marks)_ 

A market maker is a financial institution or individual which aids the provision of liquidity to certain financial markets. In this role the market maker may trade on their own account to ensure that transactions in the market of interest can be undertaken by other market participants. Through ensuring sufficient transactions are undertaken in a financial market, an effective price for financial assets can be established. These market maker roles are generally undertaken by larger financial institutions such as banks.

**A12.4** What is meant by the term ‘initial public offering’? How is such an offering undertaken? _(4 marks)_ 

An initial public offering or IPO is a first floatation of shares/equity within a company to be traded on a public exchange. There are multiple ways in which primary offerings can be undertaken: 

- An _offer for sale_ , whereby the firm the company offers shares for sale to the public and institutional investors. This will generally be coordinated by an investment bank. 

• _An offer for subscription_ involves the company soliciting potential investors as to their interest in investing within an IPO. When these subscriptions have been received the new shares can be allocated and issued. 

- An _intermediaries offer_ involves selling shares to financial institutions. These institutions can then re-sell the shares to the public. 

- _Placing_ involves an investment bank placing blocks of shares with their current clients and 

- contacts. 

- An _introduction_ does not involve an offer of shares for sale; rather, it allows a company’s existing shareholders to trade their shares on a stock exchange. 


**A12.5** Define **four** different types of financial benchmark classified under UK financial regulations. _(3 marks)_ 

UK benchmark regulation defines a benchmark as a publicly available index. These benchmarks are developed from an assessment of underlying values of pertinent variables such as prices, interest rates or alike. The UK benchmark regulation classifies benchmarks into six different types: 

- _Critical benchmarks_ – where the value of contracts underlying the benchmark is at least 

- €500 billion. 

• _Significant benchmarks_ – where the value of contracts underlying the benchmark is at least €50 billion, and/or where there are no or very few market-led substitutes for the benchmark and/or there would be an impact on financial stability if the benchmark ceased to be produced. 

- _Commodity benchmarks_ – where the underlying asset of the benchmark is a commodity 

- as defined by UK MiFID. 

- _Regulated data benchmarks_ – where the input data to the benchmark is provided directly 

- from regulated venues. 

- _Interest rate benchmarks_ – where the benchmark is determined on the basis of the rate at 

- which banks may lend to, or borrow from, other banks or agents in the money markets. 

• _Non-significant benchmarks_ – where the value of contracts underlying the benchmark is less than €50 billion, and the benchmark is not a commodity or interest rate benchmark. 

**A12.6** What is a systematic internaliser? _(2 marks)_ 

A systematic internaliser is an investment firm which deals on its own account by executing client orders outside a regulated market (RM), multilateral trading facility (MTF) or organised trading facility (OTF). In this role the systematic internaliser provides liquidity to markets. 

The systematic internaliser regime, or trading on own account acting on a ‘principal’ basis, was introduced in 2007 as part of the MiFID regime, applies to shares and was introduced to enhance market transparency. This test was subsequently amended to include posttrade reporting requirements to assist in the price discovery process for investors. MiFID II also applies to the systematic internaliser regulatory regime when non-equity financial instruments such as bonds and derivatives are traded. 


**A12.7** What is the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA)? Name **two** types of assets in scope of MiCA. _(3 marks)_ 

The Markets in Crypto Assets Regulation (MiCA) is an EU regulatory framework for the issuance of, intermediating and dealing in, cryptoassets. It introduces licensing and conduct of business requirements as well as a market abuse regime with respect to cryptoassets. MiCA applies with respect to ‘cryptoassets’, which are defined very broadly as ‘a digital representation of a value or a right that uses cryptography for security and is in the form of a coin or a token or any other digital medium which may be transferred and stored electronically, using distributed ledger technology or similar technology’. This definition is aimed at capturing not only cryptocurrencies, such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, but also stablecoins and utility tokens. 

**A12.8** What is the FCA's Early and High Growth Oversight initiative, and what are its objectives? _(3 marks)_ 

The FCA's Early and High Growth Oversight initiative provides enhanced regulatory supervision and tailored support to newly authorised firms during their first few years of operation, particularly those experiencing rapid growth. The primary objective is to ensure that these firms establish strong governance and risk management frameworks early on, which are critical for long-term sustainability and regulatory compliance. The objectives of Early and High Growth Oversight are to: 

- identify and address harm or misconduct more swiftly; 

- raise the standards of adequate systems and controls from the starts; 

- promote competition; and 

- provide firms with a better supervisory experience.
