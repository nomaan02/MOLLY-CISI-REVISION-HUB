- **A1.1** Explain the difference between authorisation (s19) and permission (s20) under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) as amended. 

## _(2 marks)_ 

All persons are required to be **authorised** or **exempt** if they carry on a regulated activity in the United Kingdom under s 19(1) of the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA). This is referred to as the “general prohibition” under s19 (2). 

Authorisation is dealt with under s31. The following persons are authorised under FSMA: 

- a) Persons holding a Part 4A permission; 

- b) EEA passport firms (under Schedule 3) _[N.B. No passporting rights currently exist,_ 

_though they could be restored under a future UK-EU trade agreement];_ 

- c) Treaty firms (under Schedule 4); or 

- d) Any other persons otherwise authorised under FSMA (s31 (1)). 

Authorisation constitutes a form of **status** or **condition** for the carrying on of any regulated activity in the UK. 

Authorised persons must also hold the relevant **permission** to carry on the specific regulated activity or activities conducted in the UK (s 20(1) FSMA). Applications for permission are made under s 55A and Part 4A generally. Breach is not an offence as such, although it is subject to FCA disciplinary action. Permission is accordingly concerned with the specific **legal right** , **authority or entitlement** to carry on the particular financial activity or activities concerned. 

**A1.2** Explain the difference between authorisation and exemption under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA). _(2 marks)_ 

Under the “General Prohibition” (s19) of FSMA a person carrying on a regulated activity must be either authorised or exempt. Authorisation includes persons holding permission under Part 4A of FSMA. Also Treaty firms (under Schedule 4) and other persons otherwise authorised under FSMA (s31 (1)).) 


Exemption is dealt with under s38 and the Financial Services and Markets Act (Exemption) Order 2001. This generally restates the earlier categories of exempt persons under the previous financial legislation in effect in the UK. These include: 

- a) Appointed Representatives; 

- b) Members of professions; 

- c) Members of Lloyd’s; 

- d) Recognised Investment Exchanges and Recognised Clearing Houses; 

- e) Other persons as listed under the 2001 Exemption Order.

**A1.4** Explain the purpose of the Regulated Activities Order (RAO). 

_(2 marks)_ 

The purpose of the RAO is to specify the scope of the regulated activities covered by FSMA including the nature of the investments and the activities covered. The RAO is necessary as the general prohibition applies to regulated activities which are defined as being specified by the Treasury under the RAO. 

The general nature of the investments and activities covered by the FSMA are listed in Schedule 2 to the FSMA although these are only indicative or illustrative as the legal meanings are set out in detail in the RAO. The purpose of the RAO is to legally define the scope of the investments and investment activities covered by the general prohibition. 


- **A1.5** Identify the main sanctions imposed for breach of the general prohibition against carrying on regulated activities without being authorised or exempt under the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA). _(4 marks)_ 

Any person who breaches the general prohibition is liable to up to two years imprisonment or a fine or both (on indictment) or six months imprisonment or a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum (on summary conviction) (s23(1)(a) and (b) FSMA). Breach of the general provision is referred to as an authorisation offence (s23 (2)). 

Agreements entered into in breach of the general prohibition are also unenforceable against the other party with the other party being entitled to recover any money or property paid or transferred or compensation for loss sustained (s26(2)(a) and (b)). 

Reference could also be made to the defence of all reasonable precaution and exercise of due diligence (s23 (3)) or the separate offence of falsely claiming to be an authorised or exempt person (s24 (1)).

- **A1.11** What is the general objective of the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) and how is the PRA required to advance this objective? _(3 marks)_ 

The PRA’s general objective is promoting the safety and soundness of PRA-authorised

- **A1.12** Explain the nature and significance of an “authorised person” and an “exempt person” under the FSMA 2000. _(4 marks)_ 

The general prohibition set out in s19 (1) of FSMA requires that any person carrying on a regulated activity within the UK is either an authorised or an exempt person. The requirements with regard to authorised persons are developed in ss.31-39. 

An authorised person is set out in s31 (1). An authorised person generally includes a person holding a Part 4A Permission, a treaty firm (qualifying under Schedule 4) or someone otherwise authorised under the FSMA. 

Authorisation refers to the general status of being able to carry on any regulated activity. The need for the separate designation arose as a result of the separate availability for UK 


firms and European Treaty and single market firms. Entitlement to carry on a particular activity is conferred under the permission regime (s20 (1)). 

Exempt firms are those excluded for public service entities (such as the Bank of England or international financial institutions) which did not carry on commercial business for financial profit alone or other statutory bodies that were separately regulated under the founding Acts. 

The list of relevant exemptions is now set out in the “Exemption Order” which is issued under s 38(1) of FSMA.

**A1.15** Explain the content and purpose of the “general prohibition” under the FSMA 2000. 

_(2 marks)_ 

The general prohibition in s19 of FSMA creates an offence of undertaking regulated activities unless the person is either authorised or exempt. The penalty for breach is two years imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine. In addition, contracts may be unenforceable. The purpose of the general prohibition is to provide investor protection by making sure that those who conduct regulated activities are fit and proper persons to do so, which will include an assessment of the business’s competence. 


**A1.16** Explain the purpose of permission under the FSMA 2000. 

## _(2 marks)_ 

To comply with the General Prohibition under s.19 of FSMA a firm may become authorised by seeking permission under Part 4A of the FSMA. To obtain permission to perform one or more regulated activities the person must meet the threshold conditions. This process ensures that those who undertake regulated activities are fit and proper persons and competent to undertake those activities. A business may not undertake activities other than those for which it has permission, which restricts the broad authorisation to comply with s.19 to the more specific activities that an authorised person may undertake. 

## **A1.17** Explain what is meant by the Bank of England’s ‘financial stability strategy’. 

_(3 marks)_ 

The Bank of England (the Bank) has been given the objective of protecting and enhancing the stability of the UK financial system. The court of directors of the Bank is required to determine the Bank’s strategy in relation to its financial stability objective (known formally as its ‘financial stability strategy’). This strategy must be reviewed at least every three years. Before determining or revising the Bank’s financial stability strategy, the court of directors must consult the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) and the Treasury. The Bank must also publish its financial stability strategy.

**A1.28** Explain the general purpose of the FCA’s Fair Treatment of Customers initiative and its relationship to the FCA’s operational objectives. _(4 marks)_ 

_[Tutor note: The Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC) initiative initially applied to any business carried out by any firm with any customer – but now is restricted to business which is_ _**out of scope** of the Consumer Duty regulations created as a result of s9 Financial Services Act 2021, which came into effect in July 2023.]_ 

The purpose of the FTOC initiative is to ensure that firms that are out-of-scope of the Consumer Duty deal with their customers in a fair and appropriate manner at all times. This is based on PRIN 6 under which firms must pay due regard to their customers’ interests and treat them fairly. 

Fair treatment of customers can be considered to fall within the FCA’s integrity objective (protecting and enhancing the integrity of the UK financial system) under FSMA. 

Product providers must ensure that all products are properly designed for their target market and sold with clear, understandable information through appropriate distribution channels. Distributors must ensure that customers have all of the information they require, that products are suitable and that post sales services comply with expectations. 


**A1.29** Briefly explain the different roles and functions of the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) and the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) under the regulatory regime bought into effect under the Financial Services Act 2012. _(3 marks)_ 

The PRA is responsible for the regulation of banks, insurance companies and large, systemically important investment firms. Operating as the PRA is one of the functions of the Bank of England. 

The FCA is responsible for conduct regulation generally as well as the direct regulation of other firms not subject to PRA control and for the recognition and oversight of stock markets and exchanges. It operates as an independent agency, replacing the former Financial Services Authority (FSA).

Any person carrying on a regulated activity in the UK must be authorised (or exempt) under s 19(1) FSMA. Authorised persons are listed in s 31(1) – (a) anyone holding a Part 4A permission, (b) Treaty firms (Schedule 4) and (c) a person otherwise authorised under the FSMA. Authorisation constitutes a form of status or condition for the carrying on of any regulated activity in the UK. 

Authorised persons must also hold the relevant permission to carry on the specific regulated activity or activities conducted in the UK (s 20(1) FSMA). Breach is not an offence as such although it is subject to disciplinary action by the appropriate regulator. Applications for permission are made under s 40 and Part 4A generally. Permission is accordingly concerned with the specific legal right, authority or entitlement to carry on the particular financial activity or activities concerned.

- **A1.32** Explain the purpose and importance of macroprudential policy in ensuring the stability of financial markets. _(3 marks)_ 

Macroprudential policy is concerned with monitoring the build up of risks and exposures across the financial system as a whole and not simply within one or more specific regulated sector. 

This was regarded as being a major omission before the recent financial crisis and especially with the failure by the authorities to deal with asset price bubbles, in particular, in the property market and the build of debt and leverage across the financial system. 

A number of new bodies have been set up to attempt to develop this form of oversight. These include the Bank of England’s Financial Policy Committee (FPC), set up by the Coalition Government to replace the previous Council for Financial Stability (CFS) in the UK, as well as the Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) in the US and European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) in the EU. Equivalent work is carried out by the Financial Stability Board (FSB) at the international level with the International Monetary Fund (IMF). 

A number of issues still remain in developing appropriate forms of information and data collection, assessment and processing and appropriate tools to respond to any identified 


difficulties as well as appropriate cooperation mechanisms between all of the other agencies involved at a national and on a cross-border basis.

**A1.34** Explain the consequences of breach of s20 FSMA. 

_(3 marks)_ 

Breach of s20 is stated not to constitute an offence directly, transactions are not unenforceable and a right of action will not immediately arise unless otherwise specified (s20(2) and (3)). Breach will nevertheless constitute a disciplinary matter subject to FCA enforcement including a fine or censure (public or private) or possibly a restriction or variation of permission. 

**A1.35** Where are regulated activities defined under the FSMA? Give four examples. _(4 marks)_ 

The principal regulated activities are listed in **Schedule 2 FSMA** although this does not contain the legal definition of regulated activities. Regulated activities are ‘ **as specified’** (under s 23(4) FSMA) which means by Statutory Instrument. The legal definition of regulated activities is then set in the **Regulated Activities Order** ( **RAO** ) issued under the FSMA as amended. 

- **A1.36** The Banking Act 2009 created a Special Resolution Regime (SRR) to deal with banks and other depositary institutions at risk of failure. Summarise **three** powers of the SRR when faced by a failing bank. _(3 marks)_ 

After the great financial crisis, a permanent regulatory framework was established to consider cases of banks and other financial intermediaries at risk of failure. This framework established under the Banking Act 2009 was termed the Special Resolution Regime (SRR). The Banking Act established both objectives to guide decision making by the different bodies involved and a number of powers. These powers (as outlined in the workbook) include: 

- To place a bank into temporary public ownership. 

- To place a bank into receivership and allow payment to insured depositors. 

- To use administration procedures on all or part of the bank. 

- To transfer part or all of the bank to a third-party private buyer or as an intermediate step to the Bank of England pending a future sale. 


- **A1.37** Explain the nature of macro-prudential policy and how this is principally managed under the regulatory system in the UK. _(3 marks)_ 

Macro-prudential policy is concerned with the monitoring of the build up of risks and exposures across the financial system as a whole. This was identified as one of the key failures in the UK, EU and US before the global financial crisis. This has allowed the formation of major asset price bubbles, in particular, in the property market and the build of debt and leverage across the financial system. Macro-prudential policy is managed by the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England.

**A1.43** Explain the role and function of the Upper Tribunal under the FSMA. 

_(3 marks)_ 

The role and function of the Upper Tribunal is to consider financial services appeals. The Upper Tribunal was established in 2008 under the Tribunal, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007 and is managed by Her Majesty's Courts and Tribunals Service. This replaced the Financial Services and Markets Tribunal in April 2010 which had originally been set up under s 132 FSMA. 

The Upper Tribunal has four Chambers with financial services matters being considered by the Tax and Chancery Chamber. The chamber specifically considers appeals against decision notices issued by the FCA and PRA. This may include decisions to discipline authorised firms or approved persons, withdrawal of approval, vary permission, market abuse or issue prohibition orders. 

**A1.44** Explain the role and function of the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the UK financial and regulatory architecture. _(4 marks)_ 

The Financial Policy Committee (FPC)  was set up under the Financial Services Act 2012 (s 9B Bank of England Act 1998 and s4 FSA 2012). 

Macro prudential policy is concerned with the monitoring of the build-up of risks and exposures across the financial system as a whole. This was identified as one of the key failures in the UK, EU and US before the global financial crisis. This has allowed the formation of major asset price bubbles, in particular, in the property market and the build of debt and leverage across the financial system. 

The function of the FPC is to examine issues that may affect financial stability with specific reference to the identification and monitoring of systemic risks and taking action to remove 


or reduce such risks to protect and enhance the resilience of the UK financial system (s 9C Bank of England Act 1998). The FPC has been given specific powers to manage housing market debt including limiting the proportion of high loan income mortgages that banks can offer or prohibiting new lending above specific loan to income ratios. 

The FPC has examined such other issues as sovereign debt exposures, forbearance and provisioning practices, bank capital levels, earnings retention (bonus payments) and opaque funding structures, such as with regard to collateral swaps or similar transactions with exchange traded funds (ETFs). 

**A1.45** Explain the role and function of the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA). 

_(4 marks)_ 

The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) is responsible for strengthening business competition and preventing and reducing anti-competitive practices within the UK. The CMA was set up on 15 March 2012 and was established under Part 3 of the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013. 

The CMA works to promote competition for the benefit of consumers inside and outside the UK and to ensure that markets work well for consumers, businesses and the economy. The CMA is an independent, non-ministerial government department with around 700 staff. 

The CMA specifically investigates mergers that could restrict competition, conducts market studies and investigations, investigates anti-competitive conduct and abuses of dominant positions, enforces consumer protection legislation, cooperates with other sector regulators and considers regulatory references and appeals. 

**A1.46** Explain the general requirement imposed under s21 FSMA on financial promotions. 

_(3 marks)_ 

s 21 FSMA requires that a financial promotion, in the form of an invitation or inducement to engage in an investment activity, must be issued by an authorised person or the content approved by an authorised person (s 21(1) and (2)). All financial promotions must be clear, 


fair and not misleading (COBS 4.2). This is subject to the exemptions provided for under the Financial Promotion Order (FPO) as amended. 

The restrictions on financial promotions do not apply to purely factual communications nor to eligible counterparties with regard to MiFID business. 

A person who breaches the restrictions on financial promotions is guilty of an offence and liable for up to two years’ imprisonment (six months on summary conviction) or a fine or both (s 25(1)) subject to a reasonable belief or reasonable precautions defence ((s 25(2)). 

**A1.47** Outline the statutory objectives of the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA). 

_(3 marks)_ 

The PRA's general objective is to promote the safety and soundness of PRA authorised persons (s2B(2) FSMA as amended). This is to be advanced by seeking to ensure that the business or failure of a PRA authorised person does not have any adverse effect on the stability of the UK financial system and that the continuity of service of ring-fenced bodies is protected (s 2B(3)(a)-(c) FSMA). 

The PRA's insurance objective is to contribute to the securing of an appropriate degree of protection for those who are or may become policyholders (s2C(2) FSMA as amended). 

The PRA’s general objective was revised in 2013 to ensure that in discharging its general functions, the business of ring-fenced bodies are carried on in a way that avoids any adverse effect on the continuity of the provision of core services in UK and protected from risks that could adversely affect the continuity of core services with the risk that failure of the body, or a member of its group, could affect the continuity of core services (s2B(3)(c) inserted under the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013). 

The PRA was also given a secondary competition objective (SCO) on 1 March 2014 of acting, in so far as reasonably possible, in a way that facilitates effective competition in the markets for authorised firms carrying out regulated activities (s 130 Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013). 


- **A1.48** In a financial regulation context, define what is meant by the term ‘financial supervision’ and give **three** examples of supervisory principles used to enhance supervision of financial firms. _(4 marks)_ 

The term financial supervision relates to the monitoring of a firm’s behaviour and compliance with financial regulations. There are many supervisory principles introduced to ensure the enhanced supervision of financial firms by financial regulators. These principles (as outlined in the Official Workbook) include: 

- Be forward looking and pre-empt poor conduct to limit the effects on markets and customers. 

- Focus on strategy and business models to identify any future risks. 

- Focus on culture and governance to determine what drives behaviours in the organisation. 

- Focus on individual and firm accountability through holding senior managers and firms to account. 

- Follow proportionate and risk-based measures in its actions, such as using its wider market knowledge to identify problematic firms and individuals. 

- Engage in two-way communication with firms and consumers. 

- Clearly communicate the expected behaviours for firms and individuals. 

- Apply these principles in a co-ordinated manner, within the FCA and with other appropriate external bodies. 

- Rectify any systematic harms identified and work to prevent their reoccurrence. 

- Where appropriate refer action/activities to the Enforcement division.

**A1.50** Explain the extent to which regulated financial institutions will be subject to a general duty of care to consumers under Section 29 (1) of the Financial Services Act 2021. 

_(3 marks)_ 

s 29(1) of the Financial Services Act 2021 required the FCA to consult on whether it should make general rules providing that authorised persons owe a duty of care to consumers. A consultation paper titled A New Consumer Duty (CP 21/13) was produced on 14 May 2021. The Consumer Duty came into effect in July 2023. This applies to retail clients, including non-professional customers and small and medium sized enterprises (SMEs), and consists of three key elements: 

(a) A new ‘Consumer Principle’ (a firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail clients); 

(b) Overarching ‘Cross-cutting Rules’ (a firm must take all reasonable steps to avoid causing foreseeable harm and to enable customers to pursue their financial objectives and to act in good faith); and 

(c) Four Outcomes (based on communications, products and services, customer service and price and value).

**A1.52** Outline the role of the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in the authorisation and supervision of firms. _(4 marks)_ 

The FCA has been tasked with maintaining confidence in the UK financial system by regulating the conduct of firms. Two of its core regulatory functions are authorisation and supervision. Authorisation is the gateway through which firms and individuals must pass before they are permitted to carry out regulated activities in the UK. Firms must meet threshold conditions, such as having adequate financial resources, being appropriately organised, and demonstrating that their senior managers are fit and proper. This process helps to ensure that only firms who are capable of acting responsibly and in the interests of consumers are allowed to participate in the market. 

Supervision is the FCA’s ongoing process of monitoring firms to ensure they continue to meet regulatory standards. The FCA uses a proactive, risk-based approach to supervision, focusing more closely on firms that pose higher potential risks to consumers or market 


integrity. Supervision may involve thematic reviews, firm-specific assessments, and routine or targeted communications and inspections. Where necessary, the FCA may intervene, impose restrictions, or take enforcement action.
