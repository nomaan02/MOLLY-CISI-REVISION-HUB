**A11.1** Outline four of the principles which shape how a firm limits issues of bribery. 

_(4 marks)_ 

A range of principles are proposed in the UK to tackle the issue of bribery. Key legislation in this includes, for example, the UK Bribery Act 2010. The principles include the following from the workbook. 

- Principle 1 Proportionate procedures. The procedures to prevent bribery are 

- proportionate to the risk of bribery within the organisation. 

- Principle 2 Top-level commitment. The upper echelon management needs to be 

- committed to preventing bribery throughout the organisation. A culture where bribery is unacceptable needs to be developed. 

- Principle 3 Risk assessment. A periodic, documented and informed assessment of 

- the internal and external risks posed by bribery needs to be undertaken within the organisation. 

• Principle 4 Due diligence. The organisation needs to undertake diligent procedures to limit identified bribery risks. 

• Principle 5 Communication (including training). Policies and procedures to limit bribery need to be developed and communicated within the organisation. This may involve the training of staff. 

• Principle 6 Monitoring and review. It is expected the firm will monitor and review its policies and procedures to limit the incidence of bribery. 

Other appropriate material can be included in an answer and can also be acknowledged. 

**A11.2** How is terrorism defined in the Terrorism Act 2000? _(3 marks)_ 

The definition of terrorism covers the use or threat of use of ‘action’ which is: 

- Designed to influence the government (UK or overseas) or to intimidate the public (UK or overseas); and 

- Made for the purpose of advancing a political or religious or ideological cause. 

‘Action’ is relevant for the offence if it: 


- Involves serious violence against a person. 

- Involves serious damage to property. 

- Endangers a person’s life. 

- Creates a serious risk to the health and safety of the public; or 

- Is designed to seriously interfere with or disrupt an electronic system. 

**A11.3** Explain the role and function of the Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO) within a regulated institution. _(2 marks)_ 

The MLRO is a controlled function and the person is a senior manager function. Firms (other than sole traders) are required to appoint an MLRO to oversee compliance with the FCA rules on systems and controls against money laundering, with the MLRO having the necessary authority, independence and ability to access sufficient resources and information, in order to allow the MLRO to carry out their responsibility (SYSC 6.3.9). 

The functions of the MLRO can be listed as follows: 

a) to receive reports of transactions giving rise to suspicious transactions; 

b) to determine whether a suspicious transaction report should be filed; and c) to make a suspicious transaction report to NCA in appropriate cases. 

The MLRO should also report at least annually to the firm’s governing body on the operation and effectiveness of the firm’s systems and controls in relation to money laundering. 

**A11.4** Outline the principal money laundering offences under UK law. 

_(3 marks)_ 

The main UK money laundering offences are now restated under Part VII of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (PCA). The PCA creates three main offences relating to Concealing (s 327), Arranging (s 328) and Acquisition, Use and Possession (s 329) as well as attempting, conspiring or inciting to commit any such offence (s340(11)(a) and (b)). Money laundering 


includes Aiding, abetting, counselling or procuring the commission of any of these offences (s 340(11)(c) and (d)). 

Two separate offences are also created of Failing to disclose (ss 330-332) and Tipping-off (s 333). 

**A11.5** Explain the role and function of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF). _(3 marks)_ 

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) was set up in 1989 as an inter-governmental agency to develop international standards governing money laundering, terrorist financing and fraud. The FATF publishes 40 core recommendations  covering Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing, as well as typologies of money laundering and financial crime. The FATF conducts mutual evaluations and reviews of country implementation and compliance. The FATF also identifies high risk and non-cooperative jurisdictions. 

## **A11.6** Define financial crime and explain two examples of financial crimes regulated by the FCA. _(3 marks)_ 

The FCA defines financial crime as any criminal activity involving money, financial markets, or financial services. This includes insider trading, which is the illegal buying or selling of securities based on non-public, material information. It also covers money laundering, the process of disguising the origins of illegally obtained money. 

Market manipulation is another form of financial crime and involves creating artificial or misleading market conditions, such as through "pump and dump" schemes. Fraud, including misrepresentation or Ponzi schemes, also falls under this definition. Bribery and corruption are included, referring to the offering, giving, receiving, or soliciting of something of value to influence a financial decision. Lastly, sanctions evasion is considered financial crime and involves circumventing international or domestic sanctions to carry out prohibited transactions or business dealings. 


**A11.7** Briefly explain the role and function of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF). 

_(3 marks)_ 

FATF was established at the G7 Summit in Paris in 1989. Its membership has subsequently been enlarged and now includes many more countries than the original G7 members. FATF is an inter-governmental body whose purpose is the development and promotion of policies, both at national and international levels, to combat money laundering and terrorist financing. It is a policy-making body, which works to generate the political will to bring about national legislative and regulatory reforms in these areas. 

FATF monitors the progress of its member states in implementing necessary measures, reviews money laundering and terrorist financing techniques and counter measures, and promotes the adoption and implementation of appropriate measures globally. In performing these activities, FATF collaborates with other international bodies involved in combating money laundering and the financing of terrorism.
