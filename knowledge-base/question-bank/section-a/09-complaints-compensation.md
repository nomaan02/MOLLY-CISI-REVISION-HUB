**A9.1** Explain the difference between the Ombudsman Scheme and the Financial Services Compensation Scheme under the FSMA. _(2 marks)_ 

Both the Ombudsman Scheme and the Financial Services Compensation Scheme are provided for under FSMA (Parts XVI and XV). The objective of each is to provide a single integrated cross sector support mechanism for clients of regulated firms. 

The purpose of the Ombudsman Scheme is to provide a mechanism for clients to make complaints concerning the conduct of regulated firms to an independent party. (This is distinct from judicial review with claims being referred to the Upper Tribunal (Tax and Chancery Chamber) under Part IX of the FSMA.) The relevant provisions are now set out within the DISP section of the FCA Handbook. 

The purpose of the compensation scheme is to make payment to former clients of regulated firms after the firm has failed and been closed down. This replaces three earlier compensation schemes maintained in the banking, securities and insurance areas. (In practice, the earlier three funds are now operated as sub-funds within the new single scheme arrangements.) The relevant provisions are now set out within the COMP section of the FCA Handbook. 

**A9.2** Specify the main financial limits on compensation that may be payable to customers in connection with deposit taking and investment business. _(4 marks)_ 

The compensation limits set out in COMP are as follows: 

- a) Deposits (£120,000); 

- b) Investments (£85,000); 

- c)  Long term insurance (100% of value attributed to policy); 

- d) General insurance (100% of valid claim (compulsory) and 90% of valid claim (noncompulsory)). 


**A9.3** Outline the time limits that apply in handling client complaints under DISP. _(3 marks)_ 

The timetables set out in DISP are as follows: 

- a) Acknowledgement of receipt – prompt notice (formerly five days); 

- c) Holding or Final response – eight weeks. 

- d) Notice of 6 months time limit to refer to FOS. 

Records of complaints and of the measures taken for their resolution must be retained for: 

a) Five years, for MiFID business 

- b) Three years, for other complaints 

**A9.4** Explain the role and function of the Office of Complaints Commissioner. _(4 marks)_ 

The FCA and PRA are required under Part 6 of the Financial Services Act 2012 to maintain a complaints scheme for the investigation of complaints arising in connection with the exercise of, or failure to exercise, any of their relevant functions and to appoint an independent person (referred to as the Complaints Commissioner) to be responsible for the conduct of investigations in accordance with the complaints scheme (the Scheme) (http://www.fscc.gov.uk/). The Scheme came into effect on 1 April 2013. 

The Complaints Commissioner considers grievances against the FCA, PRA, the Payment System Regulator and the Bank of England’s oversight of clearing houses and settlement systems. Complaints about financial firms rather than one of the regulators, should be referred to the company concerned and then the Financial Ombudsman Service. 

Complaints can be made by anyone who is directly affected by the way in which the regulators have carried out their functions, or anyone acting directly on such a person’s behalf, provided that the complaint meets the requirements of the Scheme. To be eligible to make a complaint under the Scheme, a person must be seeking a remedy (which for this purpose may include an apology) in respect of some inconvenience, distress or loss which the person has suffered as a result of being directly affected by the regulators’ actions or inaction (http://fscc.gov.uk/complaints-scheme/). 


Complaints should be made within 12 months of the date on which the complainant first became aware of the circumstances giving rise to the complaint. Complaints made later than this will be investigated under the Scheme only if the complainant can show reasonable grounds for the delay (http://fscc.gov.uk/complaints-scheme/). 

**A9.5** Explain the role and function of the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS). _(4 marks)_ 

The purpose of the Ombudsman scheme under the FSMA is to provide for the resolution of ‘certain disputes’ quickly and with minimum formality by an independent person (s 225(1)). The scheme is to be administered by a body corporate with further direction being provided with regard to jurisdiction and operation in Part XVI and Schedule 17 FSMA. 

Complainants should generally use the financial institution’s internal scheme first before making a separate reference to the Ombudsman. The Ombudsman may direct financial institutions to take any necessary action or make financial awards up to £355,000. 

The Ombudsman scheme is generally dealt with under the Dispute Resolution: Complaints sourcebook (DISP) with compensation under COMP within the Redress block of the FCA Handbook.

- **A9.7** List three principle sets of limits payable under the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS). _(3 marks)_ 

   - The limits on **deposit claims** are **100** % of up to **£120,000** per person per firm and **£240,000** on joint accounts (from 1 December 2025). 

   - The limit for **mortgages (mortgage advice and arranging)** is **£85,000** (from 1 April 2019). 

   - The limit for **debt management** is **£85,000** (from 1 April 2019). 

   - The limit on **investments is £85,000** per person per firm (for claims against firms declared in default from 1 April 2019). 

   - The limit on **compulsory insurance, long-term insurance, professional indemnity insurance and dealt or incapacity is 100% and 90% on all other claims from 8 October 2020.** 

The limits are set out on http://www.fscs.org.uk/what-we-cover/. 

**A9.8** Describe the purpose of the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS). _(2 marks)_ 

The FSCS is designed to protect consumers by providing compensation when authorised financial firms fail. One of its main functions is depositor protection, offering up to £120,000 per person, per firm if a bank or building society collapses. It also covers investment protection, compensating individuals up to £85,000 for losses caused by the insolvency of an authorised investment firm, helping to maintain trust in the financial system. In addition, the FSCS provides insurance and mortgage protection, ensuring policyholders and mortgage customers are compensated if their insurer or provider fails, safeguarding them from significant financial harm.
