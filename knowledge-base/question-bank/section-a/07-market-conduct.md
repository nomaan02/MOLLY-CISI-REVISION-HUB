**A7.1** List any six types of conduct that may constitute market abuse under the UK Market Abuse Regulation (MAR). _(3 marks)_ 

Types of market abuse behaviour prohibited by MAR are: 

   - (a) engaging and attempting to engage in insider dealing (MAR Art 8(1)) 

   - (b) recommending that another engages in insider dealing (MAR Art 8(2)) 

   - (c) unlawfully disclosing inside information (MAR Art 10(1)) 

   - (d) engaging or attempting to engage in market manipulation which includes: 

   - (i) false or misleading behaviour 

   - (ii) securing the price of financial instruments at an abnormal or artificial level 

   - (iii) deceptive behaviour 

   - (iv) disseminating false or misleading information through media (e.g. the Internet) 

   - (v) benchmark manipulation (MAR Art 12(1)). 

- **A7.2** Specify the penalties that apply for breach of the prohibition on market abuse in the UK Market Abuse Regulation (MAR). _(3 marks)_ 

In the event of breach of MAR, the FCA may: 

- impose an unlimited fine or financial penalty (s 123(1) FSMA); 

- publish a public censure (s 123(3)); 

- apply for an injunction (s 381); or 

- apply for a restitution order (s 383). 

The FCA can also commence other disciplinary proceedings for breach of the High Level Principles or other provisions in the Handbook. 


**A7.3** Identify **six** pieces of information which need to be included in a transaction report for the purpose of market surveillance. _(3 marks)_ 

The question asks for the information requirements outlined for transactions reporting within the FCA’s Supervision sourcebook. The specific information required is for the case of market surveillance. A wide range of information requirements (reported in the workbook) can be included in such an answer including those listed below. 

- a)  reporting firm’s identity – legal entity identifier (LEI) 

- b)  trading venue identifier code 

- c)  executing entity identifier code 

- d)  trading date and time 

- e)  whether the transaction is a buy/sell 

- f)  instrument identification code (name and ISIN of the instrument) 

- g)  trading capacity 

- h)  quantity – number of shares/nominal/amount of stock purchased/sold 

- i)  settlement date 

- j)  identity of the counterparty (other party to the transaction) 

- k)  price and currency 

- l)  unique transaction identifier 

- m)  buyer or seller/decision-maker details and identification code 

**A7.4** Explain what is meant by a market sounding in the context of the UK Market Abuse Regulation (MAR). _(3 marks)_ 

A market sounding comprises the communication of information, prior to the announcement of a transaction, in order to gauge the interest of potential investors in a possible transaction and the conditions relating to it such as its potential size or pricing, to one or more potential investors by: 

a) an issuer; 


   - b) a secondary offeror of a financial instrument, in such quantity or value that the transaction is distinct from ordinary trading and involves a selling method based on the prior assessment of potential interest from potential investors; 

   - c) an emission allowance market participant; or 

   - d) a third party acting on behalf or on the account of any person referred to above. 

- **A7.5** Outline the principal exemptions available for breach of the provisions on market abuse under FSMA. _(3 marks)_ 

The FSMA contains two due diligence defences to the imposition of a financial penalty of: a) believing, on reasonable grounds, that the behaviour did not amount to market abuse; and 

b) taking all reasonable precautions and exercising all due diligence to avoid market abuse (s 123). 

Additionally, safe harbours include: 

   - a) Acting in conformity with the FCA’s rules on buy-back programmes and stabilisation; 

   - b) Acting in conformity with the FCA’s rules on Chinese walls; 

   - c) Disclosures in accordance with the FCA Primary Market’s Disclosure Guidance and Transparency Rules (DTR); 

   - d) Disclosures in accordance with the Takeover Code’s rules on disclosure during a takeover bid; 

   - e) Market soundings. 

- **A7.6** Explain the general purpose and content of sections 89 and 90 of the Financial Services Act 2012 on misleading statements. _(3 marks)_ 

The Financial Services Act (FSA) 2012 created two offences of making misleading statements (s 89) and misleading impressions (s 90). 

s89 applies with regard to the making of a statement with regard to a relevant agreement that is known to be false or misleading in a material respect or being reckless as to whether 


it is or not or dishonestly concealing any material facts in connection with the statement or otherwise. Specifically, section 89 of FSA 2012 makes it a criminal offence to engage in market manipulation through: 

- a) Knowingly making a statement that is false or misleading; 

- b) Dishonestly concealing any material facts; 

- c) Recklessly (dishonestly or otherwise) making a false or misleading statement. 

s90 applies where a person acts or engages in any course of conduct that creates a false or misleading impression as to the market or the price or value of a relevant investment and intended to create the impression to induce another person to acquire, dispose of, subscribe for or underwrite an investment (or refrain from doing so) or knew that the impression was false or misleading or was reckless as to whether it was or not. 

A person guilty of an offence is liable on summary conviction for up to 12 months imprisonment or for up to ten years on indictment or a fine or both (s 92). 

**A7.7** Define and explain what is meant by the term ‘insider dealing’ _. (3 marks)_ 

Insider dealing involves the use of ‘inside’ or privileged information to trade for profit. Such inside information would include non-public information pertaining directly or indirectly to a financial asset or which may be useful to someone trading: i.e., the information would likely influence the price of the financial asset. There are many types of inside information, be this client trades, or information as to an upcoming corporate transaction. Morally such use of information is a form of theft. Under UK Market Abuse Regulation insider trading is when an individual possesses such inside information and uses this or facilitates (or recommends to) a third party using this information for trading, or limiting, cancelling or altering trading activities. 


**A7.8** Provide **three** examples of activities which would be considered market manipulation behaviours. _(3 marks)_ 

The UK Market Abuse Regulation (UK MAR) provides a number of different situations in which market manipulation is believed to exist. These include and are defined under the MAR legislation as the following. 

- A person or persons acting in collaboration, to secure a dominant position over the supply of or demand for a financial instrument. 

• Buying or selling of financial instruments, at the opening or closing of the market, which has or is likely to have the effect of misleading investors acting on the basis of the prices displayed. 

• The placing of orders to a trading venue, including cancellation and/or modification, by any available means of trading, including by electronic means, such as algorithmic and highfrequency trading strategies having the effects referred to above or by: 

- Making it more difficult for other persons to identify genuine orders on the trading 

- system of a trading venue. 

- Creating or being likely to create a false or misleading signal about the supply of, 

- or demand for, or price of, a financial instrument. 

• Taking advantage of occasional or regular access to electronic media by voicing an opinion about a financial instrument (while having a position in that financial instrument) and profiting from the impact of the price of that instrument without having disclosed the conflict of interest to the public in a proper and effective way. 

**A7.9** What is meant by the term ‘insider information’? 

_(3 marks)_ 

Broadly, the _Market Abuse Regulation_ (MAR) defines inside information as information of a precise nature which: 

- has not been made public; 

- directly or indirectly relates to one or more issuers, or to one or more financial instruments; and 


- if it were made public, would be likely to have a significant effect on the prices of those financial instruments, or on the price of related derivative financial instruments. 

MAR also sets out specific definitions for inside information for commodity derivatives and emission allowances. (See MAR, Article 7 (Inside Information), Article 7(1)(b) for commodity derivatives and Article 7(1)(c) for emission allowances). There are only a few limited and highly regulated circumstances where insider information may be used legally, such as for market sounding. 

**A7.10** Explain what is meant by a ‘market sounding’. _(2 marks)_ 

A market ‘sounding’ is where a firm is hired by an issuer that wishes to raise market funds, yet initially wishes to identify if investors have an appetite for such an issue. This would also include discovering the possible interest rate or price of this fundraising. A sounding can include the disclosure of ‘non-public’ information which is sensitive and considered to be ‘insider information’. 

Subsequently the disclosing firm must comply with the following obligations: 

- Obtain the consent of the person receiving the market sounding to receive the inside information. 

- Inform the person receiving the market sounding that they are prohibited from using that information for acquiring or disposing of financial instruments. 

- Ensure the confidentiality of the information. 

- Maintain records of all information given and to whom. 

- Advise the recipients of the sounding when the information provided ceases to be 

considered as inside information.
