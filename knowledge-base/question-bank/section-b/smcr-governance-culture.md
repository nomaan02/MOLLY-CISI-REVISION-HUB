## B5.

### Question

Sophie is the Head of Compliance at a mid-sized investment firm. The firm has recently expanded its operations, onboarding a large number of new clients and offering a broader range of financial products. However, the compliance team remains small, and Sophie is concerned that existing oversight processes may not be sufficient to monitor regulatory risks effectively. In recent internal audits several compliance breaches were identified, including failures in transaction monitoring, inadequate staff training, and a lack of clear accountability within the compliance function. Senior management has expressed concerns about increased regulatory scrutiny and wants Sophie to propose a strategy to strengthen the firm's compliance oversight.

- a) What are the key compliance risks resulting from compliance breaches identified by Sophie`s firm, and why is effective governance important in managing these risks?

   - _(5 marks)_

- b) What steps should Sophie take to enhance compliance oversight and ensure that regulatory obligations are met?

_(5 marks)_

- c) How can senior management support the compliance function, and why is a strong compliance culture essential for the firm?

_(5 marks)_

- d) What potential regulatory and reputational consequences could the firm face if it fails to improve its compliance framework?

_(5 marks)_

### Answer

This question provided a case study on how a firm's rapid growth can expose weaknesses in its compliance framework, highlighting the need for strategic oversight, effective risk management, and enhanced regulatory controls to address operational gaps and prepare for increased scrutiny.

a) The first question under this scenario asked candidates what the key compliance risks faced by the firm are, and why effective governance is important in managing these risks.

The compliance weaknesses identified in the scenario, such as failures in transaction monitoring, staff training, and accountability, could have serious regulatory and operational consequences. These weaknesses could lead to regulatory breaches, increased financial crime risk, and harm to clients or market integrity. The FCA's Senior Management Arrangements, Systems and Controls (SYSC) rules require firms to have effective systems and controls in place to manage their regulatory obligations. Inadequate compliance infrastructure would breach SYSC requirements, particularly SYSC 6, which outlines obligations regarding internal controls. Under the Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR), specific individuals are accountable for ensuring appropriate systems are in place within their areas of responsibility. If the failings identified result in consumer harm or regulatory breaches, senior managers may be subject to regulatory enforcement. The firm may also be seen as failing to meet key threshold conditions, which require that authorised firms are properly resourced and effectively managed. In this context, poor compliance oversight could lead to FCA intervention, enforcement action, or reputational harm.

b) The second question under this case study asked candidates what steps the Head of Compliance should take to enhance compliance oversight and ensure regulatory obligations are met.

To enhance compliance oversight, the Head of Compliance should first ensure that the firm has adequate governance and control frameworks in place. This includes improving internal systems, clearly defining roles and responsibilities, and ensuring that governance structures meet SYSC expectations. She should also work closely with senior management to ensure compliance with SMCR requirements, including the appointment of appropriately accountable senior managers (e.g. SMF16 and SMF17) and regular review of their responsibilities. Improving resourcing within the compliance function is also critical. The Head of Compliance should make a case for increased budget to recruit additional compliance staff or invest in compliance technology, particularly for transaction monitoring and training systems. She should lead a compliance risk assessment, strengthen internal reporting procedures, and ensure that senior management receives timely and accurate information about key risks and breaches. To reinforce effectiveness, the Head of Compliance should also consider periodic independent reviews of the compliance function and recommend structured, ongoing training programmes for all staff. Together, these measures will ensure that the firm's compliance function is equipped to meet regulatory expectations and support sustainable growth.

c) The third question asked candidates to discuss how senior management can support the compliance function and why a strong compliance culture is essential for the firm.

Senior management plays a pivotal role in supporting the compliance function by setting the tone from the top. They must allocate sufficient resources and funding to compliance, ensuring the function is adequately staffed and equipped with the right tools. Management should actively engage with compliance reports, regularly reviewing risk assessments and supporting initiatives to enhance regulatory oversight. A strong compliance culture ensures that employees prioritise ethical conduct and regulatory adherence rather than seeing compliance as an afterthought. This can be reinforced by embedding compliance-related performance metrics into employee evaluations and offering regular compliance training at all levels of the organisation. By fostering open communication and encouraging employees to escalate concerns without fear of retaliation, senior management can reinforce a culture where compliance is fully integrated into daily operations.

Responses to part c) of the question missed marks by giving generic statements about the importance of compliance without explaining how senior management can actively support it in practice. Some also missed the importance of open communication and the need for staff to feel safe escalating concerns, which is a core part of building a strong compliance culture.

d) The fourth question under this scenario asked candidates to consider what potential regulatory and reputational consequences the firm could face if it fails to improve its compliance framework.

If the firm fails to improve its compliance framework, it could face significant regulatory and reputational consequences. The FCA may impose fines or sanctions for compliance failures, particularly if breaches result in client harm or financial misconduct. The firm could also face increased scrutiny and mandatory remediation programs, requiring it to overhaul its compliance structure under regulatory supervision. If non-compliance persists, the FCA may impose restrictions on the firm's business activities, affecting its ability to operate in certain markets. From a reputational perspective, non-compliance could lead to a loss of investor confidence and damage relationships with clients. Firms with weak compliance frameworks may struggle to attract institutional investors or partners due to concerns about regulatory risks. Reputational damage can also impact employee morale, leading to higher turnover rates and difficulties in recruitment, as skilled professionals prefer to work in firms with strong compliance cultures. Internally, poor compliance oversight could result in staff misconduct, increased operational errors, and internal disputes, further undermining the firm's stability. In extreme cases, compliance failures could lead to direct legal action against senior executives, particularly if they are found to have neglected their regulatory responsibilities.

Responses to part d) of the question were generally strong in identifying that regulatory breaches can lead to fines and reputational damage. However, marks were missed by not exploring the full range of regulatory consequences, such as increased FCA scrutiny, mandatory remediation programmes, or business restrictions.

## B21.

### Question

A new divisional director (Herbert) has been hired from overseas to head up your Equities division. He will be required to be certified by the firm to allow him to direct the division and advise clients.

When he joins, as the firm's Compliance Officer you state that, despite his considerable experience, he will need to pass the regulatory module of an appropriate UK qualification before he can start taking investment decisions or provide investment advice. It is not considered feasible for him to be supervised given his seniority and experience.

Both the Divisional Managing Director and Herbert indicate that this should not be a problem and that, while studying for the examination, Herbert will restrict himself to strategy and non-trading activities.

Herbert begins to study for the examination and, over the following period, the performance of the Equities division improves substantially. You learn from an unattributable, but reliable, source that Herbert has, in fact, been providing detailed direction to his department including taking investment decisions and offering investment advice for which he is not currently approved.

On raising these concerns with both Herbert and his Managing Director, they reassure you that he is not taking investment decisions or providing any investment recommendations or advice. However, your source remains adamant that, despite the reassurances that you have received, Herbert is conducting regulated activities while performance within the Equities division continues to strengthen.

Herbert is scheduled to take the regulatory examination in the near future and is expected to pass, which will allow him to be judged to be competent and therefore registered for the appropriate certified functions.

- a) Outline the nature and operation of the regime for certified persons.

_(8 marks)_

- b) Given the above facts, what action would you take from a compliance perspective, if any, and what factors would you take into account? _(10 marks)_

- c) Would your answer differ if you were aware that the FCA were due to visit and have indicated that they will look at your firm's internal compliance procedures and TC arrangements? _(2 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with possible breach of the certified persons regime and relevant ethical issues. Candidates should have noted the uneven assignment of marks and provided appropriate detail under each section.

- a) A general review of the certified persons regime should have been provided for the full eight marks. This includes all relevant core statutory provisions and principal provisions contained in FIT and COCON.

It would appear that Herbert would have been required to be approved to conduct a significant harm function. Herbert must also satisfy the requirements set out in FIT including honesty, integrity and reputation, competence and capability and financial soundness. The firm would have to have certified Herbert, and also informed the FCA so as to ensure Herbert is entered into the FCA Directory.

The FCA may take disciplinary action specifically under s 66 FSMA against persons guilty of misconduct under the approved persons requirements. This is subject to appeal to the Upper Tribunal (Tax and Chancery Chamber).

- b) Relevant factors may have been listed with the answer structured with reference to the separate parts of the Handbook including FIT and COCON as well as PRIN, COND and TC. The 'whistle-blowing' provisions in the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA) should also have been referred to as well as the FCA's other enforcement powers. Additional ethical considerations may also have been noted including the FCA's general approach to ethical issues or possibly the CISI Code of Conduct.

Relevant factors could have included:

- i. Breach of s59 FSMA;

- ii. Possible liability under s71 FSMA;

- iii. Possible breach of FIT and COND;

- iv. Breach of SYSC 27 (certification regime);

- v. Breach of PRIN 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 3 (management and control) and 11 (regulatory relations);

- vi. Breach by the firm of SYSC 2 (senior management arrangements), 4 (organisational requirements), 5 (procedures), 6 (compliance), 7 (risk controls) and 10 (conflicts of interest);

- vii. Breach of COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 5 (market conduct), and 3 (regulatory relations); and

- viii. Breach of CISI 1 (honesty and fairness), 2 (integrity), 3 (compliance), 4 (good conduct), 5 (conflicts), 6 (professional competence) and 7 (personal standards).

Relevant action includes:

- i. Holding immediate meetings with Herbert and relevant staff including Divisional Managing Director;

- ii. Conducting internal review;

- iii. Producing early compliance report for senior management;

- iv. Possible suspension of Herbert and prevent contact between Herbert and his team as well as possible suspension of Managing Director;

- v. Refer matter to audit committee or possibly non-executive board committee;

- vi. Report to FCA;

- vii. Confirm with FCA whether any transactions had to be reopened;

- viii. Confirm relevant internal information and resources;

- ix. Confirm Divisional Managing Director knowledge and complicity;

- x. Consider possible disciplinary action against Herbert and Divisional Managing Director;

- xi. Confirm suitability of Herbert and whether Herbert may have sufficient experience from internal perspective including possible conduct of internal examination to allow to act in advance of taking separate external qualification examinations; and

- xii. Confirm whether firm breached SYSC in absence of adequate supervisions.

Relevant corrective action may include:

- i. Reviewing internal procedures concerning assessment of experience and expertise of staff and need to undertake external examination;

- ii. Confirm adequacy of internal TC arrangements;

- iii. Confirm internal review procedures;

- iv. Confirm internal disciplinary procedures; and

- v. Confirm 'whistle-blowing' procedures in the event that senior management do not follow advice of Compliance Officer.

The Compliance Officer may also wish to confirm possible available exemptions under the approved persons and TC rules and possibly take advice from FCA or CISI.

The factors which would be taken into account may also include relevant enforcement action that the FCA may take for breach of the provisions referred to. The FCA's enforcement powers may be summarised. This would be in addition to possible liability under s71 FSMA.

- c) The policy would generally not differ, although additional reference may be made to preparing a specific report for the FCA on the breach and proposed corrective actions to be adopted. Reference may also have been made to the FCA's early settlement procedure.

## B50.

### Question

You are the newly appointed compliance officer at Trapezium Capital Ltd, which deals in asset management for high-net-worth clients. As compliance officer, you are part of the senior management team and work closely with other stakeholders in the firm. The chief executive officer (CEO) of Trapezium is also the head of finance and manages the firm's risk. Since the departure of the last chief operating officer (COO), the position remains vacant. There is only one non-executive director and only two executive directors have been attending the board meetings in the last few months.

Whilst reviewing the financial regulatory returns to be submitted to the FCA for the previous year, you become aware that the highest paid director of the firm is the CEO, who gave himself a 30% pay rise last year. The average pay rise of other employees was around 8-10%. The firm does not have a formal remuneration policy committee and only has a brief guideline policy on firm remuneration that was last updated in 2011. Trapezium is a subsidiary of the parent company Zane Holdings Ltd, which has another regulated subsidiary firm called New World Broking Ltd. New World deals in stock-broking and contracts for difference (CFDs) as principal with permission to hold client money. On further investigation, you realise that Trapezium holds a client account with New World Broking and regular order flow is put through that account. On analysing the organogram you learn that your CEO is a majority shareholder of Zane Holdings, with a 40% stake in the group.

- a) What is your impression of Trapezium's governance structure? Provide reasons for your answer. _(4 marks)_

- b) Apart from statutory and regulatory considerations, what breaches of ethical behaviour do you think are relevant? _(4 marks)_

- c) What conflicts of interest arise from Trapezium and New World Broking being under the same group and with Trapezium also having an account with New World?

   - _(4 marks)_

- d) If the FCA commissioned a Section 166 Skilled Persons Report concerning the matters referred to above, which areas do you think would be highlighted in the report? _(4 marks)_

- e) As compliance officer, what action would you recommend be taken to address the above concerns? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regards to the governance structure of an asset management firm within a larger corporate group. This involves a combination of governance, conflicts, regulatory and other ethical considerations.

a) The governance arrangements raised a number of immediate concerns:

- (i) The chief executive officer (CEO) acts as CEO and head of finance and risk management;

- (ii) The position of the chief operating officer (COO) has remained vacant for an uncertain period;

- (iii) While the point is not confirmed, finance and risk management may have reported separately to the chief operating officer (COO) rather than CEO;

- (iv) Attendance by the executive directors at asset management company meetings is irregular with only two directors having been appointed;

- (v) The asset management company only has one non-executive director;

- (vi) There is no remuneration policy committee and presumably no separate statement of remuneration policy.

- (vii) The CEO was apparently able to secure the awarding of a 30% pay increase for himself without any apparent performance review or discussion;

- (viii) The asset management company holds a client account with another subsidiary company with regular client order flow being placed through the account;

- (ix) The CEO is a majority shareholder in the parent company of both the asset management company and the separate stock broking firm.

The governance structure of the asset management company and group can accordingly only be described as being weak. Specific regulatory concerns then arise with regard to:

- (i) FCA PRIN 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 3 (management and control), 4 (financial prudence), 6 (customers' interest), 7 (client communication), 8 (conflicts of interest), 10 (client asset protection) and 11 (regulatory relationships);

- (ii) SYSC 2 (senior management arrangements), 3 (systems and controls), 4 (general organisational requirements), 6 (compliance and internal audit), 7 (risk control), 10 (conflicts of interest), 12 (groups risk systems and controls), and 19A (remuneration code);

- (iii) COND 2.7 (business model);

- (iv) COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence);

- (v) FIT 2.1 (honesty, integrity and reputation), 2.2 (competence and capability) and 2.3 (financial soundness);

- (vi) Possibly TC 2 (competence) and 3 (recordkeeping);

- (vii) CASS 1.A (oversight responsibility), possibly 6 (custody rules) and 7 (client money rules).

Separate issues may arise with regard to money laundering controls and the apparent absence of any MLRO as well as market abuse and insider dealing controls.

- b) The question refers to separate ethical breaches apart from the statutory and regulatory matters already referred to in (a) above. No specific answers were expected. Relevant issues may have included:

   - (i) The general attitude of the CEO to regulatory compliance;

- (ii) The general culture of the firm in terms of weak governance and compliance;

- (iii) The potential dominance of the CEO in managing the firm's affairs;

- (iv) The potential conflicts of interest between the firm and clients in using the separate stock broking subsidiary;

- (v) The potential losses suffered by clients through intra-group order flow;

- (vi) The potential client loss with client accounts being held by a separate subsidiary within the group;

- (vii) The apparent lack of disclosure of the order flow activity and separate client account with the group subsidiary;

- (viii) The lack of disclosure of the CEO's majority shareholding in the group parent company;

- (ix) The absence of a remuneration code;

- (x) The absence of any effective non-executive committee or non-executive director oversight;

- (xi) The lack of involvement of executive directors in regular board matters;

- (xii) The more general perception of a lack of care for client interests and assets;

- (xiii) Apparent lack of concern with conduct risk;

- (xiv) Apparent lack of concern with potential regulatory damage and risk.

Reference may also have been made to additional provisions within, for example, the CISI Code including with regard to acting honestly and fairly at all times (CISI 1), avoiding reputational damage (CISI 2), observance and compliance (CISI 3), conflicts (CISI 5), professional competence (CISI 6) and possibly failing to act with the highest personal and professional standards (CISI 8).

- c) A number of potential conflicts of interest may have been identified including between:

   - (i) The CEO and other members of the firm in terms of pay aggravated by the absence of a separate remuneration policy committee and remuneration policy;

   - (ii) The asset management company and clients in connection with order flow through separate subsidiary company without apparent disclosure;

   - (iii) The asset management company and separate subsidiary with clients with the holding of client accounts with the separate subsidiary without apparent disclosure;

   - (iv) The possible lack of independence between the asset management company and separate subsidiary;

   - (v) Possible control exercised by the CEO over activities of the group and of the separate subsidiary companies to client detriment.

- d) Reports by skilled persons may be commissioned (s166 FSMA) in connection with any matter in relation to which information or the production of documents could be requested (s165 FSMA).

   - Information or documents may be requested in connection with the exercise by an authority of any functions conferred by or under the Act (s165 (4) FSMA). The report may then cover any of the matters referred to in (a) or (b) above including specifically:

      - (i) Potential breach of PRIN 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 10 and 11 (from sub-section (a) above);

      - (ii) Potential breach of SYSC 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 10, 12 and 19A;

      - (iii) Potential breach of COND and COND 2.7;

      - (iv) Potential breach of COCON 1, 2, 3 and FIT 2.1, 2.2 and 2.3 as well as TC 2 and 3;

      - (v) Potential breach of CASS and CASS 1.A and 6 and 7;

      - (vi) Potential breach of specific COBS;

      - (vii) Other potential governance failures;

      - (viii) Absence of a remuneration committee and remuneration policy;

      - (ix) Apparent absence of any conflicts of interest policy;

      - (x) Other potential conduct risk and compliance failures and consequent client damage to clients.

- e) Firms are required to deal with the authorities in an open and cooperative manner and to provide all reasonably expected information (FCA PRIN 11 and PRA FR 7). Appropriate remedial action should be taken and fully reported to the authorities. This may include:

- (i) Reviewing the firm's governance arrangements especially with regard to CEO role and function;

- (ii) Appointing a COO and separating the finance and risk management from CEO function;

- (iii) Reviewing the firm's systems and controls;

- (iv) Reviewing the firm's remuneration policy;

- (v) Reviewing the conduct of executive functions within the firm;

- (vi) Reviewing the conduct of non-executive functions within the firm;

- (vii) Reviewing risk management policy and function;

- (viii) Reviewing firm's internal audit policy and function;

- (ix) Reviewing compliance policy and function;

- (x) Reviewing compliance with PRIN, SYSC, COND, COCON and FIT;

- (xi) Reviewing compliance with CASS and client money arrangements;

- (xii) Reviewing the firm's training and policy function under TC;

- (xiii) Possibly ensuring appropriate disclosure of CEO's shareholding under relevant Listing and Disclosure Guidance and Transparency Rules (DTR) as necessary;

- (xiv) Reviewing the firm's conduct risk policy and environment;

- (xv) Reviewing the firm's ethics policy and environment;

- (xvi) Reviewing recruitment policy and HR;

- (xvii) Reviewing conflicts of interest policy and disclosure arrangements;

- (xviii) Reviewing wider group arrangements especially with regard to client account management;

- (xix) Reviewing compliance with the Skilled Persons Report;

- (xx) Cooperating closely with the regulatory authorities under PRIN 11 and COCON 3.

## B55.

### Question

You work in the UK Compliance and Ethics department of a major U.S. bank with a separate large subsidiary in Canary Wharf, London. The bank is one of the largest suppliers of prime brokerage services to many of the biggest hedge funds. This involves providing a range of services including cash account management, securities settlement, stock lending, foreign currency exchange and global custody. The board of the bank has become increasingly concerned about the large number of recent fines imposed on major financial institutions especially by the US and UK authorities and a number of medium-sized hedge funds which are clients of the bank have also been subject to recent investigation in the US. You are asked to advise on the ethical and conduct risk issues that may arise in the UK.

You are specifically asked to prepare a memorandum covering each of the following:

- a) Outline some of the types of hedge funds activities that may cause specific concerns from a regulatory perspective. _(4 marks)_

- b) Explain the general nature of the UK authorities' expectations and requirements to promote an appropriate culture within firms. _(5 marks)_

- c) Outline the FCA approach to conduct risk management. _(5 marks)_

- d) Explain the various courses of action that your bank may take to meet regulatory expectations in identifying and mitigating culture and conduct risk.

   - _(6 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with of concern with regard to the activities of a US investment bank in Canary Wharf. This reflected the circumstances surrounding the closure of Lehman Bros in the UK (Re Lehman Bros International (Europe) [2012] UK SC 6). The purpose of the question was to separate the UK regulatory treatment of culture from financial ethics and conduct risk. Some candidates mixed the two points together in the separate subsections (b) and (c).

- a) Candidates were asked to identify initial relevant regulatory concerns. The first hedge fund was a $100,000 equity fund set up by Alfred Winslow Jones in 1949 using short sales and leverage. Hedge funds are established with limited membership to benefit from exemptions available under the US Securities Act 1933, Securities Exchange Act 1934, Investment Company Act 1940 and Investment Advisers Act 1940.

The question could either have been dealt with by listing specific facts or activities that may raise regulatory issues or by referring to specific sets of regulatory provisions that may be breached. Referring to specific regulatory provisions nevertheless overlapped with the treatment of culture and conduct in subsections (b) and (c) and was therefore not necessary for subsection (a) although not penalised.

Relevant activities or aspects of hedge fund operations include:

- (i) adopting aggressive trading practices including short selling and excessive stock lending

- (ii) using aggressive trading strategies (including possibly global macro, event driven, directional, market neutral, arbitrage, short/long, emerging markets, managed futures and funds of funds strategies)

- (iii) high leverage

- (iv) trading in high-risk instruments including structured products and natural derivatives

- (v) trading on the over-the-counter (OTC) market

- (vi) establishment in off-shore financial centres

- (vii) lack of transparency and limited reporting

- (viii) lack of direct regulatory oversight and control

- (ix) high fee and remuneration structures

- (x) potential market abuse of insider trading

- (xi) potential money laundering

- (xii) more general possibly weak cultural and ethical frameworks.

These may create a full range of higher financial risks (including credit, market, interest rate, falling currency, commodity and derivative exposures), operational risks (including delivery and settlement risks), legal risks, management risks and wider environmental risks.

Against these disadvantages, candidates could have referred to hedge funds advantages although this was not necessary. These include higher investment return, improved portfolio management, greater portfolio diversification and increased market correction, market volatility, market liquidity, market efficiency, financial engineering, financial vision, market return to profitability and overall market stability.

- b) Candidates were asked to explain the general nature of the UK authorities' expectations and requirements with regard to culture within financial firms. The authorities have increasingly discussed ethics in terms of culture although this remains a difficult issue.

A number of general documents and speeches have been issued on ethics over time. The FSA's initial general approach to ethics was set out in FSA, An ethical framework for financial services (October 2002) with competence being examined in FSA, Competence and ethics (June 2010) CP 10/12. Ethics was considered as part of the Retail Distribution Review (RDR) with the FSA recommending the creation of an enforceable Code of Ethics for investment advisors in FSA, Retail Distribution Review (November 2008) Feedback Statement 08/06. This was referred to again in FSA, Distribution of retail investments: Delivering the RDR (June 2009) CP 09/18 with eight general principles proposed. These can be summarised in terms of acting honestly and fairly at all times, integrity, legal and regulatory compliance, market integrity standards, conflict, professional competence, declining to act and upholding the highest personal and professional standards. FSA Chairman, Howard Davies, discussed ethics in 'Ethics in regulation' in 2001.

FSA CEO Hector Sants switched the focus from ethics to culture in 'Do regulators have a role to play in judging culture and ethics?' in June 2010 and 'Can culture be regulated?' in October 2012. Hector Sants noted in the first speech that when he joined the FSA, he was told by senior management that 'the FSA does not do ethics'. Howard Davies had earlier stated that the FSA could not 'seek to act as the conscience of the square mile.' Rather than focus on ethics, the 'better focus for regulators would be culture, which no doubt reflects the ethical framework of the organisation'. Sants considered that the work ethics was 'not precise enough' and carried 'too much baggage for regulators'. There was nevertheless 'already a consensus of what constitutes the right behaviour' which included through the codes produced by the CISI and Worshipful Company of International Bankers which had 'broad acceptance in the community'. Firms should have 'a culture which encourages individuals to make judgements and deliver the outcomes we want' and that 'at all times we want an undertaking to act with integrity'. The regulators had therefore to focus on 'what an unacceptable culture looks like and what outcomes that drives'.

A number of speeches were subsequently made by FCA Chief Executive, Martin Wheatley (such as 'Competing on integrity' (November 2013); 'Ethics and economics' (March 2014); 'The fairness challenge' (October 2013); and 'Good conduct and market integrity' (June 2014)) and other FCA staff (including Clive Adamson, FCA Director of Supervision, 'The importance of culture in driving behaviours of firms and how the FCA will assess this' (April 2013); and 'Trust and confidence – ensuring firms' ethics are built around their customers' (November 2013); and later Mark Steward, 'Culture and governance' (November 2015)).

Culture was referred to in the FCA Business Plan 2015/16 as a key driver in behaviour, with an effective culture being one that supports a business model and business practices that have the fair treatment of customers and clients at their core and support market integrity. Culture began at the top with the FCA ensuring that firms and boards dealt with conduct issues as a priority. Boards had to engage in conduct issues by specifically investigating products or business lines, understanding cross selling product strategies, possible loss and suitable product sales. Customer focus had also to be translated into how business decisions were made, the treatment of consumer responses, individual behaviour, issue escalation and remuneration and incentive awards.

The emerging UK ethical framework is also reflected in the more specific provisions contained within the High Level Standards and other more specific laws and regulations designed to ensure ethical outcomes. No separate ethical sourcebook has been prepared at this time although the issue of banking standards is to be considered by the new Banking Standards Board (BSB) set up following the Banking Standards Review (May 2014) by Sir Richard Lambert after the earlier recommendations for the establishment of a professional body by the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) final report on Changing banking for good (12 June 2013) Vols I and II.

Candidates could also have referred to the specific requirements imposed under FCA PRIN, SYSC, COCON (and PRA FR) as well as COND for firms and FIT for individuals. While a number of PRIN are more prudential based including 3 (management and control) and 4 (financial prudence), others are more ethical and conduct based including 1 (integrity); 6 (customers' interests); 7 (communication); 8 (conflicts of interest); 9 (trust); and asset protection (10) as well as skill, care and diligence within PRIN 2. This is also reflected in COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), and 5 (proper standards of market conduct). Conflicts of interest are dealt with under SYSC 10 and remuneration under SYSC 19A. Elements of this are also reflected under more specific COBS including 3 (categorisation), 4 (communication and financial promotions), 6 (disclosure), 8 (agreements), 9 (suitability), 10 (appropriateness), 11 (dealing and managing), 12 (investment research), 13 and 14 (product information), 15 (cancellation) and 16 (reporting).

Reference may also have been made to the business standards sourcebooks including Conduct of Business (COBS), Insurance: Conduct of Business (ICOBS), Mortgages and Home Finance: Conduct of Business (MCOB) and Banking: Conduct of Business (BCOBS).

A number of other statutory provisions can also be considered to include an ethical element such as the prohibition on market abuse in MAR and misleading statements and impressions in ss89-90 FSA 2012 as well as separate insider dealing offences in the CJA.

c) Conduct risk also remains a difficult issue. The FCA has generally been made responsible for conduct regulation with the PRA managing prudential regulation under the revised institutional structure established under the FSA 2012. The FCA is specifically responsible for 'conduct risk' although this is a difficult issue to define precisely. The term conduct risk is not referred to in the FSMA as amended. Conduct risk is not separately defined in the FCA Glossary nor is the term 'risk' separately defined. ### Answer

This question provided a case study related to the selection of a new CEO for a UK-based fintech firm that has grown rapidly in recent years. The case study provided information in relation to two candidates for the CEO position: candidate A, and candidate B.

(a) The Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR) was introduced as part of the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013, following recommendations from the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS). The regime was established with the aim of enhancing accountability and governance within financial institutions, specifically targeting the roles and responsibilities of senior management. It is designed to promote a culture of responsibility and integrity at the top levels of financial firms. The SM&CR framework is structured around three core components: the Senior Managers Regime (SMR), the Certification Regime, and the Conduct Rules.

The SMR focuses on the most senior individuals in a firm who hold key decision-making roles. Under this regime, senior managers are required to have clearly defined responsibilities and must be approved by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) or the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) before they can perform their roles. Each senior manager is assigned a "Statement of Responsibilities" that outlines their specific duties and areas of accountability within the firm. This ensures that there is a clear understanding of who is responsible for each aspect of the firm's operations, which enhances transparency and accountability.

The Certification Regime applies to employees who are not senior managers but whose roles could cause significant harm to the firm or its customers. Unlike the SMR, individuals in these roles do not require approval from regulators. Instead, the responsibility lies with the firm to assess and certify that these individuals are 'fit and proper' to perform their roles. This certification must be renewed annually, and it covers aspects such as competence, honesty, integrity, and financial soundness.

The Conduct Rules set out basic standards of behaviour that apply to all employees within a regulated firm. These rules are intended to ensure that individuals act with integrity, due skill, care, and diligence, and in the best interests of customers. The rules are divided into two tiers: the first tier applies to all employees, while the second tier includes additional rules specifically for senior managers. Firms are required to train their staff on these rules and ensure compliance.

The SM&CR was specifically designed to address weaknesses in corporate culture and governance that contributed to past financial crises. By imposing stringent accountability measures, the regime aims to instil a culture of responsibility among senior managers and key employees, reducing the likelihood of misconduct and promoting ethical behaviour. The requirement for firms to certify the fitness and propriety of individuals in significant roles reinforces the emphasis on high standards of conduct throughout the organisation.

(b) The Chief Executive function (SMF1) is the function of having responsibility, under the immediate authority of the governing body, alone or jointly with others, for carrying out the management of the conduct of the whole of the business (or relevant activities) of a firm. The role of the CEO in a financial services firm, particularly within a UK subsidiary of a foreign bank, is one of the most critical Senior Management Functions (SMFs) as defined under the Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR). The CEO holds overarching responsibility for the firm's operations, strategy, and governance, ensuring that the firm complies with regulatory requirements and operates in a manner that promotes the interests of its clients and the integrity of the financial markets.

The CEO is responsible for the day-to-day management of the firm's business activities, ensuring that operations are conducted efficiently, effectively, and in compliance with all relevant laws and regulations. This includes overseeing the implementation of strategic objectives, managing resources, and ensuring that the firm's financial performance meets the expectations of the Board and shareholders. The CEO must provide strong leadership, setting the tone from the top regarding the firm's culture, values, and commitment to ethical conduct. This involves ensuring that the firm's governance framework is robust, with clear lines of accountability and decision-making processes that support the firm's strategic goals while maintaining high standards of corporate governance. The CEO is also responsible for fostering a culture of compliance and risk management, ensuring that the firm's policies and procedures align with regulatory expectations and that all employees understand their roles in maintaining the firm's integrity.

As the primary point of contact between the firm and its regulators, the CEO is responsible for ensuring that the firm adheres to all applicable regulatory requirements. This includes overseeing the implementation of the SM&CR within the firm, ensuring that all senior managers and certified persons are fit and proper, and that the firm's compliance and risk management frameworks are effective. The CEO must also ensure that the firm has appropriate systems and controls in place to identify, manage, and mitigate risks, particularly those related to conduct, financial crime, and operational resilience. The CEO plays a key role in managing relationships with a wide range of stakeholders, including regulators, shareholders, clients, and employees. This involves communicating the firm's strategy, performance, and risk management practices to these stakeholders and ensuring that their interests are considered in the firm's decision-making processes. In the context of a UK subsidiary, the CEO must also navigate the relationship with the parent company, ensuring that the subsidiary's operations align with the broader strategic goals of the group while complying with UK-specific regulatory requirements.

The Board should assess each candidate's knowledge and understanding of UK regulatory requirements, particularly those related to the SM&CR, and the Board should evaluate each candidate's leadership style and ability to uphold the firm's culture of compliance, customer focus, and ethical behaviour. The Board should consider each candidate's approach to risk management, particularly in the context of the CEO's responsibility for overseeing the firm's risk framework; and consider how each candidate will handle stakeholder relationships, both within the UK and with the parent company. The Board's decision should be influenced by each candidate's ability to fulfil these responsibilities, particularly in maintaining the firm's strong culture of compliance and ethical conduct, managing regulatory risks, and leading the firm in a way that aligns with both UK and group-wide strategic objectives. The right choice of CEO will ensure that the firm continues to thrive while upholding its commitment to regulatory excellence and stakeholder trust.

(c) The appointment of a new CEO under the SM&CR is a process that must be handled with the utmost care and diligence to ensure full compliance with the FCA and PRA regulations. The SM&CR emphasises individual accountability, and the CEO is one of the most critical Senior Management Functions (SMFs) within a regulated firm. The first step in the process is to clearly define the CEO's role and prepare a statement of responsibilities (SoR). This document must outline the specific duties and areas of accountability that the CEO will hold within the firm. The SoR should be precise, clear, and reflect the actual responsibilities that the CEO will undertake, ensuring there is no overlap or ambiguity with other SMFs within the organisation.

One of the critical requirements under the SM&CR is that the firm must assess whether the candidate is 'fit and proper' to perform the role of CEO. This assessment is not a one-time event but an ongoing obligation. The fit and proper assessment involves three key elements:

- **Honesty, Integrity, and Reputation** – The firm must ensure that the candidate has a history of honesty, integrity, and good reputation. This includes reviewing the candidate's previous conduct, any legal or regulatory sanctions, and their overall standing in the industry.

- **Competence and Capability** – The candidate must demonstrate the necessary skills, experience, and qualifications to perform the role effectively. This involves evaluating the candidate's track record, knowledge of the financial services industry, and understanding of the specific challenges and opportunities facing the UK subsidiary.

- **Financial Soundness** – The firm must assess the financial stability of the candidate to ensure they are not susceptible to financial pressures that could compromise their judgment or performance in the role.

To conduct this assessment, the firm should gather detailed information from the candidate, including references, background checks, and any relevant certifications or qualifications. The assessment should be documented thoroughly to provide evidence of compliance with regulatory requirements.

Once the firm has completed the fit and proper assessment and prepared the statement of responsibilities, it must submit an application for regulatory approval to the FCA or PRA. The application should include all relevant documentation, such as the SoR, evidence of the fit and proper assessment, and any other required forms.

The FCA will review the application to ensure that the candidate meets the necessary standards and that the firm has followed the correct procedures. The regulator may request additional information or clarification during this process.

Although the CEO role falls under the SMR, the firm must also ensure that any other roles that the candidate will undertake are compliant with the Certification Regime. This includes assessing whether the candidate requires certification for additional functions that could pose significant risks to the firm or its customers.

The Certification Regime requires that the firm annually assess and certify that the individual remains fit and proper to perform their role. This ongoing obligation reinforces the importance of continuous monitoring and evaluation of the CEO's performance and conduct.

After the appointment, the firm must continue to monitor the CEO's performance and conduct, ensuring that they adhere to the responsibilities outlined in the Statement of Responsibilities and the Conduct Rules. Any significant changes to the CEO's role or responsibilities must be reflected in an updated SoR and submitted to the FCA or PRA. The firm must also be prepared to report any breaches of the Conduct Rules or any other concerns about the CEO's fitness and propriety to the regulators promptly.

(d) Under the Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR), the CEO plays a pivotal role in setting the tone from the top, influencing the firm's overall approach to compliance, risk management, and ethical conduct. Each candidate presents distinct potential risks to the company's established culture of compliance and ethical behaviour, and these risks must be carefully considered by the Board.

Candidate A, as an internal candidate with a deep understanding of the firm's operations and strong ties to the regulators, offers a lower immediate risk to the firm's compliance culture. Their familiarity with the firm's values, policies, and existing regulatory framework suggests they are likely to maintain the current high standards of ethical behaviour and customer focus. However, there is a risk of complacency. While Candidate A may preserve the status quo, they might be less inclined to challenge established practices or innovate in response to changing regulatory requirements or market conditions. This could limit the firm's ability to adapt to new regulatory demands, such as those related to the growing focus on technological resilience or data protection in fintech. Additionally, if Candidate A becomes overly comfortable with existing processes, they may miss opportunities to enhance the firm's risk management frameworks as the business evolves.

Candidate B, with their background in rapidly scaling tech-driven financial services firms, brings a fresh perspective and new strategies that could fuel the firm's growth. However, this candidate presents a higher risk to the firm's culture of compliance and ethical behaviour. Their focus on fast expansion could lead to overlooking compliance risks or taking shortcuts in regulatory processes. If Candidate B prioritises growth and profitability over the firm's compliance obligations, the firm could be exposed to regulatory breaches or unethical conduct. Furthermore, as Candidate B comes from outside the UK, there is a risk that they may not fully understand the nuances of the UK's regulatory environment or the expectations of the FCA, particularly in areas specific to fintech, such as cybersecurity and AML compliance.

To mitigate these potential risks, the Board must take a proactive approach during both the selection and onboarding processes. First, the firm should conduct a rigorous fit and proper assessment for both candidates, with particular emphasis on their approach to compliance and their track record in leading within a regulated environment. This assessment should focus not only on their technical competence and leadership skills but also on their commitment to upholding ethical standards and ensuring regulatory compliance. Second, the Board should carefully evaluate how each candidate's values align with the firm's established culture. This involves gathering feedback from colleagues, regulators, and other stakeholders who have worked with the candidates in the past, focusing on their ability to balance growth with compliance. Additionally, the Board should ensure that clear expectations regarding compliance and ethical behaviour are communicated during the onboarding process. This should include a detailed review of the CEO's responsibilities under the SM&CR, particularly the requirement to lead by example and set the right tone from the top. Ongoing monitoring should also be a priority.

Regular compliance reviews and feedback mechanisms can help the Board ensure that the new CEO, regardless of their background, adheres to the firm's compliance obligations and ethical standards. Any deviations from the firm's established culture should be identified and addressed promptly, with the CEO held accountable for maintaining the firm's integrity and regulatory standing.

**a) (4 marks)**

**Explain the general purpose and key elements of the Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR).**

The Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR) arose after recommendations from the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) and was included in the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013. A major element of the regulatory reform was to enhance the corporate culture and increase the accountability of senior management of financial firms. This regulatory regime initially affected banks and insurers and was later extended to most regulated financial services firms. These dual goals of increased accountability and enhanced corporate culture are to be achieved through several measures.

The definition of a 'senior manager' is a person responsible for managing the firm on a day-to-day basis. The SM&CR expects that individuals undertaking a senior management role in a regulated financial firm will be allocated responsibility for specific activities, business areas and management functions of their firm. These individuals will be either a member of the firm's governing body or will report directly to this body. The individual must be sufficiently senior and credible and have sufficient resources and authority.

Individuals subject to this Certification Regime are approved by their firms (rather than regulators). The firms have to assess whether these persons are deemed 'fit and proper'. This process involves firms providing a 'certificate' to individuals, which outlines the individuals' role and responsibilities and states if the firm is satisfied that the individual is 'fit and proper' to perform their function.

**d) (4 marks)**

**Which documents and procedures are required to assist in the change-over of a senior manager in a financial services firm?**

A lot of documentation is required for a change in the senior management of the firm. This might include, for example, the statement of responsibilities, the responsibility map, receiving an appropriate regulatory reference, and providing relevant handover materials.

Handover materials should include all the necessary information and material expected for a new senior manager to perform their responsibilities effectively.

A statement of responsibility and a responsibilities map are provided in a single document describing management and governance arrangements. This identifies the senior managers within the firm and outlines the responsibilities allocated to these persons to ensure no gaps are present in the terms of accountability. These responsibility maps must be approved by both the individual involved and by the financial regulator.

Lastly, a firm is required to have a regulatory reference in place for senior managers. This pertains to whether the applicant is deemed to be a fit and proper person to undertake the role. This reference should include instances of conduct that occurred or existed for six years from the date of the reference request. The firm must also apply to the FCA for permission, as the FCA must approve the change.
