## B6.

### Question

You are the Head of Compliance at Megabank, a large integrated securities house. Megabank has recently appointed Roy "Brass" Neck to re-invigorate its Equities Division, which includes both Equity Research and Equity Sales. Neck's reputation is as a practical, hands-on manager, with aggressive ideas about the ways to generate business and motivate staff.

After two weeks in the post, he comes to you with the following proposals, which he wants implemented without delay:

- a) Whenever the Corporate Finance Department goes to "pitch" for a new client they must be accompanied by an analyst from the Equities Department. He explains that this will show the potential client that Megabank has a strong team of experts; it will also enable the analysts to provide a "reality check" to control over-enthusiastic Corporate Financiers and turn down unsuitable new clients.

- b) The specialised dealers who currently work in the Private Client Department must be redeployed within the Equity Department and report to Neck. This is so that he can control the dealers' activities and ensure that all the order flow in equities is retained within the firm. At present, too many trades are being executed with other houses via SETS and Retail Service Providers. He will also be able to give the dealers large lines of stock, which they can place with Private Clients.

- c) If a Corporate Client is going to make an announcement, the Corporate Finance Department must brief the Equity Division well before the announcement is made, in order for them to be prepared for the inevitable questions from clients that will arise when the announcement is made.

- d) The Nominee and Safe Custody Department must notify all Equities Division staff of every "S793 Notice" they receive. (s793 of the Companies Act gives companies the power to demand details of the underlying shareholders within a reasonable time. Company secretaries usually write to nominee companies to find out who the beneficial owners are.)

What advice would you give to Mr Neck with regard to each of the arrangements that he wants to put in place? _(20 marks)_

### Answer

The objective of this question is for you to identify potential problems and discuss particular issues. These would cover areas arising under the principles for business such as integrity, skill, care and diligence, market conduct, customers' interests, conflicts of interest, relationship of trust and client assets, as well as market abuse, insider dealing and misleading statements.

- a) It is not appropriate for research analysts to be taken to new client meetings to pitch for business. The availability of research teams may be used to strengthen the profile and ability of the firm more generally, but it seems as though the independence of the research is not sacrosanct. Reference should therefore have been made to the need for Chinese walls, also to issues of receipt of unpublished price sensitive information and relevant information not generally available.

The need for a reality check for staff suggests that there are issues that the firm needs to address internally and in relation to market abuse and Part 7 of FSA 2012 if not in relation to fair, clear and not misleading in its research publications.

- b) The issues with regard to conflicts of interest and acting in the client's best interest as well as best execution and suitability should have been raised. Reference should also have been made to the obligations on systemic internalisers under MiFID.

- c) Again this creates potential problems in managing inside information, and the offences of misusing or passing on such information other than in the proper performance of their duties. You should describe the offences and sanctions in such cases, and reference to the managing of conflicts of interest should also be made.

- d) Issues arise again of conflicts of interest as s793 notices are often used to ascertain whether a takeover bid is imminent. The information may fall to be regarded as unpublished price-sensitive information. The importance of Chinese walls in maintaining barriers to the flow of information should again have been emphasised.

## B12.

### Question

You are the head of a division within a London investment bank which covers a broad range of activities including Merger and Acquisition ('M&A') business and a separately incorporated Private Equity ('PE') operation.

You are aware of the current high level of regulatory scrutiny attached to conflicts of interest by the FCA, especially in the area of investment banking and some of the recent scandals that have occurred. You have also recently participated in a training video, which emphasised the ethical culture of the bank and reminded colleagues of the need for trust and integrity in dealing with clients whose interests must always be considered of paramount importance.

a) Explain the importance of the need to maintain high standards of behaviour and integrity under the FCA Handbook and how this is generally secured in practice.

_(4 marks)_

You become aware that your M&A team are advising client X on the acquisition of ABC Limited which is the subject of an auction process. You are also aware through the conflict management escalation process that your PE colleagues are also participating in the auction process. The potential conflict has been managed on the basis of Chinese walls and independence policies and you have satisfied yourself on the reputational issues through disclosure.

b) Explain the FCA Handbook requirements that apply with regard to independence policies and disclosure of conflicts of interest. _(4 marks)_

c) Explain what action should be taken to ensure the integrity of the Chinese walls in place and that they are effectively monitored and maintained. _(4 marks)_

The M&A director responsible for advising client X comes to see you to provide an update on the transaction and confirms that his client is through to the final round of the auction process with one other bidder, which is believed to be the affiliated PE operation. During the discussion you learn that following a review of the information made available to all potential bidders in the data room, the M&A team conducted its own comprehensive due diligence exercise which resulted in an assessment that the value of ABC Limited is significantly less than generally believed. The M&A director questions whether this will affect the bid strategy of his PE colleagues. You are also concerned to know whether PE have picked up on the over-valuation of ABC Limited and are not about to commit the Group and its Fund Investors to an investment of several hundred million dollars on the basis of erroneous information.

d) What courses of action are open to you and what factors would you take into account in deciding how to act? _(4 marks)_

The next day, the M&A director responsible for advising client X comes to you in a state of excitement and informs you that their due diligence has uncovered certain accounting irregularities at ABC Limited.

e) What are your responsibilities to report such issues? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

- a) The purpose of this question was to test the ability of candidates to understand the general ethical background to the FSMA and FCA Handbook and to be able to identify and deal with specific types of difficulties that may arise in practice.

The general environment includes the importance attached to management function and integrity as developed in the Principles for Businesses (PRIN), Senior Management Arrangements, Systems and Controls (SYSC) and Conduct Rules (COCON). Reference may also have been made to the FCA's statutory objectives and supervisory principles, as well as to the threshold conditions (Schedule 6 and COND) and fit and proper requirements (FIT).

Some of the more important specific requirements dealing with misconduct could have been referred including market abuse (MAR) and misleading statements and impressions (Part 7 of FSA 2012). Remedies may also have been noted. Reference should also have been made to more general common law duties of obedience, care and skill, good faith and confidentiality and the uncertain relationship that still exists between these potential heads of liability and the new statutory scheme of regulation set up under the FSMA.

- b) The specific requirements in COBS and SYSC concerning conflicts of interest and independence policies should have been explained. Firms should seek to avoid conflicts, in particular, under PRIN 8 and cannot advise customers or act in a discretionary capacity where a material interest or conflict arises. The general obligations imposed concerning independence, the maintenance and notification of independence policies to staff and customers as well as specific disclosure obligations imposed should have been explained.

- c) The particular conditions imposed under SYSC with regard to the operation of Chinese walls should have been outlined. The nature of Chinese walls should have been explained as wall as relevant operational conditions and use of stop or restricted lists and watch lists referred to.

- d) Possible options including no action, declining to act or 'crossing the wall'. The situation may initially be dealt with by asking the PE operation to confirm its valuation and to ensure that proper due diligence has been carried out. It is essential to respect the Chinese wall and to protect the client's interest. In the event that the over valuation is not identified, the PE team may have to be directed to withdraw although this should be confirmed first.

Possible insider dealing and market abuse breaches should nevertheless be considered in participating in the auction (and then withdrawing) as well as in subsequently dealing (or not dealing) on behalf of other clients. More information may be required. Difficult issues must be discussed with compliance and the FCA.

- e) The discovery of accounting irregularities must initially be brought to the client's attention by the M&A team. Breach of relevant company law and market abuse requirements as well as the criminal law may be involved. This would have to be disclosed to the FCA and possibly the relevant accountancy bodies. Any potential money laundering concerns would have to be separately reported. Additional disclosure obligations may also apply under the UK Listings Rules although it is not clear whether ABC is listed or not. Possible breach of insider dealing and market abuse restrictions within your firm should also be considered to avoid any liability issues. No breach of any common law confidentiality obligations should arise in this case due to the irregularities and possible rules breaches involved.

## B14. *(Compulsory)*

### Question

Andy, Head of Equities Trading, is holding his weekly team meeting.  The team is not performing well and Andy's boss has made it clear that he expects Andy to turn things around.

Andy: 'OK – main point for our team meeting today – we must come up with some bright revenue generating ideas and quickly.  Any thoughts?'

Bob (Head of Proprietary Trading): 'We need to get more insight into the market and capitalise on that.  Why don't we Premises to re-locate us so that we are more in the swim of client order flow?  Right next to the market makers would make best business sense.  And we could arrange to see the list of client indications of interest – they're not confirmed orders at that stage and the clients would value the additional liquidity that would be provided in the market.'

Charles (Deputy Head of Prop Trading and formerly a Research analyst): 'Better still, get us all access to the pending research recommendation changes and take advantage of the window after they have been approved by the research committee, but before they have been released to clients.  Don't let on, but I still have systems access to the draft reports being prepared in the Utilities sector from my time there as an analyst, and I intend to keep it that way!'

Dave (Head of Sales): 'I'm on the board of one of our best clients – maybe I could exercise some influence there to bring more business to us?'

Ed (Head of Syndication, Capital Markets): Personally, I think we should allow personal account shareholdings in the shares of our clients – that way we align our objectives with those of our clients.  Anyway, these are all good ideas guys, they could really help the firm.  Andy, why don't you give a prize for the best idea – a week's skiing for the winner and his best customer would incentivise us most – we should use the classiest resort in the US, with travel, accommodation and all incidental expenses paid?'

Fred (aside to George): 'I'm not entirely comfortable with some of this, are you?  I think we should run it by Compliance and definitely escalate Charles's continuing access to draft research if Andy won't take it up with him.'

George (aside to Fred): 'Fred, listen, you are a long-standing close personal friend of Charles.  20 years working together and he's given you a lot of support in your career. How could you even think of putting him in a difficult position and potentially jeopardising his job?  Have a heart for him and his family and don't go there.  And remember, the rest of the team's jobs will be on the line if you block our route to improved performance. What's more, if previous experience is anything to go by, you'll very likely be disadvantaged or sidelined if you rock the boat right now.'

## From the above scenario:

- a) Identify four potential conflicts of interested, explain their regulatory and ethical nature and indicate appropriate tools for their management and control.

   - _(12 marks)_

- b) Why is appropriate management of conflicts important to firms?

_(4 marks)_

- c) Comment on George's and the firm's apparent attitude to whistle blowing.  What provisions to facilitate whistle blowing would you expect within a firm?

   - _(4 marks)_

### Answer

- a) A number of separate conflicts of interest were raised in this question.  These were principally concerned with client and proprietary trading, use of research and front running, personal account dealing and board representation.  The other main issues were concerned with remuneration and inducements and failure to report (whistleblowing).

References should have been made to all available PRIN, COCON, SYSC and COBS provisions.  The CISI Principles could also have been noted.  As much detail as possible should have been provided with discussion of each relevant factual situation.

Separate reference may also have been included to possible market abuse (MAR), insider dealing (CJA) and misleading statements and impressions offences (ss 89 and 90 FSA 2012).  Breach of relevant duties of a fiduciary under the common law could also have been noted.

Relevant tools include general systems and controls obligations as well as specific conflicts of interest measures.  This, in particular, includes disclosure, declining to act, Chinese walls or maintenance of an independence policy.

- b) Justification for the imposition of controls on conflicts of interest could have been explained principally in terms of market inefficiency and treating customers fairly (an FCA priority).  Some reference could have been included to the FCA's operational objectives including specifically consumer protection and integrity. Reference may also have been made to possible sources of risk (including financial risk, operational, legal and reputational risk) as well as to breach of specific regulatory provisions including FSMA and the FCA Handbook (PRIN, COCON and SYSC as well as COBS).  Relevant ethical issues could have been highlighted with specific reference to the CISI Principles. Some candidates did refer to the nature of the penalties and sanctions imposed although this is more concerned with the consequences rather than justification for the imposition of such controls.

- c) George's failure to report should have been criticised in terms of relevant reporting and whistle-blowing obligations.  Specific reference should have been made to COCON and SYSC.  The prohibition on the imposition of penalties on whistle-blowers under the Public Interest Disclosure Act should have been noted.  Additional reference may also have been made to the CISI Principles.

## B19.

### Question

You have recently started work as the Compliance Officer of a small city firm which has three different business units: Research, Brokerage and Corporate Finance.

After carrying our a review of the firm's business practices during your first couple of weeks you are now ready to present your findings to the Chief Executive and there are a number of matters that you wish  to bring to his attention. Specifically, you are concerned about the following observations.

The Head of Research appears to report to the Head of Brokerage, although you cannot find a formal staff organisational chart to confirm this. You have also been told that in the interests of transparency, all of the Broking team attend the weekly Research team meeting in which Research discuss the companies that they are currently covering, the views that they have on them and the nature of the reports that will be issued the following week.

As a result of these close links between Brokerage and Research, it is possible for the Research staff to be remunerated in accordance with the number of transactions that are generated from their research reports. Considerable emphasis is placed on this direct link between research reports and client transactions and as a result the heads of the two departments have agreed that the most active clients of the previous six months should receive research reports a couple of hours before other clients, to give them time to react to it before the market changes. The Head of Research has explained to you that in order to fully monitor the impact of research reports on client transactions it is necessary for the Brokerage and Research teams to sit next to each other, and in fact the dividing wall that used to separate the two teams has recently been taken down.

You also have some concerns about the Corporate Finance department. It appears to be very successful and has recently expanded to take on two new trainees who you are told have been such quick learners that they have already been able to give some sound advice to clients despite the fact that they have not yet had the time to sit their exams and that that Head of the Department has been too busy to give them much attention as he is busy working on a hostile takeover.

When you asked the Head of Corporate Finance more about the hostile takeover mandate that is keeping him busy he explained proudly that he is so well regarded in the market that two firms bidding to buy the same company have given him a mandate to work for them on the transaction. He has not felt the need to inform the two client firms of the two mandates as he tells you that the situation is adequately managed by the use of a Chinese Wall – the Corporate Finance team is in a restricted-access area of the office.

During the course of your review work, however, you have noted that even though the Corporate Finance team is indeed in a separate office, the door is normally kept open as it can get very stuffy. And anyway, with the door locked it makes it more difficult for the Heads of Research and Brokerage to get in, which you have seen them do on a regular basis, although you are told that it is only to discuss golf!

Comment briefly on the soundness of the regulatory control infrastructure within this firm with special reference to:

- a) Any practices you consider to be inappropriate; _(5 marks)_

- b) Any controls that you consider should be implemented or strengthened; _(5 marks)_ c) Any rule breaches that you think might have taken place; _(5 marks)_

- d) The potential consequences of any rule breaches you have identified. _(5 marks)_

### Answer

The question was generally concerned with the identification of a series of breaches in the brokerage, research and corporate finance operations within a securities firm. Each part carried equal marks.

- a) Practices which should have generated concerns with regard to the quality of the control infrastructure maintained by the firm would include:

- (i) Head of Research reporting directly to the Head of Brokerage;

- (ii) Absence of any formal organisational chart which suggests ineffective demarcation of functions and responsibilities within the firm more generally;

- (iii) Broking team attending weekly research team meetings;

- (iv) Research payments tied to brokerage use;

- (v) Provision of privileged information to separate sets of client;

- (vi) Failure to maintain effective separation between brokerage and research personnel;

- (vii) Failure to complete training of staff in corporate finance before being allowed to conduct certified functions;

(viii) Absence of effective oversight over trainee staff;

(ix) Conflicts of interest in acting on both sides of corporate acquisitions;

- (x) Failure to maintain effective demarcation between corporate finance and research and broking teams (including conduct of allegedly social discussions within office situation).

- b) The following revisions should be considered to the control systems maintained within the firm:

- (i) Research should report to a senior manager outside brokerage;

- (ii) The organisational structure of the firm should be reviewed to ensure that all relevant operations function effectively and independently where necessary with a revised organisational chart being provided to all personnel;

- (iii) The broking team should not be required to attend research meetings although they may be entitled to see the final reports produced the following week;

- (iv) Remuneration packages should be reviewed within research and across the firm generally;

- (v) The extent and nature of any preferential treatment given to clients should be reviewed. This is not inappropriate as such provided that relevant dealing and managing rules are complied with including ensuring customer order priority and best execution;

- (vi) Effective separation (Chinese walls) should be maintained between brokerage and research at all times;

- (vii) The training of junior members of staff should be reviewed with personnel not being entitled to carry out any specific certified function until they are appropriately qualified and a certificate issued by the firm;

- (viii) Effective oversight over all staff should be reviewed and maintained at all times;

- (ix) Corporate finance should be prohibited from acting on both sides of any particular acquisition in light of the clear conflicts of interest that arise;

- (x) The effective separation (Chinese walls) should be maintained between corporate finance and the other core sections within the firm with all personnel being made aware of the firm's policy with regard to the management of conflicts of interest.

- c) Failure to maintain appropriate management and control systems could lead to a breach of the following provisions:

- (i) Various principles within PRIN may have been breached including 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 3 (management and control), 5 (market conduct), 6 (customers' interests), 8 (conflicts of interest) and 9 (trust).

- (ii) Many of the core provisions within SYSC may have been breached (including on apportionment, organisation, compliance function, employees and remuneration).

- (iii) Many of the core provisions within COCON may also have been breached (including 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence) and 5 (market conduct).

- (iv) Various provisions within COBS may have been breached (including Chinese walls, 'spinning', conflicts of interest, investment research and research recommendations).

- (v) Consequential statutory provisions including ss 59, 71 and 138D FSMA.

- (vi) A number of core provisions with the CISI Code of Conduct may also have been breached (including Principles 1 (acting honestly and fairly), 2 (integrity), 4 (market conduct), 5 (conflicts of interest), 6 (professional competence) and 7 (personal standards)).

- d) The FCA may exercise any of its enforcement powers against the firm including:

- (i) Varying or withdrawing permission (s 31 FSMA);

- (ii) Unlimited fines (s 206 FSMA);

- (iii) Issue public of private censure;

- (iv) Prohibit individuals from carrying on specified functions (s 56 FSMA).

Restitution may also be available (ss 382-386 FSMA) with individuals suffering loss of being able to proceed under a separate action for damages for breach of an FCA rule (s 138D FSMA).

The FCA may also consider applying for an injunction (s 380 FSA) or ultimately for the firm's insolvency (Part XXIV FSMA).

## B26. *(Compulsory)*

### Question

You work in the Far East for PB Private Bank which is a part of a large multinational financial services group (MSG) with your firm's client profile being principally ultra high net worth individuals and families.

Among this group of clients is the Chang family whose wealth derives from a highly successful manufacturing business. Five years ago, the founder of the business died leaving his 3 sons to run the business and with a financial interest each but with his wife holding a controlling financial interest although taking no part in the management.

Under the direction of the sons, the business has continued to prosper to the point at which they wish to expand significantly although this has produced tensions with regard to the way in which this could be achieved. Due to the close nature of their relationship with the Private Bank, the Changs have maintained their commercial banking interests within your group at MSG and have only taken advice on their financing discussions from MSG until now.

PB's relationship manager responsible for the Changs' non-commercial interests is JM who has been based in the Far East for many years. Since JM's wife is a family friend of the Changs, you believe that the relationship with the Changs is secure. Consequently you have every expectation that, whatever form of financing is deemed appropriate, the investment banking arm of MSG (MSGIB) will win the mandate for the new instructions.

In due course, you learn that the Changs have discussed the various options available with MSGIB who have suggested that they should consider raising the funds through a 'bought deal' with 60% of the shares being issued to outside professional investors.  This would reduce the Chang family's holding to 40% of the capital of the company, with Mrs Chang's interest being diluted to 20% overall. She is not happy with this proposal as she sees herself as the guardian of her late husband's legacy, over which she will then have little influence.

As Mrs Chang's agreement is essential to the new financing proceeding in the manner envisaged, MSGIB have asked that PB try to convince her to accept the proposal, explaining, among other things, that she will receive a large amount of money from the sale and will still own a substantial piece of the business. MSGIB analysts believe the business is likely to become even more successful following the external capital injection.

JM accordingly arranges a meeting with Mrs Chang to try to resolve the issue and afterwards advises you that Mrs Chang considers that the deal is designed solely to enrich her sons and the bank at the expense of the business. She has nevertheless suggested to JM that in light of the Changs' long friendship with JM's wife, the only way in which she would agree is if JM is made a non-executive director of the business to manage Mrs Chang's interests. JM informs you that he considers that this is a good idea, as he would then be able to look after Mrs Chang's interests at the same time as those of all of PB's other clients.

JM further believes from conversations with MSGIB, that they will expect PB's client managers to recommend investment in the issue to their clients or, where they are discretionary managers, to buy on their clients' behalf. JM also believes that the issue will be substantially sold to MSG Fund Management's Far East Fund. You are concerned about the potential conflicts of interest that may arise. While some of them may be resolved, you are not clear whether the full implications of these potential conflicts have been fully considered and the potential damage that this may cause to the bank's reputation especially in the private client area.

a) Explain the various conflicts of interest that may arise with regard to Mrs Chang, JM, MSGIB, PB or elsewhere. _(16 marks)_

- b) How should Private Bank and MSG deal with the proposal? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The fact pattern raised difficult issues of conflicts of interest and ethical and reputational risk. As PB is based in the Far East, it is unclear whether the FCA rules will strictly apply. MSG and MSGIB may nevertheless be based in London with PB either being a branch or subsidiary operation of a London regulated institution. PB, MSG and MSGIB may also adhere to the CISI Code of Conduct or equivalent provisions in the Far East. The question can accordingly be answered by applying equivalent relevant principles with or without specific reference to the FCA or CISI provisions.

a) The questions asks for the relevant conflicts of interest in connection with each party to be considered separately. The most comprehensive answers would have dealt with each set of relations individually although this was not strictly necessary.

## Mrs Chang

As Mrs Chang is not a regulated person, conflicts of interest and ethical standards do not strictly apply. JM, PB, MSGIB and MSG more generally must nevertheless consider relevant conflicts and ethical issues in their relations with Mrs Chang. Mrs Chang's relations with her three sons again do not raise any regulatory conflicts issue although their differences of opinion mean that they should at minimum be advised separately to take independent legal advice concerning their own interests in the company and proposed restructuring.

The other relevant issue to Mrs Chang personally is her status as a client and the degree of protection required. Mrs Chang would be classified as a retail client with an appropriate full client agreement having been entered into and with all other relevant provisions within COBS applying.

In terms of JM's relationship with Mrs Chang. As the client agreement only covers advice on non-commercial matters, JM cannot act in connection with the proposed restructuring without amendment which will, in turn, depend upon JM's relevant experience and expertise. JM should have considered referring this to a separate department or relationship manager at an early stage. JM's wife's close relation with Mrs Chang is not strictly relevant from a regulatory perspective although JM should have referred this to compliance and ensured that this does not interfere with his professional ability or judgement. JM must, of course, also be careful not to use his wife's relationship with Mrs Chang directly or to pass information back to Mrs Chang through his wife. In light of the close nature of these relations and size of the transaction, JM may again have elected to disqualify himself from acting at an early stage either on grounds of conflict or ethics and have the matter referred to a separate relationship manager.

MSG Group

Strict Chinese walls must be maintained between each of the divisions and separate entities within the MSG group. The restructuring more generally and Mrs Chang's interest in the restructuring may be passed on to a separate division within PB and/or MSGIB provided that full separation is respected. Necessary security arrangements must be put in place to ensure that any sensitive information remains confidential. Appropriate security arrangements must also be maintained between PB and MSGIB This may include documenting any contact between employees and information transfers. The compliance departments within each operation should be notified appropriately.

## MSGIB

The introduction by PB of Mrs Chang (and possibly her sons) to MSGIB should also have been properly documented with written consent taken to pass on names, identities and other file information.

MSGIB must properly identify and document its relationship with its client which will be the company in this case. The sons should already have been advised to take separate advice on their personal interests to the extent relevant. MSGIB must properly identify its client as a professional party and carry out all necessary pre-service actions including agreeing appropriate terms of business and disclosing all relevant costs, charges and other payments.

The recommendation of the 'bought deal' does not raise any direct regulatory issues provided that the MSGIB staff consider that this is the most appropriate recommendation in the particular circumstances. This advice would, of course, normally include other options with comment on their relevant merits and demerits. MSGIB will be required to act in the interests of the company as a whole as its client with each of the other parties concerned taking their own independent legal and financial advice on the merits of the transaction.

MSGIB must not attempt to place any pressure in any way on PB or JM to 'convince' Mrs Chang or any other party to support the restructuring proposed. This would be contrary to PRIN, SYSC, COCON, FIT and CISI. The severity of the breach would require immediate notification to the FCA to the extent relevant or other local authority (below).

Investment Analysts

Investment analysts working within MSGIB corporate finance will provide documentation in support of their recommendation. Any other analyst within MSGIB or MSG issuing independent advice must be separated by appropriate Chinese walls and comply with relevant regulatory provisions including, in particular, COBS 12 or the local equivalent standards.

## Client Managers

Chinese walls must also be maintained between MSGIB and PB client managers and discretionary portfolio managers. MSGIB must not attempt to influence their decisions with investment decisions being taken on an independent and client basis. This also applies with regard to MSG Fund Management.

Mrs Chang has requested that JM act as a non-executive director on the restructured company board to represent her interests. This would be wholly inappropriate. It would also be inappropriate for JM to attempt to act on behalf of other PB clients as suggested. As a nonexecutive director, JM would be required to act solely in the interests of the company under relevant company law provisions.

Information must not be passed between MSG companies or divisions in connection with the proposed restructuring and share offer which may otherwise constitute insider trading, market abuse or be contrary to Part 7 FSA 2012.

Insider lists may also be considered under the requirements in MAR.

- b) PB and MSG would have to deal with both the significant breaches that have already occurred as well as avoid any further infraction. No further action should be taken on the restructuring until all relevant matters have been dealt with. These would include the following:

- i. The issue must be referred to compliance within PB, MSGIB and MSG to the extent relevant;

- ii. JM should consider withdrawing immediately and passing responsibility on to dealing with Mrs Chang to another relationship manager within PB;

- iii. The terms of service for Mrs Chang should be confirmed and extended if necessary;

- iv. A separate relationship manager may be appointed to Mrs Chang to advise on commercial matters either within PB or independently;

- v. Mrs Chang's sons should be advised to take separate advice on their own positions;

- vi. JM must not be allowed to be appointed a non-executive director to the board of the restructured company;

- vii. Any independent research advice must comply with all relevant provisions;

- viii. PB client managers and discretionary managers must act independently;

- ix. MSG Fund Management must also act independently;

- x. As MSGIB is already conflicted, it may consider withdrawing its initial tender for the restructuring mandate;

- xi. Following discussion with the company and full disclosure, MSGIB may be given permission to re-tender and resubmit;

- xii. Strict Chinese walls must be maintained between all parties;

- xiii. Any transfers of information between the various divisions must be strictly controlled;

- xiv. In light of the inappropriateness of the action already taken by MSGIB staff (in trying to convince PB to persuade Mrs Chang and in expecting PB client managers and discretionary managers to purchase the new company stock), compliance may consider referring the matter to the FCA, CISI or any other appropriate regulatory authority with full details of the remedial action and proposed arrangements set up to ensure that no future breach of the conflict provisions arise;

- xv. To the extent that any pressure may be placed on you not to disclose any matters, relevant 'whistle-blowing' provisions may apply;

- xvi. Senior management within PB and MSG may consider writing to Mrs Chang and her sons explaining the regulatory issues concerned and the action taken in the interests of good client relations and to avoid any reputational damage;

- xvii. The board of PB, MSGIB and MSG may also consider withdrawing from the tender to avoid any reputational damage and to be seen to promote high ethical standards;

- xviii. Internal disciplinary action may be considered against the MSGIB staff involved with approaching PB;

- xix. Separate issues may arise with regard to the disclosure of short positions during a rights issue under FINMAR.

[Tutor's note: These provisions in FINMAR have since been superseded by the European Short Selling Regulation.]

## B29.

### Question

The investment management division of a major European investment bank is anxious to off-load a substantial amount of residual 'super-senior' debt attached to a series of US sub-prime related asset-back securitisations. A team within the bank's structured finance division had set up the programme although all of the former members of staff have since left or otherwise lost their positions. The price of the debt has recovered substantially since the crisis and the investment team is anxious to try to move it into the portfolios of certain wealthy individuals for whom the firm acts on a discretionary basis. The debt had been downgraded from AAA to BBB- with the investment team portfolio guidelines allowing up to 10% of the portfolio to be invested in BBB- securities provided they have longer term capital growth potential. You work in the compliance department and are asked to sign-off on the proposed asset transfer.

- a) Comment briefly on any conflicts of interest issues that may arise in the circumstances. _(4 marks)_

- b) Explain the documentation requirements that would apply before any work could have been carried out on behalf of clients. _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline the main regulatory restrictions that would apply with regard to the management of the investment portfolios on behalf of individual clients. _(4 marks)_

- d) Advise on any rights of recovery that may be available in the event that the super-senior debt substantially decreases rather than increases in value. _(4 marks)_

- e) Comment on any separate rights the individual clients may have under the Financial Ombudsman Scheme or Financial Services Compensation Scheme. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question dealt with the conflicts of interest that may arise where an investment bank attempts to off-load distressed (toxic) structured finance debt through its discretionary investment management division. Difficult issues can arise where large investment banks act on behalf of large numbers of clients in different capacities as well as deal and trade on their own account. The question also raises a number of other issues with regard to the provision of investment management services and client redress and recovery.

- a) The specific conflict of interest that arises is concerned with the attempt by the firm to dispose of the residual tranche of debt in a structured re-securitisation of US sub-prime debt. This relates to the un-covered portion of the debt that is usually held by the originating firm and not sold on to typical institutional investors (super-senior debt). This either has to be insured or sold on separately. Super-senior debt may not generally attract a high credit rating.

As this is not specifically held on behalf of another client, the conflict is between the firm and its portfolio management clients rather than between the sub-prime debt holders and investment clients.

Conflicts of interest are now dealt with under SYSC 10. Specific types of conflicts are referred to in SYSC 10.1.4R with firms being required to establish, implement and maintain an effective conflicts of interests policy (SYSC 10.1.10R) which deals with specific matters (SYSC 10.1.11R). Appropriate records of conflicts must be maintained (SYSC 10.1.6R). Disclosure of conflicts is dealt with under SYSC 10.1.8R and Chinese walls under SYSC 10.2.

Candidates should also have referred to the implications of breach of the conflicts of interest requirements including with regard to possible recovery by clients under s138D FSMA and regulatory action by the FCA (above).

- b) Documentation requirements generally depend on client classification although a client agreement will be required in this case as investment management or discretionary investment management services are provided. Managing investments constitutes designated investment business under the FSMA and FCA Handbook with client agreements being dealt with under COBS 8.1. Other documentation requirements relate to firm, service and fee information, conflicts of interest policy, complaints policy and designated investments holdings.

- c) Apart from client agreements and suitability reports ((b) above), relevant provisions with regard to dealing and management are imposed under COBS 11. These include best execution (COBS 11.2), client order handling (COBS 11.3), client limit orders (COBS 11.4), record keeping (COBS 11.5), dealing commission (COBS 11.6), personal account dealing (COBS 11.7) and recording (COBS 11.8). Reference may have been made to the investment research requirements (COBS 12) and client reporting (COBS 14). Additional protections also apply with regard to client money, securities and collateral under CASS.

- d) Where the question refers to recovery, clients would generally initially consider proceeding under the firm's complaints procedures and then Ombudsman. Damages may be available for breach of Handbook rules under s138D FSMA with restitution also being available by the court (s382 FSMA) or by the FCA directly (s384 FSMA), having served relevant warning and decision notice (ss385 and 386 FSMA).

- e) Candidates should have reviewed the separate payment entitlements under the Ombudsman (£350,000) and Financial Services Compensation Scheme. Relevant procedures may also have been referred to.

## B31.

### Question

The fixed interest desk of a large Asian investment house in London has over committed itself in southern European euro government debt. It has become increasingly concerned about possible rescheduling talks following the announcement of additional financial support for another large European country.

The head of the team contacts your group who work in the area of discretionary portfolio management and asks if you can pass on some of their holdings on to your institutional investor clients. They only wish to transfer around 20% of their euro bond portfolio to stay within internal risk management limits and there are no limits on the amount of government debt that you can hold within your discretionary portfolios. There has also been no confirmed announcement of rescheduling or default on the bonds held yet although clearly this may occur in the future. You are asked on the following.

- a) Explain the initial procedures and documentation arrangements that you would already have put in place to carry on business for your clients. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline briefly any potential conflicts of interest that may arise in this case.

   - _(4 marks)_

- c) Explain what action you, as an approved person, propose to take to resolve the conflicts, to avoid any potential liability for the firm or you personally. _(4 marks)_

- d) One of the members of the fixed interest desk asks you separately whether there was any action that could be taken to support the price of the debt held until it could be disposed of separately. Explain the regulatory implications of taking any such action. _(4 marks)_

- _e)_ Outline the remedies available to your institutional clients in the event that they suffer loss following a forced rescheduling of the government debt positions transferred into their portfolios. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raises a number of issues with regard to possible sovereign debt restructuring within the Eurozone area and possible implications on portfolio management within firms.

- a) General client categorisation and documentation requirements would apply, although with additional provisions on portfolio management. Reference may also have been made to relevant reporting and record requirements (COBS 16) and client asset rules (CASS).  Separate reference may also have been made to specific COBS obligations including Dealing and Managing (under COBS 11) and, in particular, best execution.

- b) It is arguable that no strict conflicts of interest arise insofar as the purchase of a small amount of government debt is within the portfolio investment limit. The value of the bonds may also remain high if additional financial support is provided. There is nevertheless a potential risk that the bonds may default with a conflict of interest arising between the fixed interest desk and the management team and the clients' interests. Relevant provisions under PRIN, SYSC and COCON should have been referred to. Separate issues with regard to investment research under COBS 12 may have been relevant although insufficient information is provided. Even if it is arguable that no strict conflicts arise, ethical issues arise under relevant FCA and CISI provisions.

- c) Firms must be able to identify all relevant conflicts of interest, maintain an effective written conflicts of interest policy for their resolution and keep separate up-to-date records of all past and present conflict situations. Relevant requirements and procedures under SYSC 10 should have been reviewed, including through disclosure and consent, Chinese walls, or declining to act. An independence policy would be of no use once a conflict has arisen.

- d) Price support is only permitted under the Price Stabilisation rules within MAR. Any other attempt to interfere with market prices would constitute market manipulation under Part 7 FSA 2012 or possibly market abuse under MAR. Relevant conditions, defences and penalties should have been reviewed.

- e) Clients would be able to make complaints to the firm and to the Financial Ombudsman Service under DISP or claim for compensation in the event of firm failure under COMP (with relevant investment limits being specified). Injunctions and restitution may be available (Part XXV FSMA). Damages may also be available under ss71 and 138D FSMA.

## B34.

### Question

One of the lead managers in a major asset management firm is concerned with the poor performance of his fund over the past year. He has just placed a deposit for the purchase of an expensive new apartment in a central new development within the City of London. He is purchasing the apartment 'off plan' and will move in next year after his 'Christmas bonus'. The manager concludes that the Eurozone will not be able to withstand a possible default by a large southern European country and decides that he will 'short sell' the euro using all of the freely available funds in his investment portfolios which is managed on a discretionary basis. You work in the compliance, division of the firm and are informed about this by another colleague over a coffee break.

- a) Explain the initial procedures and documentation requirements that would have been complied with in setting up the portfolios under management. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline briefly any potential conflicts of interest issues that may arise in this case. _(4 marks)_

- c) Explain any relevant restrictions that may apply with regard to the proposed short selling. _(4 marks)_

- d) Identify any other more general regulatory breaches that may be involved and the action you would take to prevent any firm liability arising. _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline any remedies available to the firm's institutional clients in the event that they suffer loss following that short selling on the portfolio. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised possible compliance issues with regard to short selling activities in anticipation of a possible collapse in the euro.

- a) Relevant issues arise with regard to client classification (COBS 3), communications (COBS 4 and 5), client agreements and disclosures (COBS 6, 8 and 14) as well as possibly suitability (COBS 9) and appropriateness (COBS 10).

- b) The manager has an immediate conflict of interest between his own personal desire to receive his bonus and the clients for whom he acts. This also creates a conflict between the manager and the firm. There is also a conflict between the colleague who becomes aware of the activity and advises you and the manager involved. Conflicts are dealt with SYSC 10 and PRIN 6 and 8.

- c) Short selling rules were introduced following the global financial crisis with the (then) FSA's short selling ban continuing until January 2009. Relevant provisions are now set out in FINMAR 2 concerning disclosable short positions of 0.25% or more in the issued capital of a company with ongoing disclosures having to be made through a Regulated Information Service (RIS) by 3.30 on the following business day where the position moves above or below 0.25%, 0.35%, 0.45% and 0.55% and other 0.1% movements. The short selling rules strictly only apply where the manager was taking positions in securities rather than currencies directly.

[Tutor's note: These provisions in FINMAR have subsequently been superseded by the European Short Selling Regulation.]

- d) A number of other more general breaches may have occurred of PRIN, SYSC and COCON in addition to the more specific provisions referred in subsection a) above. Reference may also have been made to the reporting requirements (COBS 16) and Client Assets Rules (CASS). The manager may also be liable for market abuse under MAR or Part 7 FSA 2012.

- e) Client remedies would depend upon classification. Institutional clients would have limited protections including, in particular, in respect of compensation under COMP or complaints and financial awards under the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS). Private persons may have an action in damages for breach of any rule under s138D FSMA although this would generally exclude institutional clients. Restitution orders may nevertheless be available under s382 FSMA with specific orders in relation to market abuse available under s383 FSMA.

Other more general remedies would also be available in a court of law. They could only otherwise rely on the FCA's exercise of its other enforcement powers such as through fines or public or private censure although this would not benefit them directly.
