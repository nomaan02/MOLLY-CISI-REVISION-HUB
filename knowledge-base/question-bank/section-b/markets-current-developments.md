## B23.

### Question

One of your firm's clients wishes to raise new funds on the London Stock Exchange (LSE) through a primary offering of company equity. The client is concerned it may need to prepare a prospectus for this activity to communicate to potential investors the details of this fund raising. You have been asked by your firm's management to prepare a short memorandum to answer a range of questions posed by the client, as to what is required when preparing a prospectus.

a) Outline if the firm needs to provide a prospectus. In your answer detail what criteria would justify the development of a prospectus. _(5 marks)_

b) Discuss what information and content the prospectus should contain. _(5 marks)_

c) What are the proposed changes to the rules on issue of prospectuses made within the UK Listing Review? _(5 marks)_

d) Are the changes proposed in the UK Listing Review justified? _(5 marks)_

### Answer

a) The guidance for providing a prospectus is outlined within the FCA Prospectus Regulation Rules sourcebook. This process has also been influenced by the HM Treasury Prospectuses Regime Review. The regulation surrounding the issue of a prospectus requires that a prospectus is provided when any transferable securities are issued to the public or are to be admitted to a regulated exchange. This applies to offerings of €1m or more in the UK or €8m under EU directives. There are exceptions, for example for nontransferable shares, for cases where a private placement of shares is made on certain exchanges such as AIM, for bonuses or payments to directors etc., for dividends paid out to existing shareholders in the form of shares, for shares issued in replacement of other equity removed, for financial instruments with a maturity of less than one year, for EU member states that may not require a prospectus for an offering up to €8m, if the offering is not public and made to qualified investors, if the offering involves non-equity securities offered in a continuous and repeated manner, and for a unit trust or an open ended investment company. There are also other premium listing requirements over prospectuses that can be usefully raised and discussed. Consideration of these points or other valid points is important.

b) It is essential a prospectus allows a potential investor to make an informed decision as to whether to invest or not invest in the financial offering. This would require any prospectus to include details as to the risks, profitability and potential future cash flows arising from the financial offering. In the case of company equity, for example, this would include assets and liabilities, profits and debts and prospects of the firm.

To ensure an investor can make an informed decision this information needs to be provided in clear English and a comprehensible manner suitable for who may be investing in the financial offering. Throughout, the document needs to be accurate and not misleading. Key elements of any prospectus are the summary of the offering, containing key information required and the nature of the risks of the offering and information as to the issuer and the securities being offered. The prospectus also needs to contain a registration document including information as to the securities issued and the issuer. Lastly a securities note needs to be issued including detailed information about the securities offered. In addition, there is other associated documentation which needs to be developed and provided as part of the prospectus development process. This would include the application form of potential investors in the offering, documents referenced in the prospectus and checklists cross-referencing the prospectus to its requirements and contact details for questions an investor would pose. The documentation needs to be submitted to the FCA. These documents can take 10 to 20 days to be approved by the FCA. There are also other premium listing requirements regarding prospectuses that can be usefully raised and discussed.

c) The UK Listings Review is a review for possible future changes to the regulation surrounding the perceived competitiveness of UK financial markets and addressing the issue of raising of corporate finance in the City of London and for UK businesses. Key areas of development listed in the review include the following (The Listing Review, p.13):

• changing prospectus requirements so that in future, admission to a regulated market and offers to the public are treated separately.

• changing how the prospectus exemption thresholds function so that documentation is only required where it is appropriate for the type of transaction being undertaken and suits the circumstances of capital issuance.

• use of alternative listing documentation where appropriate and possible, e.g., in the event of further issuance by an existing listed issuer on a regulated market.

There are also other amendments as to the provision of past information and what projections of estimated future cash flow might be and how prospectuses for dual listings are considered.

d) There are many perspectives which may be considered when discussing this major change to the prospectus rules. As part of this wide-ranging discussion are a number of changes to the rules for prospectuses. It is proposed that the current regulations for retail investments are too onerous and need to be altered and simplified considerably. Part of this discussion incorporates the view that a downside of the prospectus approach is that it draws together differing regulatory objectives. That of the issue of securities to markets and the public issue and trading of securities and that these regulatory properties may not always be consistent. This is a viewpoint arising from a particular opinion as to how we view regulation i.e., where less regulation is preferred to more state control and supervision over the private sector. These neoliberal views have been disputed in many other nations but persist in the UK. Overall, the question probes an opinionated area in which serious commentators are split. Any coherent discussion really following whatever perspective the learner considers right is fine: what we would like to see is an informed and considered discussion.

## B48.

### Question

One of your firm's clients wants to list on the London Stock Exchange through a primary offering of company equity. This has been in the pipeline for a couple of years, but due to significant changes in the client's business has been delayed.

Your firm has previously provided the client with various pieces of information, but due to the time lapse and changes in the client's management team, the client would like an update. You have been asked by your firm's management to prepare a short memorandum to answer a range of questions posed by the client.

- a) Provide a summary of changes to the new listing rules for the London Stock Exchange, which came into effect on 29 July 2024. _(4 marks)_

- b) What are the criteria for the development of a prospectus? _(8 marks)_

- c) What information and content should be included in the prospectus? _(8 marks)_

### Answer

a) As of 29 July 2024, significant changes have been made to the listing rules for the London Stock Exchange (LSE) aimed at enhancing the flexibility and attractiveness of the UK's capital markets. Key changes include:

- The previous distinction between the Premium and Standard listing segments has been removed. There is now a single listing segment that streamlines the requirements and focuses on high corporate governance standards without the need for dual requirements.

- The minimum free float requirement has been reduced from 25% to 10%. This change is designed to make it easier for companies to list by allowing them to retain a larger portion of their equity.

- The eligibility criteria for listing have been simplified, with a focus on ensuring companies have sufficient working capital and governance structures in place rather than meeting complex financial thresholds. This includes the removal of the requirement for a three-year track record for eligibility.

- The new rules allow for dual-class share structures (DCSS) for a period of up to 10 years, which can enable founders or major shareholders to retain control of the company even after it is publicly listed.

- There are increased disclosure requirements around environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors, reflecting the growing importance of sustainability in investment decisions. Companies are now expected to provide more detailed information on their ESG practices and impacts.

These changes aim to attract more companies to the LSE by reducing the complexity of the listing process while maintaining high standards of transparency and corporate governance.

b) A prospectus is the document prepared in connection with an application for admission of securities to trading on a UK regulated market or an offer of securities to the public in the UK pursuant to the Prospectus Rules. The prospectus serves as the primary source of information for investors, enabling them to make informed decisions regarding their investments. A prospectus is mandatory when a company proposes to make a public offer of securities. A public offer is defined as any communication to the public, in any form, that provides sufficient information on the terms of the offer and the securities to be offered, allowing an investor to decide to purchase or subscribe to those securities. This applies whether the offer is made to retail or institutional investors, ensuring that all potential investors receive adequate information. A prospectus is also required when securities are to be admitted to trading on a regulated market like the LSE. The prospectus must be approved by the FCA before the securities can be admitted to trading. The purpose of the prospectus in this context is to ensure that investors are fully informed about the company's business, financial position, and the risks associated with the securities being listed.

There are specific exemptions where a prospectus may not be required. For instance, offers made exclusively to qualified investors, such as financial institutions or large corporations, are exempt from the prospectus requirement. This is because these investors are deemed capable of assessing the risks associated with the investment without the need for the detailed information typically provided in a prospectus. Offers with a total consideration of less than €8 million over a 12-month period are also exempt from the prospectus requirement. This threshold is intended to reduce the regulatory burden on smaller companies seeking to raise capital without compromising investor protection. Offers made to fewer than 150 persons per EEA state (excluding qualified investors) also qualify for exemption. This allowance facilitates more streamlined fundraising processes for private companies or limited-scale offerings. The prospectus must comply with the content and format requirements set out in the UK Prospectus Regulation. This includes presenting information in a structured and consistent manner, covering key areas such as the company's business model, financial statements, risk factors, and details of the offering. The prospectus should be written in clear, concise language, avoiding jargon and ensuring that it is accessible to a wide range of investors. The FCA expects the prospectus to be comprehensive yet digestible, providing investors with all the necessary information to make an informed decision. Before the prospectus can be published, it must be submitted to and approved by the FCA. The approval process involves a thorough review to ensure that the document meets all regulatory requirements and provides a true and fair view of the company and the securities being offered. Companies and their directors are legally accountable for the accuracy and completeness of the information contained in the prospectus. Any material misstatements or omissions can lead to significant legal consequences, including fines, sanctions, and potential civil liability from investors who suffer losses as a result of relying on the misleading information.

c) The contents of the prospectus are governed by the UK Prospectus Regulation and must be clear, comprehensive, and presented in a manner that is easily understandable by investors. The prospectus should begin with a thorough description of the company's business, including its history, core activities, principal products or services, and markets served. This section should provide an overview of the company's operational structure, including any subsidiaries, and its position within the industry. Information about the competitive environment in which the company operates, including key competitors and the company's market share, should be included. This helps investors understand the company's strategic positioning and the external factors that may impact its business. The risk factors section must outline all the significant risks associated with the company's business, industry, and the securities being offered. This includes operational risks, financial risks, market risks, legal and regulatory risks, and any other factors that could materially affect the company's performance or the value of the securities.

The FCA requires that risk factors are presented prominently and in a way that reflects their importance. Risks should be described clearly and concisely, without downplaying their potential impact. Investors should be able to easily understand the nature and magnitude of the risks involved. The prospectus must include audited financial statements covering at least the last three financial years, or since the company's incorporation if it has been operating for less than three years. These statements should provide a comprehensive view of the company's financial health, including its balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement. The prospectus should provide management's analysis of the company's financial performance over the reporting period. It should include a discussion of significant trends, events, or uncertainties that have affected or are expected to affect the company's financial condition and results of operations. If the company has undergone significant transactions, such as mergers or acquisitions, or if it is planning to do so, pro forma financial information may be required. This information shows how the company's financial statements would have looked if the transaction had occurred at an earlier date. The prospectus must provide detailed information about the company's directors and senior management, including their qualifications, experience, and roles within the company. This section should also disclose any potential conflicts of interest and the mechanisms in place to manage them. The prospectus should include information on the company's governance framework, including its board committees, internal controls, and policies on executive remuneration, risk management, and ethical conduct. The prospectus must clearly outline the terms and conditions of the securities offering, including the type and number of securities being offered, the offer price, and the offer timetable. The company must explain how it intends to use the funds raised from the offering. This could include specific projects, debt repayment, capital expenditures, or general corporate purposes. The prospectus should include information about any regulatory approvals that the company requires or has obtained in connection with the offering or its business operations. This might involve licensing, environmental permits, or compliance with industryspecific regulations. Any ongoing or pending legal proceedings that could have a material impact on the company's financial position or operations must be disclosed.

Given the increasing importance of ESG factors in investment decisions, the prospectus should include information on the company's environmental, social, and governance practices. This might cover areas such as sustainability initiatives, workforce diversity, community engagement, and corporate responsibility. The prospectus should also address how ESG factors impact the company's business and what strategies it has in place to manage ESG-related risks and capitalise on opportunities. This information is increasingly critical for investors who are focused on sustainable and responsible investing. The prospectus should explain the company's dividend policy, including its historical dividend payments (if applicable) and future dividend plans. Investors need to understand how the company intends to return value to shareholders. Information on the company's capital structure, including its current indebtedness and any plans to raise additional capital, should be provided. This helps investors assess the company's financial leverage and its ability to service debt. For companies listing securities, the prospectus should include information about the market where the securities will be traded, including details on the trading currency, market practices, and any special considerations for investors.

## B51.

### Question

The investment bank in which you work has just taken a short lease on a small singleroom office in premises immediately beside the London Stock Exchange (LSE). You are advised that the purpose is to host a server that will be connected to the various LSE markets, including the Main Market, the Professional Securities Market (PSM), the Specialist Fund Market (SFM), Alternative Investment Market (AIM) and Turquoise electronic platform. The server will only engage in high-frequency trading (HFT) carrying out trades within fractions of seconds. The bank's IT department has advised that the server has to be as physically close to the LSE servers as possible, in order to obtain maximum market advantage, in terms of speed of message delivery and receipt (referred to as 'colocation'). The bank's Irish Government Bond desk asks you whether it can use the new HFT facilities to unload a backlog of two year government debt that it cannot otherwise sell. You are asked to advise on the following:

- a) Outline the principal statutory offence that will apply in attempting to use the HFT facilities to increase the price of two year debt stating any defences and relevant penalties. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline any separate statutory civil prohibitions that may apply to the action proposed and state any defences and relevant penalties. _(4 marks)_

- c) Identify any further restrictions that may apply if the Irish Government Bond desk decides to act in this way having just received information from a contact within the Government who had confirmed that the Irish debt position will be worse than expected the following year. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain any separate issues that may arise if it transpires that the only potential purchaser of the two year debt, using the HFT facilities, is another client of the firm who has paid for separate trading access, using the same HFT server, software and connections. _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline any separate action that you may take in dealing with the FCA in connection with the HFT generally (under a)-c) above) and client host trading (under d) above). _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised issues with regard to the use of a high frequency trading (HFT) facility to dispose of otherwise unsellable government debt. New provisions were introduced under MiFID II on HFT and algorithmic trading. This specifically requires authorisation of proprietary trading through HFT facilities, market making arrangements, order to trade (message) ratios, tick (volume) sizes and venue pricing to ensure transparent, fair and nondiscriminatory fees. Rather than raise regulatory issues directly, the question was concerned with different forms of market manipulation.

- a) The principal potential offences are concerned with misleading statements and impressions (ss89-90 Part 7 FSA 2012). These apply with regard to the making of false or misleading statements or creating a false or misleading impression as to the market, price or value of relevant investments. Penalties are provided under s92 with up to ten years imprisonment or a fine or both with specific defences included under ss89 and 90. As much detail as possible should have been provided in the time available.

[Tutor's Note: The penalties have increased to ten years under FSA 2021]

- b) The FCA may impose penalties on market abuse under MAR including with regard to manipulating transactions, manipulating devices, dissemination and misleading behaviour and market distortion.

- c) Acting on the information received may amount to dealing on inside information contrary to MAR or insider dealing under the Criminal Justice Act 1993. Relevant defences and penalties could have been provided although not specifically referred to.

- d) Disposing of the debt through the HFT facilities which another client has paid for separate access to may constitute a conflict of interest contrary to PRIN 8 and SYSC 10. Firms are required to identify all conflicts of interest between the firm and its clients or between clients under SYSC 10.1.3R with guidance on the types of conflicts covered provided in SYSC 10.1.4R. Firms have to keep a record of conflicts (SYSC 10.1.6R) and maintain and operate effective organisational and administrative arrangements to manage conflicts (SYSC 10.1.7R) or disclose the conflicts to the client (SYSC 10.1.8R) with a separate conflicts of interest policy also being produced (SYSC 10.1.10R). Chinese walls are provided for although these are not relevant in this case (SYSC 10.2).

e) Regulated firms are required to deal with regulators in an open and cooperative manner and disclose anything that the regulator would reasonably expect notice of under PRIN 11 and PRA FR7. The investment bank could specifically consider:

- (i) Undertaking an internal review of the request made by the bond desk;

- (ii) Considering any appropriate disciplinary action;

- (iii) Reviewing compliance arrangements, in particular, to ensure that the firm maintains appropriate systems to prevent market manipulation, market abuse and insider trading or other benchmark offences;

- (iv) Reviewing reporting arrangements;

- (v) Reviewing whistleblowing procedures;

- (vi) Reviewing training under TC;

- (vii) Reviewing whether the client suffered any loss and make appropriate disclosure and compensation as necessary;

- (viii) Providing a full report on all action taken to the FCA.

## B59.

### Question

A small start-up financial technology (FinTech) firm (operating beside Old Street Tube Station on City Road) in London wishes to offer a new digital application (app) on mobile telephones and approaches you to ask whether any regulatory issues may arise and how these might be dealt with. The app will provide access to a new digital platform which invites customers to open 'Digital Trading Accounts' with the firm, with the funds being invested through a specialist algorithm that the team has developed. Customers will receive all of the profits generated (less a fixed 'digital trading fee') and bear any losses (with the fee still being paid in all cases). The platform will also offer a 'RoboAdvice' service using the team's new audio IT speaking programme and trading algorithm. A third full 'Digital Discretionary Trading Service' will be made available for wealthy clients. The platform will also operate a full set of payment services and may wish to offer separate 'Digital Deposit Accounts' later with customers having automatic credit facilities on these accounts. You have been informed that the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) considers applications by such FinTech companies for authorisation and permission through its special 'Project Innovate' division. Before the firm decides on whether to approach the FCA or not, you are asked to advise on the following:

- a) Outline the general regulatory requirements that may apply. _(5 marks)_

- b) Explain the separate permissions that may have to be applied for, depending upon whether the firm wishes to provide some or all of its proposed services. _(5 marks)_

- c) Explain the principal regulatory agencies that may be involved in the UK and their general functions. _(5 marks)_

- d) Outline the application procedure that would have to be complied with. _(5 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to the establishment of a new financial technology (FinTech) firm that would presumably operate through a mobile platform out of London. The company intends to undertake a number of specific activities and may consider subsequent expansion across Europe and into the US or Asia. The FCA has created a dedicated section on Fintech and innovative businesses on its new website (https://www.the-fca.org.uk/firms/fintech-and-innovative-businesses). This specifically explains the nature and operation of Project Innovate and the new Innovation Hub and Regulatory Sandbox which are intended to support the development of innovative financial products and services in the UK.

- a) All of the principal statutory provisions contained the FSMA as amended would apply. This includes the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA) and restrictions on promotion (s21 FSMA). The firm would have to apply for Part 4A permission (s40 FSMA) to become authorised (s31(1)(a) FSMA) and comply with the threshold conditions imposed (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended and COND). Relevant personnel would have to be approved persons (s59 FSMA) and comply with the suitability requirements imposed under FIT and conduct requirements in COCON. The firm would be subject to the rules and guidance imposed by the FCA and PRA (ss137A, 137G and 139A FSMA including the FCA PRIN, PRA Fundamental Rules and all other relevant provisions).

- b) The firm would have to apply for permission in each of its proposed principal areas of activity. Regulated activities are defined in s22 FSMA and the Regulated Activities Order 2001 (as amended) and the FSMA 2000 (PRA Regulated Activities) Order 2013. The 2013 Order divides activities between the FCA and PRA by specifying that anything not specifically assigned to the PRA under the Order is reserved to the FCA. Permissible activities overseen by the FCA will include dealing in investments, arranging deals in investments, managing investments and providing investment advice. Deposits and deposit taking would fall within the PRA RAO and consumer credit and mortgage services (arranging home finance transactions) within the RAO (as amended by the FSMA 2000 (Regulated Activities) (Amendment) (No 2) Order 2013) which transferred consumer credit regulation from the Office of Fair Trading to the FCA.

Payment activities would be regulated under the new payment systems regime set up under Payment Services Regulations (PSRs), and Part 5 of the Financial Services (Banking Reform) Act 2013. The FSA produced an approach document in April 2009 which was updated in June 2013 (FCA, the FCA's role under the Payment Services Regulations 2009 - Our Approach).

Any dealing in foreign exchange is not a regulated activity under the FSMA as such although foreign exchange dealing as principal is governed by the Bank of England's Non-Investment Products (NIP) Code (April 2009).

The bank would be dual regulated by the PRA for regulatory purposes and the FCA in respect of conduct. The bank would apply for Part 4A permission (s40 FSMA) to become an authorised person (s31(1)(a) FSMA). Compliance with the revised threshold conditions would be confirmed (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended by the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Threshold Conditions) Order 2013 (SI 2013/555) and COND). The threshold conditions generally apply with regard to office location, effective supervision, appropriate resources and suitability as well as business model condition (COND 2.7 and para 2F Schedule 6 FSMA.) Conditions may be imposed under s 137O FSMA with guidance provided under the Perimeter Guidance Manual (PERG).

Reference may also have been made to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), Financial Service Compensation Scheme (FSCS), the Complaints Scheme and Complaints Commissioner as well as the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), Competition Commission and the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England although this was not strictly necessary.

- c) Relevant application forms are available on the PRA and FCA websites with full and restricted options and applications considered within six months if complete and 12 months if incomplete. Separate PDF documents are available on Banking Authorisation Process (January 2014) and Banking Authorisation Process – Applicant Journey (June 2013). The application forms generally cover core details, business plan, scope or permission, financial resources, recovery and resolution, human resources, compliance, internal audit and policy, infrastructure, regulatory reporting, fees and levies, and fee, checklist and declaration. The PRA and FCA cooperate in the application process.

- d) Passport rights only apply in relation to the provision of financial services on a services or establishment basis within the EU. The firm would not be able to carry on financial services in any EU country unless it applies for local (EU) authorisation. For business in the US or Asia, the extent to which the firm would have to make a full new original application would partly depend upon the relationship between the separate third country and either the UK or the EU depending upon whether the new services were to be carried on from London or the EU country (assuming the firm gains the EU licence).

## B66.

### Question

A new start-up company based in Old Street in London has been developing a computer programme to provide 'Robo advice' services on portfolio management to online clients. It has been working on the software for a number of years and is now ready to go to market. It has applied to the FCA to participate in its Project Innovate and regulatory sandbox to ensure that it develops the necessary internal staffing and control systems and ensures full compliance with all relevant regulatory obligations. The company is waiting to hear whether it has been accepted into the latest group of companies which will participate in the most recent Project Innovate round. The company has approached you to provide outline initial advice as to its expected compliance obligations.

a) Explain the principal issues that arise from a regulatory perspective in determining whether or not the company will require authorisation and permission under the FSMA. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline the core obligations that will have to be complied with under section/s 19, 20 and 21 FSMA. _(4 marks)_

- c) Explain the penalties that may be imposed for breach of these requirements. _(4 marks)_

- d) Outline the specific issues that arise with regard to whether the Robo advisory programme will itself constitute a regulated activity. _(4 marks)_

- e) Explain the additional action that the company would have to take if is wishes to expand the range of financial activities that it wishes to carry on through its new online facilities. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to the establishment of a new 'Robo advisory' service in London. Robo advice constitutes part of the range of new financial technology (FinTech) platforms under development at this time. The firm has been placed within the FCA regulatory sandbox as part of its Project Innovate initiative (https://www.thefca.org.uk/firms/fintech-and-innovative-businesses).

- a) The FCA published a Financial Advice Market Review (FAMR) in March 2016 jointly with the Treasury which examined the quality of financial services advice and the 'advice gap' in the UK with specific recommendations being made on affordability, accessibility and liability and consumer redress (https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/corporate/famr-final-report.pdf).

Recommendation 2 of the FAMR specifically called for the definition of regulated advice based on personal recommendation following MiFID with other initiatives being adopted to assist the provision of 'streamlined' advice subject to appropriate safeguards. The FAMR also considered non-advice services, fact-finding processes and employer and trustee fact sheets (Recommendations 3, 10 and 11). A Financial Advice Working Group (FAWG) was set up following the review (Recommendation 1).

The FCA set up a separate specialist Advice Unit (FAMR Recommendation 9) to assist firms with regulatory feedback on developing automated models for advice delivery. This includes the provision of individual responses and other tools and resources. The Unit initially focused on investments, pensions (accumulation and decumulation) and protection with this to be extended to mortgages, general insurance and debt sectors.

- b) All of the principal statutory provisions contained the FSMA as amended would initially apply. This includes the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA) and restrictions on promotion (s21 FSMA). The firm would have to apply for Part IV permission (s40 FSMA) to become authorised (s31(1)(a) FSMA) and comply with the threshold conditions imposed (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended and COND). Relevant personnel would have to be approved persons (s59 FSMA), or subject to the new senior managers and certification regimes, and comply with the suitability requirements imposed under FIT and conduct requirements in COCON. The firm would be subject to the rules and guidance imposed by the FCA and PRA (ss137A, 137G and 139A FSMA including the FCA PRIN, PRA Fundamental Rules and all other relevant provisions).

The firm would have to apply for permission in each of its proposed principal areas of activity. Regulated activities are defined in s22 FSMA and the Regulated Activities Order 2001 (as amended) and the FSMA 2000 (PRA Regulated Activities) Order 2013. The 2013 Order divides activities between the FCA and PRA by specifying that anything not specifically assigned to the PRA under the Order is reserved to the FCA. Permissible activities overseen by the FCA will include inter alia providing investment advice (RAO art 53) as well as managing investments (RAO art 37), dealing in investments (RAO arts 14 and 21) and arranging deals in investments (RAO art 25).

- c) The full range of regulatory penalties may be available against the firm including variation of permission (s45 FSMA), suspension of permission (s206a FSMA), cancellation of Part 4 permission (s54 FSMA), withdrawal of authorisation (s33 FSMA), public censure (s205 FSMA), financial penalties (s206 FSMA), requests for information (s165 FSMA and Schedule 12 FSA 2012), independent inquiry (s68 FSA 2012), administration, receivership, winding-up or bankruptcy (Part 24 FSMA, Banking Act 2009 and Schedule 14 FSA 2012), injunctions (s380 FSMA), restitution (ss382 and 384 FSMA), suspension of financial instrument from trading if appropriate (s36 FSA 2012), complaint by a consumer body (super-complaint under s234c FSMA and s43 FSA 2012), regulated person reference (s234d FSMA), OFT investigation (s234h FSMA), action under product intervention rules (s137d FSMA 2000 and s24 FSA 2012) and skilled persons report (s166 FSMA).

- d) Financial guidance rather than advice would not generally be regulated. HM Treasury published a consultation paper and response on Amending the Definition of Financial Advice in September 2016 and February 2017. Advice would only be regulated where the firm made a personal recommendation. An exemption would be provided to art 53(1) where no personal recommendation was made. This applied the definition under MiFID although the existing provisions are retained in respect of the boundary between regulated and unregulated firms. Regulated firms are to be able to provide more advanced guidance services without these constituting regulated advice with unregulated firms being regulated where they advise on investments. The changes would come into effect from 3 January 2018.

The FCA issued a Guidance consultation on Streamlined Advice in April 2017 (GC 17/4). This distinguishes full advice and streamlined advice. Streamlined advice is limited to one or more of the client's specific needs and does not examine circumstances not directly relevant to those needs. This includes simplified advice (with the firm setting out the boundaries) and focused advice (with the client specifying the boundaries). Streamlined advice will include Robo advice and automated services as well as face to face advice.

Firms are required to obtain necessary information on the client's knowledge and experience, financial situation and investment objectives under the fact finding process. Fact finding processes would be simplified and made more portable. Additional guidance is provided on generic advice that would not be covered. Employees can also be provided with information by employers and trustees through fact sheets.

- e) The company would have to apply for permission to cover any new regulated activities. ### Question

As an external consultant, you have been asked to advise the Board of a fund management firm about the threats and opportunities posed by financial technology to their business. The firm sells its products solely to retail investors in the UK via telephone dealing, its website and platforms. All back-office functions are performed in-house.

- a) Describe the components and developments of financial technology that may be relevant to the retail fund management sector. _(5 marks)_

- b) Assess the potential of these to disrupt the firm's current business model. _(5 marks)_

- c) Describe the FCAs initiatives to support financial technology innovation within the regulatory regime. _(5 marks)_

- d) Assess the challenges that financial technology developments pose to the FCAs statutory objectives. _(5 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to the effects on a fund management firm in the UK of developments in the Financial Technology (FinTech) area. The firm provides retail services principally through telephone dealing, website and platform relations. The FCA has set up a dedicated section on Fintech and innovative businesses on its new website (https://www.the-fca.org.uk/firms/fintech-and-innovative-businesses). This specifically explains the nature and operation of Project Innovate including the new Innovation Hub and Regulatory Sandbox which are intended to support the development of innovative financial products and services in the UK.

a) Financial technology could impact on the firm's activities in a number of ways. These include the following:

**Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) or Blockchain** – Distributed Ledger Technology is a system of replicated and synchronised digital records geographically spread over multiple sites, countries or institutions. There is no centralised administrator or centralised data storage. One form of distributed ledger design is the blockchain system, which can be either public or private. A blockchain is a continuously growing list of records called blocks, which are linked and secured using cryptography. Each computer linked to the network, ("node") receives a copy of each block as it is created. Each block contains an encrypted reference to the previous block called a hash. Once recorded, the data in any given block cannot be altered retrospectively without the alteration of all subsequent blocks, which requires collusion of the network majority. The blockchain provides an open, distributed record of transactions or other information shared between two or more parties which is efficient and verifiable.

**Robo-Advice or Automated Advice** – Robo-advice comprises financial advice provided with limited or zero human intervention by software applications that combine digital user interfaces and decision-making algorithms. Such advisors may be standalone firms or platforms or can be the in-house applications of mainstream financial institutions. A typical robo-advisor collects information from clients about their financial situation, investment goals and risk tolerance, and then uses the data to offer advice and/or automatically invest client funds.

**Chatbots** – Chatbots use artificial intelligence to simulate a human conversation. They employ a technique called natural language processing to interpret human inputs (voice or text) within a defined context and respond accordingly. Chatbots can be used to answer customer queries and to action customer trade execution orders.

**Crowdfunding** – Crowdfunding is a method of raising finance to fund a business, project or venture, by asking a large number of people for a small amount each. Traditionally, financing has involved asking a few people for large sums. Crowdfunding uses the internet to contact thousands of potential investors typically using social media and then directing investors to a website set up for the purpose.

**Artificial Intelligence** – Artificial intelligence is the theory and development of computer systems which can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence. These tasks include natural language processing, visual perception, speech recognition, decision making and translation between languages. This is supported by processes including machine learning (the automated acquisition of information and the inference of rules for using that information), and the application of algorithms to reach approximate or definite conclusions.

**Analytics or "Big Data"** – Big data refers to very large volumes of structured or unstructured data that can be analysed, or mined, to identify patterns or trends in, for example, customer behaviour or market activity. This data can be collected from a range of sources including news reports, comments on social networks, personal electronic devices and applications, questionnaires, and financial data feeds.

**Cloud Computing** – Cloud computing is the delivery of IT services from the internet via web-based tools and applications, rather than using a direct connection to an in-house server. Information is kept in a remote database accessed through the internet, rather than a local hard drive, and can be accessed from any location. Software-based services can be accessed and charged for on an on-demand basis rather than being purchased or licenced.

**RegTech** – RegTech (regulatory technology) describes software applications used for regulatory reporting and compliance purposes by financial institutions. The term also refers to firms that offer such applications. RegTech offers firms the ability to deliver accurate regulatory information to regulators at reduced cost based on the consistent application of standardised reporting requirements.

**SupTech** – SupTech (supervisory technology) is the use of innovative tools and systems by financial regulators to improve their supervision of regulated individuals, firms and markets and/or to reduce costs.

b) The firm's business model may be disrupted by their adopting, or their competitors adopting, FinTech services which potentially offer the following:

**Improved Marketing** – The use of big data processing can be used to identify appropriate new customers or information about existing customers can be used to improve products and their delivery. The use of social media, and particularly links with existing tech firms such as Amazon and Facebook, using the information they hold on their customers, can be used to target sales and marketing messages at the appropriate individuals.

**Enhanced Fund Management** – The use of big data processing can provide additional information about the performance and activities of investment targets which may improve fund performance. One example being the analysis of satellite photographs of retail stores' carparks to assess their trade volumes. Artificial intelligence can be used to manage trading either in the automated selection of investment opportunities based on a defined strategy, or in managing the timing and size of trades to achieve the best price.

**Innovative Distribution** – The use of mobile phone apps, perhaps combined with access to consumers' bank accounts under Open Banking, may replace the existing retail distribution platforms that the firm currently relies on. Existing, or emerging internet firms, such as Amazon and Google, may enter the market as distribution channels leveraging their large customer base and the detailed information they hold on them. Such entrants could take significant market share away from traditional distribution channels.

**More Efficient Back Office Processing** – The use of blockchain links between investors, fund managers, banks, brokers and custodians could reduce costs and errors in back office processing and potentially reduce the risk of fraud. Chatbots could be used to respond to customer queries and requests more cheaply than human support staff.

**Improved Customer Service** – The use of robo-advisors to suggest investment strategies to customers based on analysis of their financial position, objectives and risk tolerance, could provide the benefits of a tailored wealth management service without the costs associated with traditional human service.

c) Relevant FCAs initiatives to support FinTech innovation within the regulatory regime include the following:

**Project Innovate** – The FCA launched Project Innovate in October 2014 to help encourage and support innovation in the financial services industry. This work is described as central to delivering against the FCA's competition objective – it aims to encourage innovation in the interests of consumers, and to promote competition through disruptive innovation that offers new services to consumers and challenges existing business models. The FCA uses Project Innovate to establish constructive engagement with innovative firms, from small start-ups to mass market new business models, and seeks to remove unnecessary barriers to innovation. The project helps innovative firms gain access to timely and frank feedback on the regulatory implications of their concepts, plans and choices. It also seeks to tackle the structural issues that impede the progress of innovators entering the market.

**The Innovation Hub** – A central component of Project Innovate is the Innovation Hub which provides support to new and established businesses (both regulated and unregulated) who seek to launch innovative products and services into the market. There are no restrictions on the areas of financial services covered by the Innovation Hub: firms innovating in mortgages, capital markets and pensions can all access the Hub for help in introducing the products to the market. The Innovation Hub also performs a horizon-scanning role by identifying new technologies and areas where the regulatory framework needs to adapt to enable further innovation in the interests of consumers. It provides an end-to-end experience for new entrants. Firms that receive initial support from the Hub have their applications for authorisation handled by a specialised authorisation process. The FCA provides firms with dedicated supervisory support, usually for one year post-authorisation, which gives firms a seamless regulatory experience and minimises the risk of unnecessary delay.

**International Engagement** – Through engaging with international firms and overseas regulators, Project Innovate supports the FCA's competition objective by promoting the UK as a centre for innovation in financial services. It does this by: facilitating the entry of innovative overseas firms into the UK, thereby increasing innovation and competition in the UK financial services markets, and facilitating the expansion of UK-based innovative firms into overseas markets, potentially making them more sustainable challengers in the UK. As at May 2018, the FCA has signed Co-operation agreements and frameworks with regulators in Australia, Singapore, Hong Kong, Canada, China, Japan, US and Korea.

**Regulatory Sandbox** – Launched in May 2016, this allows firms to test innovative products, services, business models and delivery mechanisms in the real market, with real consumers without incurring all the normal regulatory consequences of engaging in those activities. The Sandbox provides firms with: the ability to test products and services in a controlled environment; reduced time to market at potentially lower cost; support in identifying the appropriate consumer protection safeguards to build into new products and services.

**The FCA Advice Unit** – The FCA Advice Unit provides regulatory feedback to firms who are developing automated advice models for consumers. This could include: an initial meeting with the Advice Unit, and other specialized areas of the FCA if needed, to discuss the proposition; for firms requesting ongoing engagement, a dedicated point of contact in the Advice Unit to provide regulatory input at agreed milestones; specific feedback on the regulatory implications of the model – this could include follow-up meetings with relevant FCA staff to discuss specific issues, informal steers and individual guidance as appropriate; advice on how to apply for authorization if the firm is currently unauthorized. The advice and feedback provided will focus on helping the firm to understand the regulatory implications of their model.

d) The challenges that FinTech developments could create to the FCA's statutory objectives include the following:

**Impact on the Consumer Protection Objective** – Poorly designed robo-advice algorithms, or their use without adequate Know-Your-Customer information gathering, may result in large numbers of customers receiving inappropriate advice or being sold inappropriate products or services. The use of cryptographic and/or biometric identification may improve the security of financial transactions and reduce the opportunities for fraud. Consumers dealing with firms over the internet may unwittingly be transacting with unregulated firms or be taken in by fraudsters.

**Impact on the Integrity Objective** – RegTech has the potential to increase the efficiency and effectiveness of market supervision by providing regulators with near real time data access and automated regulatory reporting. Big data has the potential to improve supervisors' monitoring of regulated individuals and firms through insights gained from automated analysis of data, for example through the application of natural language processing to the analysis of news reports, annual reports and accounts, broker research, market trade data and press releases. Unregulated third party service providers providing critical services to large numbers of financial institutions may pose systemic risk to the financial system. They may also have access to large volumes of client and market data which could be used to support market abuse activities.

**Impact on the Competition Objective** – Smaller firms may be disadvantaged relative to larger firms which have bigger technology budgets, access to in-house technical expertise and who can realise efficiencies through economies of scale. This could result in reduced competition and the market domination of a few large players. Alternatively, the entry of new players competing with incumbent fund managers and distribution channels could result in lower costs for customers and reduce the systemic risk associated with the dominance of large investment houses and distribution platforms.

These tasks include natural language processing, visual perception, speech recognition, decision making and translation between languages. This is supported by processes including machine learning (the automated acquisition of information and the inference of rules for using that information), and the application of algorithms to reach approximate or definite conclusions.

**(vi) Analytics or "Big Data"** – Big data refers to very large volumes of structured or unstructured data that can be analysed, or mined, to identify patterns or trends in, for example, customer behaviour or market activity. This data can be collected from a range of sources including news reports, comments on social networks, personal electronic devices and applications, questionnaires, and financial data feeds.

**(vii) Cloud Computing** – Cloud computing is the delivery of IT services from the internet via web-based tools and applications, rather than using a direct connection to an in-house server. Information is kept in a remote database accessed through the internet, rather than a local hard drive, and can be accessed from any location. Software-based services can be accessed and charged for on an on-demand basis rather than being purchased or licenced.

**(viii) RegTech** – RegTech (regulatory technology) describes software applications used for regulatory reporting and compliance purposes by financial institutions. The term also refers to firms that offer such applications. RegTech offers firms the ability to deliver accurate regulatory information to regulators at reduced cost based on the consistent application of standardised reporting requirements.

**(ix) SupTech** – SupTech (supervisory technology) is the use of innovative tools and systems by financial regulators to improve their supervision of regulated individuals, firms and markets and/or to reduce costs.

(b) The firm's business model may be disrupted by their adopting, or their competitors adopting, FinTech services which potentially offer the following:

**(i) Improved Marketing** – The use of big data processing can be used to identify appropriate new customers or information about existing customers can be used to improve products and their delivery. The use of social media, and particularly links with existing tech firms such as Amazon and Facebook, using the information they hold on their customers, can be used to target sales and marketing messages at the appropriate individuals.

**(ii) Enhanced Fund Management** – The use of big data processing can provide additional information about the performance and activities of investment targets which may improve fund performance. One example being the analysis of satellite photographs of retail stores' carparks to assess their trade volumes. Artificial intelligence can be used to manage trading either in the automated selection of investment opportunities based on a defined strategy, or in managing the timing and size of trades to achieve the best price.

**(iii) Innovative Distribution** – The use of mobile phone apps, perhaps combined with access to consumers' bank accounts under Open Banking, may replace the existing retail distribution platforms that the firm currently relies on. Existing, or emerging internet firms, such as Amazon and Google, may enter the market as distribution channels leveraging their large customer base and the detailed information they hold on them. Such entrants could take significant market share away from traditional distribution channels.

**(iv) More Efficient Back Office Processing** – The use of blockchain links between investors, fund managers, banks, brokers and custodians could reduce costs and errors in back office processing and potentially reduce the risk of fraud. Chatbots could be used to respond to customer queries and requests more cheaply than human support staff.

**(v) Improved Customer Service** – The use of robo-advisors to suggest investment strategies to customers based on analysis of their financial position, objectives and risk tolerance, could provide the benefits of a tailored wealth management service without the costs associated with traditional human service.

(c) Relevant FCAs initiatives to support FinTech innovation within the regulatory regime include the following:

**(i) Project Innovate** – The FCA launched Project Innovate in October 2014 to help encourage and support innovation in the financial services industry. This work is described as central to delivering against the FCA's competition objective – it aims to encourage innovation in the interests of consumers, and to promote competition through disruptive innovation that offers new services to consumers and challenges existing business models. The FCA uses Project Innovate to establish constructive engagement with innovative firms, from small start-ups to mass market new business models, and seeks to remove unnecessary barriers to innovation. The project helps innovative firms gain access to timely and frank feedback on the regulatory implications of their concepts, plans and choices. It also seeks to tackle the structural issues that impede the progress of innovators entering the market.

**(ii) The Innovation Hub** – A central component of Project Innovate is the Innovation Hub which provides support to new and established businesses (both regulated and unregulated) who seek to launch innovative products and services into the market. There are no restrictions on the areas of financial services covered by the Innovation Hub: firms innovating in mortgages, capital markets and pensions can all access the Hub for help in introducing the products to the market. The Innovation Hub also performs a horizon-scanning role by identifying new technologies and areas where the regulatory framework needs to adapt to enable further innovation in the interests of consumers. It provides an end-to-end experience for new entrants. Firms that receive initial support from the Hub have their applications for authorisation handled by a specialised authorisation process. The FCA provides firms with dedicated supervisory support, usually for one year post-authorisation, which gives firms a seamless regulatory experience and minimises the risk of unnecessary delay.

**(iii) International Engagement** – Through engaging with international firms and overseas regulators, Project Innovate supports the FCA's competition objective by promoting the UK as a centre for innovation in financial services. It does this by: facilitating the entry of innovative overseas firms into the UK, thereby increasing innovation and competition in the UK financial services markets, and facilitating the expansion of UK-based innovative firms into overseas markets, potentially making them more sustainable challengers in the UK. As at May 2018, the FCA has signed Co-operation agreements and frameworks with regulators in Australia, Singapore, Hong Kong, Canada, China, Japan, US and Korea.

**(iv) Regulatory Sandbox** – Launched in May 2016, this allows firms to test innovative products, services, business models and delivery mechanisms in the real market, with real consumers without incurring all the normal regulatory consequences of engaging in those activities. The Sandbox provides firms with: the ability to test products and services in a controlled environment; reduced time to market at potentially lower cost; support in identifying the appropriate consumer protection safeguards to build into new products and services.

**(v) The FCA Advice Unit** – The FCA Advice Unit provides regulatory feedback to firms who are developing automated advice models for consumers. This could include: an initial meeting with the Advice Unit, and other specialized areas of the FCA if needed, to discuss the proposition; for firms requesting ongoing engagement, a dedicated point of contact in the Advice Unit to provide regulatory input at agreed milestones; specific feedback on the regulatory implications of the model – this could include follow-up meetings with relevant FCA staff to discuss specific issues, informal steers and individual guidance as appropriate; advice on how to apply for authorization if the firm is currently unauthorized. The advice and feedback provided will focus on helping the firm to understand the regulatory implications of their model.

(d) The challenges that FinTech developments could create to the FCA's statutory objectives include the following:

**(i) Impact on the Consumer Protection Objective** – Poorly designed robo-advice algorithms, or their use without adequate Know-Your-Customer information gathering, may result in large numbers of customers receiving inappropriate advice or being sold inappropriate products or services. The use of cryptographic and/or biometric identification may improve the security of financial transactions and reduce the opportunities for fraud. Consumers dealing with firms over the internet may unwittingly be transacting with unregulated firms or be taken in by fraudsters.

**(ii) Impact on the Integrity Objective** – RegTech has the potential to increase the efficiency and effectiveness of market supervision by providing regulators with near real time data access and automated regulatory reporting. Big data has the potential to improve supervisors' monitoring of regulated individuals and firms through insights gained from automated analysis of data, for example through the application of natural language processing to the analysis of news reports, annual reports and accounts, broker research, market trade data and press releases. Unregulated third party service providers providing critical services to large numbers of financial institutions may pose systemic risk to the financial system. They may also have access to large volumes of client and market data which could be used to support market abuse activities.

**(iii) Impact on the Competition Objective** – Smaller firms may be disadvantaged relative to larger firms which have bigger technology budgets, access to in-house technical expertise and who can realise efficiencies through economies of scale. This could result in reduced competition and the market domination of a few large players. Alternatively, the entry of new players competing with incumbent fund managers and distribution channels could result in lower costs for customers and reduce the systemic risk associated with the dominance of large investment houses and distribution platforms.

(e) The crisis has had a specific impact on the FinTech and RegTech markets. While these have principally provided substantial value, some difficulties have arisen.

FinTech and RegTech use and applications have strengthened across all sectors and all functions as well as in specific areas of extension and with specific advances in RegTech tools and wider infrastructure technology (InfraTech) and applied technology (AppliedTech). FinTech and RegTech benefit from their inherent natural technology advantages and especially in terms of decentralisation, disintermediation, speed, low cost and efficiency.

Work conditions are also inherently more favourable with remote working practices, remote conference, meeting and collaborative project and file sharing skills, substantially low relative staff numbers, low fixed costs including on property and IT and business development and response flexibility.

Many Digital Coin projects are well funded through earlier Initial Coin Offerings (ICOs) with many Blockchain applications being retooled and reused to respond to post-crisis needs and conditions such as with regard to online identification, financial statement verification, financial history validation, remote business and complex project management especially with Blockchain solutions, remote payment, algorithmic and AI tied risk and investment management, robotic enquiries, advice and complaints handling.

Other non-ICO related start up and small and medium-sized businesses may nevertheless suffer from a shortfall in liquidity and investment during and immediately following the crisis with some countries providing support in this area such as in Singapore.

More generally, however, the crisis has confirmed the very substantial advantages of technology in modern business and social environments and uses with financial related aspects and FinTech and RegTech forming a significant part of this and of any future recovery.

## B85.

### Question

A young Financial Technology (FinTech) platform has been developing its business plan and model operations from a shared WeWork office space in Old Street in London. It has developed a new set of integrated computer algorithms with exceptional speed and low latency, which can replicate the functions of a major investment bank including carrying out dealing, arranging deals and investment management (on a discretionary or nondiscretionary basis). This may also be extended to provide investment advisory services (and bots) later. The platform has been operating within a FinTech incubator maintained by a larger UK commercial bank, although it now wishes to make an application to enter the FCA Regulatory Sandbox. It is anxious to learn as quickly as possible about the various regulatory obligations that may be imposed on its activities to prepare its Sandbox application.

a) Explain the extent to which the various activities to be carried out by the platform would need to be regulated in the UK. _(4 marks)_

b) Explain the specific provisions that would apply with regard to algorithmic trading. _(4 marks)_

c) Outline the principal statutory restrictions that will apply if the platform wishes to carry on regulated activities in the UK. _(4 marks)_

d) Outline the enforcement provisions that would apply in the event of a breach of any of the applicable regulatory obligations. _(4 marks)_

e) Explain the procedure that would apply if the platform wished to extend the range of activities covered over time. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised numerous issues with regard to the control of algorithmic trading and other regulated activities under the FSMA. The digital platform has developed a number of possibly highly lucrative new programmes and is considering entering the FCA Regulatory Sandbox.

**(a)** Explain the extent to which the various activities to be carried out by the platform would need to be regulated in the UK. **(4 marks)**

The platform would have to be authorised under s 19 FSMA and hold relevant permission under 20 FSMA to the extent that it carried on a regulated activity in the UK. Regulated activities are defined in s22 FSMA, the Regulated Activities Order 2001 (as amended) and the FSMA 2000 (PRA Regulated Activities) Order 2013. The 2013 Order divides activities between the FCA and PRA by specifying that anything not specifically assigned to the PRA under the Order is reserved to the FCA. Permissible activities overseen by the FCA would include inter alia managing investments (RAO art 37), dealing in investments (RAO art 21) and arranging deals in investments (RAO art 25). This would also include sending dematerialised instructions (RAO art 45).

The RAO distinguishes between specified investments and specified activities. Specified investments include: investments; deposits; contracts for insurance; shares; instruments creating or acknowledging indebtedness; government and public securities; instruments giving entitlement to investments; certificates representing certain securities; units in a collective investment scheme; rights under a stakeholder pension scheme; options; futures; contracts for differences; Lloyd's syndicate capacity and membership; funeral plan contracts; regulated mortgage contracts and rights to or interest in investments (RAO arts 73-89).

**(b)** Explain the specific provisions that would apply with regard to algorithmic trading. **(4 marks)**

Algorithmic trading is defined in the FCA Glossary as trading in financial instruments with a computer algorithm automatically determining individual parameters of orders (including whether to initiate the order, the timing, price and quantity of order or how to manage the order after submission) with limited or no human intervention, excluding systems only used for routing orders to one or more trading venues and simple processing, confirmation or post-trade execution activities. Authorisation and permission would be required to the extent that the activities of the platform involve the activities referred to above (including investment management, arranging and dealing and sending dematerialised instructions) in relation to the investments as defined. The FCA published a report on Algorithmic Trading in Wholesale Markets (12 February 2018) with the PRA issuing a Consultation Paper on Algorithmic Trading (12 Feb 2018). The FCA document reviews activities and themes with the PRA paper outlining governance and risk management expectations in relation to algorithmic trading. A number of recommendations are made with regard to management approval of suitable governance frameworks, sound approval processes for algorithms, algorithm and risk control testing, algorithm and risk control inventory maintenance and effective oversight of algorithmic control functions and risk management.

**(c)** Outline the principal statutory restrictions that will apply if the platform wishes to carry on regulated activities in the UK. **(4 marks)**

All of the principal statutory provisions contained in the FSMA as amended would apply to the specific regulated activities involved. This includes the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA) and restrictions on promotion (s21 FSMA). The firm would have to apply for Part IV permission (s40 FSMA) to become authorised (s31(1)(a) FSMA) and comply with the threshold conditions imposed (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended and COND). Relevant personnel would have to be approved persons (s59 FSMA), or subject to the new senior managers and certification regimes, and comply with the suitability requirements imposed under FIT and conduct requirements in COCON. The platform would be subject to the rules and guidance imposed by the FCA and PRA (ss137A, 137G and 139A FSMA including the FCA, PRIN and all other relevant provisions).

**(d)** Outline the enforcement provisions that would apply in the event of a breach of any of the applicable regulatory obligations. **(4 marks)**

The full range of regulatory penalties may be available against the firm including variation of permission (s45 FSMA), suspension of permission (s206a FSMA), cancellation of Part 4 permission (s54 FSMA), withdrawal of authorisation (s33 FSMA), public censure (s205 FSMA), financial penalties (s206 FSMA), requests for information (s165 FSMA and Schedule 12 FSA 2012), independent inquiry (s68 FSA 2012), administration, receivership, winding-up or bankruptcy (Part 24 FSMA, Banking Act 2009 and Schedule 14 FSA 2012), injunctions (s380 FSMA), restitution (ss382 and 384 FSMA), suspension of financial instrument from trading if appropriate (s36 FSA 2012), complaint by a consumer body (super-complaint under s234c FSMA and s43 FSA 2012), regulated person reference (s234d FSMA), OFT investigation (s234h FSMA), action under product intervention rules (s137d FSMA 2000 and s24 FSA 2012) and skilled persons report (s166 FSMA).

**(e)** Explain the procedure that would apply if the platform wished to extend the range of activities covered over time. **(4 marks)**

The platform would have to apply to have the scope of permission extended to include any other additional activities and forms. This may include advising on investments (RAO art 53), safeguarding and administering investments (RAO art 40), establishing collective investment schemes (RAO art 52) and agreeing to carry on activities (RAO art 64) as well as possibly accepting deposits (RAO art 5), effecting and carrying out insurance contracts (RAO art 10), issuing regulated mortgage contracts (RAO art 61), funeral plan contracts (RAO art 59) and Lloyds-related activities (RAO art 56). These would apply in relation to any of the investments referred to above.
