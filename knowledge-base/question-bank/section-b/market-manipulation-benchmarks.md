## B37.

### Question

You are the compliance officer for a small boutique broking firm in Canary Wharf in London. The head of the UK Trading Desk approaches you concerning a transaction executed by one of his star brokers the previous day. The firm had a large position on an outstanding Structured Global Depositary Product entered into some years ago that had matured the day before. The product was tied to the share price of a South African mining company listed on the London Stock Exchange, with your firm losing £3 million if the closing share price was below £125 and realising a profit of £10 million if the share price was £125 or above. The star broker had entered the market 10 seconds before it closed and placed substantial buy orders, the effect of which was to move the price from £122.5 to £125.3 forcing the counterparty on the structured product to pay out the £10 million. The broker had also informed another broker within his team about the proposed strategy with the other broker being able to make a separate profit on the rise in the share price on the mining stock when the market opened on the following morning.

a) Explain the general legal and regulatory issues that arise with regard to the conduct of the star broker. _(4 marks)_

b) Identify the principal offences, penalties and defences that apply. _(4 marks)_

c) Outline any separate criminal law provisions that may apply with regard to the conduct of the second broker. _(4 marks)_

d) Explain the action that you would take on behalf of the firm to limit any enforcement or reputational damage. _(4 marks)_

e) Explain any separate action that you would recommend to the firm's board to avoid any similar breaches in future. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

This question raised a number of issues with regard to the conduct of a broker in a boutique firm in London who attempted to move the price of mining shares immediately before the market closed.

(a) The principal legal and regulatory issues that arose were with regard to possible market abuse in manipulating transactions (MAR) and market manipulation (Part 7 FSA 2012). The clear objective of the conduct was to move the price of the shares to avoid a loss and realise a profit on the Structured Global Depositary Product.

There may have been a separate issue of insider trading either under MAR or the Criminal Justice Act (CJA) in passing information on to the other broker.

A number of separate potential issues arise with breach of PRIN, SYSC and COCON.

(b) All of the relevant offences, defences and penalties applicable under MAR and Part 7 FSA 2012 should have been reviewed with regard to the initial broker.

(c) All of the relevant offences, defences and penalties should have been reviewed with regard to possible insider dealing with regard to the second broker. The first broker may also have been guilty of a range of behaviours that amount to market abuse. There may also have been a separate element of collusion.

- (d) Relevant remedial action may include:

   - (i) Conducting a full internal review of the conduct of both brokers;

   - (ii) Reviewing the involvement of any other members of staff;

   - (iii) Commencing internal disciplinary procedures as necessary;

   - (iv) Reviewing any possible client loss;

   - (v) Contacting any other clients affected with offers of possible compensation;

   - (vi) Liaising with any other relevant regulatory or criminal authorities involved in the UK or elsewhere;

   - (vii) Notifying the FCA.

- (e) Possible corrective action may include:

   - (i) Reviewing trading practices;

   - (ii) Reviewing trading limits;

   - (iii) Reviewing trading teams' oversight;

   - (iv) Reviewing relevant Systems and Controls;

   - (v) Reviewing risk management in the trading unit;

   - (vi) Reviewing disciplinary procedures;

   - (vii) Reviewing the compliance function and other relevant reporting lines within firm;

   - (viii) Reviewing the Approved Person arrangements;

   - (ix) Reviewing training and competence procedures.

## B41.

### Question

You work in the compliance department of a major UK commercial banking group. Senior management has become concerned with the Group's possible involvement with the London Interbank Offered Rate (LIBOR) and European Interbank Offered Rate (EURIBOR) scandal. You were asked to undertake some research into the activities of the Group staff responsible for LIBOR and EURIBOR submissions and, in particular, to confirm the content of e-mail exchanges within the Group and between the Group and other banks. You discover that traders within the Group had asked the rate setters to lower rate submissions both before and during the global financial crisis. You also find out that some traders have contacted rate setters at other banks to attempt to adjust the rates. You are asked to advise on the following:

- a) Outline the principal statutory offence that may apply with regard to attempting to manipulate the LIBOR and EURIBOR rates with any applicable defences and penalties. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline any separate statutory civil prohibition that may apply to the activities of the traders concerned and any defences and relevant penalties. _(4 marks)_

- c) Identify any rights of the FCA to obtain information and to take any interim or protective action in the circumstances. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain any separate regulatory breaches that may have been committed by the Group and the possible penalties that the FCA may impose. _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline the advice that you would give to the Board in managing its relations with the FCA in connection with this matter. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The fact pattern reflects the issues that have arisen with regard to a number of major international firms concerning the possible criminal and regulatory enforcement action in connection with manipulation of benchmark rates including specifically LIBOR and EURIBOR.

- a) The principal regulatory offence that arises is with regard to possible breach of Part 7 FSA 2012 concerning market manipulation. The relevant offences should have been referred to including dishonestly or recklessly making any statement that is false or misleading, or dishonestly concealing any material facts. It also applies where any person commits any act, or engages in any course of conduct which creates a false or misleading impression as to the market in, or the price or value of, any investment. Relevant defences and penalties should also have been referred to. These provisions would nevertheless generally not apply in the present case as they are only concerned with 'relevant investments' which would not include benchmark rates as such. Reference may also have been made to other possible offences in connection with common law or statutory fraud conspiracy.

[Tutor's note: Manipulation of benchmarks is now covered by new provisions in s.91 FSA 2012.]

- b) The most relevant civil provision would be market abuse and possibly either manipulating transactions or manipulating devices or dissemination. Relevant defences and penalties could have been referred to including relevant due diligence defences. This would nevertheless again not apply to the extent that it only covers 'qualifying investments' traded on prescribed markets.

- c) FCA information gathering and investigatory powers are dealt with under ss165176 FSMA. The FCA has power to require any authorised person, appointed representatives and certain other persons to provide it with relevant information, reports or other documents. The FCA may appoint an investigator possibly in collaboration with other agencies or overseas authorities. The FCA can require persons under investigation (and connected persons) to attend for questioning. The FCA can enter premises and obtain a court warrant where necessary. It is a criminal offence to falsify, conceal, destroy or dispose of any relevant document (s177 FSMA) subject to two years imprisonment or an unlimited fine. The FCA may also apply for an injunction to restrain or prohibit any person from breaking a rule or other requirement (s380 FSMA).

- d) A number of relevant provisions of PRIN and SYSC may have been breached. Specific requirements also apply with regard to group systems and controls under SYSC 12. Firms must, in particular, ensure that they have adequate, sound and appropriate risk management processes and internal control mechanisms for the purpose of assessing and managing its own exposure to group risk, including sound administrative and accounting procedures, and that ensure that the group has adequate, sound and appropriate risk management processes and internal control mechanisms at the group level (SYSC 12.1.8R). Specific provisions apply with regard to financial conglomerates and banks and investment firms under BIPRU with additional risk management requirements (SYSC 12.1.11-12, 13-15 and 18-22).

- e) The firm must make full disclosure to and co-operate fully with the FCA and all other relevant UK and overseas authorities. It should conduct its own internal reviews and investigations into possible breaches and internal systems and controls, governance, reporting and record keeping failures. It should implement any necessary internal changes with immediate effect. It should consider the recommendations of any external review reports, such as the Wheatley Review in September 2012, and make any relevant internal adjustments as necessary. It should suspend any staff involved and commence appropriate disciplinary action conditional upon any separate external action being taken. It should investigate the extent to which any specific customers may have suffered loss or injury and consider contacting them and making appropriate compensation offers, although this may again be dependent upon continuing external investigatory action.

## B47.

### Question

A boutique brokerage firm in Tower 42 in the City of London is a heavily active participant on the International Petroleum Exchange (ICE Futures) in London dealing in oil futures and options contracts specifically in Brent Crude oil. The firm has been on the panel that sets the exchange's benchmark interest rate for oil contracts on a daily basis for a number of years with estimated daily rates being provided by the firm's 'submitter'. These rates are collected from other firms on the market and averaged to generate the market's 'standard offered rate'. One of the oil traders in the firm, on a separate floor of the building from the submitter, emails the submitter and asks him to submit a high figure to allow him to close out on a very large deal that will earn a substantial amount of money for the firm and a large bonus for him at the end of the year. He promises to 'divvy' the bonus with the submitter. You work in compliance in the firm and become aware of the activities having overheard two other dealers discussing the transaction over coffee in the firm's common room.

- a) Outline the principal concerns that you would have immediately with regard to the approach by the trader to the submitter. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline the general principal statutory civil prohibition that may apply to the activities of the trader and submitter and any defences that may be available and penalties. _(4 marks)_

- c) Explain any separate more specific offences that may apply to the benchmark setting activities including relevant offences and penalties. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain any separate regulatory breaches that the firm may have committed and the penalties that the regulatory authorities may impose. _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline the advice that you would give to the firm in dealing with the authorities in connection with these events. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was based on the recent LIBOR offences uncovered in the UK, US and elsewhere. The question is concerned with oil contract rates although the same principles would apply.

- a) Concerns arise with regard to the following:

   - (i) Inappropriate requests made manipulate reported rates;

   - (ii) Commission of offence of making false or misleading statements by the submitter with regard to a relevant benchmark under s91 FSA 2012;

   - (iii) Commission of offence of creating a false or misleading impression by the oil trader as to the price or value of any investment contrary to s91 (2) FSA 2012;

   - (iv) Possible market abuse contrary to MAR;

   - (v) Possible separate misleading statements or impressions contrary to ss8990 FSA 2012;

   - (vi) Conflict of interest between the trader and submitter;

   - (vii) Conflict of interest between the firm and clients or other market counterparties affected by the manipulation of the benchmark rate;

   - (viii) Defective supervision;

   - (ix) Defective systems and controls;

   - (x) Breach of the Remuneration Code in SYSC 19A;

   - (xi) Possible breach of specific COBS;

   - (xii) Defective Chinese Walls between separate departments;

   - (xiii) Breach of a number of PRIN by the firm;

   - (xiv) Breach of a number of provisions of SYSC by the firm;

   - (xv) Breach of COCON by the trader, submitter and two other dealers;

   - (xvi) Weak compliance.

- b) The principal statutory prohibition would be on market abuse under MAR. This applies with regard to insider dealing, improper disclosure, breach of reasonable market behaviour, false or misleading impression, use of fictitious devices, dissemination of false information or other similar behaviour. The FCA may impose appropriate penalties. The courts may impose a penalty on the request of the FCA (s129 FSMA). Various statutory defences and safe harbours are available.

- c) New offences relating to the issuance of misleading statements in relation to benchmarks were created under s91 and Part 7 FSA 2012. Two offences are introduced in respect of a person making a statement in relation to the setting of a relevant benchmark knowing that the statement is false or misleading and a person who acts or engages in a course of conduct creating a false or misleading impression as to the price or value of an investment or the interest rate appropriate to the transaction which is relevant to the setting of the benchmark (s91 (1) and (2) FSA 2012).

Defences are available in relation to stabilisation rules, control of information rules and EU buy-back programmes and financial instrument stabilisation (s91 (4)). A person is liable on summary conviction to up to 12 months imprisonment or ten years on indictment or a fine or both.

[Tutor's Note: The penalties have been increased to ten years under FSA 2021.]

- d) Separate regulatory breaches may apply with regard to:

   - (i) Breach of PRIN 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9 and 11;

   - (ii) Breach of SYSC 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 18 and 19A;

   - (iii) Breach of COND 2, 4 and 5;

   - (iv) Breach of COCON 1, 2, 3 and 5 as well as possibly COCON senior manager rules with regard to the controlled function of setting benchmarks;

   - (v) Breach of FIT 1, 2 and 3.

- e) Relevant penalties include:

   - (i) Variation of Part 4A permission;

   - (ii) Withdrawal of authorisation;

   - (iii) Withdrawal of approval in respect of the trader, submitter and other dealers;

   - (iv) Public or private censure;

   - (v) Financial penalty;

   - (vi) Prohibition order;

   - (vii) Restitution order;

   - (viii) Investigation and information requests;

   - (ix) Approved persons reports.

- f) The firm should consider:

   - (i) Immediately suspend the trader and submitter;

   - (ii) Conduct an internal review of the immediate conduct;

   - (iii) Review any previous transactions carried out by the trader and submitter and other traders and submitters;

   - (iv) Review benchmark setting procedures;

   - (v) Review systems and controls;

   - (vi) Review Chinese Walls;

   - (vii) Review conflicts of interest, policy and controls

   - (viii) Review escalation procedures;

   - (ix) Review record-keeping procedures;

   - (x) Review wider compliance functions;

   - (xi) Confirm whether any clients or other counterparties suffered loss and offer appropriate compensation arrangements;

   - (xii) Notify and cooperate fully with the FCA and other regulatory and police authorities.

   - (xiii) Review general firm compliance with PRIN, SYSC, COND, COCON and FIT.

## B49.

### Question

A large UK commercial bank is heavily involved in the international foreign currency markets. The markets are now worth over $5.3 trillion per day with the bank generating substantial earnings through these activities. The bank participates in an official exchange rate setting scheme. You work in the compliance department and the head of the foreign exchange section asks for a meeting with you to discuss recent reported events in the newspapers. The head of foreign exchange is particularly concerned with the reported investigations into the alleged activities of foreign exchange traders in London and elsewhere and to the possible involvement of the foreign exchange desk at the local central bank. The foreign exchange desk at the bank regularly deals with other large international banks currently being investigated and with the central bank. The head of exchange is anxious to avoid any possible investigation and criminal or regulatory penalties. He is also keen to protect his bonus and the bonus pool for his team for the year. He has asked for your advice on a number of issues.

- a) Outline the principal statutory offences that may apply with regard to foreign exchange rate benchmark setting and relevant penalties. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline any more general statutory prohibitions that may apply in terms of market abuse or manipulation and relevant penalties. _(4 marks)_

- c) Explain any separate regulatory breaches that the firm may have to consider and the penalties that the regulatory authorities may impose. _(4 marks)_

- d) Outline the advice that you would give the head of foreign exchange and the firm to prevent any statutory or other regulatory penalties being imposed. _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline the advice that you would give the firm in dealing with the authorities in connection with these events. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The fact pattern was concerned with the compliance issues that may arise for firms involved with foreign exchange markets and exchange rate setting. This follows the LIBOR scandal and separate concerns with regards to possible gold market fixing. Revised provisions were introduced on misleading statements and impressions with regards to benchmark setting under the FSA 2012.

- a) The principal statutory provision on benchmarks is now set out in s91 FSA 2012. This creates two specific offences of making a false or misleading statement to another person in connection with benchmark setting arrangements or acting or engaging in a manner that creates a false or misleading impression as to the price or value of an investment or the most appropriate interest rate (s91(1) and (2) FSA 2012). Defences apply with regard to price stabilisation and control of information rules and buy-back programmes. A person found guilty of the offence is liable for up to ten years imprisonment or a fine on indictment. Reference may also have been made to the more general offences of making a misleading statement or impression under ss89 and 90 FSA 2012.

   - [Tutor's Note: The penalties have increased to ten years under FSA 2021.]

- b) Separate offences may have been committed under MAR on market abuse depending upon the particular circumstances. This may, in particular, have included such behaviour as improper disclosure, misuse of information, manipulating transactions, dissemination or misleading behaviour or distortion.

- c) A number of provisions of FCA PRIN (1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9 and 11), PRA FR, SYSC (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 18 and 19A), COCON (1, 2, 3 and 5 as well as possibly the senior manager rules), COND (2, 4 and 5) and FIT (1, 2 and 3) may have been breached with the full range of regulatory penalties applying. These would principally relate to integrity, systems and controls, organisation, compliance and well as the Remuneration Code in SYSC 19a.

- d) The head of foreign exchange and firm could consider:

   - (i) conduct of internal review into activities of the bank's foreign exchange traders;

   - (ii) review relations with traders and other firms under investigation to ensure no firm involvement;

   - (iii) consider possible disciplinary action with regard to any traders involved in any of the activities referred in (i) or (ii) above;

   - (iv) investigate any potential client losses with regard to any affected transaction;

   - (v) review benchmark setting procedures;

   - (vi) review systems and controls;

   - (vii) review conflicts of interest policy and control;

   - (viii) review recordkeeping procedures;

   - (ix) review wider compliance functions;

   - (x) review any relevant escalation procedure to the extent relevant;

   - (xi) review reporting lines and governors' functions.

- e) The firm is required to cooperate with the authorities under PRIN 11, PRA FR 7 and COCON 3. The firm may, in particular, consider:

   - (i) discussing any concerns with the regulators following the internal reviews conducted;

   - (ii) explaining all of the internal action carried out to the firm's principal line supervisor;

   - (iii) review board oversight of foreign exchange operations;

   - (iv) ensure that specific director has operational responsibility for the firm's foreign exchange operations:

   - (v) ensure that foreign exchange operations monitored by non-executive directors;

   - (vi) confirm internal training with regard to compliance with internal benchmark setting arrangements;

   - (vii) review internal whistle blowing procedures with regards to internal staff reporting;

   - (viii) consider reporting any instances of criminal activity or conduct contrary to regulatory provisions involving other firms to the regulators.

   - (ix) review remuneration policy in light of findings;

   - (x) review firm's ethics policy with regard to findings.

## B64.

### Question

A large UK commercial bank is a regular operator in the London inter-bank markets. You work in the compliance division and have been asked to review some old files that the Head of the bank's Treasury Department has become concerned about. These involve the bank's borrowing activities during the UK financial crisis in 2007-2009 and, in particular, with regard to a series of liquidity auctions that the bank participated in to raise funds during this period. The borrowing was used to support the bank's general deposit taking and lending as well as other investment activities. You learn separately that concerns have been raised that some banks may have provided incorrect information to the central bank with regard to their liquidity positions and the value of the collateral offered to support their borrowings at the time. The Head of the Treasury Department has asked you to keep this information confidential and for you only to report back to him on any issues that may arise following your review of the files.

- a) Outline the principal statutory offence that may apply with regard to the incorrect information provided to the central bank and any relevant defences and penalties. _(4 marks)_

- b) Explain any separate regulatory concerns that may arise. _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline any action that you may take in the event that the Head of the Treasury Department does not report any concerns that you raise and asks you not to discuss this with anyone else within the bank. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain any further remedies available to the regulatory authorities and any enforcement penalties that they may impose once they become aware of the issues involved. _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline any action that would you propose that the bank takes in response to the issues involved. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to inter-bank market activity and specifically the under reporting of positions during the UK financial crisis as part of Bank of England liquidity auctions. The Serious Fraud Office (SFO) in the UK opened an investigation into alleged malpractices in the auctions in March 2015. The Bank's Oversight Committee had earlier published a separate report by Lord Grabiner QC on the Bank of England Foreign Exchange Market Investigation (November 2014) covering foreign exchange activity between 2005 and 2013.

- a) Specific concerns arise with regard to possible market abuse having been carried out by the bank in the liquidity auctions. Market abuse was previously controlled under s 118 FSMA with this being repealed with the coming into effect of the EU Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) 596/2014 which contains specific directly applicable provisions in this regard. The MAR is implemented through European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) Guidelines and amendments to the FCA Handbook. The MAR came into effect on 3 July 2016 although the question could have been answered with regard to either FSMA or the MAR. Candidates should have referred to the general nature of the offences created and defences and penalties available. Reference may also have been made to the new offences on benchmarking imposed under a 91 of the Financial Services Act 2012 although this was possibly not strictly a benchmarking question.

- b) Separate regulatory concerns specifically arise with regard to PRIN 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 3 (proper management and control), 6 (act in the best interests of customers), 8 (avoid conflicts) and 11 (be open and corporative with the authorities) as well as PRA FR 1, 2, 3 (prudent manner), 5 (effective risk strategies and management systems), 6 (responsible and effective organisation and control) and 7. Concerns also arise with regard to breach of the CISI Code of Conduct.

- c) You have to consider escalating the matter within the firm. You should report to your immediate manager in or outside compliance failing which to the board or non-executive directors. If necessary, you could consider using the whistleblowing procedures provided for under the Public Interest Disclosure Act (PIDA) 1998 as amended by the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act (ERRA) 2013. You could also consider reporting under the CISI 'Speak Up' procedure (Prepare, Evaluate, Act and Keep trying).

- d) The authorities may consider imposing a full range of sanctions on the firm. These include imposing temporary restrictions on activities, permission restrictions, withdrawal of authorisation, public and private censure and fines.

- e) The bank has to act in an open and cooperative manner in dealing with the FCA and PRA (PRIN 11 and FR 7). The firm should make full disclosure of the internal report to the authorities with a full list of corrective action intended to prevent any similar occurrence in future. This may include taking disciplinary action against the original staff involved as well as the Head of Treasury Department for failing to take necessary action following the completion of your report and failing to disclose this to the authorities.

## B88.

### Question

A small boutique hedge fund in London, Parchegos, has been unable to meet some of its margin calls during the first quarter of 2021. It held a large number of investment positions through total return swaps (TRSs) with major investment banks in London and elsewhere which have decided to liquidate their positions. The authorities are concerned that allegations have been raised that the management team at Parchegos were engaged in market manipulation in supporting the share price of certain Asian stocks. Despite these efforts, the share prices in these stocks collapsed, which led to the increased margin demands which the firm was unable to cover. It is alleged that the market manipulation was carried out by the internal management team at the fund in collusion with two individuals working at the fund's prime brokerage firm in London.

a) Explain the principal statutory offences that may apply regarding the alleged market manipulation. _(4 marks)_

b) Explain any separate regulatory concerns that may arise. _(4 marks)_

c) One of the members of staff at the prime brokerage firm wishes to report details of the activities concerned to the authorities. Explain the options available. _(4 marks)_

d) Explain any further remedies available to the regulatory authorities and any enforcement penalties that they may impose once they become aware of the issues involved. _(4 marks)_

e) Outline any action you would propose the prime brokerage firm takes in response to the issues involved. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised several issues with regard to the activities of the managers of a London based hedge fund. The fact pattern very loosely reflected the circumstances surrounding the difficulties at the family firm, Archegos, in New York in March 2021 and the fine imposed on Corraddo Abbattista, the former chief investment officer, at the London hedge fund Fenician Capital Management in May 2017.

## a)

Specific concerns arise with regard to possible market abuse having been carried out by the management team Asian stocks. Market abuse was previously controlled under s 118 FSMA with this being repealed with the coming into effect of the EU Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) 596/2014 which contains specific directly applicable provisions in this regard. Offences consist of insider dealing and unlawful disclosure (art 14) and market manipulation (art 15) which include false or misleading signals, using fictitious devices or deception or contrivance or transmitting false or misleading information through the media or in relation to a benchmark (art 12). The MAR is implemented through European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) Guidelines and amendments to the FCA Handbook. The MAR came into effect on 3 July 2016 although the question could have been answered with regard to either FSMA or the MAR. Candidates should have referred to the general nature of the offences created and defences and penalties available.

While reference is made to dealings in Asian stocks, it is not clear where this was carried out although it is assumed that this was in London. Philippe Jabre was nevertheless fined £750,000 on 11 February 2003 in respect of activities carried out in Asia and outside the UK although the stock was also listed in London. Reference may have been made to the new offences on benchmarking imposed under a 91 of the Financial Services Act 2012 although this was possibly not strictly a benchmarking question.

## b)

Separate regulatory concerns specifically arise with regard to PRIN 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 3 (proper management and control), 6 (act in the best interests of customers), 8 (avoid conflicts) and 11 (be open and corporative with the authorities) as well as PRA FR 1, 2, 3 (prudent manner), 5 (effective risk strategies and management systems), 6 (responsible and effective organisation and control) and 7. SYSC (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 18 and 19A), COCON (1, 2, 3 and 5), COND (2, 4 and 5) and FIT (1, 2 and 3) may also be breached with the full range of regulatory penalties applying. These may relate to integrity, systems and controls, organisation, compliance and possibly the Remuneration Code in SYSC 19A. Concerns also arise with regards to breach of the CISI Code of Conduct to the extent that any parties involved are members.

## c)

The matter could be escalated within the firm. This should be reported to the immediate manager in or outside compliance failing which to the board or non-executive directors. If necessary, the individual could consider using the whistleblowing procedures provided for under the Public Interest Disclosure Act (PIDA) 1998 as amended by the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act (ERRA) 2013. A separate reporting system is also available under the CISI 'Speak Up' procedure (Prepare, Evaluate, Act and Keep trying).

## d)

The full range of regulatory sanctions may be available against the firm, in addition to the individual penalties referred to above, including variation of permission (s45 FSMA), suspension of permission (s206a FSMA), cancellation of Part 4 permission (s54 FSMA), withdrawal of authorisation (s33 FSMA), public censure (s205 FSMA), financial penalties (s206 FSMA), requests for information (s165 FSMA and Schedule 12 FSA 2012), independent inquiry (s68 FSA 2012), administration, receivership, winding-up or bankruptcy (Part 24 FSMA, Banking Act 2009 and Schedule 14 FSA 2012), injunctions (s380 FSMA), restitution (ss382 and 384 FSMA), suspension of financial instrument from trading (s36 FSA 2012), complaint by a consumer body (super-complaint under s234c FSMA and s43 FSA 2012), regulated person reference (s234d FSMA), OFT investigation (s234h FSMA), action under product intervention rules (s137d FSMA 2000 and s24 FSA 2012) and skilled persons report (s166 FSMA).

## e)

The firm must act in an open and co-operative manner in dealing with the FCA and PRA (PRIN 11 and FR 7). The firm should make full disclosure of the internal report to the authorities with a full list of corrective action intended to prevent any similar occurrence in future. This may include taking disciplinary action against all of the original staff involved. The firm could specifically consider:

(i) conducting an internal review into activities of the bank's hedge fund related activities.

(ii) reviewing relations with managers and other firms.

(iii) possible disciplinary action that could be applied in relation to any breaches.

(iv) investigating any potential client losses with regard to any affected transactions.

(v) reviewing benchmark setting procedures.

(vi) reviewing systems and controls.

(vii) reviewing conflicts of interest policy and controls.

(viii) reviewing recordkeeping procedures.

(ix) reviewing wider compliance functions.

(x) reviewing any relevant escalation procedure to the extent relevant.

(xi) reviewing reporting lines and governors' functions.

The firm must co-operate with the authorities under PRIN 11, PRA FR 7 and COCON IC 3 and SM 4. The firm may also consider:

(i) discussing any concerns with the regulators following the internal reviews conducted.

(ii) explaining all of the internal action carried out to the firm's principal line supervisors.

(iii) reviewing board oversight of foreign exchange and other benchmark operations. (iv) ensuring that a specific director has operational responsibility for the firm's foreign exchange and other benchmark operations.

(v) ensuring that foreign exchange operations are monitored by non-executive directors.

(vi) confirming internal training with regards to compliance with internal benchmark setting arrangements.

(vii) reviewing internal whistle blowing procedures with regards to internal staff reporting.

## B92.

### Question

An increased liquidity requirement had also been imposed on its operations by the PRA which it breached at the same time through other systems failures which have again been resolved. The directors had nevertheless decided not to notify these events to the PRA for a four-month period until the issue could be resolved internally. The board of the bank is now anxious to ensure that any potential liabilities that may arise in this regard are reduced in so far as possible.

You are asked to advise on the following:

a) Outline the principal statutory offences that may apply with regard benchmark rate manipulation and any defences and penalties. _(4 marks)_

b) Outline any separate statutory civil prohibitions that may apply with any defences and relevant penalties. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with the conduct of a review of internal benchmark and liquidity compliance procedures. This followed the £46.55 million fine imposed by the PRA on Standard Chartered Bank (SCB) in December 2021 for failings in its regulatory reporting governance and controls in relation to a tailored PRA liquidity expectation and for failing to be open and cooperative with the PRA.

## **a) (4 marks)**

The principal statutory offences that may arise are in connection with benchmarks as set out in s91 FSA 2012. This creates two specific offences of making a false or misleading statement to another person in connection with benchmark setting arrangements or acts or engages in a manner that creates a false or misleading impression as to the price or value of an investment or the most appropriate interest rate (s91(1) and (2) FSA 2012). Defences apply with regard to price stabilisation and control of information rules and buy-back programmes. A person found guilty of an offence is liable for up to ten years' imprisonment or a fine on indictment. Reference may also have been made to the more general offences of making a misleading statement or impression under ss89 and 90 FSA 2012.

## **b) (4 marks)**

Separate offences may have been committed under the Market Abuse Regulation depending upon particular circumstances. This may, in particular, include insider dealing, unlawful disclosure, manipulating transactions, manipulating devices, dissemination, and misleading behaviour and distortion. These provisions are generally restricted to apply in regard to insiders, qualifying investments and prescribed markets which may not be relevant in this case. Inside information is defined. A regular user test is applied with the relevant defences. Market abuse is dealt with under the FCA MAR sourcebook within its Handbook https://www.handbook.fca.org.uk/handbook/MAR/1/?view=chapter).

## **c) (4 marks)**

The bank has also failed to be cooperative with the PRA in not notifying it immediately of the systems errors(PRA FR 8). This was considered a significant element in the SCB£46.55 million PRA fine in 2021.A number of provisions may also have been breached including FCA PRIN (1, 2,3, 5, 6, 8, 9 and 11),PRA FR(1,2, 3, and 6), FSA SYSC (2, 3,4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 18 and 19A), COCON (1,2, 3 and5), COND (2, 4 and 5) and FIT (1, 2 and 3).These would principally relate to integrity, systems and controls, organisation, compliance and reporting. A full range of regulatory penalties would apply.

## **d) (4 marks)**

The full range of regulatory sanctions may be available against the firm, in addition to the individual penalties referred to above, including variation of permission (s45 FSMA), suspension of permission (s206a FSMA), cancellation of Part 4 permission (s54 FSMA), withdrawal of authorisation (s33 FSMA), public censure (s205 FSMA),financial penalties (s206 FSMA), requests for information (s165 FSMA and Schedule 12 FSA 2012), independent inquiry (s68 FSA 2012), administration, receivership, winding-up or bankruptcy (Part 24 FSMA, Banking Act 2009 and Schedule 14 FSA 2012), injunctions (s380 FSMA), restitution (ss382 and 384 FSMA),suspension of a financial instrument from trading (s36 FSA 2012), complaint by a consumer body(super-complaint under s234c FSMA and s43 FSA 2012), regulated person reference (s234d FSMA), OFT investigation (s234h FSMA), action under product intervention rules (s137d FSMA 2000 and s24 FSA 2012) or a skilled person's report(s166 FSMA).

## B100.

### Question

An investment firm has been dealing with a particular client for many years. In the last month some large and unusual transactions have been undertaken by the client. These transactions are observed and reported by an employee, who is worried that the client may be engaging in market abuse.

a) How should the firm respond to this concern raised by the employee? _(4 marks)_

b) What processes need to be in place to ensure that transactions which raise suspicions of market abuse are identified by the firm? _(8 marks)_

c) What are the current laws and financial regulations which try to limit market abuse? _(4 marks)_

d) Why is it important for all financial firms to tackle the issue of market abuse? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

## **a) (4 marks)**

The firm should examine any suspicious transactions on a case-by-case basis to determine if the transaction includes elements which may constitute market abuse.

If there are reasonable grounds for suspecting a transaction involves market abuse, the firm must notify the FCA without delay.

A further part of this process is knowledge of the client/customer and the business relationship the client has with the firm. This will help the firm understand what types of transactions the client should be reasonably and normally undertaking. This information may be used to develop a suspicious activity report (SAR) and/or suspicious activity and order report (STOR), which should include all relevant information about the individual(s) or entity believed to be acting suspiciously. Therefore, the key to knowing when to report suspicious activity is to have a good understanding of your customer- from understanding their business, building relationships with them, to understanding the activities and transactions that they will, and do, undertake.

As UK MAR also applies to unexecuted orders and requests for quotes it is important to examine current transactions, past transactions, unexecuted orders and requests for quotes.

## **b) (8 marks)**

First it is helpful to acknowledge process to tackle market abuse varies according to the risk of the financial firms and business activity it undertakes, i.e. an investment bank will have more developed processes than a small financial firm.

The scope of investigations includes both transactions and unexecuted orders and requests for quotes. Processes for tackling market abuse need to allow individual and/or comparative analysis of every transaction executed, and orders placed, modified, cancelled or rejected. This analysis should indicate which activities require further assessment to detect market manipulation or abuse.

This analysis requires human involvement in the monitoring, detection and identification of transactions and orders that could constitute actual or attempted insider dealing and/or market manipulation.

An important aspect of this process is to ensure staff are appropriately trained to undertake such monitoring, detection and identification of orders and transactions. The training needs to be ongoing. In cases where an employee may be involved in the market abuse the firm requires systems to manage any conflicts of interest.

## **c) (4 marks)**

The Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) is a key piece of legislation that aims to prevent market abuse and protect the integrity of financial markets. It covers various forms of market abuse, including insider dealing, unlawful disclosure of inside information, and market manipulation. MAR requires firms to implement measures to detect and report suspicious transactions and activities.

The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA) provides the framework for the regulation of financial markets and services in the UK. It includes provisions to combat market abuse and empowers the FCA to investigate and take enforcement action against individuals and firms involved in market abuse.

Part V of the Criminal Justice Act 1993 specifically addresses insider dealing. It makes it a criminal offence for individuals to deal in securities on the basis of inside information, or to encourage others to do so, and imposes severe penalties for breaches.

The FCA Handbook, including the Senior Management Arrangements, Systems and Controls (SYSC) Sourcebook, outlines requirements for firms to establish effective systems and controls to prevent market abuse. This includes having appropriate policies, procedures, and training in place to ensure compliance with market abuse regulations.

## **d) (4 marks)**

Market abuse undermines the integrity of financial markets. Ensuring fair and transparent market practices helps to build and maintain investor confidence, which is essential for the proper functioning of financial markets. Firms that actively work to prevent market abuse contribute to a stable and trustworthy market environment.

Market abuse, such as insider trading and market manipulation, can lead to significant financial losses for investors. By preventing such practices, firms protect their clients from unfair practices and potential losses, fostering a sense of security and trust among their customer base. Addressing market abuse is essential for the long-term sustainability of financial markets. By promoting fair practices, firms help to create a level playing field, which is crucial for the long-term health and sustainability of the financial sector. This also contributes to the overall economic stability and growth.

A firm's reputation is one of its most valuable assets. Involvement in market abuse can severely damage a firm's reputation, leading to a loss of client trust and potential business. Firms that are seen to uphold high ethical standards and actively prevent market abuse are more likely to attract and retain clients and business partners.
