## B10.

### Question

You are the compliance officer of Osprey Private Bank, a UK-incorporated bank which is regulated by the FCA for the conduct of investment business. Osprey is a subsidiary of Global Bank Inc, a US-based universal bank, which provides a variety of banking services to both retail and wholesale clients.

- a) Global Bank Inc. is considering setting up a global website for the group. The website will be hosted in the US, with its content managed by Global Bank Inc. in the US. The website will advertise Osprey's personal and corporate banking services. You are asked to advise on anything required to ensure that the website does not "raise any UK compliance issues". _(6 marks)_

- b) Mr Corleone, a native of Sicily, but now resident for most of the year in the Caribbean, has an account with Osprey through which he mainly conducts share dealing activities. You are approached by an officer from the National Crime Agency asking for a print-out of all the transactions on Mr Corleone's account for the last twelve months.

   - (i) Should you give the information to the officer?

   - (ii) Should you notify Mr Corleone of the approach and/or the disclosure?

Please give reasoned answers. _(6 marks)_

- c) Mr Corleone introduces his Russian friend, Mr Staksovcashsky, to Osprey telling Osprey Mr Staksovcashsky is dissatisfied with the performance of his current bankers. Mr Staksovcashsky proposes to ask his old bankers to transfer the balance on his account (worth several hundred thousand pounds in cash and securities) to Osprey. Mr Green, a young account executive at Osprey, is keen to add Mr Staksovcashsky to his client list and asks you what he needs to do in order to take on Mr Staksovcashsky as a customer. Please advise. _(8 marks)_

### Answer

- a) Your answer should commence with s21 and that to the extent that the website will be a financial promotion that its content must be approved and comply with the requirements of COBS 4. Your answer should distinguish between real and non-real time promotions and should also have made reference to the E-commerce Directive.

b)

   - (i) The information should be given to the NCA officer. There might be concerns that passing this information on is a breach of the duty of confidentiality owed to customers; however, the obligation to co-operate with NCA supersedes this. There are specific exemptions from breach of confidentiality lawsuits for such cooperation.

   - (ii) Mr Corleone should not be informed. Whilst the FCA Principles for Businesses do require authorised firms to communicate effectively with their customers, the act of informing Mr Corleone would constitute "tipping off". This is a criminal offence under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, punishable by an unlimited fine and/or imprisonment for up to two years.

- c) In order to accept Mr Staksovcashsky (S) as a customer, Osprey will need to undertake identification procedures and will not be able to rely on the personal introduction of Mr Corleone.

S should be asked to provide either his passport or a valid ID document. Additionally, a verifiable postal address must be given.

If S is based outside the UK, then separately verifiable details of a permanent address must be given - if based in the UK, normal evidence (voters roll, utilities bill, etc.) would suffice.

## B30.

### Question

You work for a small boutique bank in the West End of London which has a number of clients in the Middle East and Central and East Asia. You receive instructions from an overseas government agency to transfer a large amount of funds from the government account with your bank to the account of another association held with a separate bank in London. You regularly handle transactions on behalf of the government although not the specific agency concerned. You are advised by a colleague that you should check whether the association is listed on any of the restricted lists of terrorist groups. After some further research, you also uncover that the association is based in a compound in a major city in a Central Asian country which does not appear to carry on any separate business activities or send or receive funds on a regular basis.

- a) Explain any immediate concerns that may arise with regard to the requested funds transfer and what action you should take within the bank. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline the principal offences and penalties that may apply and any possible defences.

   - _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline any separate anti-terrorist considerations that may apply either within the UK or internationally. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain the action that you should take to prevent any firm or personal liability from arising. _(4 marks)_

- e) How should you respond to any requests for further information from your client with regard to progress or completion of the transaction and why this is necessary?

_(4 marks)_

### Answer

The fact pattern is loosely based on the location of Osama Bin Laden in Pakistan by US Special Forces. This raised a number of issues with regard to potential money laundering and the funding of terrorist activity.

- a) Immediate concerns arise with regard to potential money laundering and the funding of terrorist activity. Relevant factors include:

   - The large sum of money involved;

   - The unknown source of the money;

   - The unknown destination of the transfer;

   - The fact that the agency is not registered;

   - The location of the compound;

   - The absence of any separate business activities;

   - The absence of any other funds movement; and

   - The geographic location of the agency.

The possible listing of the agency on the 'Prescribed Terror Groups or Organisations' maintained by the UK Home Office should be confirmed against their website. A _Consolidated List of Financial Sanctions Targets in the UK_ is also maintained on the HM Treasury website. A separate 'Current List of Designated Foreign Terrorist Organisations' is maintained on the US Department of State website with the FATF also maintaining a further list of 'Non-Co-operative Countries and Territories'.

The matter should be reported immediately to the firm's Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO) who should submit a Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) to the National Crime Agency (NCA) as appropriate.

- b) All of the relevant money laundering offences, penalties and defences should be outlined for the full four marks.

- c) Equivalent provisions under the Terrorism Act 2000, Anti-Terrorism Crime and Security Act 2001 and Counter-Terrorism Act 2008, including relevant regulations issued under Schedule 7 CTA, should have been referred to. Reference may also have made to equivalent provisions under the US Patriot Act.

- d) All of the relevant provisions with regard to institutional compliance with the UK money laundering statutes and regulations should have been referred to. This includes 'Know your Customer' (KYC) and Customer Due Diligence (CDD) and Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) with Simplified Due Diligence (SDD) not being appropriate in this case. Standard identification checks should have been carried out. Special rules apply on introductions although it is not clear to what extent these apply in this case. Other relevant provisions under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 as well as the Joint Money Laundering Steering Group (JMLSG) Guidance Notes as well as FCA policy statement requirements should have been referred to.

Staff training obligations and the role and function of the MLRO could have been noted. Individual liability arises under the principal statutes referred under (b) above with necessary action being outlined in a) above.

- e) Care has to be taken not to commit the separate offence of 'tipping off' under s333a of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002. Individuals must not disclose that information has been passed to the police, HMRC or the firm's MLRO or NCA or that an investigation into money laundering allegations is being contemplated or carried out. This is subject to the relevant exceptions and defences provided. The offence is punishable with up to two years' imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine (or three months' imprisonment and a £5,000 fine in the Magistrates' Court). The client should simply be advised that the instructions are being carried out and that the transaction is proceeding.

## B32. *(Compulsory)*

### Question

Glisten is a recently privatised mining company in the central Asian republic of Manowar but the Life President of the republic is believed to retain a substantial stake via the government's retained 40% stake, together with undisclosed private interest arising from the IPO.

The company dominates industrial activity in its domestic market and exports its products around the world. With the strength of mineral prices this year was a bumper year for Glisten, which generated substantial profits leading to the expectation of significant dividends.

Public disquiet both domestically and internationally about the huge profits of the company, its opaque governance structure and the large current account deficit in Manowar mean that Glisten is frequently criticised in the media.

You work as a senior manager for Chimera Investment Bank who advised on the IPO of Glisten some years ago and your firm continues to provide a broad range of services to the company as well as to the Manowar government, which plans further privatisations of state assets. As a result of the commentary about Glisten, Manowar has asked Chimera for a proposal to capitalise on the value of various state assets without the unwelcome publicity resulting from public ownership. The Structured Finance Department of Chimera has originated a proposal that will, as its principal attribute, generate significant cash to the government via a sophisticated leasing arrangement undertaken through a British Virgin Islands incorporated entity.

Initial discussion with Manowar representatives suggests that they are interested, but they have no previous experience of such a structure and their Finance ministry representative, who is a nephew of the President has told Chimera that because of the sensitivity of the President it is imperative that no hint of this transaction should become public knowledge.

The transaction will use a special purpose vehicle and will earn Chimera a substantial fee, which would be particularly valuable to the company, which has seen little activity this year and would be a key factor in enabling you to meet your personal income target for the year.

The proposed structure has not been subject to comment or scrutiny by any tax, accounting or regulatory agency but, as part of your approval process, you have received two legal opinions, one from a global and one from a local Manowar practice. Both state, unequivocally, that the proposed transaction does not contravene any existing Manowar laws.

- a) State, with reasons, whether or not you would support this transaction. _(10 marks)_

- b) What further information might you seek?

   - _(5 marks)_

- c) Are there ways in which you might mitigate some of the risks?

- _(5 marks)_

### Answer

The fact pattern raises a number of sensitive issues with regard to balancing fee income within an investment bank and being engaged in ethically questionable transactions for overseas governments and private entities.

- a) All candidates concluded that they would not support the transaction with various grounds being identified. The following sets of issues could have been identified:

## i. **Regulatory Perimeter**

Some candidates initially focused on the relevance of UK financial law and the scope and competence of the FCA and relevance of the Handbook provisions in this case. It is assumed that Chimera is based in London and holds relevant permissions from the FCA. While the point is unclear, much of the proposed work may be carried out of London with the full application of the FCA Handbook and other civil and criminal provisions. Short reference may have been made to the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA), authorised persons (s31 FSMA), Part 4A permission (s40 FSMA), approved persons (s59 FSMA) and FIT and COCON. General reference may also have been made to PRIN (and COND) although this may also have been dealt with, with regard to specific points of concern (below).

## ii. **Client Classification**

The nature of the client classification of the Republic as well as the President and his nephew could have been commented on.

## iii. **Asset Ownership**

A number of immediate concerns arise with regard to the source of assets and beneficial share ownership, including:

- a) The President's interest in the IPO;

- b) The President's control of the government's 40% stake;

- c) The President's other undisclosed private interests;

- d) The ownership of proposed future state assets to be disposed of; and

- e) The nephew's beneficial interest in any of the assets.

The clear implication is that the President may have misappropriated state assets as part of the IPO or proposed future arrangements with the involvement of his nephew.

## iv. **Transaction Structure**

Further concerns arise with regard to the proposed future transaction structure:

- a) The complex nature involving sophisticated leasing arrangements;

- b) Prepared by the Structured Finance Department within the investment bank;

- c) The use of a special purpose vehicle (SPV) in the British Virgin Islands;

- d) The lack of information with regard to potential investor groups; and

- e) The lack of information with regard to payment arrangements.

Specific product approval issues may arise under the (then) FSA Discussion Paper 11/1 on _Product Intervention_ (January 2011) and Feedback Statement 11/03 (June 2011).

- v. **Governance and Transparency**

Further concerns arise with regard to governance and transparency arrangements:

- a) Lack of local experience in proposed transaction arrangements;

- b) Opaque nature of the transactions proposed;

- c) Specific request for non-disclosed involvement of President;

- d) Presumed exclusion from Parliamentary and other public oversight;

- e) Local and international criticism of IPO activities;

- f) Lack of proper governance within IPO; and

- g) Arguable misappropriation of assets with large profits but with current account deficit.

## vi. **Conflicts of Interest**

A number of potential conflicts of interest may arise:

- a) The President's involvement with the IPO, including undisclosed private interests and his state position;

- b) The involvement of the nephew in the proposed future arrangements;

- c) The bank's acting for the President and government;

- d) The bank's potential involvement in future IPOs and separate structured finance arrangements;

- e) The manager's and the bank's interests with possible compliance procedures and ultimate 'whistle-blowing' under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA); and

- f) The manager's compliance concerns and project bonus.

## vii. **Money Laundering**

A number of separate issues arise with regard to potential money laundering:

- a) The President and/or nephew may be involved in having used the IPO or future IPOs and leasing arrangements for money laundering purposes;

- b) Enhanced due diligence (EDD) would be required with the President being a 'politically exposed person' (PEP);

- c) General 'know your customer' (KYC) compliance;

- d) Suspicious Transaction Reporting (SAR);

- e) Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO) functions;

- f) Money laundering penalties under the Proceeds of Crime Act;

- g) Offences under the Money Laundering Regulations (MLR); and

- h) Recordkeeping and other regulatory breaches.

## viii. **Bribery**

Potential concerns arise under the Bribery Act 2010 in connection with possible corrupt payments made by a commercial organisation, as a Glisten, or under equivalent laws in the Republic.

## ix. **Legal Opinions and Tax, Accounting or Regulatory Approval**

The legal opinions obtained as part of the approval process are only concerned with the local law. Confirmation of no separate breach under UK law or regulatory requirements should be considered in addition to general ethical considerations.

No tax, accounting or regulatory approval has been obtained presumably in the Republic and to the extent relevant in the UK. This would include breach of any local or UK company law requirements on IPOs and privatisations.

## x. **Regulatory and Ethical Compliance**

All of the above concerns raise significant further dangers of reputational risk and damage to the bank. The bank must also comply with the CISI Code of Conduct as well as breach of other more specific FCA provisions including:

- a) Breach of PRIN 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 3 (management and control), 6 (customers' interests), 7 (communications with clients), 8 (conflicts of interest), 8 (customers' relationship of trust) and possibly10 (client assets) and 11 (regulatory relations);

      - b) Breach by the firm of SYSC 2 (senior management arrangements), 3 (systems and controls), 4 (organisational requirements), 5 (procedures), 6 (compliance), 7 (risk controls) and 10 (conflicts of interest);

      - c) Breach of COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (skill, care and diligence), 5 (market conduct), and 3 (regulatory relations); and

      - d) Possible breach of COND and FIT;

      - e) Possible breach Suitability (COBS 9);

      - f) Possible breach Appropriateness (COBS 10);

      - g) Possible breach of s59 FSMA;

      - h) Possible liability under s71 FSMA; and

      - i) Possible liability under s138D FSMA.

- b) Further information could be requested on any of the matters of concern referred to above, including:

   - i. All beneficial shareholder interests in the IPO;

   - ii. All beneficial interests in future privatisations;

   - iii. Proposed acquisitions by the President or nephew under future privatisations;

   - iv. Accounts for IPO;

   - v. Governance arrangements for IPO;

   - vi. Proposed income streams for future privatisations;

   - vii. Any potential conflicts of interest;

   - viii. All relevant money laundering identity and source and application of funds checks;

   - ix. Confirmation of no corrupt payments;

   - x. Relevant tax approvals;

   - xi. Relevant accounting approvals;

   - xii. Relevant regulatory approvals from authorities in the Republic and possible legal and regulatory opinion on UK compliance.

- c) A number of possible risk mitigation steps may be taken:

   - i.  Complete full approval process for future work, including obtaining new mandate letter from the Republic;

   - ii. Separate work for the Republic and President or nephew on a personal basis;

   - iii. Consider separate product approval procedure for proposed structured finance arrangements;

   - iv. Ask for possible Executive or Parliamentary confirmation for proposed privatisations to proceed;

- v. Confirm beneficial ownership interests in privatised assets with the Finance Ministry and future projected payment streams following IPOs;

- vi. Consult with financial and company regulatory authorities in the Republic;

- vii. Obtain relevant tax, accounting and regulatory approvals referred to above;

- viii. Obtain UK legal opinion;

- ix. Discuss any residual concerns with the FCA;

- x. Discuss any residual concerns with the CISI.

## B39.

### Question

You have just joined the compliance team within a major international bank with its headquarters in the City of London. Your boss has become very concerned with regard to investigations being conducted by US regulatory authorities into the activities of one of the bank's overseas branches (rather than subsidiaries) in South America. The press has already reported possible money laundering allegations with the branch allegedly having been involved in channelling funds from major drug groups from various South American countries into the US and into the UK. Separate reports have been made of accounts having been open for major terrorist groups in South America. Senior Management in London have raised specific issues with regard to the conduct of the local regional manager at the branch and the extent of control exercised over his activities from the head office. You have been asked to prepare a memo outlining the bank's possible liability under UK law and to deal specifically with each of the following questions.

- a) Explain the immediate concerns that arise with regard to the activities of the South American regional manager. _(4 marks)_

- b)  Outline the principal offences and penalties that may apply and any possible defences. _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline any separate anti-terrorist considerations that may apply. _(4 marks)_

- d)  Explain the possible additional breaches that have occurred in terms of the head office's supervision of the activities of the regional branch manager and the possible penalties that may be imposed. _(4 marks)_

- e) Explain any action that would propose to prevent any further liabilities from arising. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question generally reflected the facts surrounding the $1.9b (£1.17b) fine of HSBC by US authorities in relation to activities in Mexico involving criminal groups in Colombia and Russia. This raises a number of issues with regard to potential money laundering and group controls. Other banks, such as Credit Suisse, ING and Standard Chartered have also been fined for alleged breach of US sanctions against Iran, Libya and other countries.

- a) Immediate concerns arise with regard to potential money laundering and the funding of terrorist activity. Relevant factors include:

   - (i) the press reports of alleged money laundering activities;

   - (ii) possible earlier investigations;

   - (iii) unidentified account holders;

   - (iv) the possibly large sums of money involved;

   - (v) the recurrent nature of the transactions;

   - (vi) the unknown source of the monies concerned;

   - (vii) the unknown destinations of the transfers;

   - (viii) the geographic location of the branch.

- b) All of the relevant money laundering offences, penalties and defences should have been outlined for the full four marks. The principal UK money laundering offences under Part VII of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (PCA) include Concealing (s 327), Arranging (s 328) and Acquisition, Use and Possession (s 329) as well as attempting, conspiring or inciting to commit any such offence (s 340(11)(a) and (b)). Money laundering includes aiding, abetting, counselling or procuring the commission of any of these offences (s 340(11)(c) and (d)). Other relevant provisions under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 as well as the Joint Money Laundering Steering Group (JMLSG) Guidance Notes as well as FCA policy statement requirements could have been referred to.

- c) The separate anti-terrorist breaches may have occurred under the Terrorism Act 2000, Anti-Terrorism Crime and Security Act 2001 and Counter-Terrorism Act 2008, including relevant regulations issued under Schedule 7 CTA, should have been referred to. Reference may also have been made to US sanctions including equivalent provisions under the US Patriot Act. Staff should be directed to confirm all relevant outstanding US laws and regulations to avoid substantial penalty. Private rights of action for damages may also be available under s138D FSMA.

- d) A number of potential breaches arise under SYSC (including SYSC 6) and PRIN (1,2,3,6,9, AND 11) with the FCA being able to impose a full range of sanctions including public or private censure, financial penalty, restriction of permission and ultimate withdrawal of authorisation. Private rights of action for losses suffered as a result of breach of a rule may also be available under s138D FSMA. The FCA may separately prosecute firms for breach of criminal laws such as money laundering under ss401-422 FSMA. Specific requirements with regard to group systems and controls are imposed under SYSC 12 although these would not strictly apply in the present case as this is a branch and not a subsidiary operation.

- e) Action could be taken within the firm with regard to each of the following:

   - (i)   Commission a full review of the regional manager's actions;

   - (ii)   Review possible involvement of other staff in the local office or elsewhere;

   - (iii)   Review the effectiveness of local money laundering control systems;

   - (iv)   Confirm the identity and effectiveness of the MLRO functions with regard to branch and group levels;

   - (v)   Review all other local and group systems and controls;

   - (vi)   Review risk management;

   - (vii)  Review compliance function and other relevant reporting lines at front and group level;

   - (viii) Review approved person arrangements;

- (ix)   Review training and competence procedures;

- (x)   Discuss concerns with relevant executive and non-executive and audit committees.

Relevant external action would also include.

- (i)   Notifying FCA of conduct and corrective action taken;

- (ii)   Contacting any possible clients affected;

- (iii)   Liaising with any other relevant regulatory or criminal authorities including in the UK or elsewhere and attempting to agree early settlement where possible to avoid higher potential fines.

## B45.

### Question

Your bank has agreed to offer safeguard facilities to a newly established Electronic Money Institution (EMI). Under this arrangement, your bank will hold funds for the EMI's customers. The EMI is responsible for performing due diligence, ongoing monitoring, including transaction monitoring, and screening. As part of the agreement, your firm has requested that the EMI conduct an annual Anti-Money Laundering (AML) audit and provide the audit reports to your bank. Sam, the new Head of Partnerships at your bank, has received pushback from the EMI regarding the necessity of the annual AML audit. Sam has scheduled a meeting with you to discuss whether this requirement is essential and to understand the implications of potentially waiving it.

- a) Explain the importance of conducting an annual AML audit for such arrangements in the context of the FCA's Financial Crime Guide. How does this practice contribute to mitigating the bank's own financial crime risks?

   - _(6 marks)_

- b) Explain to Sam the potential consequences for the bank if it fails to enforce robust AML controls, such as waiving the requirement for an annual AML audit. Consider both legal and reputational risks.

   - _(4 marks)_

- c) Evaluate the role of Senior Management in overseeing AML compliance within a financial institution. How does their engagement, or lack thereof, impact the effectiveness of the institution's AML programme?

   - _(4 marks)_

- d) What other elements, in addition to due diligence, ongoing monitoring, transaction monitoring, and screening, should the audit focus on to ensure the EMI has a suitable financial crime framework?

_(6 marks)_

### Answer

This question provided a case study related to anti-money laundering systems and controls in relation to safeguard facilities.

- a) While not a regulatory requirement, conducting an annual AML audit serves as a proactive measure to ensure compliance with AML regulations and to mitigate the risks associated with financial crime. The FCA's Financial Crime Guide outlines the need for firms to establish and maintain effective systems and controls to counter the risk of financial crime, including money laundering and terrorist financing. An annual AML audit can provide the bank with assurance that the EMI is adhering to these regulatory requirements by systematically reviewing its policies, procedures, and operational practices. The audit provides an independent assessment of the effectiveness of the EMI's AML framework, ensuring that it complies with the FCA's standards. The financial sector is constantly evolving, with new risks and threats emerging regularly. An annual AML audit allows the EMI to identify and address vulnerabilities in its systems and controls. By regularly auditing its AML practices, the EMI can adapt to changes in the regulatory environment and respond to new typologies of financial crime. This continuous improvement process is essential for managing risks effectively and ensuring that the EMI is not exploited by criminals. An annual AML audit also benefits the EMI, by enhancing the oversight function within the EMI and keeping senior management appraised of the firm's AML practices. It ensures that the senior management and board are fully aware of the institution's AML performance and are taking appropriate actions to address any deficiencies. This level of oversight is critical for maintaining a strong culture of compliance and for demonstrating to regulators that the EMI is committed to preventing financial crime.

For the bank providing safeguarding services to the EMI, the annual AML audit serves as a crucial line of defence. It ensures that the EMI is not engaging in practices that could expose the bank to financial crime risks. By requiring the EMI to conduct an annual AML audit and reviewing the audit reports, the bank can gain assurance that its partner is compliant with AML regulations and that the funds it holds are not linked to illicit activities. This practice helps to protect the bank from regulatory penalties, reputational damage, and potential legal liabilities. The audit fosters transparency and accountability within the EMI by ensuring that all aspects of its AML framework are scrutinised and reported on. This transparency helps to build trust with stakeholders, including customers, regulators, and business partners, who rely on the EMI to conduct its operations ethically and in compliance with the law. Finally, the audit process is helpful in driving continuous improvement within the EMI. By identifying gaps and areas for enhancement, the audit provides a roadmap for strengthening the firm's AML framework. This ongoing refinement helps the EMI stay ahead of potential threats and maintain a robust defence against financial crime.

b) Failing to enforce robust AML controls can expose a bank to severe legal and reputational risks, which could have long-lasting consequences. The FCA requires banks to implement and maintain adequate systems and controls to prevent money laundering and terrorist financing. By not having sight of the EMI's AML arrangements, the bank may be in breach of its obligations under SYSC 6.1.1R and SYSC 3.2.6R, which could result in significant regulatory sanctions. These could include financial penalties, restrictions on the bank's ability to operate, or even the revocation of licences for severe breaches. The FCA has the authority to impose substantial fines, which not only affect the bank's financial stability but also its operational capabilities. A failure to enforce AML controls could trigger heightened scrutiny from regulators. The bank may face more frequent inspections, audits, and demands for detailed reports on its AML practices. This increased scrutiny can strain the bank's resources, both financially and operationally, as it attempts to demonstrate compliance in the face of regulatory concerns. Additionally, a history of non-compliance can lead to a loss of the regulator's trust, making it more challenging for the bank to gain approvals for new products or services in the future. If the EMI, under the bank's safeguard facilities, engages in money laundering activities due to inadequate AML controls, the bank could be held legally liable. Investors, customers, or third parties who suffer losses due to the bank's failure to prevent money laundering might pursue legal action, seeking damages for negligence. This could lead to costly litigation, further financial losses, and additional strain on the bank's resources. In some cases, senior executives could also face personal liability if it is found that they were negligent in their duties to enforce AML controls.

A bank's reputation is one of its most valuable assets. If the public or clients perceive that the bank is not taking its AML responsibilities seriously, it could lead to a significant loss of trust. Clients may withdraw their funds and move their business to more reputable institutions, leading to a decline in the bank's customer base and revenues. Additionally, potential clients may choose to avoid the bank, fearing that it may be involved in or susceptible to financial crimes. The media plays a critical role in shaping public perception. If the bank's failure to enforce AML controls leads to a money laundering scandal, it could attract widespread negative media coverage. This not only damages the bank's reputation but can also influence investor sentiment, potentially leading to a decline in stock prices and market value. The negative publicity could also have a long-term impact, making it difficult for the bank to recover its reputation even after corrective actions are taken. The bank's relationships with key stakeholders, including regulators, investors, and business partners, could be severely damaged if it is found to be lax in enforcing AML controls. Regulators may become more cautious and impose stricter requirements on the bank, while investors might lose confidence in the bank's ability to manage risks effectively. Business partners, such as other financial institutions, may reconsider their associations with the bank to protect their own reputations, leading to a potential loss of strategic alliances and partnerships. The consequences of failing to enforce AML controls can also extend to the bank's day-to-day operations. The need to address regulatory concerns, manage legal actions, and restore its reputation can divert significant resources away from the bank's core business activities. This diversion can hinder the bank's ability to innovate, expand, and compete effectively in the market. Moreover, the internal culture of the bank may suffer, with employees becoming disengaged or demoralised if they perceive that ethical standards are being compromised. Waiving the requirement for an annual AML audit could therefore have farreaching legal and reputational consequences for a bank.

c) Senior management plays an important role in the effectiveness of a firm's AML compliance programme. Their involvement is crucial for setting the tone at the top, ensuring that AML policies and practices are prioritised, adequately resourced, and effectively implemented across the organisation. The FCA's Financial Crime Guide emphasises the importance of senior management in actively overseeing financial crime risks, which includes AML compliance. Senior management is responsible for establishing a robust governance framework that supports AML compliance. This includes setting clear expectations for compliance, creating accountability structures, and ensuring that there is a dedicated team or department responsible for AML activities. Senior leaders must also ensure that there is effective communication between the board, management, and operational teams regarding AML issues.

Active engagement with regulators helps to build trust and can lead to more constructive regulatory interactions. If senior management is not engaged, the institution may struggle to meet regulatory expectations, leading to more frequent inspections, stricter oversight, and potential sanctions. When senior management is not actively involved in overseeing AML compliance, the effectiveness of the programme can be severely compromised. A lack of leadership can result in insufficient resources, poor risk management, and a weak compliance culture. ## B99.

### Question

You are employed to examine a financial firm's arrangements for tackling money laundering and terrorist financing. As you investigate, you are questioned by senior management as to the importance and meaning of many of the regulatory terms and expectations used to tackle money laundering and terrorist financing. You are asked to prepare a short discussion considering the following questions.

a) What are the laws and regulations used to address money laundering and terrorist financing?

_(4 marks)_

b) What are the specific offences which may lead to non-compliance with these laws and regulations?

_(4 marks)_

c) In what way does terrorist financing threaten to use the financial services system and how do the requirements arising from the acronyms CDD, EDD and SDD defend against it?

_(8 marks)_

d) What are the primary considerations related to PEPs, relatives and close associates? How should a firm manage the risks arising from having them as customers?

_(4 marks)_

### Answer

## **a) (4 marks)**

The Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA), which defines money laundering and establishes offences for concealing, arranging, acquiring, using, and possessing criminal property. It mandates that firms report suspicions of money laundering, with failure to disclose constituting an offence.

The Terrorism Act 2000 (TA), which criminalises the financing of terrorist activities and requires the reporting of suspicions.

The Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001, which provides additional powers to combat terrorism and enforce anti-terrorism measures.

The Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (MLRs 2017), which require firms to conduct risk assessments, implement controls, and perform customer due diligence.

The Serious Organised Crime and Police Act 2005 (SOCPA), which amended POCA to address activities legal in their origin country but illegal in the UK, clarifying reporting obligations and providing defences for unknowing involvement in such activities.

Additionally, candidates could have mentioned:

The FCA's SYSC Sourcebook, which mandates that firms have systems and controls to manage money laundering risks, with senior management accountable for compliance and a Money Laundering Reporting Officer overseeing these activities.

The FCA's Financial Crime Guide, which consolidates guidance on countering financial crime risks, offering practical examples for firms to reduce their financial crime risk and enhance compliance.

Joint Money Laundering Steering Group (JMLSG) Guidance, which provides industry guidance on implementing anti-money laundering regulations, outlining best practices for risk management, customer due diligence, and reporting suspicious activities, serving as evidence of compliance for the FCA and courts.

These laws and regulations ensure that UK financial services firms can prevent, detect, and report money laundering and terrorist financing activities to maintain the integrity of the financial system.

## **b) (4 marks)**

POCA establishes five offences, namely:

**1. Concealing** – it is an offence for a person to conceal or disguise criminal property.

**2. Arrangements** – it is an offence to be involved in an arrangement which the person knows, or suspects, facilitates the acquisition, retention, use or control of criminal property for another person. These arrangements may be widely interpreted and includes advising on a transaction.

**3. Acquisition, use and possession** – acquiring, using or having possession of criminal property.

These three offences are punishable by a jail term of up to 14 years and a fine.

**4. Failure to disclose** – three conditions need to be satisfied for this offence:

   - The person knows or suspects that another person is laundering money.

   - The information giving rise to the knowledge or suspicion came to her/him whilst undertaking business in a regulated sector.

   - The person does not make the required disclosure as soon as is practicable.

The offence of failure to disclose suspicions of money laundering is committed not only when the person knows or suspects money laundering, but also when there are reasonable grounds to know or suspect money laundering: i.e. would a 'reasonable person' known or been suspicious? This offence is punishable by a jail term of up to five years and a fine.

**5. Tipping off** – providing another person with information likely to prejudice a money laundering investigation.

This offence is punishable by a jail term of up to two years and a fine.

## **c) (8 marks)**

Terrorist financing threatens the financial services system by using legitimate financial channels to move and store funds, often through small transactions that are difficult to detect, thereby compromising the integrity of these institutions. The exploitation of global financial networks by terrorists poses significant regulatory challenges and increases compliance costs for financial institutions, which face severe penalties for non-compliance. This undermines public trust in the financial system, leading to potential economic instability and loss of confidence among customers and investors.

CDD is an acronym of customer due diligence – this term is related to the process through which a client is identified and checked to ensure they are who they say they are, and that there are no legal reasons preventing a business relationship being developed between the firm and the individual. In some situations, this process has developed into a risk-based assessment to determine any particular risks associated with a client and/or business relationship. For a corporate client it is important to undertake customer due diligence to determine the beneficial ownership of the corporation.

EDD is an acronym of enhanced due diligence. In an EDD process a firm conducts additional checks and measures relating to an individual or beneficial owner deemed or assessed to be higher risk, and when the information collected in the CDD process is insufficient. This includes when there is a high risk of terrorist financing, where a transaction or business relationship is established in a high-risk third country; or any other circumstances where complex/large/unusual transactions occur without an obvious economic or legal purpose. As part of EDD measures, firms should examine the background or purpose of the transaction and increase the degree of client monitoring.

SDD is an acronym of simplified due diligence – this arises in low-risk situations. For some organisations checks on public registers such as the FCA's Register can be used in a SDD process.

## **d) (4 marks)**

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) are individuals who are or have been entrusted with a prominent public function at any time in the preceding year, excluding middle-ranking or junior officials. This definition includes heads of state, government ministers, senior judicial or military officials, senior executives of state-owned corporations, and important political party officials. When dealing with PEPs, their relatives, and close associates, financial institutions must exercise enhanced due diligence due to the heightened risk of involvement in bribery, corruption, and other illicit activities.

Firms must implement robust risk management systems to identify whether a client or beneficial owner is a PEP, a family member, or a known close associate, requiring senior management approval to establish or continue business relationships with such individuals.

It is essential to take adequate measures to establish the source of wealth and source of funds involved in business relationships or transactions with PEPs. This involves conducting enhanced ongoing monitoring to detect and report suspicious activities effectively.

Given the potential for significant control or influence by PEPs, firms must adopt a proportional, risk-based approach, adjusting their due diligence measures based on the level of risk posed by each PEP.

Domestic PEPs are generally treated as a lower risk than foreign PEPs.
