## B13.

### Question

You are the FCA member of staff responsible for supervising General Research and Advisory Brokers Ltd (GRAB). The Financial Promotions Unit has drawn your attention to an advertisement published by GRAB in a major national newspaper about the shares in an unlisted oil services company.

The promotion states that:

- (i) The shares have grown by 10% more than the shares of listed companies in the same industry;

- (ii) The company has total assets of £128 million, and sales in the last year were £514 million;

- (iii) GRAB will provide a commission-free dealing service in the company's shares;

- (iv) Investors can expect to make a 25% return on the shares within 12 months.

a) Explain what issues the advertisement raises concerning compliance with relevant FCA rules and with relevant statutory provisions. _(5 marks)_

b) In view of your findings in (a) above, describe the measures which you will now take to address the issues which you have discovered. Refer to the powers available to the FCA under statute and to the tools and policies referred to in the Regulatory Processes Block of the FCA Handbook. Also explain how your proposed course of action complies with the duties of FCA and the regulatory objectives set out in FSMA. _(5 marks)_

You subsequently find out that GRAB:

- (v) Has no records of who confirmed that the promotion could be issued, when or why;

- (vi) Was dealing in the shares as principal at a bid-offer spread of 20% of the bid price;

- (vii) Did not have permission to deal as principal in any type of investment.

The firm's chief executive believes that it was issued on the approval of an account executive who specialised in the sale of life assurance and collective investment schemes. This person has since been asked to leave the firm, because he has provided a poor standard of service to clients, giving rise to frequent complaints.

You also find out that:

- (viii) The oil-services company has liabilities of £350 million;

- (ix) GRAB has invested £22 million from clients' discretionary portfolios in the company's shares, and look unlikely to recover more than 20% of their investments;

- (x) The chief executive of GRAB is also a director of the oil-services company.

c) Describe the regulatory issues arising from your new findings. _(5 marks)_

d) Explain your views on the powers, tools and policies to be used on GRAB in view of the wider range of regulatory issues that you are aware of as a result of the information set out in c). Also set out your views on the measures to be taken in respect of key approved persons at the firm and state whether you would take more stringent measures than in your answer to (b). Explain the reasons for your decision. _(5 marks)_

### Answer

a) The question was concerned with the action that the FCA can take in connection with misleading financial promotions. GRAB is an authorised person as the question refers to it being supervised by a member of the FCA staff. The relevant restrictions on financial promotion would then apply under s.21 FSMA subject to the Financial Promotion Order (FPO) and COBS. Advertisements must generally be fair, clear and not misleading.

The nature of the advertisement as a specific non-real time financial promotion should have been noticed. Relevant obligations with regard to the description of the investment, commitment and risks should have been noted with the firm's interest and accurate past performance reporting with a warning that performance may not be repeated. Other information should have included the firm's name and address or contact point. Relevant record keeping requirements could also have been referred to.

Relevant issues that arise from the advertisement include the fact that the shares are unlisted, the period over which the performance comparison was made and nature and definition of relevant industry, the relevance of the relationship between total asset and sales without further information, any conditions applicable to commission free dealing and any other charges imposed and the unsubstantiated nature of 25% projection return period.

b) The relevant supervisory and enforcement powers of the FCA should have been explained. This included the use of supervisory, warning and decision notices and the full range of enforcement powers set out in the FSMA and DEPP. The most likely initial action taken would be to request that the advertisement is initially withdrawn and substantiated. In the event that the company fails to do so, an injunction could be obtained. In the event that the promotion was considered to be misleading, appropriate disciplinary action would be taken including a fine or public and private censure.

c) The additional information provided confirms that appropriate records have not been kept contrary to COBS. The firm's interest has not been properly disclosed and it did not have necessary permission to deal as principal. The asset figure is also completely inaccurate with liabilities of £350 million not having been taken into account. The interest of the directors should also have been declared. Unlisted securities would generally be unsuitable for discretionary portfolios. Conflicts of interest arise under PRIN (1, 6 and 8), COCON (1 and 2) and SYSC10. There has also been a general failure of PRIN (1, 2, 3 and 7) and COCON senior manager rules as well as SYSC.

d) GRAB has since clearly breached a number of principles set out in PRIN as well as COCON and SYSC. Appropriate fines and public or private censure would be considered. The advertisement would also have to be immediately withdrawn through injunction if this has not already been achieved. Individual action would also be considered against the Chief Executive for a breach of PRIN and COCON as well as SYSC. Fines, censure and prohibition would all be considered.

## B16. *(Compulsory)*

### Question

You are a compliance officer working for a financial services firm focused on the sale of investment products to retail customers. A new chief executive officer (CEO) appoints a friend to become Head of Marketing for the firm. This new Head of Marketing joins the firm after a successful marketing career working in supermarkets and industrial products. After a first week at the firm, the new Head of Marketing suggests that current approaches to marketing the firm's financial service are insufficiently market focused and involve far too much bureaucracy. They propose that the form of advertising and the sales processes need to be slim-lined and become more focused on meeting sales targets and profitability metrics. As the compliance officer, this proposal causes you some professional concerns.

After several months have passed, you discover that several advertisements have been placed on the firm's website and distributed over social media for an investment product you were unfamiliar with. You were not part of this development and weren't granted sight of any of the documentation in the development of these proposed investments. The advertisement relates to investing in a 'holiday time share' in a non-EU eastern European holiday resort. The investment scheme claims to provide investors with a 'rock solid investment', 'guaranteed returns', and access to a holiday home for a few weeks of the year. In the scheme it is proposed the investors can choose to stay at the holiday resort in an eastern European non-EU nation at certain times of year or choose to sell-on the right to stay at the property to a third party. It is stated this investment will provide a 'regular and reliable source of income for all investors'. The investment scheme is presented in a very upbeat and positive manner with the suggestion that any investment in the scheme will result in 10% annual returns and has no downside risk. Indeed, no mention of the risks of the investment or any downside of the investment are contained in the advertisement. The advertisement also includes sentences stating that the investment is 'only for qualified and discerning investors' and is 'approved under section 21 of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000'.

On seeing these advertisements, you are alarmed and write to the CEO outlining your concerns as to the appropriateness of this advertisement. The CEO sets up a meeting with you, the Head of Marketing and the other senior managers. In the discussion, the Head of Marketing declares the advertisement and associated business have been very profitable for the firm, with several clients already signed up. They also state that compliance was not included in the new product development as compliance acts as an 'anti-business' element within the firm. After a fraught discussion of these issues, it is reported the CEO was aware of these sales. Eventually, the CEO decides that these advertisements and taking on clients for these investment schemes must stop immediately and asks the Head of Marketing to step down. You are asked if you can further explore the ramifications of this investment scheme and can address some of the outstanding regulatory questions which it has raised for the firm.

a) Discuss the key points which would need to be raised in response to discovering this investment scheme has already been marketed and sold to clients. In your answer, outline and discuss the regulatory priorities in the sale of investment services. _(6 marks)_

b) The advertisement of the time share investment has several deficiencies. What are these failings and why do they raise concerns? _(4 marks)_

c) After discovering the non-EU eastern European time share scheme has accepted funds from clients, what are the next steps the firm should follow? As the compliance officer how would you resolve this issue? _(4 marks)_

d) How might you judge the behaviour and actions of the compliance officer and the Head of Marketing in relation to the CISI Code of Conduct? _(6 marks)_

### Answer

a) Financial regulation on promoting investments is used to ensure the promotion is fair, clear and not misleading. This arises from repeated concerns that many customers have been mis-sold financial services and through this process have received financial services which are inappropriate for their needs. This mis-selling and the subsequent regulatory responses have been a substantial cost of the financial services industry in the last two decades. It is important to acknowledge that many customers can be vulnerable to the actions of financial services firms if promotions are delivered in a manner which may mislead and lead to sub-optimal decisions.

This can result in substantial customer harm. Reducing such customer harm is one of the key motivating elements of the regulation on financial promotions. The promotion of investments is influenced by Principles 6 and 7 of the Principles for Businesses where:

- **Principle 6** – a firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly.

- **Principle 7** – a firm must pay due regard to the information needs of its clients and communicate information to them in a way which is clear, fair and not misleading.

Section 21 of FSMA also imposes a restriction on the communication of financial promotions by unauthorised persons. In all such advertising it is imperative that the information provided is accurate, balanced, does not obscure important information and includes the risks of the investment and does not just outline benefits of the investment. Such marketing communications should also include the firm's name and be presented in a manner which is easy to comprehend by the target of the promotion activity. If comparisons are used as part of the marketing materials, the data sources should also be indicated among other requirements. It is also important that the promotional material is cognisant of the guidance on prominence whereby risk warnings and the benefits of the investment would be displayed with similar prominence, such as using the same font size.

There are multiple outcomes for inappropriate and misleading financial promotions. It is important to consider issues of consumer harm in such an answer. In cases where consumer harm seems possible, the Financial Services Act 2012 can ban and immediately remove misleading financial promotions from the market, without going through the enforcement process. Perhaps the most headline catching outcome for firms has been the requirement that many firms which have mis-sold financial services have had to provide refunds and compensation to their clients/customers. These costs can be staggering, for example, up to nearly £40bn in the case of payment protection insurance. There are other consequences we should observe in an answer. These include an awareness that only authorised persons should communicate financial services. The penalty for a breach of Section 21 of FSMA considering this concern is two years in jail and an unlimited fine. Other offences introduced by the Financial Services Act 2012 relating to the making of misleading statements and practices include:

- making misleading statements (Section 89)

- creating false or misleading impressions (Section 90), and

- making misleading statements in relation to benchmarks (Section 91).

b) There are a number of troublesome elements to the advertisement. Time share investments have been an area in which a range of regulatory and mis-selling concerns have arisen previously and should have raised wider concerns. Other issues with the advertisement include the suggestion there is a guaranteed return. The investment is a high risk and offshore unregulated investment scheme. The promotion is subject to UK regulation (COBS4) as the promotion has occurred in the UK, rather than involving an investment in a non-EU nation. There is a range of misleading and inaccurate information provided. This would include the suggestion of risk solid or guaranteed returns, and the provision of reliable and regular income when clearly this is not provided in the investment product as it is sold. Suggesting the investment has no risk and the returns would be 10% is also misleading. The statement that the scheme is approved under Section 21 of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 is particularly worrisome in that it suggests this is a protected or safe investment when clearly this is not the case. In the answer we would hope to see a discussion of a range of these issues and any further pertinent points as to the advertising of financial services.

c) In responding to this outcome, there are several actions to take. Initially it is essential to be transparent to the regulator and report that these failings have occurred. This will involve an examination of the firm and its processes. The clients of this investment need to be contacted immediately and refunded for their investment and compensated for any losses they might have incurred for this outcome. Working with the CEO and the Head of Marketing is more challenging. The CEO clearly has not involved you in all discussions of new products and the Head of Marketing has not acted in an appropriate manner. Discussion of this issue is a grey area, and the marking should reflect considered responses to the situation.

d) When considering the ethical dimensions of this case it is imperative that the answer consider behaviours needed to focus on prevention of future concerns, following the spirit of the law rather than considering whether the firm may get 'caught' for wrongdoing. For instance, has the marketing of the investment product been clear and honest, impartial, transparent, or fair? In this case all these values are breached. In relation to the code of conduct we would hope that the answer would include some consideration of CISI conduct principles of Personal Accountability, Client Focus, Respect for Market Participants, Professional Development, Aware of Capabilities, Respect Others and the Environment, and Speak Up and Listen Up. In this case the Marketing Director clearly has failed in terms of many of these, including Personal Accountability, Client Focus and Respect for Market Participants. The compliance officer clearly has chosen to speak up and listen when needed and has displayed an awareness of these principles.

More generally, how marketing has been considered under financial regulation has been an ongoing issue where a diversity of opinion has been raised. Additionally, the suggestion that marketing should be a regulated function was raised repeatedly during the PPI scandal but has since receded in its profile. This area does sometimes involve a balance between protecting customers and ensuring that market competition can occur, and firms effectively communicating the features of their services. Section 21 of FSMA also imposes a restriction on the communication of financial promotions by unauthorised persons.

In such an answer we would like to see a range of personal characteristics, expected for working in financial services, to be raised such as being competent, solvent, honest and with an intact reputation. For the 'profits come first' concern, it is essential to indicate that the responsibilities of the compliance officer go beyond those of the firm alone and do include responsibilities to act in accordance with statutory and also fiduciary expectations. Of course, it is important to indicate that all appointments should be made formally and with regards to such requirements rather than through friendship networks.

## B24.

### Question

You are the compliance officer of an FCA regulated firm which markets unregulated collective investment schemes.

a) You have been asked to advise on whether such financial products will be covered by FSMA. What do you say? _(2 marks)_

b) Explain the main restrictions that will apply with regard to the marketing and sale of such products. _(4 marks)_

c) The firm then decides to market the schemes to individuals who fall within the Certified High Net Worth individuals exemption. Advise on the action that the firm must take in this case. _(4 marks)_

d) In addition to persons who fall within the Certified High Net Worth individuals exemption, you are also asked to outline four other categories of person to whom unregulated collective investment schemes may be marketed. _(6 marks)_

Mary Cherry, one of the firm's fund managers, subsequently proposes that the Offering Memorandum which contains details of these unregulated investment schemes be displayed on your firm's website. The website is to allow investors to apply for interests in these unregulated collective investment schemes using an application form displayed on your firm's website.

e) Explain the action that may be taken to ensure that the restriction on promoting unregulated collective investment schemes contained in s238 of the FSMA is not breached. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was generally concerned with the marketing and sale of unregulated collective investment schemes.

a) Financial products are covered by the FSMA to the extent that they fall within the definition of an 'investment'. The general category of investments covered by the FSMA are listed in Schedule 2 although investment means of a 'specified kind' (s.22(1) a)) with specified meaning by order of the Treasury (s.22(5)). The categories and investments covered are accordingly as set out in the Regulated Activities Order (as amended). This includes unregulated collective investment schemes.

Reference may also have been made to the treatment of collective investment schemes under Part XVII of FSMA. A 'collective investment scheme' is, in particular, defined in s.235.

b) FSMA s.238(1) and (4) prohibits an authorised person from communicating an invitation or inducement to participate in any collective investment scheme (other than an authorised unit trust scheme, an authorised open-ended investment company or a recognised scheme) unless permitted under the FCA rules (s.238(1) and (5)) or by order of the Treasury under s.238(6). Relevant exemptions are set out in the Promotion of Collective Investment Schemes Order (FSMA 2000 (Promotion of Collective Investment Schemes)(Exemptions) Order 2001).

The promotion is accordingly only permitted to the extent that it falls within the exemptions listed in the Treasury Order or the FCA rules.

Additional reference may have been made to the specific conditions set out in Article 21 of the Exemptions Order 2001 although not necessary to obtain the main marks. Relevant conditions include the exemption only applying to specified investments, the communication being non-real-time or solicited real-time and the customer having signed an exemption statement.

c) The certified high net worth exemption requires that the recipient has a current certificate in writing or other legible form signed by their accountant or employer or be self certified.

The certificate must be signed and dated within 12 months ending on the date of the communication. The certificate must confirm that the person has had an annual income of not less that £100,000 during the financial year immediately preceding the date on which the certificate is signed or has held net assets to the value of not less that £250,000.

Net assets exclude primary residence or loan secured on the residence, rights under a qualifying contract of insurance or pension or other benefits payable on termination of service or death or retirement.

d) Other exempt persons include investment professionals, existing participants in unregulated schemes, high net worth companies and unincorporated associations, sophisticated investors or persons in the business of disseminating information.

e) The promotion should generally only be directed at relevant permitted persons. Communications should accordingly be accompanied by a restriction on directed persons, a warning against non-professional persons participating in unregulated schemes and confirmation that proper systems and procedures are maintained to prevent recipients other than investment professionals from acquiring units in such schemes.

The alternative would be to adopt some form of password protection with supporting systems and procedures to ensure that the identities of relevant persons are verified before passwords are provided.

## B36.

### Question

You work within the compliance department of a major UK banking group. Concerns have been raised at board level with regard to the conduct of a small group of sales staff within the private client department who formerly worked in the structured finance unit within the investment banking division of the bank before the crisis. The group had developed a new form of 'Structured Mortgage Product' which was sold on the basis that it would allow wealthy private clients to increase their loan to value (LTV) borrowings to up to 10 times LTV and to reduce the nominal interest rates paid to only 0.25 basis points above Bank Rate in the UK. The product had nevertheless been designed using interest rate swaps and credit default swaps (CDSs), which had been modelled on the assumption that interest rates would rise substantially towards the end of 2011 and early 2012. The purchasers of the product now find that they are locked into paying substantially in excess of UK Bank Rate for the foreseeable future and have no rights of review or cancellation under the product documentation.

a) Outline the general provisions that would apply with regard to the promotion of the Structured Mortgage Product to the wealthy clients approached and any potential exemptions. _(4 marks)_

b) Explain any specific restrictions that would have applied with regard to cold calling and any rights of a client having been cold-called. _(4 marks)_

c) Outline any separate requirements that should have been complied with in designing the new product and having it approved within the firm before launch. _(4 marks)_

d) Outline the general enforcement powers that might be available to the FCA against the firm or individuals involved. _(4 marks)_

e) Explain the advice that you would provide to the board to attempt to mitigate any damage suffered or penalties imposed. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to the promotion of Structured Mortgage Products.

(a) The general provisions applicable with regard to financial promotion should have been outlined. All financial promotions to be approved by an authorised person (s21 FSMA) and financial promotions must be 'fair, clear and not misleading' (s21 FSMA and COBS 4.2).

Relevant client classification provisions may have been referred to determine the nature of the client as either a retail or professional client (COBS 3.4 and 3.5). The following additional Handbook provisions may also have been referred to: retail client communications (COBS 4.5); Past and future information (COBS 4.5); Promotions with an overseas element (COBS 4.9); Approving financial promotions (COBS 4.10).

(b) The cold calling provisions provided for under COBS 4.8 should have been reviewed. These include the application rules (COBS 4.8.1), restrictions on cold calling (COBS 4.8.2) and non-written promotions requirements (COBS 4.8.3). Clients have a right to damages for loss suffered as a result of a breach of rule under s 138D FSMA.

(c) The relevant provisions with regard to product design and approval should have been outlined, such as with regard to New Product Due Diligence (NPDD). New product regulation was dealt with under Discussion Paper 11/1 on Product Intervention in January 2011 and Feedback Statement in 11/3 in June 2012. Products are specifically to be assessed across the whole product cycle, including with a Retail Conduct Risk Outlook Analysis and Business Model and Strategy Analysis (BMSA).

(d) The full range of the FCA's enforcement powers include:

- (i) Fines;
- (ii) Private or public censure;
- (iii) Injunction;
- (iv) Varying or withdrawing approval;
- (v) Varying or cancelling permission;
- (vi) Information gathering;
- (vii) Investigations;
- (viii) Reports by skilled persons;
- (ix) Restitution;
- (x) Bankruptcy or insolvency.

(e) Possible remedial action may include:

- (i) Carry out a full internal review of the promotions used;
- (ii) Confirm all relevant breaches of COBS 4;
- (iii) Confirm the number of possible clients affected and losses incurred;
- (iv) Contact relevant clients with offers of possible assistance;
- (v) Confirm possible applicability of Financial Services Compensation Scheme payments;
- (vi) Review any possible relevant Systems and Controls;
- (viii) Review Approved Persons arrangements;
- (ix) Review Training and Competence procedures;
- (x) Notify FCA immediately.
