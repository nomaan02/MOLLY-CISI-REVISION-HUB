## B20.

### Question

One of the rich private clients of a leading United Kingdom investment bank has become increasingly dissatisfied with the quality of the service provided. You are instructed to advise the client on any rights of redress or recovery that may be available against the firm.

- a) The client considers that it has been given the wrong advice with regard to some of the investments made as a result of which a significant amount of money has been lost especially in Asian equity and derivatives holdings. Advise the client on what action should initially be taken. _(4 marks)_

- b) Having obtained further information from another client of the firm as to the firm's knowledge of the weak prospects of the markets concerned and following the firm's failure to provide satisfactory explanation, you are asked to explain if any further rights of complaint are available. _(4 marks)_

- c) The client is then informed that the losses suffered have actually resulted from the fraudulent dealings of the unsupervised office manager in Bangkok. How should the client proceed? _(4 marks)_

- d) Following a separate investigation into the activities of the office manager, the FCA becomes aware of the extent of the loss. Can it assist the client in any way with regard to the losses suffered? _(4 marks)_

- e) In the event of the firm's subsequent collapse and closure, does the client have any separate right of claim apart from against the firm directly which is now in insolvency? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to the management of complaints and redress available to private clients in dealing with UK authorised institutions. Candidates may have dealt with the question solely in terms of rights of redress and remedy although the best answers also referred to specific instances of breach of legal or regulatory obligation. Candidates should have read the question in full initially and dealt with each subpart in turn.

- a) The structure and operation of the internal complaints procedures that are required to be maintained within all financial firms should initially have been explained under DISP. Firms must maintain written procedures that are notified to and made available to all customers on request. Eligible complaints must be dealt with under relevant policies with a senior independent member of staff considering the complaint fully and promptly and with appropriate redress being provided as necessary including compensation by the firm. Records of the decision and response must be kept for three years (five years in the case of MiFID business). The procedure must be carried out in accordance with the specified timetable with all of the relevant responses being carried out within eight weeks as specified. Complainants must also be notified of their right to apply to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) separately with firms also reported to the FCA on a 6 monthly basis of all complaints received or that no complaints had to be dealt with.

- b) The client's further rights of redress are to the FOS in the event that the Firm's internal procedures fail to deliver a satisfactory response. As much information as possible should have been provided on the nature and operation of the FOS procedure. This includes the definition of 'eligible complainants' (essentially private individuals and small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), charities and trusts). Reference may also have been made to FOS investigation, hearings, representations and reporting as well as the power of the FOS to make an award of up to £350,000 and reasonable costs.

- c) The office manager may or may not have been an approved person carrying out a controlled function within the firm. If the manager was not properly approved, there may be a right under s 71 FSMA or a separate more general right of action against the firm under s 138D FSMA for breach of relevant rules under the FCA Handbook of Rules and Guidance. There may be a separate breach of the CASS rules. Other common law rights of action may also be available including damages for breach of contract, negligence or possibly conspiracy.

- d) While the FCA will generally not become involved in individual client redress matters, it has power to require authorised firms to provide compensation or restitution without a court order or to apply for a court order for restitution requiring any person who has breached a rule or other requirement of the FSMA 2000 to provide compensation or restitution (ss 382-386 FSMA). The FCA would probably be unlikely to act in the event that adequate redress was already available under internal procedures or through the FOS ((a) and (b) above). It may nevertheless assist in the event that both of these options failed in the particular case. Action may then be available against the firm as an authorised person or against the manager individually assuming that he was still in possession of the funds misappropriated. Reference may also again be made to common law rights of action. Injunctions may also be available under s 380 FSMA if this may assist.

- e) In the event of the firm's collapse, compensation would be available from the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) under COMP. Relevant entitlements (including the definition of 'relevant firm' and 'eligible claimant') should also have been explained and the relevant compensation limits.

## B25.

### Question

You have taken over as Compliance Officer of a firm which trades securities with professional clients and eligible counterparties. The Chief Executive has appointed you because he feels that your predecessor has not done an adequate job and he is looking for you to resolve the problems and to ensure that they do not happen again. He is particularly concerned that there have been material thefts of client money, made possible by inadequate segregation of duties in the accounts department. This has been exacerbated by infrequent reconciliation of client money bank accounts. The Finance Director is to be sacked.

- a) The Chief Executive has explained the above to you at a preliminary meeting in his office. He continues: "It is so embarrassing that this has happened – I mean, it's not as if I don't care about protecting our clients' assets but I don't want the FCA to find out about this. They get so uptight about client money you know, and if they ever find out, remember they are judge, jury and executioner – they can have both of us thrown out of the industry at the drop of a hat. So make sure that our clients don't lose a penny, and see to it that the back office work out some sound procedures to make sure that this doesn't happen again."

What advice would you give to the Chief Executive on the regulatory implication of this proposed course of action? _(6 marks)_

- b) As you start to investigate the client money rule breaches, you find out that the firm holds client money in respect of on-exchange futures transactions. However, the firm's Part 4A permission does not include dealing in futures.

What are the regulatory implications of this, and what action do you recommend to the Chief Executive? _(6 marks)_

- c) The Finance Director is currently holding the Finance Function, and the Chief Executive asks you to give him a "clean" withdrawal _on Form C – Notice of ceasing to perform controlled functions_, on the grounds that dismissal is adequate punishment. Advise the Chief Executive of the regulatory implications of this course of action. _(3 marks)_

- d) This incident leads you to conduct an overall review of client files, to see what clients have opted out of client money protection. You find out that, not only do you have no documentation on file for some of the clients, but that the firm does not even have their addresses. The clients in question have all been classified as professional clients.

What are the main regulatory implications of this? _(5 marks)_

### Answer

- a) Certainly the discovery of losses of client money demonstrates that the firm is in serious breach of FCA rules. It is not stated how 'infrequent' the reconciliations of client money have been but it seems likely that the firm has failed to comply with the requirement to reconcile client money accounts as regularly as is necessary.

It is also true that the FCA, having written the rulebook, do have the power to investigate breaches of rules and to impose penalties on firms for serious breaches.

That said, the firm's own internal controls appear to have identified the problem, corrective action is being taken and the firm appears to be able to make good client losses.

The firm has an obligation under Principle 11 to be open and co-operative with the regulator and this principle extends to notifying the regulator if there have been serious breaches.

It is likely that the firm will escape with a much lesser penalty (possibly even only a private reprimand) for disclosing the discovery and corrective action now than would be the case were it discovered later by the FCA having gone unreported.

- b) When applying for Part 4A permission an applicant must request permission for each category of investment business it wishes to conduct. The FCA will then grant permission for such specified activities as it sees fit.

Whilst a firm is not in breach of the general prohibition and so is not in breach of FSMA it is in breach of FCA rules and could again find itself open to the FCA's enforcement powers.

The extent of any penalty by the FCA will depend on a variety of issues – the number of clients concerned, the length of time the firm has been in breach and the circumstances under which the firm came to be trading outside its permissions.

The FCA has the powers to impose unlimited fines and to remove a firm's permission to conduct investment business; however, it is also under the statutory duty to use its enforcement powers in a manner that is proportionate.

- c) In view of the fact that the FCA should be informed of the problems identified in a) and b) above, the proposal to give the outgoing Finance Director a 'C' certificate is a far from good idea. It would constitute actively misleading the regulator and would almost certainly result in the person signing the 'C' certificate and/or those requiring him/her to sign it being considered by the FCA not to be fit and proper persons.

It is for the FCA to decide whether the outgoing FD is a fit and proper person in relation to his future employment and not a matter of whether he has 'suffered enough'.

- d) There are a number of regulatory implications of this. Firstly not being able to identify clients' physical addresses and not having kept records of client identification puts the firm in breach of the Money Laundering Regulations. This could open up the possibility of the firm's directors becoming liable to imprisonment for up to 2 years.

The failure to record client data does also mean that we are unable to determine whether or not they are rightly categorised as professional clients or whether they should be categorised as retail clients.

There should be an immediate check to see whether any of these clients has been party to any of the futures transactions in part (b). Under the rules on suitability and appropriateness, the firm is responsible for assessing whether the client has the necessary experience and knowledge to understand the risks involved in relation to a product or service. While some experience and knowledge may be assumed in the case of professional clients, no such assumption may be made in the case of retail clients.

When taken with the weaknesses in Systems and Controls stated in parts (a) and (b) as well as the suggestion of active dishonesty in part (c) it appears that the firm is in danger of failing the fitness and properness test: as Compliance Officer I would consider both directly notifying these issues to the FCA and resigning from the firm.

## B28.

### Question

You learn that one of your investment firm's former star traders in global equity instruments has recently been sentenced by the courts for market distortion related offences. The trader had been removed from his position over a year ago although the firm had decided not to take any further action until the court case had been settled. You work for the senior compliance officer and are asked to prepare a short memorandum advising the board on the regulatory implications of the losses suffered by the trader and the trader's subsequent conviction for market related offences.

- a) Outline to the board the principal regulatory restrictions that may have been breached by the trader. _(4 marks)_

- b) Explain the general regulatory provisions that apply with regard to maintaining adequate systems and controls within the firm. _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline possible oversight and monitoring systems that may be set up above traders to limit their activities and potential losses to the firm in extreme cases. _(4 marks)_

- d) Advise the board on any interim or longer term corrective action you recommend should be taken. _(4 marks)_

- e) Explain the regulatory action that the FCA may take against the firm in the event that it fails to monitor traders and manage the exposures created adequately. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised the types of compliance review issues that arise, for example, following the conviction of Jerome Kerviel with the losses suffered at Societe Generale in Paris. A number of important compliance considerations apply apart from in connection with the conviction of the trader concerned directly.

- a) The board is to be informed about relevant possible offences. No further information is provided in the question on this. The trader may have been guilty of carrying out market abuse under MAR as well as possible market manipulation (Part 7 FSA 2012) and insider trading (CJA 1993). Relevant offences should have been outlined in a summary form.

Reference may also have been made to breach of relevant provisions in PRIN, SYSC, COCON and FIT and COND as well as specific obligations in COBS and CASS. Reference may have been made to the Listing Rules (LR), Prospectus Rules (PR) and Disclosure Guidance and Transparency Rules (DTR) as well as the obligations imposed by the specific exchange on which the trader was dealing.

- b) Relevant provisions include PRIN 2 (skill, care and diligence) and 3 (management and control) as well as SYSC and COCON.

- c) Relevant oversight and monitoring procedures are generally maintained under SYSC and COCON (above). Particular obligations may be imposed on staff dealing or arranging deals in investments including:

   - (i) Trading price and volume limits;

   - (ii) Stop loss and limit orders;

   - (iii) An order execution compliance policy;

   - (iv) Risk appetite and trading policy limits including maintaining a trading parameters policy;

   - (v) Supporting management information systems (MIS) including trade capture and trade execution systems;

   - (vi) Regular reporting of all open positions and risks;

   - (vii) Daily mark-to-marking of net positions;

   - (viii) Daily value-at-risk (VAR) calculations on the firm's trading risk for Basel II and Basel III capital compliance purposes;

   - (ix) Senior management approval for breach of trading parameters;

   - (x) Maintenance of a Personal Account Dealing (PAD) system;

   - (xi) Insider lists;

   - (xii) Front running and 'churning' limits;

   - (xiii) Appropriate remuneration and incentives policy;

   - (xiv) Appropriate conflicts of interest management policy;

   - (xv) Full compliance with relevant provisions in COBS and exchange rules; and

   - (xvi) Full separation of front and back office functions.

- d) Interim action would include confirming that no clients of the firm suffered loss as a result of the trader's activities. All appropriate corrective action should be taken to ensure that no individual loss is suffered ad otherwise the firm may be liable for damages (s138D FSMA), restitution (ss382 and 384 FSMA) and injunction (s380 FSMA) as well as other regulatory penalty (above).

- e) The firm should consider a review of all relevant systems longer term including:

   - (i) PRIN, SYSC, COCON and FIT and COND compliance;

   - (ii) Trading and risk strategy (above);

   - (iii) Relevant trading limits (above)

   - (iv) Risk management policy;

   - (v) Remuneration policy;

   - (vi) Reporting procedures;

   - (vii) Conflicts of interest policy;

   - (viii) Training and competence policy and practices;

   - (ix) Transaction reporting policies and practices;

   - (x) Capital adequacy compliance policy and practice

## B40.

### Question

You have been asked to review the conduct of the Head of Corporate Banking within a large European bank operating in London. The manager had pursued an aggressive lending policy immediately before the UK financial crisis which culminated in the government's recapitalisation of a number of large banks in October 2008. The manager had constructed a property portfolio principally made up of high risk but previously high returning commercial property mortgages. While the manager had been advised by the bank's risk committee to reduce some of the exposure in the portfolio, he had expanded the portfolio to attempt to generate further returns in the six month periods before and after October 2008. Some of the funds used to build up the portfolio had been taken from separate client investment accounts. Enlarging the portfolio would also have increased his bonus entitlement if the higher returns had continued rather than collapsed following the crisis.

You are asked to consider and explain the following:

- a) The potential breaches of High-Level Standards that may have been committed by both the bank and the manager in this case. _(4 marks)_

- b) The possible conflicts of interest that may have arisen and how these should have been managed by the bank. _(4 marks)_

- c) The provisions that should have applied with regard to determining the manager's bonus or remuneration entitlements. _(4 marks)_

- d) The sanctions that the FCA may impose against the firm and how this liability may be mitigated in practice. _(4 marks)_

- e) The further sanctions that may be imposed on the manager individually and any rights of appeal or redress available. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The fact pattern raises a number of issues similar to those involved with the management of the corporate portfolio at HBOS immediately before the financial crisis and the responsibility of the senior personnel involved. Although this is now quite an old question, it is still valuable as an example as to how the examiner likes to set questions around 'hot topics' from the real world. You are also advised to read through the commentary provided as useful 'background knowledge' on these topics.

- a) A number of potential breaches of PRIN (1,2,3,4,6,7,8,9,10 and 11) and SYSC (4,5,6,7,9,10,11 and 12) could have been committed by the bank and of COCON by the manager. Relevant provisions of COND (and especially 2.4 and 2.5) and FIT (2.1, 2.2 and 2.3) could also have been breached as well as in relation to training and competence and TC.

- b) Conflicts of interest may specifically have arisen between the bank and the holders of client investment accounts, some of which funds were used to support the portfolio. A more general conflict may also be considered to have arisen between the property portfolio division and the banks' clients more generally in light of the potential instability and risk of closure created. It may also be argued that a conflict arises between the manager's remuneration entitlements and client interests. Conflicts of interest must be managed under SYSC 10 with the firm maintaining an effective conflict of interest policy (under SYSC 10.1) including declining to act, Chinese walls, independence and disclosure. The specific issues that have arisen may also be dealt with through strengthening the firm's CASS controls and remuneration policy (paragraph c) below) and improving its compliance culture more generally.

- c) Relevant provisions with regard to remuneration are now set out in the FCA's Remuneration Code which is included within SYSC 19A. Firms are generally required to implement and maintain remuneration procedures and practices that are consistent with and promote sound and effective risk management (SYSC 19A.2.1) with a number of more specific remuneration principles being imposed on banks, building societies an investment firms (SYSC 19A.3 principles 1-12 and 12(a)-(h)). As much relevant detail as possible should have been provided within the time available. Candidates may have noted that the Code generally only came into effect from January 2011 with earlier provisions being governed by the general provisions imposed under the High Level Standards.

- d) The full range of sanctions may be available against the firm including public or private censure, financial penalty, restriction of permission and ultimate withdrawal of authorisation. Interim remedies include injunctions (s380 FSMA) with the FCA also having power to apply for restitution orders (ss382-386 FSMA). Private rights of action for losses suffered as a result of breach of a rule may also be available (s138D FSMA). Relevant factors for consideration include the nature and seriousness of the breach, the conduct of the firm and previous regulatory infractions. Reference may also have been made to the FCA's credible deterrence policy on enforcement adopted since 2007.

- e) Individuals are subject to a fine or public or private censure or possible restitution orders. Approval may also be varied or withdrawn where a person is no longer fit and proper to conduct a controlled function. Withdrawal of approval must be referred to the Regulatory Decisions Committee (RDC) which will serve a warning notice and then a decision notice, with the person concerned being able to refer the matter to the Tax and Chancery Chamber of the Upper Tribunal. A number of factors should be taken into account in determining whether withdrawal is appropriate and the size of any fine imposed. Specific issues relevant in the particular case concern the extent to which the Head of Corporate Banking acted with the full knowledge and possible support of other senior managers and the Board despite the concerns expressed by the Risk Committee. Individuals should also be treated in a consistent manner with individual, selective and disproportionate penalties being avoided.

## B54.

### Question

YouWin CFDs is an FCA regulated market maker based in London with branches in other financial centres in the UK offering contracts for differences and spread betting. YouWin CFDs recently launched a promotional offer whereby a client's initial deposit would be matched 100% by them and any trading losses would be covered up to 50% by the firm. A loophole was nevertheless discovered by some clients in the promotion, whereby they could hedge their trades with competitors and lock in profits if they got "stopped out" with YouWin CFDs, since 50% of their losses were covered. This was highlighted to the firm by certain sales staff, but since the number of abusers was small and the dealing desk was still making money, the firm did not conduct any further investigation. However, the problem escalated over 3 months and about 40% of the firm's new clients turned out to be using such trading strategies which the firm considered to be abusive. On discovering this, the firm blocked all withdrawals by these clients and marked the accounts as market abusers. No further action was taken by the firm and withdrawals were overdue by six weeks.

The head of sales issued the following statement, "We are not here to donate free money to these abusive traders. We will wait and watch. If they go to court, then we will fight them there. All sales managers are to avoid client calls whose withdrawals are pending and marked as abusive on our systems."

The firm's compliance team believe that their terms and conditions cover them adequately for this kind of action and are not concerned. The client money belonging to these so called "abusive clients" is also not currently being segregated by the firm as it believes that it is not owed to them. The regulatory authority has not been informed of any of the above developments. You work for a compliance consultancy firm who is appointed to advice YouWin CFDs on possible further action and potential regulatory action by the FCA.

- a) Which specific section of the FSMA has been breached and which of the Principles for Businesses (PRIN)? Provide explanations. _(4 marks)_

- b) What other sections of the FCA Handbook may have been breached by the firm? _(4 marks)_

- c) Has YouWin CFDs acted correctly from a Consumer Duty perspective? Justify your answer. _(4 marks)_

- d) What action can the FCA take against the senior management and compliance team of YouWin CFDs? _(4 marks)_

- e) Outline an appropriate remedial programme and the next steps that could be recommended by the consultancy firm to be taken by YouWin. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

An FCA regulated firm offering contracts for differences and spread betting has launched a promotional product with the firm covering part of the clients' losses. Some clients realised that they could lock in profits by hedging the trades with other firms. While the firm initially thought that only a few clients would attempt to take advantage of this, forty per cent of new clients adopted the strategy within three months. The firm decided to treat these clients as abusive and block withdrawals from the accounts.

Financial promotions are generally dealt with under s21 FSMA and the Financial Promotion rules as amended. New powers were brought in under the s137S Financial Services Act 2012 to ban misleading financial promotions through directions. Candidates should have referred to s21 FSMA and also the new direction powers set out in s137S FSMA as amended.

Relevant PRIN should have been listed and other Handbook sections noted including SYSC, COCON, COBS, CASS, TC and PERG. The nature of the Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC) initiative could have been explained and relevant outcomes identified. FCA penalties should have been reviewed and the most appropriate remedial action to be taken by the firm outlined.

_[Tutor note: Part c) of the original exam referred to FTOC in terms of YouWin CFD's behaviour. I have edited and re-written both the requirement, and the answer, to Part c) so as to reflect the Consumer Duty, and how this could be tested and answered. Part c) provided here, therefore, no longer follows the original Examiner's Report.]_

- a) The principal statutory section breached is s21 FSMA on financial promotion restrictions which specifies that a financial promotion may only be communicated in the course of business by an authorised person or if the content has been approved by an authorised person. Exemptions are set out in the Financial Promotions Order (FPO) as amended. The Financial Promotion rules are provided for under s137R FSMA as amended. Financial promotions must be fair, clear and not misleading (COBS 4.2.1R). Candidates may also have referred to the directions contained in s137S FSMA as amended by the FSA 2012 through the serving of a notice on the persons concerned. The firm may also have breached PRIN 1 (integrity), 3 (management and control), 6 (customers' interest), 9 (customers' relationship of trust), 10 (clients' assets) and 11 (regulatory relationship).

- b) The firm may have breached COBS 2.1 in failing to act honestly, fairly and professionally (COBS 2.1.1R) and COBS 2.2 in failing to provide appropriate information in a comprehensive form (COBS 2.2.1R). The firm may have breached COBS 4, in particular, in failing to ensure that communications are not fair, clear and not misleading (COBS 4.2.1R), COBS 4.10 in failing to maintain proper systems and controls for approving and communicating financial promotions (COBS 4.10.1 and 2) and COBS 4.11 adequate record keeping (COBS 4.11.1R). The firm may have breached SYSC 3 systems and controls and 6 on compliance.

The client may also have breached CASS 7 on client money rules including treatment of client money (CASS 7.11.1R) and client segregation (CASS 7.13.1, 2 and 3). The firm will also have breached DISP 1 in failing to treat complainants fairly including awareness (DISP 1.2), complaints handling rules (DISP 1.3), resolution rules (DISP 1.4), the next business day rule (DISP 1.5) and record keeping (DISP 1.9). Reference may also have been made to PERG 8 financial promotion and related activities and COBS 11 on dealing and managing.

The firm may also have breached COCON 1 (integrity), 2 (due skill, care and diligence), 5 (market conduct standards), and 3 (regulatory cooperation).

- c) Whether YouWin CFDs has acted correctly from a Consumer Duty perspective would depend, first of all, upon whether or not YouWin CFDs is within scope of the Consumer Duty. Assuming that consumers can take part in the services offered by YouWin CFDs, and there is nothing in the scenario to suggest that consumers are prevented from opening accounts with YouWin CFDs, then YouWin CFDs is in scope of the Consumer Duty. As such, it is clear that YouWin CFDs has not 'acted to deliver good outcomes to retail customers', as PRIN 12 requires.

_[Tutor Note: There are two methods we can employ so as to justify our belief that YouWin CFDs has failed in the Consumer Duty expectations. One would be to look at the FCA's guidance on 'good outcomes'; the other would be to examine YouWin CFDs's conduct in terms of the Cross-cutting Rules. Either would do well in terms of scoring good marks in the exam; remember to be careful with your time management here.]_

_[First method]_

The FCA's Consumer Duty guidance refers to 'good outcomes' as meeting four criteria. These are:

- The Products and Services Outcome: products are fit for purpose.

- The Price and Value Outcome: products represent fair value

- The Customer Services Outcome: customer service from firms meets customers' needs.

- The Customer Understanding Outcome: customer understanding is maintained at each step of the customer's journey.

YouWin CFDs has not ensured that its service has been correctly designed before launch; if it had, it seems clear that the product would not allow the hedging with competitors activities of the so-called 'abusers' to take place. Additionally, YouWin CFDs would have made it clear that such activities are disallowed in both its terms and conditions, and also in its promotional material.

Furthermore, YouWin CFDs has taken a decision to stop withdrawals by all 'abusive' clients without checking to see that each of these clients is actually abusing YouWin CFDs's service – this is unfair to those customers that are being accused by YouWin CFDs without evidence or justification. Finally, YouWin CFDs's decision to stop all communications with these customers, and instead wait to see if those customers take their case to court, is a breach of the requirement to provide customer service meeting those customer's needs.

_[Second method]_

PRIN 12 requires in-scope firms to comply with three 'cross-cutting rules':

**Acting in Good Faith Towards Retail Customers** – Firms must act with honesty, fairness, and openness in all dealings with retail customers. Actions should align with what customers reasonably expect, and this principle should be embedded at the design stage of products and services to ensure fairness from the beginning.

It is clear that YouWin CFDs is breaking this rule – it is not fair to target a large proportion of customers (over 40%) and claim they are 'abusers' without evidence and without a proper investigation. Nor is it 'open' to withdraw from communication with these customers, unless they take YouWin CFDs to court.

**Avoiding Foreseeable Harm** – Firms are responsible for ensuring that retail customers understand and accept the inherent risks in products and services. They must take proactive steps to prevent foreseeable harm within their control and must not exploit customers' vulnerabilities, behavioural biases, or lack of financial literacy.

YouWin CFDs should have carried out proper testing of its products before launch, and identified the so-called 'abuse' of hedging exposures with other firms, so as to be able to warn customers that this action would be perceived as abusive by YouWin CFDs, triggering freezing of their account.

**Enabling and Supporting Retail Customers to Pursue Their Financial Objectives** – Firms should provide relevant information and understanding to support customers in making their own financial decisions. This approach empowers customers to take responsibility for their choices, fostering an environment of trust and confidence in the firm's products and services.

Spreadbetting accounts are necessarily much more complex than many retail customers are used to, and so they would find these accounts difficult to understand. YouWin CFDs should have provided adequate factsheets and risk warnings to customers so that such customers would have had a clear idea of how the product worked before investing.

- d) The FCA may proceed against the firm by issuing a direction under s137S FSMA as amended. A direction may be issued where the FCA considers that the communication or approval breaches the financial promotion rules with the direction requiring withdrawal, refraining from making the communication, publishing details of the direction or taking any other action (s137S (1) and (2)). Directions generally take effect immediately although the person may make representations within the period specified (s137S (4) and (6)). The FCA may revoke the direction following representations (137S (8)).

A person breaching s21 is guilty of an offence and liable for up to six months imprisonment (on summary conviction) or two years (on indictment) or a fine or both (s25 (1) FSMA). Agreements entered into in consequence of an unlawful communication are nevertheless unenforceable against the other party who is entitled to recover any money or property paid and compensation for loss sustained (s30 (2) (a) and (b) FSMA as amended). The FSA may impose a public or private censure (s205) or general financial penalty (s206 (1)). An action for damages may be available under s138D FSMA as amended. Injunctions are available under s380 and restitution orders under s382 FSMA. The firm's permission may be varied and authorisation withdrawn if necessary.

- e) The firm should consider the following remedial action:

   - (i) Suspend or cancel the promotion until a full investigation and review have been carried out;

   - (ii) Conduct an investigation to determine whether any statutory or regulatory provisions have been breached;

   - (iii) Determine whether any clients have suffered loss and offer compensation as appropriate;

   - (iv) Advise the FCA accordingly and cooperate fully under PRIN 11 and COCON 4;

   - (v) Review the firm's systems and controls under SYSC;

## B94.

### Question

A well-respected financial firm is due to be audited in relation to the client money CASS regulations. As you investigate, you are asked to comment on a range of questions as to the importance of this regulatory exercise and its importance for the firm. Prepare a short report considering the following questions raised by the firm:

a) What is the role of the annual CASS assurance audit? Discuss how this event is undertaken and what its purpose is. _(5 marks)_

b) What information should be included in the CASS Resolution Pack? _(5 marks)_

c) Summarise the regulatory expectations when a financial firm, other than a depositary institution, holds client funds. _(5 marks)_

d) Why are the rules for holding client money important? Discuss wider consumers' and firms' perspectives on why compliance with CASS rules is important. _(5 marks)_

### Answer

a) If a firm holds client money and assets, then it must have an annual CASS assurance audit. While the audit assurance report is submitted to the FCA, the standards, format and assurance standards of the report are overseen by the Financial Reporting Council (FRC). The annual CASS assurance report is required under conditions detailed in the Supervision Manual (SUP). This report examines the business model of firms holding client assets, the internal controls and procedures and controls concerning clients' funds, and methods used to consider any deficiencies which may develop. The report will include sections on where there have been no breaches and a separate element where breaches have occurred.

The main objectives of the CASS Assurance Standard as stated in the workbook are to:

1. improve the quality of CASS audits and other CASS assurance engagements.

2. adequately support and challenge CASS auditors when undertaking CASS assurance engagements and to define the nature and extent of the work effort required for both reasonable assurance and limited assurance CASS assurance engagements without undermining the importance of the CASS auditor's judgment.

3. support the objectives of the FCA's client assets regime regarding the effective safekeeping of client assets and client monies and, in particular, to guard against systemic failure of the CASS regime.

4. manage the expectations of the management of firms that hold client assets, and thirdparty administrators (when a CASS auditor is engaged to provide assurance to the FCA on client assets that it handles or accounts for).

5. support the effective training of CASS auditors by both the accounting bodies and other training organisations.

6. help to establish realistic expectations regarding the integrity of the UK client assets regime with the beneficial owners of client assets, and

7. underpin the effectiveness of the FRC's enforcement and disciplinary activities with respect to CASS assurance engagements.

b) A CASS Resolution Pack will enable firms that hold client money and client assets to maintain and retrieve certain documents and information relating to the assets and cash that they hold on behalf of clients in the event of a crisis, such as insolvency.

As stated in the workbook the following documents and information must be immediately retrievable:

- **Institutions** – identification of the institution appointed to hold client money, to hold or control client money in a client transaction account or for the deposit of safe custody assets.

- **Individuals** – identifies each senior manager and director and any other individual, and the nature of their responsibility within the firm, who are critical/important to the performance of operational functions related to any regulatory obligations, and the individual to whom the firm has appointed the carrying out/performance of the CASS operational oversight function.

- **Agreement with institutions** – a copy of the executed agreement, including any side letters, or

- **Other agreements** – used to clarify/modify the terms of the executed agreement, between the firm and institutions, that relate to the holding of client money or safe custody assets – including written notification or trust acknowledgement letters sent/received.

- **Custody assets** – the most recent internal and external reconciliation relating to safe custody assets.

- **Client money** – the most recent internal and external reconciliation relating to client money. The following documents and information must be retrievable as soon as possible, but within no later than 48 hours.

- **Master documents** – containing information sufficient to retrieve each document in the firm's CASS resolution pack.

- **Agents** – documents identifying each appointed representative, tied agent, field representative or other agent of the firm which received client money or safe custody assets in its capacity as the firm's agent.

- **Group structure** – documents identifying and providing certain other information with respect to each group member and/or third party (a 'third-party outsourcer') involved in the performance of operational aspects of the firm's obligations under the client money/client assets rules.

- **Service providers** – copies of each executed agreement, side letter and/or amendment agreement with any third-party outsourcer.

- **Access to service providers** – documents which describe how to gain access to relevant information and effect a transfer of client money or safe custody assets held by each third-party outsourcer.

- **Procedures** – copy of the firm's manual in which procedures for the management, recording and transfer of client money and safe custody assets that the firm holds are found.

- **Due diligence** – records of the grounds upon which the firm satisfied itself of the appropriateness of its selection of each third party with which it deposits safe custody assets and client money.

- **Client agreements** – if safe custody assets have been used for securities financing transactions, a copy of the consent from clients to use the assets and the assets used (number/type).

- **Records and accounts** – documents which enable the firm to distinguish client money and safe custody assets of one client from those of another client or the firm's own money.

- **Reconciliation methods** – explanation of the methods of internal reconciliation of client money balances.

- **Client categorisation and agreements** – copies of all agreements with retail and professional clients, records of client categorisation and supporting documentation and relevant documentation relating to re-categorising of a client.

c) The CASS rules considering the custody and safeguarding of client money and client assets are contained in the FCA CASS sourcebook. These rules exist to ensure that firms take adequate steps to protect those client assets for which they are responsible. It is expected that sufficient client consent has been provided for actions involving their funds. This is undertaken through the mandate systems operating in this area. This is a point of major concern when wider financial crises occur and the rules do much to add confidence in the financial system. Within CASS, the requirement to segregate client money from a firm's own money is aimed at ensuring that, if the firm fails, client money will not be used to repay its creditors. Usually this is done by ensuring that it is placed promptly in a designated client money account with a bank and ensuring that the bank treats client money as separate from the firm's own money.

As stated in the workbook: CASS 6 contains the custody rules that apply when a firm holds financial instruments for a client during MiFID business, and when it is safeguarding and administering investments during non-MiFID business. CASS 7 contains the client money rules, applying to a UK MiFID investment firm either when:

- it holds client money during its UK MiFID business, or

- in the course of investment business that is non-UK MiFID business in respect of any investment agreement entered into, or to be entered into with or for a client, or

- it holds money in respect of which CASS 5 – Client Money: Insurance Mediation Activity applies, and the firm elects to apply the provisions of CASS 7.

Firms must, when holding safe custody assets belonging to clients, make adequate arrangements to safeguard clients' ownership rights, especially in the event of the firm's insolvency and to prevent the use of safe custody assets belonging to a client on the firm's own account, except with the client's express consent. Firms must also introduce adequate organisational arrangements to minimise the risk of loss or diminution of a client's safe custody assets. When a firm accepts client money, it must take the necessary steps to ensure that the client money is deposited in accordance with the requirement of CASS 7.13.3, which stipulates that a firm, on receiving any client money, must promptly place this money into one or more accounts opened with an appropriately regulated bank or qualifying money market fund.

After the great financial crisis many changes were made to the CASS rules. These included:

- Increased reporting to clients

- Holding client money with group banks

- Prohibiting the use of general liens in custodial agreements

- Creating a new controlled function with specific responsibility for client money and assets

- Introducing a Client Money and Assets Return (CMAR)

d) It is of course important to comply with rules and the consequences on noncompliance. Firms and individuals which breach CASS and associated regulations are liable to a wide range of regulatory sanctions from fines to prohibitions. It is also important to discuss market confidence and transparency. These rules exist to ensure that firms take adequate steps to protect those client assets for which they are responsible. This is very important when a firm collapses to ensure clients do not lose their money. This is a point of major concern when wider financial crises occur and the rules do much to add confidence in the financial system.

## B97.

### Question

The complaints handling division of a major financial services firm receives a disturbing phone call. In the phone call, a customer states that they have purchased an investment product from your firm through an agent. The customer elaborates that on the maturity of the product they contacted the agent as to the payout. The customer then claims they were told they were not entitled to a payout from the product due to an unexplained internal process. The customer is very annoyed, claims the agent has acted deceitfully and has suggested contacting the Financial Ombudsman Service unless this matter is resolved promptly. Following up on the case, you contact the agent who confirms the customer was sold an investment product. Further the agent argues the customer is not entitled to a payout due to the agent's internal discussions and policies. Further the agent describes the customer as a troublemaker, has indicated they are ignoring all correspondence from the customer and has strongly argued that their decision as an agent of the firm must not be overruled, nor should the customer receive a payout.

- a) What are the expectations of the financial firm when managing its agents? What concerns do you observe in this case? _(5 marks)_

- b) What actions should the firm undertake in this scenario in relation to the agent? _(5 marks)_

- c) What actions should the firm undertake in this scenario in relation to the customer? _(5 marks)_

- d) Why are fair and unbiased complaints handling procedures important for the financial services industry? _(5 marks)_

### Answer

**a) (5 marks)**

**What are the expectations of the financial firm when managing its agents? What concerns do you observe in this case?**

In many regards the treatment of agents from a regulatory perspective is similar to that of all employees. The firm must ensure that there are appropriate internal systems, policies and procedures existing for all employees and agents. It is also important for a firm to ensure an appropriate business culture operates for all their agents. Like all employees, agents should operate with honesty and competence and have the skills, knowledge and expertise necessary to fulfil their responsibilities. The firm is responsible for establishing the policies and procedures to ensure compliant behaviour by their agents for financial compliance and for countering financial crime.

Concerns in this case include the agent defining their own policies, treating the customers inappropriately and not following appropriate complaint handling procedures. More widely there is an issue of not treating customers fairly arising in the case, such as the use of irrelevant information in how the customer is judged (such as describing them as a troublemaker). It is expected that firms have appropriate written procedures for handling expressions of dissatisfaction. These procedures should be followed regardless of whether the complaint is oral or written, whether the complaint is justified or not, as long as it relates to the firm's provision of a financial service. This should occur whether the business is sourced directly or through an agent.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made up to five marks.

**b) (5 marks)**

**What actions should the firm undertake in this case in relation to the agent?**

The firm needs to undertake several actions in relation to the agent. First it needs to explain to the agent that they do not define the policies and procedures for the sale and administration of the firm's financial services. Rather they are obliged to follow the procedures of the firm. The complaint and contacts from the customer need to be recorded and responded to appropriately, following the firm's procedures. In practice the firm needs to deal with complaints promptly and fairly with effective and transparent procedures. Once the firm has received a complaint, it must investigate competently, diligently, and impartially, obtaining additional information as necessary. As it is important to consider the root cause of the complaints through analysing the causes of complaints, an investigation of the agent may be considered. If a complaint is made it is good practice to consider if other products and customers have been affected. Therefore, a wider review of the business undertaken by the agent may be appropriate. It is important to determine if more cases similar in nature to the one raised has occurred, through the agent or indeed other agents working for the firm.

It is important to keep records as to all complaints. The record keeping of complaints by the agent also needs to be investigated. Records must also be kept as to the complaints, for regulatory monitoring and to comply with any future contacts with the FOS. These records should include the name of the complainant, the substance of the complaint, and details of any correspondence between the firm and the complainant, including details of any redress offered by the firm. Potentially the agent may be encouraged to join a professional body and undertake further training. Training can also be provided by the firm. In light of worrisome circumstances seen in this case the firm may also wish to reassess its employment of this agent.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made up to five marks.

**c) (5 marks)**

**What actions should the firm undertake in this case in relation to the customer?**

The firm needs to contact the customer, appropriately investigate the complaint, and notify the customer of their right to go to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) if appropriate. To bring the case to a suitable conclusion, the firm should follow the following process. Once a complaint has been received by the firm, a prompt written acknowledgement should be provided assuring the customer the complaint has been received and is being dealt with. The firm must keep the customer informed as to the progress of the complaint investigation and resolution. The complaint needs to be addressed in eight weeks. If the firm rejects the complaint, they must enclose a copy of the FOS standard explanatory leaflet and inform the customer that if they remain dissatisfied, they may refer the complaint to the FOS within six months. In this case, it must be determined whether the payout from the product should be made and, if this is justified, the payment should be made promptly. Potentially, and considering the issues raised in the complaint, the firm may also consider providing redress to the customer if this is deemed appropriate.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made up to a maximum of five marks.

**d) (5 marks)**

**Why are fair and unbiased complaints handling procedures important for the financial services industry?**

Effective complaints handling is an essential aspect of the financial services industry and critical to fulfilling many of the regulatory principles underlying the UK form of financial regulation. These complaints handling procedures are critical in ensuring that customers are treated fairly, and that financial services business is undertaken in a transparent and fair environment. Crucial in this process is ensuring that trust exists in this industry and when interacting with financial firms.
