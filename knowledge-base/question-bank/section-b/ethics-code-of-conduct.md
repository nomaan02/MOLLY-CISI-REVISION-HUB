## B18.

### Question

Emma is a senior financial adviser at a wealth management firm. One of her long-standing clients, David, has expressed interest in a high-risk investment opportunity that promises substantial returns. Emma is aware that David has limited investment experience and a lower risk tolerance. However, her firm has an internal sales target for this investment, and senior management has encouraged advisers to promote it aggressively. Emma is facing pressure to meet her targets and is aware that she may receive a significant bonus if she successfully sells this product to David.

Michael, the firm's compliance officer, learns about the situation and raises concerns. He reminds Emma of the firm's responsibility to adhere to FCA regulations and ensure that all investment recommendations align with clients' risk profiles and financial goals. Michael warns that proceeding with the sale without fully assessing suitability could have regulatory and ethical repercussions.

- a) Identify and evaluate the ethical issues present in this scenario.

_(8 marks)_

- b) How should Emma apply the CISI Code of Conduct in handling this situation?

   - _(6 marks)_

- _c)_ What potential consequences could Emma, Michael, and their firm face if she proceeds with selling the product to David? _(6 marks)_

### Answer

This question posed a case study involving a situation related to ethical and regulatory conflict between sales pressure and client suitability, highlighting the responsibility of financial advisers to act in the client's best interests despite internal incentives.

- a)   There are several ethical issues present in this scenario. Emma is being encouraged to sell a product that is not aligned with the client's risk profile or investment needs, which raises concerns around mis-selling and a potential breach of duty to the client.

Pressuring an adviser to recommend an unsuitable product represents a serious ethical failure. There is also a clear conflict of interest. Emma's incentive to meet sales targets appears to be compromising her ability to act in the best interest of her client. The scenario suggests that the firm's culture is placing commercial objectives ahead of client outcomes, which undermines trust in the advice process and could cause financial harm to the client. A badly designed bonus scheme is likely contributing to the issue, as it encourages behaviour that prioritises product sales over suitability. This kind of scheme can create systemic ethical risks, especially when combined with pressure from line managers. Emma may feel unable to challenge the firm's expectations, which further exacerbates the risk of poor client outcomes. The ethical environment of the firm is central to this problem. Firms have a responsibility to promote a culture where employees feel empowered to prioritise the client's interests and uphold professional standards. In this case, the firm is failing to support ethical conduct and may be placing its staff in situations where doing the right thing is made more difficult. From a professional ethics perspective, Emma should reflect on the CISI's principles, including integrity, fairness, and acting in the client's best interests. While she may be under pressure, her obligation is to provide suitable, objective advice to her client and to escalate any concerns if she feels her professional judgement is being compromised.

- b) The adviser should apply the CISI Code of Conduct by acting with integrity and ensuring the client is given clear and accurate information about the product, including the risks and likely returns. Transparency is essential to avoid misleading the client and to maintain trust. The adviser must also demonstrate client focus by placing the client's interests above the firm's sales targets, ensuring that any recommendation is based on what is suitable for the client's financial needs and risk appetite. Professional competence is key, and the adviser should only proceed after fully assessing the client's situation, goals, and ability to absorb losses. Accountability is also important. The compliance officer must document their concerns and escalate them to senior management to help prevent regulatory breaches and reinforce the firm's commitment to ethical conduct. If the product is sold and later deemed unsuitable, serious consequences could follow. The adviser may face disciplinary action, loss of FCA approval, or a prohibition from working in financial services. If it is shown that the sale was driven by internal pressure rather than client need, this could be treated as a breach of professional standards. The manager who encouraged the sale may also face individual sanctions under the Senior Managers and Certification Regime, including personal fines or regulatory action. The compliance officer could come under scrutiny if it emerges that concerns were not properly followed up. The firm itself could be penalised financially, required to compensate affected clients, and forced to undertake a mis-selling review or root cause analysis. The FCA may also impose additional controls or even commission an independent skilled person reviews to assess the firm's systems and controls.

- c) If the product is sold and is later deemed unsuitable, there could be serious consequences for the individuals involved and for the firm. The adviser may face professional and regulatory sanctions, such as disciplinary action, withdrawal of FCA authorisation, or even a prohibition from working in regulated financial services. Selling an unsuitable product under sales pressure may amount to a serious breach of professional standards and could significantly affect future career prospects. A line manager who encouraged the sale may face similar consequences. If it is established that pressure was knowingly applied to act against the client's interests, the manager could be held personally accountable under the Senior Managers and Certification Regime, which may result in personal fines, prohibition, or reputational damage. The compliance officer, although not directly responsible for ensuring regulatory compliance, has a duty to support the firm's framework. If aware of misconduct and failing to escalate or address it, that inaction could prompt regulatory scrutiny and questions about the strength of the compliance function. The firm itself could also face serious outcomes. If the sale causes financial loss or harm to the client, the FCA may impose a financial penalty and require the firm to carry out a mis-selling review, root cause analysis, and client redress. The firm may also be instructed to improve its internal controls, retrain staff, and strengthen product governance. In more severe cases, the FCA could suspend or restrict permissions, or appoint a skilled person under section 166 to conduct an independent review.

## B38.

### Question

Bob is an experienced senior manager who has spent most of his working life in the securities industry, during which time he has been an approved person and held Board level positions in FCA regulated firms for many years. He has always enjoyed good relationships with other industry practitioners, as well as serving on an FCA advisory panel regarding the RDR.

Bob is now semi-retired and is a non-executive director with Optimist, a small wealth manager, where he has been asked to undertake an informal review of how they meet customer due diligence obligations, such as 'know your customer', when taking on board a new client and subsequently monitoring their transactions.

Bob's initial findings are that Optimist's processes are not of the standard suggested by the industry guidance and he reports his findings to the non-executive Chairman of the Audit Committee and Board Chairman, Kevin.

Kevin is not surprised and explains that this confirms his beliefs, which is why he asked for the review. Kevin mentions to Bob that he has discussed this matter informally at an industry dinner with a mutual colleague of theirs, Guy, who now works in the supervisory division of the FCA.

Bob is asked to present his findings to Adam, the Chief Executive, who angrily refutes the findings saying that Optimist's processes are in line with all the firms that he has ever worked for and states he does not intend to waste resources on this or worry his clients over trifling administrative matters.

Bob accepts that no customers appear to have been disadvantaged, and he has no evidence of the firm being associated with financial crime, but says there is not sufficient management information to confirm the issue either way. Bob's report is then discussed at the Audit Committee where, in view of Adam's apparent hostility to Bob's report, Kevin thanks Bob for his "excellent wok, which has provided much food for thought" adding that, in due course, it may be felt appropriate to look at some of the individual parts of the report.

As a result of this decision Bob tells Kevin that he feels that since the main focus of his position on the board was the production of the report, he does not feel that it is in the interest of either party for him to remain on the board and he tenders his resignation as a non-executive director of Optimism.

Shortly after that, Bob receives an email from Guy saying that he understands that Bob has left Optimist and asks him if "he is aware of anything that he would like to disclose". Bob is somewhat surprised by this and wonders whether it would be unethical for him to respond at all, now that he has left Optimist. Nevertheless, he considers various possibilities, and writes them down so that he can mull over how best to proceed. The four courses of action which he considers are:

- Ignore the email and do nothing;

- Respond saying that he would be happy to meet Guy to discuss anything specific that Guy may have in mind;

- Respond saying that he left Optimist as a result of a disagreement over policy;

- Respond saying that he had nothing specific to raise, but then to phone the whistleblowing line to report Optimist's poor standards regarding basic compliance issues.

Explain your considerations in each case and the conclusion you reach as to why the course of action is, or is not, your preferred response.

_(20 marks)_

### Answer

This question raised a number of difficulties with regard to the conduct of a retiring nonexecutive director in dealing with senior staff within a regulated firm and with the FCA. The question could have been dealt with in a number of different ways. Candidates should generally have identified the principal regulatory and ethical issues that arose and then have commented on each of the four possible response options referred to.

Bob had been specifically engaged to prepare an internal report on Customer Due Diligence (CDD) that identified possible failings, although without any confirmation of disadvantage or financial crime. Initial reference could have been made to the need to verify customer identity (ID) and collect relevant 'know your customer' (KYC) information under the Money Laundering Regulations 2007. This may either be carried out on a standard CDD, Simplified Due Diligence (SDD) or Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) basis with additional requirements applying in regard to continuing monitoring.

The failure by the Board Chairman (and Chairman of the Audit Committee) as well as by the Chief Executive to consider the findings contained in the report seriously raised a number of potential breaches of PRIN (1,2,3,6,7,8,9,10 and 11), SYSC (6,7,10 and 18) and COCON (1,2,3, 5, and senior manager rules) as well as potentially COND and FIT. Reference could also have been made to certain COBS including COBS 3 (client classification) and COBS 9 (suitability).

In terms of relevant ethical provisions, reference could have been made to the FSA's / FCA's general approach to ethics (Discussion Paper October 2002) and draft Code of Ethics (CP09/18), Competence and Ethics document (CP10/12) and the Conduct Rules (COCON). Reference may also have been made to the earlier speeches by Howard Davies and Hector Sants' speech on 'Regulatory Culture' in October 2010. Members of the CISI are also subject to the eight principles set out in its Code of Conduct.

Candidates should then have commented on each of the four possible conduct options listed.

## a) No Response

Making no response as a former non-executive director, approved person and member of the CISI cannot be considered a credible option. Former approved persons can be held responsible for their conduct for up to three years. Notification of Bob's resignation would presumably have been given to the FCA on Form C.

## b) Informal Meeting

While agreeing to attend an informal meeting with Guy may appear to be a credible option, especially in cooperating with the regulator authority under PRIN 11 and COCON 3, Bob is no longer a member of staff, Guy has not approached him formally and potential conflicts of interest arise with Guy appearing to have been a former member of staff of the regulated firm concerned. Potential difficulties also arise with regard to continuing contractual confidentiality obligations even after Bob has left the firm.

Bob could agree to meet Guy although this would have to be on a formal basis with any information being provided in response to formal requests under relevant statutory powers and preferably with, at least, one other member of the FCA staff present including from the firm's line management team within the FCA.

## c) Departure Over Policy Disagreement

Informing Guy that Bob had left over a policy disagreement is a possible option as this is accurate in fact and invites Guy subsequently to approach the firm formally and ask for additional disclosure and information on the nature of the disagreement which would have revealed the difficulties over the lack of proper treatment of the findings of the CDD report.

The issue does arise as to whether Bob should have taken more action before resigning. He had nevertheless discussed the matter with the Chief Executive directly (and the Chair of the Audit Committee) and the Board although he could strictly have referred the matter separately to compliance. As there was no actual evidence of customer disadvantage or financial crime, there did not appear to have been any offence committed in connection with money laundering failure to disclose.

Comment could have been made on the fact that Kevin was Chair of the Board and of the Audit Committee which may be considered to amount to a conflict and undermine his ability to carry out both roles adequately.

- d) Whistleblowing under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA) and SYSC18 Whistleblowing may also appear to have been an attractive option, although the question states that Bob would initially have had to state that no issues had arisen which would have been inaccurate, with whistleblowing generally being used by current rather than former members of the staff. This may have been relevant if Bob was looking for reinstatement, although this is not referred to in the facts provided. If Bob was considering whistleblowing, his could have been affected while he was still at the firm. There is also an issues as to whether there was sufficient evidence of wrongdoing and impropriety, although Bob is principally concerned with the manner in which his report was considered by the Chief Executive, Board and Audit Committee.

The most attractive option then may appear to have been c) on the basis of the limited information provided, subject to further formal action being taken by the FCA. Bob could discuss the matter with the CISI or with a solicitor if he wished to obtain independent advice.

## B52.

### Question

William and Alana work in the sales team at Wheelwright brokers, where the team is known for its supportive culture and positive working environment. William, who is particularly popular, is five years older than Alana, whom he was instructed to mentor when she joined the firm, as a result of which they have become good friends in the office and occasionally socialise outside work.

William invites Alana to a dinner which he is hosting at a restaurant for friends and acquaintances and Alana is slightly surprised to find out that she is the only other person from Wheelwright's at the dinner. Later during the evening as the men are bragging about their sporting achievements and contacts, William throws in that he has been invited on an all-expenses paid trip to the next US Masters Golf tournament in Atlanta. Alana, not being at all interested in golf, excuses herself from the table for a few minutes and hears no more.

A few weeks later as Alana passes William's desk she hears him discussing commission payments with a major new client and is struck by the fact that William seems to be indicating a scale of payments far outside the firms published rates. She also hears William mentioning Atlanta, saying that his visit to the golf tournament had been "tacked on" to his much talked about "boys group" holiday to New York so it was "under the radar." Alana is surprised at what she has overheard and wonders whether she should do anything about what she thinks was said.

- a) Discuss the risks posed by William's behaviour.

_(4 marks)_

- b) What policies and procedures might firms have in place to deal with situations of this nature? _(3 marks)_

- c) Discuss the various courses of action that could be open to Alana.

_(4 marks)_

- d) Briefly discuss the advantages and disadvantages of each of these.

_(4 marks)_

- e) How would you recommend that Alana acts following what she thinks she has heard and why? _(5 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to the conduct of a sales broker and the responsibility of a co-broker. Candidates were required to identify relevant issues, regulatory and ethical requirements, implementing firm policies and procedures and comment on the options available to the co-broker.

Candidates had to identify the risks generated by William's behaviour. This was principally concerned with identifying the facts that raised issues from a compliance and ethical perspective. Candidates could also have referred to the FCA's conduct risk framework or to background papers on financial ethics. They could also have referred to the nature of risk and compliance more generally.

Relevant procedures should have been identified. Alana essentially had four choices in terms of taking no action, discussing the matter with William, raising concerns within all of the relevant office procedures or taking the matter outside the firm. Candidates were invited to discuss the relative advantages and disadvantages of each. Candidates then had to decide on the most appropriate action.

- a) Candidates were asked to identify the relevant risks. This could have been dealt with in a number of ways with no specific answer being required. Candidates could have identified relevant factual concerns that may have presented risks to the individuals involved and the firm, the specific regulatory and ethical provisions that may have been breached or the more general financial risks involved as discussed in the CISI Regulation & Compliance workbook.

Relevant issues from the facts provided that may be of concern include:

- (i)   No specific difficulties may arise with regard to the dinner as this was for friends and acquaintances rather than clients;

- (ii)   Reference to the 'all-expenses paid trip' to a golf tournament appears unusual for a sales broker;

- (iii)   Commission payments outside the grade scales for the new client;

- (iv)   The possible connection between the golf trip and new client commission rates;

- (v)   The reference to 'under the radar' suggests that whatever is involved is outside approved firm policies and procedures;

- (vi)   The possible validity in terms of firm policy of the separate 'boys group' holiday to New York and its connection with the other matters raised;

- (vii)  William's apparent failure to disclose the gift or benefit;

- (viii) The possibility of previous misconduct by William including failure to disclose earlier gifts or benefits;

- (ix)   Possible damage to the new client relationship;

- (x)   Potential regulatory breaches of PRIN, SYSC, COCON, FIT, COND, COBS and TFC;

- (xi)   Possible misappropriation of funds from the new client by William to fund the golf trip;

- (xii)  Possible breach of conduct by Alana through failure to disclose;

- (xiii) Possible regulatory sanctions against the firm;

- (xiv) Possible reputational damage to the firm.

The specific regulatory and ethical provisions that may have been breached include:

- (i) PRIN (integrity), PRIN 2 (skill, care and diligence), PRIN 3 (management and control), PRIN 6 (customers' interests), PRIN 7 (client communications), PRIN 8 (conflicts of interest), PRIN 9 (customers' trust) and PRIN 11 (regulatory relationship);

- (ii)   SYSC 2 (senior management arrangements), SYSC 3 (systems and controls), SYSC 4 (general organisation requirements), SYSC 5 (employees), SYSC 6 (compliance), SYSC 7 (risk control), SYSC 10 (conflicts of interest) and SYSC 19a (Remuneration Code);

- (iii)  Threshold conditions s41(2) and (Schedule 6 FSMA) and COND (Suitability);

- (iv)   COCON 1 (integrity), COCON 2 (due skill, care and diligence), COCON 5 (market conduct), COCON 3 (regulatory relationship);

- (v)   FIT (honesty, integrity and reputation and competence and capability);

- (vi)   Possible breach of COBS 11 (use of dealing commission);

- (vii)   Possible breach of Treating Customers Fairly outcomes;

- (viii)   Possible breach of CASS through misappropriation of client funds;

- (ix)   Breach of FCA conduct risk policy;

- (x)   Breach of FCA ethical policy;

- (xi)   Breach of CISI Code of Conduct including CISI 1 (act honestly and fairly), CISI 2 (integrity), CISI 3 (regulatory observance), CISI 4 (market integrity), CISI 5 (conflict of interest), CISI 6 (professional competence), CISI 7 (decline to act) and CISI 8 (highest personal and professional standards).

Candidates may also have referred to the specific risks that William's activities created. These include credit, market and liquidity risk, investment risk, operational risk, enterprise risk, business risk and corporate governance and risk oversight. Reference may have been made to the ten principles imposed under the BIS paper on Compliance and the compliance function in banks (April 2005) and the MiFID new compliance function requirements and the MiFID Implementing Directive and relevant European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) guidelines in 2012 although this was not necessary.

Candidates may also have referred to possible enforcement action by the FCA outlining all possible investigatory and other penalties that may have been imposed.

- b) The three most important firm policies and procedures were possibility on Gifts and Inducements, Remuneration and Conflicts of Interest (with the question having three marks). The full range of relevant policies and procedures may have included:

   - (i) Gifts and Inducements Policy (including limits and reporting requirements);

   - (ii)   Remuneration Code;

   - (iii)  Conflicts of Interest Code;

   - (iv)  General Compliance Manual;

   - (v) Client Commission Rate policy;

   - (vi)  Treating Customers Fairly policy;

   - (vii)  Conduct Risk policy;

   - (viii)  Bribery and Corruption Act policy;

   - (ix)  Systems and Controls (SYSC) policy;

   - (x) Governance policy (if separate);

   - (xi)  Suitability and Appropriateness policy (if separate);

   - (xii)  Training policy;

   - (xiii)  Mentoring policy (if separate from Training);

   - (xiv)  Disciplinary policy;

   - (xv)  Whistleblowing policy (under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA));

   - (xvi)  Reporting policy;

   - (xvii)  Commission Rate policy (if separate);

   - (xviii) Compliance policy;

   - (xix)  CISI Reporting or Speak Up policy.

- c) Alana may consider taking, at least, four principal courses of action (with the question having four marks) although these may include various sub-alternatives:

   - (i) Take no action;

   - (ii) Discuss the matter with William;

   - (iii)  Refer the matter to her Line Manager, failing which escalate this through the Compliance Department and then Non-Executive Directors (NEDs) or Audit Committee;

   - (iv)  Whistleblowing under the PIDA including referring the matter to the FCA or CISI.

Alana may also want to take independent legal advice. No fixed answer was necessary.

- d) Candidates may have discussed the advantages and disadvantages of the options they identified in different ways. The principal issues that arise include:

   - (i) Take no action;

      - Alana is an approved person (presumably CF30 (Customer-dealing function)) and cannot responsibly take no action. She does not appear to carry out a significant management or governing function although she cannot support or condone misconduct. Her team is specifically known for its supportive culture and positive working environment. She should support the firm's overall conduct risk management and compliance function. She may also be considered to be involved if no separate action is taken. Former approved persons can also be held responsible for conduct for up to three years. She should, at minimum, try to obtain further information as to whether any misconduct has been carried out by William.

   - (ii) Discuss the matter with William;

      - Alana may wish to discuss the matter with William although it has to be expected that he may deny any wrongdoing. This would nevertheless give William the opportunity to explain his conduct and possibly take any remedial action such as by reporting the gift or correcting the commission rate. Alana may consider this appropriate as William is popular within the team. He was also her mentor which may include some personnel responsibility within the firm. It may also be the case that no misconduct was involved.

   - (iii)  Refer the matter to a line manager or compliance;

      - Alana may always report any matter to her Line Manager although as the team is supporting and as William is popular, it is possible that Alana and William have the same Line Manager who may not wish to take any action. Alana should be aware of this and if she reports the matter to her Line Manager, follow-up to ensure that some action is taken, at minimum, to confirm that no misconduct has been involved. Alana can discuss the matter with her Line Manager in confidence if she does not wish William to know due to their friendship and close working relationship.

Alana can consider escalating the matter if no action is taken by the Line Manager by reporting it to Compliance and if no action is taken by Compliance to one of the NEDs or the NED Co or Audit Committee. This will ensure that the appropriate personnel within the firm have been properly and fully advised and that appropriate action should be taken. Escalating the matter immediately would nevertheless generally be inappropriate as the firm's proper procedures should be followed first. While formal investigations and possible disciplinary action may damage William's reputation within the firm and possibly result in his eventual dismissal, this may also confirm that no misconduct has been involved. Alana should be entitled to ask for any enquiries and investigations to be conducted in a confidential manner with William not knowing that she reported the matter and to avoid any recrimination within the team or within the wider firm.

- (iv)  Refer the matter to the CISI or FCA;

Alana can always consider external reporting under the PIDA with the FCA being a 'prescribed Person' under the Act. The FCA attempts to ensure that the anonymity of whistleblowers is protected unless released by the whistleblower or required by court. The FCA policy with regard to whistleblowers is set out in its statement on 'How we handle disclosures from whistleblowers' (February 2015) with Whistleblowing being dealt with on the FCA website at http://www.fca.org.uk/siteinfo/contact/whistleblowing.

Alana may also consider referring the matter to the CISI. The CISI has specifically promoted its own 'Speak Up' policy which was launched on 22 September 2014. The five checks referred to consist of 'See, Prepare, Evaluate, Act and Keep trying'. CISI also refers to 'four steps to Speak Up' consisting of informal disclosure, formal disclosure, regulatory disclosure and public disclosure. These are set out in CISI, Speak Up (2015) and http://www.cisi.org/speakup.

e) Alana would be advised initially to try to obtain some further information to confirm whether her suspicions have any basis or not. If this indicates that there may have been some misconduct, she may refer this to her Line Manager (HR) on a confidential basis. She should ensure that some further enquiries and investigations are conducted to confirm whether any misconduct has been involved. If no action is then taken, she should escalate through Compliance and the NED Co or Audit Committee failing which she should refer the matter to the CISI and FCA. Separate public disclosure may be considered although in practice this would be unlikely especially if the CISI and FCA were already involved.

## B67.

### Question

Henry is a junior compliance officer at Winter Bank. He reports into Charlotte, although she is only two years older than Henry and has only recently been promoted to a manager role. Henry was pleased when Charlotte was promoted, as he saw the amount of hard work and effort she put into getting the promotion, including long hours in the office as well as often answering her phone and emails from home.

Grace, the Compliance Director, who is responsible for overseeing the whole Compliance function within Winter Bank, has assigned Henry and Charlotte to work together on an important project, reviewing Winter Bank's Anti-Money Laundering processes. They are also instructed to report their findings and progress to the firm's Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO), Gavin, for the duration of this project. Gavin has a bad reputation within Winter Bank, known as being someone who is surly and unapproachable. Part of this stems from the fact that Gavin is very protective of his personal time - he does not answer calls or emails after he has left the office for the day, and gets angry if anyone tries to contact him after 6:00pm.

Henry and Charlotte work hard together on the project. Henry is assigned the task of researching the current Anti-Money Laundering laws and regulations, and mapping these next to the bank's current policies and processes, whilst Charlotte takes responsibility for working with Gavin on gathering the necessary statistics, such as how many reports have been made, the time they took to resolve etc. They have worked well together, but Henry notices that Charlotte is becoming increasingly 'burned-out'. The amount of effort she put into getting her promotion, her new management responsibilities, and the pressure working with Gavin on this project are clearly getting to her. It gets so bad that Charlotte tells Gavin that she has booked a few days of annual leave next week (which has been approved by Grace), and is going away for a few days. Her flight home is due to land on Thursday night, meaning she will be back in the office on Friday in time for last-minute finalisation of the report before they need to submit it to Grace on Monday. In the project brief, Grace had stated that she needed the report on the Monday so that she could prepare for the Board meeting later in the week, so Henry and Charlotte have plenty of time, and besides, Charlotte assures Henry that her part of the report will be done before she goes on leave.

However, last thing on Thursday (around 05:00pm) Grace calls Henry and tells him to get the report to her first thing on Friday morning. She sounds very forceful on the phone and says, "I don't care what you need to do to get this done, but I just need it by 09:00am tomorrow". Henry is panicked, as Charlotte's half of the presentation is not in the shared team folder, but in her personal area of Winter Bank's network. Henry tries to call Charlotte several times, but her phone is turned off and he realises that she must be on the plane. By this time, it is past 06:00pm and Henry knows that if he calls Gavin he will be extremely angry. After receiving an email from Grace saying "I'm leaving for the day. Make sure I have the AML report by 08:30am tomorrow LATEST!", Henry takes the only option which he thinks is available to him, and convinces a colleague, Jess, to call IT support pretending to be Charlotte to say that she forgot her password, and can they please reset it? Unbelievably, Sam from IT support believes he is talking to Charlotte, resets the password and tells Jess what he has reset it to! Henry thanks Jess profusely, finds the information he needs for the report in Charlotte's folder, adds it to the report and emails the whole thing to Grace. Before he leaves the office, he resets Charlotte's password again so that Jess cannot login to her folders – thinking to himself that he will tell Charlotte what he has done in the morning.

| a) | Who is responsible for Henry gaining access to Charlotte's personal folder? |
|---|---|
| | List all the individuals responsible and discuss to what extent you believe |
| | one or more characters are 'more' responsible than the rest. (_8 marks_) |
| b) | Outline the risks involved in this situation being able to occur. (_4 marks_) |
| c) | What could have been done to stop this situation occurring? (_4 marks_) |
| d) | Are Grace and Gavin more liable than the other characters in the story? |
| | (_4 marks_) |

### Answer

The question was concerned with the responsibilities of various parties within a bank where difficulties arose in managing the preparation of an Anti-money laundering review process. The initial report was to be prepared by a specific deadline although this has brought forward with one of the members of staff being on leave and not leaving her section of the report in a shared team folder. IT was misled into agreeing to reset the password access to allow access to confidential files to complete the report in time.

- a) The parties concerned were responsible as follows:

   - (i) Henry, the junior compliance officer:

   - (a) Henry was the person who convinced Jess to call IT and pretend to be Charlotte and could be considered responsible for gaining access to Charlotte's folder;

   - (b) However, he cannot be held responsible for the position he found himself in (having been left alone with a changed deadline for completion of the project);

   - (c) There were still other choices available to him including:

### Answer

The question raised a number of issues with regard to implementation of the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) within the UK. The Data Protection Act 2018 also come into effect in May 2018. A boutique wealth management firm has adopted new GDPR complied processes which include a checklist facility. A number of parties are involved including a student, the head of human resources, the person responsible for data protection compliance and one of the firm's star fund managers. A number of difficult issues arise with regard to their actions and relations in terms of GDPR and ethical compliance.

a) Staff members responsible for data protection within Secure Wealth:

- Ganesh, the Head of Operations

   - The story mentions that Ganesh is 'responsible for implementing the data protection process' at Secure Wealth.

   - Whilst this is a task that is most likely going to be assigned to the company's Data Protection Officer (DPO), it is not explicitly mentioned that Ganesh is the DPR for Secure Wealth.

   - Secure Wealth may not even need to appoint a DPO. A data controller or processor is only obliged to appoint a DPO where:

- It is a public authority – Secure Wealth is not a public authority.

- Core activities involve regular and systematic monitoring of individuals on a large scale – As a boutique wealth management firm, one can assume that the company does not have enough clients to be classed as 'large scale'.

- Large-scale processing of sensitive data or criminal records takes place – Sensitive data includes information about racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic or biometric information, data concerning health or a person's sex life or sexual orientation. It is unlikely that Secure Wealth hold this kind of information. The only exception could be if they hold information about their client's criminal history, obtained through the firm's due diligence process, in which case they may wish to appoint a DPO.

- A member state's law requires a DPO to be appointed – UK law does not require a DPO to be appointed.

   - Nevertheless, Ganesh does seem to be in a position of authority regarding the data protection policies within secure wealth and may well held at least partly responsible for any failing in that process.

- Nina, the Graduate Trainee

   - Nina has been asked by Ganesh to ensure all the teams are following the data protection process carefully and diligently.

   - However, she did not write the policy, is not responsible for assessing its overall effectiveness, and is merely assisting Ganesh with implementation.

   - Whilst she is therefore involved in the process, she is a junior member of staff employed as a Graduate Trainee and should not be held ultimately and individually accountable for data protection within Secure Wealth.

- Bob, the Fund Manager

   - As a Fund Manager, Bob is likely to be held responsible for protecting the data of the clients assigned to him. Should he lose a client file, for instance, that will be his responsibility, and he should be held accountable.

   - However, it is not easy to make the argument that Bob should be ultimately responsible for data protection across Secure Wealth.

- Hilary, the Head of HR

   - As an HR Manager, Hilary will have access to sensitive data about staff members (for example, criminal history, racial or ethnic origin, religious or philosophical beliefs, sexual orientation and data about an employee's health. She is therefore responsible for the protection of that data regarding the staff members at Secure Wealth.

   - However, she may not even be able to access the client information folders. Therefore, much like Bob, whilst Hilary is responsible for a subset of the data which is held by Secure Wealth, it does not seem appropriate that she would be ultimately responsible for data protection across Secure Wealth.

- The CEO/Board Members/The Chairman

   - The buck stops with the CEO and/or people in charge of the organisation, such as the Chairman and Board members.

   - There have been instances where companies have suffered a large-scale data breach, and the CEO has stood down as a result.

   - The Board (and perhaps Executive Team) are responsible for setting the policy which staff must adhere to on data protection, ensuring staff are properly trained on how to manage client data, and choosing how to respond to threats and breaches.

   - These senior figures also set the tone from the top, making sure that staff take data protection seriously, as well as creating an open environment where staff feel confident admitting when mistakes are made and raising concerns about processes not working or misconduct by fellow employees.

   - There are therefore the figureheads of the organisation and could be considered ultimately responsible for data protection at Secure Wealth.

- All staff members.

   - All staff members have a role to play when it comes to data protection, and there have been instances where employees have been held individually responsible for data breaches and faced dismissal or legal action as a result. It therefore cannot be said that one sole person is held responsible for data protection, as all staff have a role to play.

## b) Risks associated with the scenario.

The wider risks/implications which this fact pattern highlights are as follows:

- When it comes to data protection, an organisation is only as strong as its weakest link. For that reason, the concerns Nina has raised about Bob's attitudes towards compliance with the data protection policies are significant and suggest that the company is at risk of non-compliance with data protection rules, including GDPR.

- Hilary's response is also concerning. As a senior leader she should be more alert to possible breaches or non-compliance with the Secure Wealth data protection policy and processes and should not respond as she did to Nina ("I'm not really responsible for our data processes... that doesn't sit within my team.").

      - The risk is that, if senior managers do not feel accountable for the data protection process, it leaves Secure Wealth open to risks from certain teams within the organisation. Tone from the top (including team managers) is important.

      - Additionally, putting too much responsibility onto Ganesh (if, indeed, he is responsible for data protection at Secure Wealth) leaves the organisation open to risk should something happen, and Ganesh be on holiday, unwell, or out of the office for some other reason.

   - A wider risk can be seen about the attitude to Bob. Hilary says that Bob is reliable, but Nina has witnessed him behaving in a way that is anything but. Does Secure Wealth have an accurate impression of its managers, or, is Bob a bully who everyone is afraid to stand up to?

   - The clear risks are about whether client's data is secure (and whether or not there has already been a breach of their data) and, if the data is not secure or has been breached, the regulatory and reputational risk to Secure Wealth.

      - Under the GDPR, data protection supervisory authorities may impose penalties for non-compliance – set as the greater of €20 million or up to 4% of the total worldwide annual turnover of the company, Additionally, data protection supervisory authorities can require organisations to cease processing personal data, which could stop the organisation from carrying on business.

- c) The following incidents represent possible breaches of data protection:

   - Bob has taken documents with his clients' personal information on them outside of the office to work on whilst commuting.

      - This could be considered a breach of physical security. Data protection is not just about cyber-attacks and digital information, but also includes physical information that can be removed from an office premises.

      - It is significant that the information Bob has taken out of the office includes clients' personal details.

      - The risk is that he has ever left any of these documents on the train, or in a public space. But, even if he has never lost the documents, there is still the possibility that someone has read the documents over his shoulder or whilst sitting next to him, which could constitute a data breach.

   - Bob uses his personal email address to communicate with clients as well as his work address.

   - The use of a personal email address means that Secure Wealth are not able to monitor effectively the security or nature of the information that is being exchanged with clients. This puts Secure Wealth at risk of a data breach.

   - There is no suggestion here that this activity had led to a data breach, however, as Bob seems to be communicating with his existing clients.

   - Nevertheless, it is not best practice, and may lead to the firm breaching COBS 4: Communicating with clients, including financial promotions, in that Secure Wealth may not be able to properly record the details of any financial promotion Bob is communicating to his clients via his personal email address.

- Bob accepted a call from a client's husband and gave him an update about the status of a particular fund in which his wife had invested money.

   - He claims to have had verbal consent from his client to share this information but does not appear to have that agreement in writing.

   - This could well be considered a breach of his client's data/financial information and is not best practice.

Overall, the FCA expect businesses to have a culture of security, coming from the board downwards, with a recognition that security is not an IT issue but involves people and processes as well as technology. Therefore, all of the above could be considered a breach of the FCA's expectations regarding data protection.

- d) Yes, Nina could possibly be considered to be a whistleblower. Under the Public Interest Disclosure Act, disclosures qualifying for protection is defined as information disclosed by a worker (Nina is employed by Secure Wealth, even if she is just on their grad scheme, so can be considered to be a worker), including that a person has failed, is failing or is likely to fail to comply with any legal obligation to which he is subject. In this case, it appears that Bob has failed, is failing and is likely to fail to comply with Data Protection laws (including GDPR).

It does not matter whether Bob is actually guilty of the above, what matters is that Nina has a 'reasonable belief' that Bob is possibly breaching data protection requirements. Nina has made the disclosure in good faith to her employer (in this case, Hilary), defined as a personal senior to the worker, who has been expressly or implicitly authorised by the employer as having management responsibility over the worker. The story notes that Nina is expected to report directly into Hilary, although it would still have counted as a qualifying disclosure if Nina has raised her concerns with Ganesh.

This means that, should Nina be dismissed or victimised as a result of raising her concerns about Bob's conduct, she could claim protection under the Public Interest Disclosure Act.

## e)

Possible actions Nina could take, and the advantages and disadvantages associated with each:

- Do nothing:

   - Nina is not necessarily obligated to take this any further.

   - This approach is appealing as it might seem to be the one with the least amount of risk involved for Nina.

   - However, it is the approach which represents the most amount of risk for Secure Wealth.

- Hilary's response to Nina implies that she is not going to take this matter any further.

- Doing nothing means that Bob will continue to behave in the same way, increasing the risk of a data breach.

   - Additionally, Nina has been asked by Ganesh to ensure that all the teams she works within are following the process carefully and diligently. If she does nothing, she would be failing in this task.

   - Therefore, this is not the optimal course of action.

- Ask Hilary to investigate.

   - Nina could reiterate her concerns and insist that Hilary investigate further.

   - As Nina's line manager, it is Hilary's responsibility to look into any concerns raised by the people who report into her.

   - However, Hilary is Head of HR and not directly responsible for the implementation of Secure Wealth's data protection policies.

   - It also means that Nina would still need to explain to Ganesh why she has not completed the checklist with Bob.

   - Therefore, whilst this is a good option, it is not the best course of action.

- Confront Bob.

   - Hilary has advised Nina that she should be more insistent about meeting with Bob to go over Ganesh's checklist.

   - She could therefore try again and insist that they meet to discuss it.

   - However, Bob may react badly to this, having already dismissed the idea previously, and he could be less willing to assist Nina with the development of her knowledge about wealth management.

   - Is this really Nina's responsibility? Ganesh delegated completion of the checklist to Nina, but he is responsible for oversight of the whole project and is more senior than Nina.

   - It also puts Nina in a difficult position regarding what she has witnessed Bob doing with client information so far.

   - It is therefore not the best course of action.

- Report to Ganesh.

   - This seems like the optimal approach. Hilary herself has advised Nina to make a report to Ganesh if she is seriously concerned that there is been a breach of data protection.

   - As noted previously, Ganesh is responsible for implementing the data protection process. As Head of Operations, he would also probably wish to be informed about the way which Bob is handling customer data.

   - He has both the seniority and authority to take this further. He can also advise Nina about what he thinks she should do in this situation.

- (iii) Steven – The Harris family are Steven's key client account, and it is Steven who has been setting up various forms of trust arrangement for the family. Finally, his aggressive reaction when Holly approached him with the issue of Mrs Green's missing signature was disproportionate, and put pressure on Holly to fix the problem by using unethical or unusual means. Whilst he was not the one to insert his signature in the space designed for the witness, his behaviour contributed to that outcome, and as a senior manager responsible for the client, some blame should be assigned to him for allowing this problem to arise.

- (iv) Mr Harris' son – Mr Harris' son has a responsibility for ensuring that his signature is properly witnessed, especially on such an important document (that has already had to be returned once because of a missing signature). However, it is clear that he, and the Harris family, were going through a very difficult time, and perhaps understandable that mistakes would be made.

Furthermore, it appears that he made every effort to ensure the documents were returned in a timely manner, and kept Health & Wealth up to date regarding the state of his father's health. For these reasons, it does not appear that Mr Harris' son would be considered to be most at fault.

- a) The FCA's Anti-Greenwashing Rule is part of the broader regulatory framework aimed at ensuring that financial products marketed as environmentally sustainable or aligned with ESG (environmental, social, and governance) criteria are presented accurately and honestly. Firms are required to provide sufficient evidence to substantiate any claims about the environmental or social impact of their products. This rule is crucial for protecting investors from being misled by exaggerated or false claims, which could influence their investment decisions.

Key elements of the FCA's Anti-Greenwashing Rule include:

- Firms must ensure that all sustainability-related claims are easily understandable by investors and clearly communicate the actual ESG impact of the investment product.

• Firms are required to provide robust evidence to support any ESG claims. This includes detailed information about the criteria used to assess sustainability and how these criteria are applied in practice.

- All promotional materials, disclosures, and marketing statements must consistently reflect the true nature of the investment product's ESG characteristics.

- Firms must avoid making exaggerated, ambiguous, or unsubstantiated claims that could mislead investors regarding the sustainability of the product.

In the case of the fund, the internal review discovered that the marketing claims of '100% sustainable' and 'carbon neutral' do not fully align with the actual investments within the fund. This discrepancy suggests a potential breach of the FCA's Anti-Greenwashing Rule. To comply with the rule, the firm would need to revise its marketing materials to accurately reflect the true nature of the fund's investments. This includes providing clear, evidence-based disclosures about which parts of the fund meet sustainability criteria and which do not. Failure to adhere to these requirements could lead to regulatory penalties, significant reputational damage, and a loss of trust among investors, ultimately impacting the firm's financial stability and market position.

By ignoring the discrepancies, David compromises his own integrity and sets a poor example for others within the firm. David's focus on the potential bonuses and the fund's success creates a conflict of interest, where personal or corporate gain is prioritised over ethical responsibilities to investors and regulatory compliance.

- c) If the ethical issues related to the fund are not addressed, the firm and its leadership could face a range of significant risks, both reputational and regulatory. The investment community places a high value on transparency and ethical conduct, particularly in ESG-focused funds. The FCA's Anti-Greenwashing Rule is designed to ensure that all sustainability claims are accurate and not misleading. If a firm fails to address the discrepancies in the fund's marketing materials, the FCA could impose significant sanctions, including fines and restrictions on the firm's ability to market similar products in the future.
