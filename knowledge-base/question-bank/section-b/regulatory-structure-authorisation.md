## B4.

### Question

A US investment management company wants to set up an operation in London to launch a new pooled product under the terms of which investors will own a proportionate share in the portfolio of business property holdings across Europe. You are asked to advise on each of the following:

- a) Explain the main requirements that apply with regard to the setting up of a new financial business in the UK under FSMA. _(4 marks)_

- b) Comment on the extent to which the proposed arrangements might fall within the definition of a collective investment scheme under the FSMA. _(4 marks)_

- c) Identify any other investment activities that may be involved. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain the main restrictions that apply with regard to the sale and promotion of such schemes in that UK. _(4 marks)_

- e) If the investment management company chose to base itself in the EEA and seek local authorisation there instead, comment on any passporting issues that will arise under European legislation. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

This question looks at the application of the general prohibition under s19 and the need for permission with regard to collective investment schemes under FSMA.

- a) Reference should be made to the general prohibition under s19 and the need for permission. The concepts of authorisation and exemption should have been explained and the relationship between authorisation and permission explained. As the company is non-EEA then the provisions for passporting under MiFID or UCITS or other treaties do not arise, and so permission under Part 4A of FSMA will be required. The application procedures including the threshold conditions should be set out in your answer.

- b) The definition of a collective investment scheme set out in FSMA should have been referred to. In addition you could also set out the different types of CIS and the general requirements under FSMA.

- c) In addition to the activities of establishing operating, and winding up a collective investment scheme the other relevant activities could include the following:

Arranging deals, dealing, safe custody, advising, investment management and offering to perform regulated activities.

- d) Reference should have been made to the general restriction on financial promotion (s21) even though the question focuses upon the difference between regulated and unregulated schemes. A regulated scheme (which would include authorised unit trusts, OEICs and other recognised schemes) may be freely marketed. All other schemes are generally unregulated and subject to marketing restrictions. Such unregulated schemes may be sold to those such as eligible counterparties, professional clients and certain other exempt persons under the Financial Promotions Order. Otherwise the firm would have to establish the suitability of such schemes which would be possible in the case of persons who are or who have been a participant in such schemes within the previous 30 months, or existing or new clients for whom the firm has established that such investments are suitable.

You may also have expanded your answer with consideration of financial promotions such as the need to be fair, clear and not misleading, and the differences between real and non-real time promotions and direct offer financial promotions.

- e) Here the possibility of authorisation being available under the relevant EU directives (i.e. passporting) should have been mentioned, whether for the firm performing investment services and activities under MiFID (and these should have been explained and contrasted with ancillary services) or for the scheme under UCITS.

## B15.

### Question

You provide Compliance advice on a part time basis to Stockwell Corporate Finance Ltd (SCFL) a small corporate finance firm with permission to advise and arrange deals in investments. Its business has always consisted solely of giving corporate finance advice and arranging corporate finance deals. SCFL does not deal in investments directly and has no dealing or settlement systems. Its three account executives are all Certified Persons.

SCFL recently arranged the flotation of a business (Smallco) for a long-standing client, realising £40 million in cash for its 4 directors. The grateful directors of Smallco want to entrust the money to SCFL to invest for the long term in whatever way it thinks most appropriate. They offer to pay an annual fee of 1% of the value of the amount invested. Time Robbins, the Managing Director of SCFL, thinks that this is an excellent opportunity to earn income, diversify the business of the firm and support a good client relationship. He appears unconcerned about the regulatory implications, saying that the firm must _"strike while the iron is hot"_ and that he is not prepared to await your return from holiday which was long delayed while the flotation of Smallco took place.

- a) What are the regulatory implications of accepting the offer from the directors of Smallco to place their cash with SCFL? _(6 marks)_

Although you learn, in due course, that it was suggested to Robbins at an executive meeting that there might be regulatory problems if the firm started to carry out this new business without changing the way it operates, he decided that the opportunity was too good to miss and that the firm must go ahead immediately, in view of the risk that another firm will get the business if SCFL delayed "just to conform with yet more red tape".

Robbins emphasised the importance of providing a first rate service to the client, which he is confident of providing, because one of the account executives has past experience in investing funds for clients. He also instructed all members of staff to ensure that no one outside SCFL and Smallco find out that the business is being done.

- b) What regulatory issues could result from this proposed course of action? _(4 marks)_

When you return to the office on Monday you are called in immediately to Robbins' office where he tells you what has been going on whilst you were away and he says that although he was reluctant to do so, he had been persuaded by the other executives not to take any precipitate action but to await your return.

Robbins tells you that he is determined that the firm must undertake this new business from the directors of Smallco, as he sees it being the start of important new opportunities for SCFL but, before being irrevocably committed, he must know the regulatory implications of doing so, in particular what the likely requirements of the FCA would be.

- c) What do you tell Robbins? _(10 marks)_

### Answer

- a) The main issue raised is concerned with scope of permission and authorisation to conduct an investment and management activity. SCFL's permission is restricted to arranging deals in investments and providing advice. SCFL has contravened s 20(1) FSMA. This does not make SCFL guilty of an offence, but SCFL would be subject to disciplinary action by the FCA.

Reference could also have been made to relevant systems and controls and personnel requirements in the event of SCFL undertaking investment management or custody functions. Relevant SYSC and COCON requirements should have been referred to.

- b) Robbins and SCFL would breach various PRIN as well as SYSC and COCON requirements by continuing with the business, failing to take proper compliance advice and failing to notify the FCA.

- c) All relevant requirements for extending SCFL's scope of permission and related management, personnel and systems and controls obligations should have been referred to in taking on this new business. All relevant provisions within the FSMA, SYSC and COCON should have been referred to. Additional obligations concerning investment management and discretionary investment management set out in COBS and CASS could also have been referred to.

## B43.

### Question

A medium sized US bank that currently operates through a branch in the UK believes that if it wishes to continue its operations it should establish a subsidiary and apply for separate authority to carry on business as a UK regulated institution. The bank has approached your firm which provides Compliance Consultancy services for advice. Explain how you would respond to each of the following questions:

- a) To whom should the firm apply for authorisation and what general conditions will have to be complied with to obtain a new licence or authorisation? _(4 marks)_

- b) Will the conduct and market behaviour of the firm be subject to any separate regulation and if so by whom? _(4 marks)_

- c) Which set of Handbook of Rules and Guidance provisions will apply to its different activities? _(4 marks)_

- d) How are the operations of the separate authorities involved coordinated in practice? _(4 marks)_

- e) How will any complaints by the firm against the authorities be managed? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised the issue of making an application to act as a bank under the new regulatory regime that came into effect on 1 April 2013. This raises specific issues with regard to the respective responsibilities of the PRA and FCA and their operational relations. As a US parent bank is involved, the facts could have raised EU passporting issues although this was not referred to in any of the specific sub-questions asked.

- a) Authorisation is dealt with under s 31 FSMA. Authorisation is made available to persons holding a Part 4A permission, EEA qualifying firms under Schedule 3, Treaty firms qualifying under Schedule 4 or any person who is otherwise authorised under the FSMA (s 31 (1) (a) - (d) FSMA). Applications for Part 4A permission are now made under s 55A FSMA to the appropriate regulator (s 55A (2)). Firms subject to PRA regulation are set out in the FSMA 2000 (PRA-regulated Activities) Order 2013 (SI 2013/556) and include banks, insurance companies, designated investment firms, credit unions and building societies. Applications are made to the PRA directly with firms having to satisfy the threshold conditions set out in Schedule 6 FSMA. New firm authorisations are dealt with on the PRA website. The PRA is required to consult with the FCA with applications being determined within six months of receipt of the complete application or 12 months of receipt of an incomplete application.

- b) The FCA is responsible for the prudential regulation of all firms not subject to PRA regulation and the for conduct regulation of all firms of whatever type. A bank will become a dual regulated firm subject to PRA and FCA oversight. The FCA includes a separate section on banking applications on its website with separate PDFs on 'The Banking Authorisations Process' (including Options A and B which assist firms raise investment or capital or set up infrastructure) and 'Banking Authorisations Applicant Journey'.

- c) The FSA Handbook has been split between the PRA and FCA with a separate Combined Handbook also being available (on http://fsahandbook.info/ ). The PRA and FCA both have rule making and the FCA guidance powers (ss 137A-T and 139A-B FSMA as amended). The PRA Handbook consists of the High Level Standards (PRIN, SYSC, COCON, FIT, FINMAR, GEN and FEES), Prudential Standards (GENPRU, BIPRU, INSPRU, MIPRU, IPRU-FSOC, IPRU-INS and IPRU-INV), Business Standards (COBS), Regulatory Processes (SUP), Redress (COMP), Specialist sourcebooks (BSOCS and CREDS) and Regulatory Guides (BSOG). All of the other sections within the FSA Handbook are now included within the FCA Handbook as revised. The Treasury is to specify by order the regulated activities falling within the responsibility of the PRA under s 22A FSMA as amended which was given effect under The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (PRA-regulated Activities) Order SI 556/2013.

[Tutor's note: This part of the answer has now been superseded by the development of a separate PRA Rulebook.]

- d) The separate objectives and functions of the FCA and PRA are set out in ss 1A-O and 2A-M FSMA as amended with joint provisions being set out in ss 3A-O FSMA as amended. The regulators are to ensure the coordinated exercise of their functions (s 3D) and to enter into a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) setting out the terms on which they will operate together (s 3E). The MOU covers their respective roles and responsibilities (arts 3-5), information sharing (9-12), confidentiality (13-15), policy and rule-making (16-20), firm authorisation and individual approval (21-25), supervision of firm subject to dual regulation (26-31), overseas firms (37-38), UKLA coordination (39- 42), regulatory processes and enforcement (43-53), unauthorised regulated activities (54-55), financial crime (5657), agency coordination (58-60), fees (61-63), cross-board positions (64) and maintenance (65-67).

The Treasury may by order specify the boundaries between the FCA and PRA responsibilities (s 3G FSMA). The PRA may require the FCA to restrain from certain action (s 3H FSMA) and may revoke an FCA direction (s 3I FSMA) with the PRA being required to consult with the FCA on the giving of directions (s 3J FSMA). Separate provisions apply on the consolidated supervision of groups (ss 3KN). The FCA and PRA are required to cooperate with the Bank of England.

- e) Complaints are to be considered by a Complaints Commissioner. Complaints cover any act or omission including mistakes or lack of care, unreasonable delay, unprofessional behaviour, bias or lack of integrity. Complaints should be made within 12 months of the date on which the complainant first became aware of the relevant circumstances. Complaints must be made in a durable medium. Regulators must acknowledge receipt of complaints within five business days of receipt and complete their investigation within four weeks or write to the complainant setting out a reasonable alternative timetable.

## B62.

### Question

A medium sized Eastern European bank, from a country that has recently become an EU Member State, wishes to set up a new internet and digital based banking operation in the UK. The objective would be to provide deposit account access facilities through a major UK partner bank as well as consumer credit, residential mortgage and payment services including a currency exchange function. The bank has designed the website and software for the new internet based entity and has agreed an outline partnership agreement with a large UK banking group. You are asked to advise on each of the following:

- a) Outline the general statutory requirements that would apply if the Internet entity wished to carry out regulated activities in the UK. _(4 marks)_

- b) Explain the principal regulatory agencies that may be involved in the UK and their general functions. _(4 marks)_

- c) Identity the specific proposed activities that may require permission for the new internet services under FSMA. _(4 marks)_

- d) Outline any separate options that the bank as a whole may have to carry on business in UK. _(4 marks)_

- e) Explain the nature of any rights that the bank or Internet entity may have to provide services in other EU Member States on either an establishment or cross-border basis. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with the proposed new activities of a medium sized Eastern European bank wishing to set up in the UK. This would include a range of services that may require permission although it is unclear whether the bank will carry them out itself or through a UK partner bank.

- a) As the entity may be carrying out regulated activities in the UK, it will be subject to the specific requirements imposed under the general prohibition, permission requirement and promotion conditions (ss 19, 20 and 21 FSMA). Full details should be provided.

- b) The relevant agencies would include the PRA, FCA and CMA although reference may also be made to FOS and FSCS as well as possibly to the Bank of England and FPC. The functions of each should have been outlined.

- c) Regulated activities are defined under s20 2FSMA and the Regulated Activities Order 2001 as amended and the PRA Regulated Activities Order 2013. These outline specified investments and investment activities. This would include deposits and deposit taking, consumer credit and mortgage services (advising on and arranging finance transactions). Providing payment services do not require authorisation as such although firms providing electronic means of payment are subject to the Payment Services Regulations (PSRs) unless exempt as a small payment institution (SPI). Dealing in foreign exchange is not a regulated activity under the FSMA although foreign exchange dealing as principal would be governed by the Bank of England's Non-Investment Products (NIP) Code (November 2011).

- d) Given that the UK is no longer an EEA country, the bank will need to apply for direct authorisation in the UK.. The candidate should discuss the process used to apply for a UK licence.

The FCA provides guidance on the regulatory perimeter under its PERG manual. This includes general guidance on authorisation and regulated activities (PERG2), electronic money (PERG3), mortgage related activities (PERG4) and the Payment Services Regulations (PERG 15).

- e) The rights and relevant procedures applicable to the passporting of regulated activities by EEA authorised institutions across the EEA either on a services or establishment basis should have been explained.

## B68.

### Question

An established bank from the Middle East is considering expand its activities in London and Europe and specifically in the areas of Islamic finance. There has clearly been growing interest in the provision of Islamic finance within the UK and Europe with a large potential customer base being interested in receiving these services. The bank has been advised that it could either establish a representative office and then either a branch or are subsidiary operation in London. It also requires advice as to how it may be able to expand onto the European Continent given how Brexit has proceeded. You are asked to advise on the possible options available and regulatory implications of each.

- a) Explain the regulatory differences between a representative office and fully authorised institution and the difference between establishing a branch or a subsidiary in London. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline the principal statutory restrictions that would apply if the bank wishes to carry out general banking or Islamic banking activities within the UK. _(4 marks)_

- c) Explain the application procedure that the bank would have to comply with before it could provide any financial services in the UK? _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain the different functions of the various authorities involved in the oversight of its activities. _(4 marks)_

- e) To what extent would the new operation in London able to provide financial services in other EU Member States following Brexit? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

An overseas bank is considering establishing either a representative office or a branch or subsidiary in the UK to undertake Islamic finance activities. A number of issues are raised. The FSA, for example, examined similar issues in FSA, Islamic Finance in the UK: Regulation and Challenges (November 2007) with former Chairman, Sir Howard Davies, commenting on Islamic Banking and Finance at a conference in Bahrain in September 2003.

- a) A representative office is an office established by a bank in an overseas territory for marketing and client development with only non-transactional operations being carried out. A representative office is not separately regulated and cannot engage in regulated activities. The term representative office is not separate defined in the FCA Glossary.

A bank wishing to undertake a regulated activity in the UK can either do so through the establishment of a branch or a subsidiary. A branch is an office operating under the name of the parent institution with a subsidiary being a separately created company in the host state with separate legal personality.

- b) The bank would be subject to the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA) and restrictions on promotion (s21 FSMA). The list of authorised persons is provided under s31 FSMA with applications for Part IV permission being made under s44 FSMA. Islamic finance is not separately defined under the Combined Glossary nor in the Regulated Activities Order (RAO) as amended. Islamic finance would still generally involve one or more other regulated activities including specifically accepting deposits, which would be subject to the general prohibition. Regulated activities are 'by order' as set out in the RAO (s22 FSMA) with offences for contravention of the general prohibition being imposed (ss23-25 FSMA) and with agreements being unenforceable against the other part (s26 FSMA).

- c) The bank would have to apply for Part IV permission (s40 FSMA) to become authorised (s31(1)(a) FSMA). The bank would have to satisfy the revised threshold conditions (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended and COND). The threshold conditions were amended under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Threshold Conditions) Order 2013 (SI 2013/555) with each authority being able to issue conditions under s137O FSMA with guidance being provided under the Perimeter Guidance Manual (PERG). The threshold conditions generally cover location of offices, effective supervision, appropriate resources and suitability with an additional business model condition being added (COND 2.7 and para 2F Schedule 6 FSMA).

The bank would be dual regulated by the PRA and FCA with the application for authorisation being made through the PRA which would coordinate with the FCA. The application forms are now made available on the PRA website although held on the FCA website under the PRA and FCA names. Full and restricted application options are available with applications being considered within 6 months if they are complete and 12 months if they are incomplete.

The application form generally covers core details; business plan; scope of permission; financial resources, recovery and resolution; human resources; compliance, internal audit & policy; infrastructure; regulatory reporting; fees & levies; and fee, checklist and declaration. Separate pdf documents are available on Banking Authorisation Process (January 2014) and Banking Authorisation Process – Applicant Journey (June 2013). A separate document on 'Considerations for firms thinking of using third-party technology (off-the-shelf) banking solutions' (July 2014) is also available. Diagrammatic summaries are provided for the application process in the Applicant Journey document. The PRA and FCA cooperate in the process and either act jointly or with one authority taking the lead.

- d) As a dual regulated firm, the bank would be subject to prudential regulation by the PRA and conduct regulation by the FCA. Further information on the respective roles and functions of each authority could have been provided. Reference may also have been made to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), Financial Service Compensation Scheme (FSCS), the Complaints Scheme and Complaints Commissioner as well as the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), Competition Commission and the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England although this was not strictly necessary.

- e) Financial services may be provided in other EU and EEA Member States would require a separate licence and permission granted by an EEA member state. Relevant procedures may have been outlined depending upon whether the firm wish to exercise an establishment or service right. Firms authorised and holding permission in London have no direct rights to provide services or establish in other Member States following the U.K.'s withdrawal.

## B70.

### Question

A UK securities firm is considering the implications of the continuing difficulties that have arisen in trying to secure some new trading relationships between the UK and the EU following the UK Referendum vote to leave the EU on 23 June 2016. The firm exercises passport rights under the EU Market in Financial Instruments Directive (MiFID) and the EU Capital Requirements Directive (CRD 4) measures. The firm has a representative office in Dublin and is considering transferring some of its operations and staff to Dublin to allow it to continue to service EU clients or engage in EU related trading and other activities which may be restricted to EU authorised firms in future. You are asked to advise on the possible options available.

- a) Explain the nature of the rights currently available to the bank to carry on regulated activities on either a services or establishment basis in the UK and across the EU. _(4 marks)_

- b) Explain the application procedure that the bank must comply with before it can carry on any regulated activities in the EU and outside the UK at the present time. _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline the principal statutory restrictions that may be imposed on the bank if the UK withdraws from the EU Single Market in financial services. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain the options that may be available to the bank to allow it to continue to carry on banking and financial services in the UK and across the EU. _(4 marks)_

- e) Advise the bank on the extent to which it would be able to provide financial services in other non-EU countries depending upon the options exercised following the UK's possible withdrawal from the EU single market in banking and financial services. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with the implications for a UK securities firm following the UK withdrawal from the EU Single Market. The firm has a representative office in Ireland and can consider transferring operations to Dublin although this would require obtaining appropriate local authorisation. It can also consider obtaining authorisation in other Member States either directly or through the extended Dublin office.

_[Tutor's Note: The issue of how the UK has withdrawn from the EU's Single Market has now been resolved following Brexit. However, I have left this question in the bank as an example of how the Examiner likes to examine 'hot topics'. Questions similar to this one appeared in several consecutive exams. It also contains useful background knowledge.]_

- a) As an authorised firm with its head office in the UK, the securities firm would be able to exercise passport rights under the core European financial directives. The right to carry on securities related activities in other Member States is provided for under the Capital Requirements Directive 4 (CRD 2013/36/EU) and the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR 575/2013) and Markets in Financial Instruments Directive 2 (MIFiD 2014/65/EU) and Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MIFiR 600/2014/EU). These apply to institutions which include both credit institutions and investment firms (art 3(3) CRR) with passport rights for investment firms and credit institutions being provided for under art 34(1) MIFiD2. The firm in this case would be an EEA firm under Schedule 3 FSMA in relation to the MIFiD2 and be able to exercise separate underlying Treaty Rights as a Treaty firm under Schedule 4 FSMA to the extent necessary. Before the UK's withdrawal, the firm would be able to passport activities from the UK into other Member States of the EU and EEA through its parent operation in the UK.

- b) Relevant procedures would vary depending upon whether the firm wished to exercise service or establishment rights in other territories. Firms are required to notify the PRA of their intention to exercise passport rights. The PRA will seek assurance concerning the risks posed to its objectives by UK firms wishing to operate elsewhere within the EEA with the review being proportionate to the possible impact on the PRA objectives. The PRA is required to assess the adequacy of the firm's resources and administrative structure and whether it complies with the requirements set out in the relevant directive. EU banks passport under the CRD IV and securities firms under MiFID2. The Treasury issued regulations concerning the exercise of outward and inward passport rights in 2001 (FSMA (EEA Passport Rights) Regulations 2001/2511). Separate Commission technical standards were set out in an Implemented Regulation (EU) 926/2014 of 27 August 2014.

- (c) All of the principal statutory provisions contained the FSMA as amended would continue to apply. This includes the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA) and restrictions on promotion (s21 FSMA). The firm would have to ensure that its Part IV permission (s40 FSMA) to become authorised (s31(1)(a) FSMA) was up-to-date and comply with the threshold conditions imposed (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended and COND). Relevant personnel would have to be approved persons (s59 FSMA) and comply with the suitability requirements imposed under FIT and conduct requirements in COCON.

As an investment firm, it would principally be regulated by the FCA. The firm would be subject to the rules and guidance imposed by the FCA and PRA (ss137A, 137G and 139A FSMA including the FCA PRIN and all other relevant provisions). The question strictly referred to principal statutory restrictions although candidates may have referred to rules and guidance manuals as these are imposed under the provisions contained in the FSMA.

Reference may also have been made to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), Financial Service Compensation Scheme (FSCS), the Complaints Scheme and Complaints Commissioner as well as the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), Competition Commission and the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England although this was not strictly necessary.

- (d) A UK authorised London subsidiary would have no automatic rights following the UK's withdrawal from the EU unless replacement arrangements were agreed. If replacement rights were continued, the subsidiary may be able to exercise services and establishment rights across Europe. The relevant procedures would vary depending upon whether the firm wished to exercise service or establishment rights in other territories (above).

In the absence of specific arrangements, the firm would have three options following the UK withdrawal from the EU. It would have to retain its existing UK authorisation and permission to continue to operate within the UK. A representative office is only an overseas operation used for marketing and client development purposes without local authorisation to conduct transactional business. A representative office would have no rights to provide services or establishment in other Member States either before or after the UK's withdrawal. If the firm wishes to conduct regulated activities within the EU, it would then have to restructure its representative office as a full operation and apply for authorisation from the Irish authorities or apply for authorisation in another Member State either through a branch or separately incorporated subsidiary. A branch operates under the legal identity of the parent firm in the UK without any separate local legal status. A subsidiary constitutes a newly formed corporate entity within the UK or elsewhere.

- e) Passport rights only apply in relation to the provision of financial services on a services or establishment basis within the EU. The firm would not automatically be able to carry on financial services in any other non-EU country. The extent to which the firm would have to make a full new original application would depend upon the relationship between the separate third country and either the UK or the EU depending upon whether the new services were to be carried on from London or Ireland.

Special rules apply under the MIFiD II in respect of assessing equivalence requirements with regard to 3rd country firms coming into the EU and establishing branches (MIFiD II art 39) which replace the earlier national provisions. The UK set out its implementation approach to these provisions in an HM Treasury consultation document in March 2015. The rights that the firm would otherwise have to carry on financial services in other non-UK and non-EU countries would depend on the arrangements agreed with the specific country concerned and the UK or EU respectively.

The establishment of non-EU branches by EU firms was referred to by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) in July 2017 with national competent authorities being required to ensure that such use was based on objective reasons where services were provided in the non-EU jurisdiction and did not result in non-EEA branches performing material functions or services back into the EU (para 49 ESMA Opinion to support supervisory convergence in the area of investment firms in the context of the United Kingdom withdrawing from the European Union (13 July 2017 ESMA 35-43-762)).

## B81.

### Question

A medium sized European bank has a locally authorised subsidiary operation in London. The bank specialises in providing savings and lending facilities as well as international money transfer services across the EU. The capital of the bank is low following a number of years of low interest rates. The banks deposit base is also relatively small with the firm dependent on wholesale and interbank markets to fund its London and European operations. The bank's ability to raise wholesale funding has been further restricted due to the difficulties experienced in interbank markets following the onslaught of the Coronavirus (Covid 19) crisis at the beginning of 2020 despite the support programmes set up by different central banks and authorities across the EU. You work in the compliance department of the bank and have been asked to advise on the following:

- a) Explain the extent to which the bank may have breached its threshold conditions applicable to its London operations with the legal effects of this and whether the bank should discuss this with the regulatory authorities. _(4 marks)_

- b) Identify the other principal regulatory provisions within the FCA Handbook that may be relevant and have been breached. _(4 marks)_

- c) Identify the remedial action that the authorities may require the bank to take in connection with any breach of its threshold conditions. _(4 marks)_

- d) The bank is specifically concerned with how it should manage customers in difficulty during the crisis and asked for specific guidance on this. _(4 marks)_

- e) Explain the further disciplinary action that the authorities may take in this case. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with the loss of wholesale funding on the ability of a London subsidiary of a European our bank to comply with its threshold conditions and other requirements following the Coronavirus (Covid-19) Crisis. The bank's capital and deposit base are limited.

(a) The firm would be dual regulated by the PRA for regulatory purposes and the FCA in respect of conduct. The firm would apply for Part IV permission (s40 FSMA) to become an authorised person (s31(1)(a) FSMA). Compliance with the revised threshold conditions would be required (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended by the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Threshold Conditions) Order 2013 (SI 2013/555) and COND). The threshold conditions generally apply with regard to office location, effective supervision, appropriate resources and suitability as well as business model condition (COND 2.7 and para 2F Schedule 6 FSMA.)

Firms have to comply with the threshold conditions under s 55B and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended. Guidance on the application of the threshold conditions as provided for under COND within the FCA Handbook. COND applies to all firms (COND 1.1A). The threshold conditions represent the minimum requirements for which the FCA is responsible with which the firm must comply to obtain and retain its Part 4A permission (COND 1.2.1).

Specific concerns may arise in this case with regard to resources, suitability and business condition. The resources of the firm must be adequate in relation to the regulated activities carried on (para 2D Schedule 6 and COND 2.4.1). ## B90.

### Question

A boutique asset management firm in Hong Kong is considering relocating part of its operations back to the UK and expanding into the EU, European Economic Area (EEA) and Switzerland, where it sees specific growth opportunities. The firm has to review its physical and remote office and operational arrangements and relevant legal and regulatory compliance requirements. The board have decided that the firm will need to have a core operation in London which can provide a full range of discretionary investment management, advisory and dealing operations either from its physical offices or online. It will also wish to expand the same services on the European continent and into lucrative Switzerland. It would separately wish to have a presence in the US and provide marketing and promotional services there initially and then later replicate its London operations in New York, Boston and San Francisco. You are asked to advise on the various regulatory options available.

- a) Explain the principal options that may be available in terms of either operating a branch, subsidiary or representative office and outline the regulatory advantages and disadvantages of each. _(4 marks)_

- b) Outline the principal statutory restrictions that will apply with regard to the proposed London operations. _(4 marks)_

- c) Outline the principal EU directives or regulations that would apply if the firm decides to establish an operation in the EU or EEA. _(4 marks)_

- d) Explain the nature of the rights available to the firm if it establishes in London to carry on activities within the EU, the EEA or Switzerland, on either a services or establishment basis. _(4 marks)_

- e) Explain the extent to which the firm would be able to provide financial services in other non-EU or non-EEA countries depending upon the options exercised. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with the implications of a boutique asset management firm wishing to expand its business operations into the UK, the EU, the EEA and Switzerland. The firm is attempting to assess the relative advantages and disadvantages of the various options available.

**(a)** Explain the principal options that may be available in terms of either operating a branch, subsidiary or representative office and outline the regulatory advantages and disadvantages of each. **(4 marks)**

The firm would have three general business expansion options:

• It could establish a subsidiary or branch in the UK and apply for authorisation and permission to operate within the UK. A subsidiary would involve setting up a new separate subsidiary company under UK law.

• It may be able to open a branch depending upon the bilateral arrangements and relations between the Hong Kong and UK authorities, although it would still have to apply for authorisation and permission. A branch operates under the legal identity of the parent firm in the UK without any separate local legal status. A subsidiary constitutes a newly formed corporate entity within the UK or elsewhere.

• It could set up a representative office although this would only constitute an overseas operation used for marketing and client development purposes, without local authorisation to conduct transactional business. A representative office would have no rights to carry out regulated activities in the UK or provide services or establishment in any other EU Member States.

**(b)** Outline the principal statutory restrictions that will apply with regard to the proposed London operations. **(4 marks)**

All the principal statutory provisions contained in the FSMA as amended would continue to apply. This includes the general prohibition (s19 FSMA), permission requirement (s20 FSMA) and restrictions on promotion (s21 FSMA).

The firm would have to ensure that its Part IV permission (s40 FSMA) to become authorised (s31(1)(a) FSMA) was up-to-date and comply with the threshold conditions imposed (s41 and Schedule 6 FSMA as amended and COND). Relevant personnel would have to be approved persons (s59 FSMA) and comply with the suitability requirements imposed under FIT and conduct requirements in COCON.

As an investment firm, it would principally be regulated by the FCA. The firm would be subject to the rules and guidance imposed by the FCA and PRA (ss137A, 137G and 139A FSMA including the FCA PRIN and all other relevant provisions). The question strictly referred to principal statutory restrictions, although candidates may have referred to rules and guidance manuals as these are imposed under the provisions contained in the FSMA. Reference may also have been made to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), Financial Service Compensation Scheme (FSCS), the Complaints Scheme and Complaints Commissioner as well as the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) and the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) within the Bank of England, although this was not strictly necessary.

**(c)** Outline the principal EU directives or regulations that would apply if the firm decides to establish an operation in the EU or EEA. **(4 marks)**

As an authorised firm with its head office in the UK, the securities firm would have been able to exercise passport rights under the core European financial directives which also apply to EEA firms. The right to carry on securities related activities in other Member States is provided for under the Capital Requirements Directive 4 (CRD 2013/36/EU), the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR 575/2013), Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II 014/65/EU) and Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MIFiR 600/2014/EU) as given effect to under relevant UK implementing measures. These apply to institutions which include both credit institutions and investment firms (art 3(3) CRR), with passport rights for investment firms and credit institutions being provided for under art 34(1) MiFID II. If the firm wishes to become an EEA firm, it will become subject to MiFID II and be able to exercise separate underlying Treaty Rights as a Treaty firm under Schedule 4 FSMA to the extent necessary. Before the UK's withdrawal, the firm would be able to passport activities from the UK into other Member States of the EU and EEA through its parent operation in the UK.

**(d)** Explain the nature of the rights available to the firm if it establishes in London to carry on activities within the EU, the EEA or Switzerland, on either a services or establishment basis. **(4 marks)**

A UK authorised London subsidiary would have no automatic rights in the EU or EEA, following the UK's withdrawal from the EU, unless replacement arrangements are agreed. If replacement rights are continued, the subsidiary may be able to exercise services and establishment rights across Europe. The relevant procedures would vary depending upon whether the firm wished to exercise service or establishment rights in other territories. In the absence of specific arrangements, the firm would have to re-establish within the EU on the European continent. The firm would only have passport rights if it was authorised as a separate legal entity within the EU. It could then carry out activities in other Member States, either on a cross-border services or establishment basis, under the relevant EU measures as referred to above.

**(e)** Explain the extent to which the firm would be able to provide financial services in other non-EU or non-EEA countries depending upon the options exercised. **(4 marks)**

Passport rights only apply in relation to the provision of financial services on a services or establishment basis within the EU or EEA. The firm would not automatically be able to carry on financial services in any other non-EU country. The extent to which the firm would have to make a full new original application would depend upon the relationship between the separate third country and either the UK, EU or EEA, depending upon whether the new services were to be carried on from London or Ireland.

Special rules apply under the MiFID II in respect of assessing equivalence requirements regarding 3rd country firms coming into the EU and establishing branches (MiFID II art 39), which replaced the earlier national provisions. The UK set out its implementation approach to these provisions in an HM Treasury consultation document in March 2015. The rights that the firm would otherwise have to carry on financial services in other nonUK and non-EU countries would depend on the arrangements agreed with the specific country concerned and the UK or EU, respectively.

The establishment of non-EU branches by EU firms was referred to by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) in July 2017, with national competent authorities being required to ensure that such use was based on objective reasons where services were provided in the non-EU jurisdiction and did not result in non-EEA branches performing material functions or services back into the EU (para 49 ESMA Opinion to support supervisory convergence in the area of investment firms in the context of the UK withdrawing from the EU (13 July 2017 ESMA 35-43-762)).

## B98.

### Question

You are a managing director of a small financial advisory business. You decide that fulfilling all the administrative, regulatory and compliance demands of the firm has become too onerous. You decide to outsource some aspects of the firm's administration, regulatory and compliance activities to a specialised firm focused on supporting firms such as yours.

- a) Which administration, regulatory and compliance roles might be appropriately outsourced? _(6 marks)_

- b) Outline the regulatory expectations when outsourcing administrative, regulatory and compliance activities of the firm. _(6 marks)_

- c) Describe the risks that the firm might face when undertaking this outsourcing strategy. _(4 marks)_

- d) On balance, can the risk be managed appropriately to allow outsourcing as a useful strategy for smaller financial services firms? Will this solution reduce the workload that you are currently facing? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

**a) (6 marks) — What administration, regulatory and compliance roles might be appropriately outsourced?**

In practice a wide range of a firm's activities may be outsourced to a third party. Many concerns can arise in this outsourcing process. If an operational function is regarded as critical to the firm and might impair regulatory compliance, then this may not be a suitable course of action. This is very much the case for certain types of financial firm. For example, banks are strongly discouraged by international regulators from the outsourcing of their compliance function. Similarly, a firm needs to ensure that any outsourcing does not impede compliance with financial regulation and communications with regulators.

Areas where concerns should not arise include those of advisory services, such as legal advice, training, billing, security, using standardised services and services which are not part of the firm's relevant services. Of course, while activities can be outsourced, responsibility cannot be outsourced and is retained by the firm.

SYSC 8 of the FCA Handbook sets out rules and guidance in relation to outsourcing. This includes the requirement that firms must take reasonable steps to avoid undue additional operational risk; and must not undertake the outsourcing of important operational functions in such a way as to impair materially:

a) the quality of its internal control

b) the ability of the regulator to monitor the firm's compliance with all obligations under the regulatory system and, if different, of a competent authority to monitor the firm's compliance with all obligations under UK MiFID.

An operational function is regarded as critical or important if a defect or failure in its performance would materially impair the continuing compliance of a common platform firm with the conditions and obligations of its authorisation or its other obligations under the regulatory system, or its financial performance, or the soundness or the continuity of its relevant services and activities.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made to a maximum of six marks.

**b) (6 marks) — Outline the regulatory expectations when outsourcing administrative, regulatory and compliance activities of the firm.**

When entering into an outsourcing agreement there are many expectations. Firms must ensure the service provider has the ability, capability, and appropriate authorisations to undertake the work reliably and professionally. This work needs to be supervised, monitored, and assessed to ensure it is undertaken to a sufficient standard. The firm needs to have staff who can undertake this supervision. If work of a sufficient quality is not provided it is the responsibility of the firm to act, such as by terminating the outsourcing contract. The firm is also reliant on the service provider to provide appropriate information to interested parties such as the regulator or auditor and to keep the firm informed of any problems or issues which arise. The service provider must also protect any confidential information relating to the firm and its clients. Lastly, the firm and the service provider must establish, implement, and maintain a contingency plan for the outsourced service. For different areas of business such as investment services there may be different and extended requirements which need to be fulfilled in relation to an outsourced service provider.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made to a maximum of six marks.

**c) (4 marks) — Describe the risks the firm might face when undertaking this outsourcing strategy.**

The outsourcing relationship creates a range of risks for the firm. While the firm is responsible for the actions of the service provider and needs to supervise these relationships there are issues if this relationship breaks down. For instance, the service provider may not follow policies and procedures as expected. For example, this could lead to a breach in confidentiality or insufficient information being provided to third parties.

Even though the firm has outsourced activities, if a failure occurs in critical areas, then the firm will be held accountable. Critical in this process is ensuring there are robust internal control systems to ensure monitoring of outsourced work is undertaken appropriately.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made to a maximum of four marks. As this is an open-ended question, other appropriate responses may be awarded marks.

**d) (4 marks) — On balance, is outsourcing a useful strategy for smaller financial services firms? Will this solution reduce the workload that you are currently facing?**

On balance, outsourcing provides a useful solution to some of the demands facing smaller financial firms. Outsourcing may provide compliance functions with better access to information and lead to greater efficiency of operation. These outcomes can also be very helpful if the firm does not have an independent person available who is not involved in the provision of other services. Such independence is itself an important attribute of better compliance.

While the outsourcing decision is a trade-off between the amount of work undertaken within the firm and outside by a third party, it should not be a transfer of responsibility or control. The responsibility and accountability remain within the firm. In general, firms which outsource activities remain responsible for the outsourced actions and are required to monitor and review the operations of any third party critically to ensure regulatory obligations are fulfilled. As outlined in the workbook:

1. The outsourcing must not result in the delegation by senior personnel of their responsibility.

2. The relationship and obligations of the firm towards its clients under the regulatory system must not be altered.

3. The conditions with which the firm must comply in order to be authorised, and to remain so, must not be undermined.

4. None of the other conditions subject to which the firm's authorisation was granted must be removed or modified.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made to a maximum of four marks. As this is an open-ended question other appropriate responses may be awarded marks.
