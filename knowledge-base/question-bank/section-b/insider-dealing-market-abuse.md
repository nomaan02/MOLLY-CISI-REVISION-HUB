## B8.

### Question

- a) Imperial Computers is a leader in the manufacturing of computer chips. They have just invented the "superchip" which will have a faster rating than any other chip on the market. If they can market this product successfully, and make it cheaply, they will eclipse Intel as the market leader. The stock market knows that Imperial Computers has made great strides in this area and investors generally expect that the company will soon begin their marketing efforts on a world beating computer chip.

Investments Unlimited is regulated by the FCA. The firm's "star" electronics industry analyst anticipates that Investments Unlimited will wish to publish current research on Imperial Computers. He telephones Imperial Computers' head office to arrange a meeting with their Chief Executive, Frank Merchant. Mr Merchant's secretary says, "Sorry, Frank is in hospital. I don't know what is wrong but he asked me to call for an ambulance about an hour ago."

Consider whether the analyst has inside information and also whether Mr Merchant's secretary has acted lawfully. _(6 marks)_

- b) A day or two later Imperial Computers issues a press release giving details of Frank Merchant's physical condition. He has not suffered a heart attack but has been treated for stress and chest pains. As the analyst walks across the trading floor towards his office, he stops at the desk of one of Investments Unlimited's principal traders. In the ensuing conversation he tells the trader that he is updating his research, that he will publish his report first thing in the morning and that he now thinks investors should sell their positions in the short term. In response to this intelligence, the trader immediately sells stock so that by the end of the afternoon the book is short sufficient shares that the trader is comfortable that he will be able to meet customer selling demand.

Analyse the respective positions of the analyst and the trader.

_(7 marks)_

- c) After his conversation with the trader, the analyst decides to put the sales force in the know. He approaches a private client fund manager for the firm and has a conversation similar to that held with the trader.

The fund manager has a discretionary retail client who has a significant holding in shares of a different computer company. Indeed, the fund manager purchased those shares for the customer two days ago on the grounds that the company was a good long-term investment that would produce the income and growth that the client expects from his investments. The fund manager has now made seven trades on this account this month, each of which has shown a profit. Appropriate client documentation is in place.

The fund manager, knowing his client's account has no spare buying power, sells the shares in the other computer company to generate sufficient margin to support a short sale of Imperial. The fund manager feels this is a fairly riskless way to generate some additional trading commission. In fact, his strategy works and his client makes a substantial profit on the short sale before the fund manager covers it and repurchases the other computer shares

Ignoring the question whether or not the fund manager is dealing on the basis of inside information, analyse the fitness and propriety of his behaviour. _(7 marks)_

### Answer

## a) **Analyst**

The analyst does have inside information because:

- the information relates to a particular issuer (Imperial Computers);

- it is specific (the hospitalisation of the company's chief executive);

- it has not been made public; and

- if it were made public, it would be likely to have a significant effect on the price of the securities (because of Mr Merchant's status within the business).

## **The secretary**

On the face of it the secretary has communicated inside information to the analyst.

It might be argued in her defence that:

- she did not expect dealing (but should she have done?); and

- she disclosed the information in the proper performance of her duties, as an explanation of Mr Merchant's inability to attend a meeting (but was she indiscreetly over-informative?);

and that therefore she has not acted unlawfully.

An informed observer might take the view that the secretary to the chief executive of a quoted company should have acted with more restraint and discretion. Her disclosure to the analyst, on a strict view, comes close to a breach of CJA 1993 (insider dealing) and the EU's Market Abuse Regulation.

## b) **Insider dealing**

After the press release from Imperial Computers the information that the analyst has is no longer "inside" information (since the facts have been made public); so it does not appear that any offence has been committed here.

## **Market abuse**

Probably no problem here either. The trader is selling. That is what a user of the market would expect in the light of the bad news from Imperial Computers. So the trader's action would not give, or be intended to give, a false or misleading impression.

## **Front running**

Under the front running rule, where a firm intends to publish to clients a written recommendation, it must not knowingly effect an own account transaction in the investment concerned.

The trader's action in selling stock is clearly a breach of the rule, since the publication by a "star" analyst is likely to cause the price of the investment to move (downwards).

The only available exemptions to the ban on front running are where:

- The firm is a market maker acting as a market maker and in good faith

- The firm is dealing in order to fulfil an unsolicited customer's order.

## c) **Fitness and propriety**

## **Suitability**

A firm must take reasonable steps to ensure that it does not effect a discretionary transaction for a retail client unless the transaction is suitable for him.

Dealing in high tech companies may not be a suitable strategy for a customer requiring both income and growth. In addition, selling short is always a risky strategy.

The fact that the customer makes a profit is not relevant, and the fund manager may well be in breach of the suitability rule.

## **Churning**

Until 2007 there was a specific prohibition in the COB rules preventing a firm from dealing in the exercise of discretion for any client if the dealing would be regarded as too frequent in the circumstances. Given that the fund manager appears from the scenario to be motivated by the generation of additional trading commission, and given the number of trades on the account in the last month, the fund manager is potentially engaged in churning. The COBS provision on churning now has the status of guidance (rather than a rule), but it would clearly still represent best practice.

## **Conclusion**

In view of these potential breaches it is likely that the fund manager is acting in a way which the FCA would regard as not being "fit and proper".

## B9.

### Question

You are the Compliance Officer in the corporate finance division of Agrobank, a large UK investment bank. Sharp, a corporate finance director, has just been to see you to ask your advice on various questions arising out of a rights issue by Technoco plc, on which Agrobank has been advising and in relation to which it has acted as sponsor.

The nil-paid dealings period has now closed. Only 30% of the issue has been taken up by shareholders. Agrobank now has 48 hours (the "placing period") in which to place the remaining 70% before it goes to sub-underwriters. Surprisingly, the market appears to be unaware of the poor take up and the market price stands at a reasonable premium to the price at which new shares are being offered pursuant to the rights issue.

Agrobank is confident that it will be able to place the remaining rights. However, it would like its market maker to take 15% and has decided to allocate up to 7% to Agro Fund Management, its discretionary fund manager. It will retain 2% in house as principal and place the remaining 46% in the market. Neither the full amount taken up by the shareholders nor the amount allocated within the Agrobank group will be disclosed to places. Any amounts remaining unplaced at the end of the placing period will go to the sub-underwriters.

- a) What legislation would you advise on at this stage, and why? _(7 marks)_

- b) What, if any, disclosure obligations arise in respect of the proposed allocations to the Agrobank group? _(2 marks)_

Sharp would also like Agrobank to invest its own funds in Technoco's shares to prevent a major fall in the share price should news about the size of the rump leak out before the rump has been placed. He believes that the purchase would be a shrewd investment – he cannot understand why the rights issue was so badly supported and has shown you some management figures he received this morning from Technoco's finance director, showing how well the company is doing. The figures are considerably better than the projections in the rights issue circular. He was so impressed when he received them that he suggested the finance director send them to a number of Technoco's institutional shareholders. As a result of the information, one of the institutions has been in touch with Agrobank and would like to take an option over shares in Technoco acquired by Agrobank, rather than buying the shares in the market itself.

- c) Identify whether any criminal offences have been committed, or may have been committed, on the latest facts as described by Sharp. _(7 marks)_

- d) At the end of the fully paid dealing, members of the Agrobank group have in aggregate the following for registration:

Market maker                      9.0%

Agro Fund Management     9.0%

Principal funds                    2.9%

Which of these will require disclosure to Technoco? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

## a) **Legislation**

Under FSA 2012, s.90, a person commits an offence (punishable by a fine and/or up to ten years in jail) if he engages in any course of conduct which creates a false or misleading impression of the market for a particular security. In attempting to disguise the size of the 'rump', Agrobank would clearly be committing such an offence.

[Tutor's Note: The penalties have increased to ten years under FSA 2021.]

Under the EU's Market Abuse Regulation, a person will commit market abuse if his behaviour is likely to give a false or misleading impression as to the demand for a particular security. The burden of proof is civil, and the penalties include public censure, restitution orders and unlimited fines.

## b) **Disclosure**

The disclosure obligations under the FCA's Disclosure Guidance and Transparency Rules (DTR) are in respect of issued shares of the company. As proposed allocations do not constitute share issues, no obligation arises at this stage.

## c) **Criminal offences**

In communicating information selectively to Sharp and to the institutional investors, the finance director may be committing the criminal offence of insider dealing, since the information is clearly disclosed with a view to encouraging dealing.

Any subsequent investment by Agrobank or the institutional shareholders would constitute insider dealing, since the parties would then be dealing in price affected securities on the basis of inside information. A potential defence might be raised on the grounds that the information had been disclosed widely enough to ensure that no market participant would be disadvantaged by the lack of such information.

In addition, market abuse (with a civil burden of proof) is deemed to occur if individuals take advantage of information not generally available to the market. This is clearly the case here.

## d) **Disclosure**

Disclosure, where relevant below under the FCA's DTR, should be made to the company within two business days of the relevant transaction:

- Market maker – holdings below 10% are exempt from disclosure so long as the shares are held for the purpose of its market making business

- Agro Fund Management – this 9% holding would require disclosure as investment managers' holdings are disclosable at thresholds of 5% and 10% (and 5% changes above 10%)

- Principal funds – this 2.9% holding would not normally require disclosure, as being below the standard DTR disclosure threshold of 3%. However, it would need to be aggregated with the fund manager's holding of 9% (and therefore disclosed) if the parent company (Agrobank) controls or influences the voting rights of the fund manager.

## B11.

### Question

You are the compliance officer of an investment bank, which has just taken on as a client, Crimpleton Bains plc, a company whose shares are admitted to trading on the London Stock Exchange. The company is generally beset by scandal. Some concern has been expressed in relation to the manner in which Crimpleton Bains plc's officers and employees and advisers have conducted themselves in the past. You are asked to consider whether any market abuse issues, or indeed other regulatory or legal issues, arise from the following circumstances:

   - a) Crimpleton Bains plc makes widgets. It has recently been disclosed that your client may have sold widgets in breach of UN Sanctions against Iraq. As a result of this disclosure and the possibility of prosecution, the share price has slumped. It turns out that, one week before this information was made public, an employee of Crimpleton Bains plc sold all his shares, and then resigned. He tells you that he sold the shares as soon as he found out what his employers were up to and that his behaviour had nothing to do with a desire to avoid loss and everything to do with his personal morality. _(4 marks)_

- b) On two occasions, Crimpleton Bains plc gave an informal preview of a profit warning, which it was shortly going to announce to the market, to a journalist who had previously written favourable articles about the company. On the second occasion, the journalist was asked to sign a confidentiality agreement and was given a warning to the effect that he should not deal on the basis of that information until it had been announced to the market. On neither occasion did the journalist deal in the securities of the company. _(4 marks)_

- c) You review the text of the announcements submitted by Crimpleton Bains plc to its previous corporate finance advisers and in comparing the text which, ultimately, was published by the regulatory information services, it is clear that some significant inaccuracies have arisen. You conclude that the error has been that of the previous corporate finance advisers. _(4 marks)_

- d) Crimpleton Bains plc's profitability is closely related to the price of fuel on the world markets, which has generally affected the share value of most similar manufacturing companies, including your client. It is clear to you that earlier statements relating to the sales growth of the company are no longer accurate. However, an internal revision of Crimpleton Bains plc's sales growth forecasts has not been announced to the markets.

_(4 marks)_

- e) Crimpleton Bains plc has in the past held breakfast briefings with selected analysts. They have discontinued them, but can't remember whose idea it was to stop. They would like to start hosting them again. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

- a) The question raises a number of issues with regard to breach of the insider dealing requirements imposed under the Criminal Justice Act as well as market abuse under MAR and market manipulation offences under Part 7 of FSA 2012.

A share sale in knowledge of possible imminent publication may constitute both insider dealing and market abuse. The relevant components and conditions of each offence should have been referred to and factual relevance discussed. Available defences should have been listed and sanctions noted.

Reference may also have been made to relevant UK Listing Authority rules and the rules in MAR on managers' transactions. Reference could also have been made to restrictions on internal personal investments and Price Sensitive Information Guidance (PSI).

Reference may also be made to the enforcement measures imposed under the UN sanctions requirements referred to as well as breach of relevant internal controls procedures within the firm (although it is not a regulated entity as such).

- b) Pre-release of profit warnings raises issues of both insider dealing and market abuse. Relevant conditions, defences and penalties should have been noted.

The position of the journalist should have been referred to. While there appears to have been no direct insider dealing, the information may still have been passed on or used for the purposes of making favourable reports. While the journalist is exempt from the financial promotions regime, complaints may nevertheless be made to the Press Complaints Authority.

Possible liability by the firm and journalist under Part 7 of FSA 2012 could have been noted. Reference should also have been made to general listing requirements for information to be made available to the markets in a timely and proper manner.

- c) The earlier corporate finance advisers may have been guilty of market abuse or market manipulation under MAR and Part 7 FSA 2012. Relevant conditions, defences and penalties should have been referred to.

As an authorised firm, there would have been consequential breaches of a number of provisions of the Handbook including PRIN, SYSC and COCON. Relevant sanctions should have been noted.

Despite the earlier fault, the firm remains responsible for ensuring that all significant information is made available to the market. The need for corrective disclosure should have been noted.

- d) No difficulties arise with regard to the content of the earlier disclosures which were accurate at the time of release. Supplementary disclosure is nevertheless required to ensure that accurate current information is provided. A market abuse or market manipulation offence may otherwise be committed under MAR or Part 7 FSA 2012.

The potential liabilities involved must be drawn to the attention of the firm as you are acting as its investment adviser. Failure may amount to breach of specific Handbook provisions (including PRIN) or general common law and fiduciary duties.

- e) The provision of analysts' briefings may continue provided that these are conducted in a proper manner. Revised provisions have been included within COBS.

There must be no pre-release of price sensitive information, attendant staff must be properly trained and advised, no inducements must be provided, non-invitees must be allowed to attend and proper records must be kept.

The separate financial analysts' requirements under COBS 12.2 and inducements under SYSC 10 may also have been referred to.

## B17.

### Question

You are a compliance officer in a firm of hedge fund managers and become aware that one of your firm's most senior traders (Rapier) has been approached by a salesman from an investment bank, who is seeking market feedback on a forthcoming issue of US$ securities issued by a Brazilian Bank in New York.

Although you have not been approached for advice as to the impact that these discussion could have on Rapier's trading activities, which generate a significant portion of the firm's income, a number of concerns spring to mind.

## a) What might these concerns be and why?

## _(5 marks)_

You discuss these concerns with your boss who says that he will have a word with Rapier. As a result of this, you are approached by Rapier, who tells you that he has been an active borrower and short seller of stock in the Brazilian bank for some time and suggest that there is no reason why he should not continue to trade in this manner.

## b) What advice would you give and why?

## _(5 marks)_

Rapier also suggests to you that he believes his trading activities are permitted as he is not trading in the UK.

## c) How would you respond and why?

_(5 marks)_

Shortly after your conversation with Rapier, the Brazilian securities are issued and as a result of his short position, Rapier has booked the firm a substantial profit.

Nevertheless, you remain concerned at the position in which Rapier may have placed both himself and the firm.

- d) Explain what further issues may arise and their possible regulatory consequences.

_(5 marks)_

### Answer

The question is concerned with 'wall-crossing' and supporting restrictions in connection with short selling of internationally traded stock and potential breach of PRIN and COCON as well as the commission of insider trading and market abuse (CJA and MAR). This parallels the decision issued by the Financial Services and Markets Tribunal (as it was then called) in the case of Philippe Jabre and the FSA on 27 July 2006. The FSMT rejected the appeal by Philippe Jabre and held, in particular, that the FSA could ask that the issue of suitability was reconsidered at the appeal stage and that short selling of equivalent stock listed in London constituted market abuse under s 118 FSMA (now superseded by MAR).

- a) Rapier has 'wall-crossed' (or become restricted) with the pre-marketing (pre-sounding) discussions conducted with the salesman from the investment bank. The relevant issue is accordingly coded.

- (i) The consequences of wall-crossing and restriction in prohibiting subsequent dealing;

- (ii) The passing of inside information (specific and precise, price sensitive, unpublished and relevant);

- (iii) Whether the stock was listed on a prescribed market in London;

- (iv) Whether the stock was listed on a prescribed watch or restricted list;

- (v) Whether Rapier was already on an insider list;

- (vi) Whether and when the forthcoming issue would be announced to the public;

- (vii) Market abuse and relevant conditions (as defined in MAR);

- (viii) Whether Rapier has already disclosed or discussed any of this with Compliance;

- (ix) Whether existing reporting procedures were adequate;

- (x) Whether the firm's existing conflicts of interest, policy and, in particular, Chinese walls procedures were operating effectively;

- (xi) Whether there had been any breach of PRIN by the firm or COCON by Rapier.

As much details as possible should have been provided within the time available. The more general issue that arises is whether the firm can be held liable for any offences committed by a senior manager. Vicarious liability was confirmed in the case of Jabre where the seniority and authority of the individual concerned was sufficient. This could either be raised under sub-section a) or d) below.

- b) The completion of pre-existing orders would not constitute insider trading or market abuse under relevant defences. The entering into any new transactions to borrow or short sell would nevertheless be prohibited. No new contracts or orders should be entered into following the initial contact and discussion. All relevant offence conditions and defences could have been discussed in as much detail as possible.

Reference may also have been made to the due diligence and reasonable precaution defence in s123 FSMA.

Reference may also have been made to breach of PRIN and COCON.

- c) The issue relates to the territorial scope of application of the insider trading and market abuse offences. If Rapier is not trading in the particular securities to which the information relates, there may be no offence of insider trading under the CJA 93. The scope of application of MAR is nevertheless wider as this applies to any dealing in shares dealt with on a regulated market, MTF or OTF. If the shares in the Brazilian bank are already dealt with on any UK market, any dealing in shares in the bank in any other market will constitute market abuse as confirmed by the FSMT in the Jabre decision. It was stated that it would otherwise have been absurd for traders to misuse information by trading on any non-UK market where this would otherwise constitute market abuse under MAR.

Reference may also have been made to breach of PRIN and COCON.

- d) As Rapier has already traded and booked a substantial profit for the firm, Compliance will now be concerned with the effects of this on the operation of its internal systems and controls, the need to report the breach to the FCA (under PRIN (11)) and the internal regulatory action and external (FCA) penalties that will be imposed.

Relevant issues include:

- (i) The nature of any internal sanction to be imposed on Rapier;

- (ii) The need to review reporting and conflicts of interest policy management practice within the firm;

- (iii) Any more general corrective action to be taken to prevent substantial FCA sanction;

- (iv) The facts should be reported immediately to the FCA under PRIN (11);

- (v) Applicable sanctions for breach of MAR against Rapier (unlimited fine);

- (vi) Sanction against firm for vicarious liability of Rapier under MAR (as confirmed in Jabre);

- (vii) Possible penalties for breach of CJA 93 in the event that insider dealing may have been established (although unlikely);

- (viii) Penalties for breach by Rapier of COCON 2 (due skill, care and diligence) and 3 (market conduct);

- (ix) Possible breach by Rapier of COCON 1 (integrity) (not confirmed in Jabre);

- (x) Possible breach by the firm of PRIN 5 (market conduct);

- (xi) Possible related breaches of PRIN or SYSC as well as implication on COND and FIT;

- (xii) Possibility of public or private censure and consequent reputational damage. Reference may also have been made to difficulty for Rapier in subsequently reapplying for approved person's status under FIT in the event of previous restriction or cancellation. Even if his earlier approval was not formally withdrawn, Rapier may have significant difficulties in retaining this status subsequently. If approval had been cancelled, Rapier would have the responsibility of establishing fit and properness again. The issue was left open in the Jabre case.

Reference could also have been made to breach of the CISI Principles as well as the more general need to maintain ethical standards in financial markets.

## B22.

### Question

You are the compliance officer of a medium sized hedge fund. On Friday morning you are heading back to your desk and are approached in the corridor by the fund's top performing portfolio manager who asks for a "quick chat". During your conversation you discover that on Monday the fund manager had been contacted by an investment bank about the refinancing plans of ABC Company, an international company with its headquarters in Germany. ABC Company has issued corporate securities in the form of bonds which are to be listed on the Luxembourg Stock Exchange.

The investment bank had contacted the portfolio manager for his views on the refinancing which included the tender for certain of ABC Company's bonds ("the ABC bonds"). During the course of this conversation, the portfolio manager was informed about the size of the refinancing deal (EUR 0.7 billion), the date of the announcement (the following Thursday), and that the ABC bonds would form part of the tender offer.

Later on Monday afternoon the portfolio manager gave instructions to a junior broker in the firm to purchase 1.4 million of the ABC bonds. On Thursday ABC Company made a regulatory announcement that it had commenced a cash tender offer for certain bonds, including the ABC bonds. The ABC bonds rose sharply in value, and shortly after the portfolio manager gave instructions to sell which realised a healthy profit for the fund.

On Thursday evening while toasting the profit for the fund, one of the portfolio manager's friends from another hedge fund, who had recently attended a course on market abuse, commented that the portfolio manager may have committed market abuse. After a sleepless night, the portfolio manager decides to speak with you in the morning.

You immediately request the recordings of the telephone conversation between the portfolio manager and the investment bank. To your horror, you discover that the conversation was not recorded. You further discover that the portfolio manager's notes on the refinancing deal had been left open on his desk before the announcement was made.

- a) You immediately contact the fund's Chief Executive Officer (CEO) and inform her about the situation. She requests an analysis from you on whether any market abuse has been committed, including details of the relevant possible offences that may have been involved. How would you respond? _(6 marks)_

- b) Having given your analysis to the CEO, she further asks what corrective action the fund should take and what are the likely steps that the FCA may take, should the fund notify the regulator. What would you advise? _(4 marks)_

- c) The CEO then requests an immediate review of the fund's anti-market abuse systems and controls. In order to undertake this review, you prepare an outline of the potential risks to the firm and applicable internal policies and procedures that may be adopted to mitigate these. Explain the general content of the outline. _(6 marks)_

- d) On the Saturday morning, the worried portfolio manager calls you on your mobile. He asks you what the regulatory penalties are for his conduct and the consequences for him and the firm. What would you advise? _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised the issue of potential insider trading and market abuse in connection with the activities of a UK based hedge fund management company. This could generally be dealt with by applying insider trading and market abuse principles to the particular conversation that took place between the fund manager and an investment bank.

- a) Six marks were available for explaining the general nature of the market abuse regime including specific offences having regard to the particular circumstances. As much detail as possible should have been provided on relevant provisions. This includes the definition of market abuse in MAR.

- b) The FCA should be notified with the FCA expected to undertake an investigation that would include issuing requests for documentation and further information. Corrective action would include launching an immediate internal review, possibly reconstituting a note of the telephone recording (making it expressly clear that this was a reconstruction of the earlier conversation), reviewing the manager's notes on the refinancing deal, reviewing relevant internal policies including with regard to market abuse, conflicts of interest and the firm's open-desk policy. The firm may also offer to reopen the contract and repay the profit generated from the transaction. The FCA's power to conduct investigations and obtain information, documents or interview style could be referred to as well as the enforcement powers available to the FCA. The FCA may wish to discuss the matter with staff from the Listing department or from the Luxembourg Stock Exchange or regulatory authorities in Germany.

Reference may be made to the Tribunal decision on Paul Davidson (The Plumber) under Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights and the heightened standard of proof required by the FCA.

- c) In conducting the review, reference may be made to all types of relevant risk including market risk (equity risk, interest rate risk, currency risk and commodity risk including price, volatility, liquidity and basis risk) and operational risk (including regulatory, liquidity, delivery, political, human error, systemic, legal, IT, transaction and reputational risks). Specific reference could also be made to the relationship between reputational and ethical risk.

General requirements with the need to maintain adequate systems and controls specifically under SYSC should have been noted as well as the specific constituent elements of the market abuse and insider trading offences again. Relevant control systems should have been noted. Specific policies in connection with resolving conflicts of interest may also have be referred to including declining to act, disclosure, Chinese walls and independence policy, although this was not strictly necessary. While the question was focused on market abuse, additional reference may have been made to relevant insider trading provisions, although this was not strictly necessary. A relatively substantial answer was expected for the full six marks.

- d) The relevant penalties and offences with regard to market abuse should have been noted as well as additional potential failings in connection with PRIN, COCON and SYSC as well as the CISI Code of Conduct.

## B27.

### Question

You are the Compliance Officer in London for a brokerage firm which is 60% owned by a large US investment house. While inter-bank markets have remained relatively stable, rumours have spread around the City of London and New York about the stability of your US parent company. The brokerage firm has been able to borrow sufficient short-term funds until recently although only at increasingly higher rates over the last few weeks. Three of the non-executive directors on the Board of the UK firm approach you in connection with possibly disposing of some of their shares in the London company which is listed on the London Stock Exchange and the US parent company which is listed on NASDAQ.

The three directors had attended a full meeting of the Board two days previously where the funding difficulties of the UK operation had been discussed. The Chief Executive Officer (CEO) and Chairman both confirmed that they were satisfied with regard to solvency and continued viability of the US parent, although the UK firm may experience some credit difficulties going forward which may last a few months.

The non-executive directors had also attended a separate meeting of the Audit Committee during which the recent sharp decline in the UK firm's share price had been discussed. A copy of a separate external research report had been referred to which stated that the share price was expected to continue to fall.

The three non-executive directors had separately heard over coffee in the executive sitting room that a number of the executive members of the Board had already disposed of some of their shares. The non-execs wished to dispose of all of their current holdings and asked you whether there were any legal or regulatory restrictions that may apply.

- a) Explain the general legal and regulatory issues that arise. _(4 marks)_

- b) Identify the principal offences, penalties and defences that may apply with regard to market abuse. _(4 marks)_

- c) Advise whether any separate criminal law provisions may apply. _(4 marks)_ d) Advise on any separate reporting obligations that should have been complied with. _(4 marks)_

- e) Discuss whether any additional conflict of interest issues may arise. _(4 marks)_

### Answer

The question raised issues of share disposal following the internal notification of possible trading difficulties with specific reference to the responsibilities and possible liability of nonexecutive directors within a larger financial group. The fact pattern was loosely based on the circumstances surrounding the collapse of Lehman Brothers in September 2008.

- a) As candidates were to assume that they were the compliance officer, a short reference may have been made to the role and function of compliance within a regulated entity. The question then asked for relevant legal and regulatory issues to be identified. These include:

- i. Possible market abuse under MAR;

- ii. Possible insider trading under CJA 1993;

- iii. Possible market manipulation under Part 7 FSA 2012 (although less likely but still a relevant issue);

- iv. Role and function of non-executive directors (NEDs) and NED-COs;

- v. Possible liability of NEDs;

- vi. Possible liability of executive members for previous disposal;

- vii. Breach of PRIN, SYSC and COCON;

- viii. Breach of exchange rules on LSE and NASDAQ;

### Answer

## **a) (5 marks)**

## **What is meant by the terms 'insider information' and 'insider trading'? Why is insider trading deemed to be inappropriate?**

Inside information includes non-public information which, if made public, would be likely to influence the price of securities. A person in possession of such price-sensitive information is an insider if they know that this information is inside information and that it has been knowingly acquired from an inside source, such as a director, employee or shareholder of a firm issuing securities, or all criminal sources. The securities which may be affected by such insider information are referred to as price-affected securities. This information needs to be treated as proprietary information and not used or be allowed to influence any market transactions, by the individual or any third party. Insider dealing or trading on insider information is defined as the deliberate exploitation of insider information.

Some people consider insider dealing a victimless crime. It is not. Insider trading limits trust in markets and the financial system more generally and comes at a cost to other market participants without access to such inside information. The use of inside information for personal gain may also be viewed as theft of the information.

One mark was awarded for each valid point raised to a maximum of five marks.

## **b) (5 marks)**

## **Describe the principal laws and financial regulations which consider insider information and trading on insider information.**

A wide range of laws and financial regulations have been enacted and employed to limit insider trading and the misuse of insider information. Following the onshoring of the EU Market Abuse Regime (UK MAR), the UK's primary legislation is the Criminal Justice Act (CJA) 1993. The Criminal Justice Act 1993 outlines three ways of committing insider trading including dealing in the security, encouraging another to do so, and disclosing inside information to another. To be found guilty of insider dealing, a person must first be an insider who is in possession of inside information and, secondly, must commit one of these three specific offences.

Only certain investment instruments are affected by the insider dealing legislation. These are shares, debt securities, warrants, depositary receipts, options, futures, and contracts for difference (CFDs) based on securities, interest rates or share indices. This list notably does not include commodities, foreign exchange, metals or energy products, and shares/units in collective investment schemes and their associated derivatives. Under the CJA, insider dealing is a criminal offence. Under UK MAR, insider dealing is a civil offence. The (CJA) act was updated by the Insider Dealing (Securities and Regulated Markets) Order 2023, which amended the list of regulated markets.

One mark for each valid point made up to five marks.

## **c) (4 marks)**

## **What are the specific and general defences against trading on inside information?**

Market operators and investment firms which are involved in trading need to establish and maintain effective arrangements, systems and procedures to prevent and detect insider dealing and attempted insider dealing. These systems should detect and report suspicious orders and transactions to a competent body such as the regulator. Such systems can be proportionate to the activities of the firm, should involve both machine-led and human interrogation of transactions and orders, and the use of appropriately trained staff. As stated in the workbook these systems and procedures should:

- be appropriate and proportionate in relation to the scale, size and nature of their business activity

- be regularly assessed, at least annually (audit/internal review), and updated where necessary

- be clearly documented in writing, including any changes/updates, and documented information must be maintained for a period of five years

- allow for the analysis, individually and comparatively, of each and every transaction executed and order placed, modified, cancelled or rejected in the systems of a trading venue as well as transactions executed outside a trading venue in order to:

produce alerts indicating activities requiring further analysis for the purposes of detecting potential insider dealing or market manipulation or attempted insider dealing or market manipulation

cover the full range of trading activities undertaken.

- include control measures such as:

insider lists

personal account dealing controls

conflict of interest registers

trade monitoring

Chinese walls.

One mark was awarded for each valid point raised up to four marks.

## **d) (2 marks)**

## **Outline a situation which would qualify as insider trading.**

This is an open-ended question where there might be multiple valid answers. At the heart of situations of insider dealing, someone, associated with a listed company, or someone associated with that person buys or sells shares in that company. If this trading uses insider information, benefit is derived from this knowledge. For example, this type of insider trading might occur if someone uses information as to a future event or declaration to the market which is not publicly available. This might include a regulatory ruling or a prospective takeover by another firm. Alternatively, it includes actions such as front running whereby someone undertakes a transaction, just before a transaction undertaken by a client, in order to benefit from this trading activity.

The candidate should provide a credible example which fits all of the criteria. The criteria (which should have been given in the candidate's answer to 12a) include such points as the information being price sensitive, not public, specific and concise, as part of a regulated market. The candidate must make clear that at least four of the criteria are occurring in their example.

## **e) (4 marks)**

## **What controls should a financial services firm have in place to combat the misuse of insider information?**

Firms should have in place the following controls:

• issuers and persons acting on their behalf are required to draw up a list of all persons who have access to inside information, including where they are working for them under a contract of employment, or otherwise performing tasks through which they have access to inside information, such as advisers,

• procedures that ensure an appropriate level of human analysis in the monitoring, detection and identification of transactions and orders that could constitute insider dealing, market manipulation or attempted insider dealing or market manipulation

• processes to provide the FCA with the information to demonstrate the appropriateness and proportionality of their systems in relation to the scale, size and nature of business activity undertaken, including the information on the level of automation in their systems

• effective and comprehensive training to staff involved in the monitoring, detection and identification of orders and transactions that could constitute insider dealing, market manipulation or attempted insider dealing or market manipulation, in particular the provision of training on a regular basis and ensuring this is appropriate and proportionate in relation to the scale, size and nature of the business.

One mark was awarded for each valid point made up to four marks.
