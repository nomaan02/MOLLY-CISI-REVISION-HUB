## B1.

### Question

You have recently taken a new compliance role in a UK financial firm which sells investment products with advice. In your first few weeks at the firm, you observe a range of practices which raise concerns, as to the form and content of advice provided to clients and specifically how advice is provided on future investments. On reflection you feel it is important to develop a training course as to the needs and requirements for providing investment advice and whether this advice is suitable and recorded in an appropriate manner.

- **a)** What are the regulatory requirements and expectations for providing advice on investments and to ensure the advice provided is suitable for the client?

   - _(5 marks)_

- **b)** What factors determine whether the investment advice provided to a client is suitable and accounts for the risk tolerance of the client? _(5 marks)_

- **c)** What is a more suitable course of action for a compliance professional when discovering a range of practices which raise concerns as to the form and content of advice provided to clients? _(5 marks)_

- **d)** The market for providing financial advice is dynamic and there have been many developments in its financial regulation. Summarise the developments to the market for financial advice indicated within the Financial Advice Market Review (FAMR) (2015). _(5 marks)_

### Answer

a) The MiFID system was developed and redeveloped to ensure that advice on the provision of investments has been focused on providing appropriate investments for clients and that any advice provided is appropriate for the needs and the circumstances of the client. This might include many issues, for instance understanding the client's income, commitments, assets and ability to make decisions. Under the MiFID rules the firm needs to undertake a suitability assessment of the client's needs to inform any advice provided as to whether the client should buy, sell or hold an investment or other financial instrument. After the advice has been provided a copy of the suitability assessment and why the decisions as to the type/content of advice provided were made should be provided to the client. In some cases, repeated suitability assessment reports should be forwarded to the client. It is essential the overall advice leads to an outcome which is in the best interests of the client.

This information would include the following:

- the types of service, transaction, and financial instrument with which the client is familiar.

- the nature, volume, and frequency of the client's transactions in financial instruments and the period over which they have been carried out.

- the level of education and profession/relevant former profession of the client/potential client.

This information is used to then inform any advice provided. Such advice should adhere to the following:

- meets the client's investment objectives, including risk tolerance.

- the client has the financial ability to bear any related investment risks consistent with their investment objectives.

- the client has the necessary experience and knowledge to understand the risks involved in the transaction or in the management of their portfolio.

For non-MiFID related investment advice, the broad expectations are similar. Any advice should account for the client's knowledge and experience of making investment decisions, the circumstances of the client such as their financial situation and risk tolerance. Again, a suitability report should be provided under many circumstances. There are outcomes where such advice is not required.

b) Assessing the suitability of clients is a core activity in the provision of investment advice. Central to this requirement is ensuring that the risks a client is prepared to take are considered and incorporated in the advice and guidance provided to clients. Specifically, and as stated in the workbook, a firm should ensure it has a robust process for assessing the risks a client is willing and able to take, including:

- assessing a customer's capacity for loss.

- identifying customers that are best suited to placing their money in cash deposits because they are unwilling or unable to accept the risk of loss of capital.

- appropriately interpreting customer responses to questions and not attributing inappropriate weight to certain answers.

- tools, where used, are fit for purpose and any limitations recognised and mitigated.

- any questions and answers that are used to establish the risk a customer is willing and able to take, and descriptions used to check this, are fair, clear and not misleading.

- it has a robust and flexible process for ensuring investment selections are suitable, given a customer's investment objectives and financial situation, and it understands the nature and risks of products or assets selected for customers, and

- it engages customers in a suitability assessment process (including risk-profiling) which acts in the best interests of those customers.

c) There are many actions required when discovering a range of practices which raise concerns as to the form and content of advice provided to clients. It should be priority to undertake a thorough review of these practices. This would involve a past business review and a root cause analysis of the forms of advice which raise concerns. At the heart of such a review is an examination as to whether customers have been in any way disadvantaged. These reviews need to be undertaken in a highly structured and transparent manner as there is a high possibility the financial regulator will need to be informed as to this review being undertaken with outside scrutiny of this examination. If concerns are seen to have disadvantaged customers, the potential for future actions such as remediation is present.

d) The Financial Advice Market Review (FAMR) was released in 2016 and provides several recommendations. These include:

1. Affordability – can clients afford financial advice, developing robo-advice etc. delivering tailored advice and enhancing the ability of the public to make financial decisions?

2. Accessibility – building trust in the market for financial advice and increasing the ability for people to access these services at reasonable cost.

3. Liabilities and consumer redress – enhancing the funding of the Financial Services Compensation Scheme.

In the evaluation of these recommendations in 2020 the FCA indicated that progress had been made in improving the market, yet this progress had been very slow. Most financial advice still is provided to the relatively wealthy and most advice is holistic considering the scope of a client's financial commitments and circumstances. Further robo-advice has been quite slow to develop and a significant proportion of the population of the UK has funds to effectively invest yet are still reluctant to undertake this decision due to continued uncertainties with seeking and using financial advice.

## B2.

### Question

Mark is a sales representative at a large insurance provider. His role involves selling life insurance and critical illness policies to consumers. Mark is under pressure to meet aggressive sales targets, and the firm offers significant commission incentives for selling premium policies. One day, he meets Olivia, a single mother who is looking for a life insurance policy to provide financial security for her child. Olivia has a limited budget and is not familiar with insurance products. Instead of recommending a basic policy that meets her needs, Mark persuades her to buy an expensive policy with additional features she does not fully understand. He assures her that it is the best option, downplaying the long-term costs and the cancellation penalties. A year later, Olivia struggles to afford the premiums and realises the policy is far more expensive than she was led to believe. She files a complaint, stating that she was misled about the costs and suitability of the policy.

- a) Which of the FCA key principles have been potentially breached by Mark and the firm?

   - ( _7 marks_ )

- b) What steps should the firm take to ensure compliance with Consumer Duty in situations like this?

   - ( _7 marks_ )

- c) What are the potential regulatory consequences for the firm and Mark if they are found to have failed in their duty to Olivia?

   - ( _6 marks_ )

### Answer

This question asked candidates the FCA's Consumer Duty within the context of a fictional case study involving the sale of insurance and critical illness policies to consumers.

a) The first part of the question asked candidates how the situation relates to the FCA's Consumer Duty, and what key principles one of the characters of the case study may potentially have breached. Responses missed marks by predominantly focusing on the individual's behaviour without recognising the firm's wider failings.

Several FCA Principles for Businesses appear to have been breached in this scenario, both by the individual sales representative and the firm as a whole. The conduct raises serious concerns under Principle 1 (Integrity), as there was a failure to act honestly by downplaying the costs and features of the policy. There is also a breach of Principle 2 (Skill, Care and Diligence), as the individual did not act with appropriate care when recommending a complex and costly product to a vulnerable customer with a limited budget. Principle 6 (Customers' Interests) is likely breached, as neither the individual nor the firm acted in the customer's best interests. The recommendation appears to have been motivated by commission incentives, and the firm's culture may have enabled or encouraged this behaviour. Principle 7 (Communications with Clients) is also relevant, as the individual failed to provide clear, fair, and not misleading information, resulting in a financial decision the customer did not fully understand.

The introduction of Principle 12 (Consumer Duty) further underlines the seriousness of these breaches. Both the individual and the firm likely failed to support customer understanding, did not properly assess vulnerability, and disregarded affordability constraints. These failures could have been avoided with a proper suitability assessment, but the lack of due diligence and care led to a poor customer outcome. The firm may also be in breach of its obligations to ensure that remuneration and incentive structures do not conflict with the customer's best interests. The behaviour was clearly influenced by a poorly designed bonus scheme, and the firm failed to monitor or mitigate the risks associated with sales-driven conduct.

b) The second question of the case study asked candidate what steps the firm should take to ensure compliance with Consumer Duty in situations like this. Responses missed marks by providing broad explanations of the Consumer Duty without applying it to the specific details of the scenario. They referred to fairness or positive customer outcomes in general terms but failed to link their answers to the cross-cutting rules or the four required outcomes, resulting in responses that lacked precision and regulatory alignment.

To ensure compliance with the Consumer Duty, the firm must adopt a structured approach that addresses both the cross-cutting rules, and the four outcomes set out by the FCA. Acting in good faith means putting the customer's needs ahead of commercial targets. This requires designing sales processes and incentive structures that reflect the customer's best interests rather than focusing solely on financial gain. Firms must also take active steps to support and enable customers to make informed decisions. In this case, the customer did not receive adequate information or support to properly understand the product being purchased. All product communications must be clear, fair, and not misleading, with advisers checking the customer's understanding throughout the sales process.

Firms also have an obligation to avoid causing foreseeable harm. Indicators such as a limited budget or financial vulnerability should be identified early in the advice process. A thorough affordability and vulnerability assessment should be conducted, and any recommendation should reflect the customer's actual needs and financial capacity. When considering the four outcomes, the firm must ensure its products and services are designed with vulnerable customers in mind, using available data and insight to assess whether product features and risks are suitable for the intended market.

The price and value outcome are particularly relevant in this scenario. A high-cost product was sold that offered features unnecessary for the customer's circumstances and which were not well understood. To improve consumer understanding, the firm should strengthen its communication strategy by simplifying documentation, providing explanatory tools for complex terms, and assessing comprehension at key stages in the advice process. Communications must be accessible and appropriate for varying levels of financial literacy.

Finally, firms must clearly define and adhere to their target markets. Sales practices should be reviewed regularly to ensure that products are being sold appropriately, and that customers at greater risk of harm are not being mis-sold unsuitable solutions. Ongoing sales monitoring, frequent staff training, and root cause analysis of complaints are essential to building a customer-focused culture that aligns with the expectations of the Consumer Duty.

c) The third section of this question asked candidates what the potential regulatory consequences for the firm and its sales representative are if they are found to have failed in their duty to the customer. Responses missed marks by failing to explain both the regulatory and personal consequences of breaching Consumer Duty, often focusing only on firm-level penalties without addressing potential disciplinary action for individuals. Some overlooked key powers available to the FCA, such as imposing enhanced reporting obligations, ordering remediation programmes, or requiring changes to sales incentives.

If the FCA determines that there has been a breach of Consumer Duty obligations, both the individual involved, and the firm could face serious consequences. The firm may be required to compensate affected customers through a formal remediation process. Financial penalties could be imposed, particularly if the regulator identifies systemic failings in sales practices. The firm might also be subjected to heightened regulatory scrutiny, including mandatory audits and more stringent oversight of its insurance sales activities. Where widespread issues are identified, the firm could be instructed to revise its incentive structures or eliminate certain high-risk sales behaviours. Additional reporting obligations may be introduced, requiring the firm to provide regular updates demonstrating progress in addressing compliance weaknesses.

On an individual level, disciplinary action may include suspension, financial penalties, or prohibition from working in regulated financial services. If there is evidence of deliberate customer deception, this could trigger enforcement action with long-term consequences for career and reputation. In serious cases, personal liability may arise under Section 138D of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000, allowing affected customers to seek damages for breaches of regulatory requirements.

Beyond legal and regulatory consequences, reputational damage can be substantial. Findings of mis-selling can erode consumer trust, attract negative media attention, and lead to the loss of clients and partners. In cases where misconduct is proven to be systemic or cultural, the firm may even face restrictions on its permissions or, in extreme circumstances, a revocation of its licence to operate in the insurance sector.

## B3.

### Question

A rich far eastern domiciled individual approaches a small private securities firm in London to discuss possible investment opportunities. The individual claims to be a sophisticated investor. Consider each of the following:

- a) How would you categorise the prospective client for FCA Conduct of Business (COBS) purposes? Under which types of headings may the client be categorised and what information would you require to make any proper determination? _(5 marks)_

- b) If the prospective client is assigned one status initially, in what circumstances may this be adjusted subsequently? Comment, in particular, on the possibility that the prospective client may wish to have either more, or less protection than that already provided. _(5 marks)_

- c) What documentation requirements generally apply under the COBS rules before you can begin to conduct any regulated activities on behalf of the client? _(5 marks)_

- d) If the prospective client is interested in investing in financial derivatives on either a discretionary or execution-only basis, do any further requirements have to be complied with? _(5 marks)_

### Answer

Again a typical client categorisation question from the examiner. This needed a good explanation from you of the requirements of the rules regarding categorisation criteria, documentation and record keeping.

- a) Every client must be categorised under COBS before a firm commences investment business with or for the client. This requires the firm to take steps to establish whether the client is a retail client, a professional client or an eligible counterparty.

Since the client in this case is an individual, only retail client or professional client status will be relevant. All persons who do not fall into any other category must be categorised as retail clients (who receive the greatest protection under COBS).

The client here does not meet the criteria to be categorised as a per se professional client. He could be either a retail client or an elective professional client.

Firms are required to retain an appropriate record of the categorisation of each client, including sufficient information to support the categorisation. Then records must be retained for an appropriate period of time – usually three years (five years in the case of MiFID business and indefinitely in the case of pension transfers and opt-outs).

- b) If the client is initially categorised as a retail client, he may be re-categorised as an elective professional client if the form complies with the qualitative test and the procedures. The qualitative test requires the firm to assess the client as capable of making his own investment decisions and understanding the risks involved. The procedures require the client to state in writing that he wishes to be treated as a professional client; he must be given a clear written warning by the firm of the protections and compensation rights he may lose; and he must state in writing that he is aware of the consequences of this. (There is also a quantitative test in the case of MiFID business which relates to the frequency of the client's transactions, the value of his portfolio and his professional financial sector working experience.)

If the client is initially categorised as an elective professional client, he may be recategorised as a retail client either at the client's own request or when the firm becomes aware that the client no longer fulfils the relevant requirements for elective professional client status.

- c) There are three main items of documentation that the firm must provide to a client before conducting regulated activities:

- i.  Client agreement. This sets out the essential rights and obligations of the firm and the client and it is required when a firm carries on designated investment business for a retail client and (in relation to MiFID business) for a professional client.

- ii.   Information about the firm, its services and remuneration. This must be provided in a durable medium either before any service is provided or (if this is impossible) as soon as possible thereafter.

- iii.   Order execution policy. In order to fulfil its obligations under the best execution rule, the firm must provide its clients with its order execution policy and obtain the client's consent to this policy before transacting any business.

You should discuss the suitability COBS (for discretionary) and appropriateness COBS (for execution-only trades) in derivatives.

- d) If the client is categorised as an elective professional client, the firm would need to keep this status under review in order to ensure that it continues to be appropriate. In addition, the firm would need to keep under review its basic documentation (and notify the client of changes, as necessary):

   - Information about the firm (including contact details, conflicts of interest policy, client assets arrangements, costs and charges, etc);

   - Client agreement (changes to terms of business);

   - Order execution policy (changes to policy, execution venues, etc).

Other documentation could include:

- Initial disclosure documents and menus (packaged products);

- Occasional and periodic reporting;

- Compliance with record keeping rules.

## B7.

### Question

- a) Ruth Kendall, a retail client, telephoned Bell Jones, a firm of brokers and a member of the London Stock Exchange and regulated by the FCA. She asked the firm to sell shares on her behalf. Bell Jones effected the deal by buying some of the shares as principal and selling the remainder in the market as agent. The total due to Ruth amounted to £28,000. Two days after effecting these transactions Bell Jones sent Ruth a letter and contract confirming the deal together with the transfer documents. The latter were completed and returned promptly by her with the relevant share certificates. Two weeks elapsed during which time Ruth heard nothing from Bell Jones. She then telephoned the firm and was told that her funds were available. She was asked whether she would prefer to reinvest the money in a combination of gilts and options or alternatively whether she would prefer the cash. The firm's employee with whom she spoke emphasised several times that as a widow she should have her money working for her on the market. Ruth requested Bell Jones to pay a quarter of the sum into her bank account and to retain three quarters to invest on her behalf. The firm sent Ruth a cheque for £7,000 drawn on its current account and placed £21,000 into a client bank account (designated as the Ruth Kendall account). Bell Jones subsequently purchased from that account gilts and when it received the certificates the firm used them as collateral for its bank overdraft. Bell Jones has since been put into receivership by the bank for defaulting on loan payments.

Advise Ruth about the following matters:

   - (i) What rule breaches have, or may have, occurred? _(8 marks)_ (ii) What are her prospects of recovering her money? _(2 marks)_

- b) ABC Co is a member of the London Stock Exchange and regulated by the FCA. It acts in a broker dealer capacity. It is a wholly owned subsidiary of XYZ Co, a registered market maker in all leading and many smaller company shares. One of ABC retail clients, Brown, a non-discretionary retail client, has a modest portfolio of 'blue chip' investments and wants to buy some smaller AIM shares as long-term investments. He has also been reading about LIFFE equity options and thinks that they might offer him a 'bit of excitement'. Brown tries to contact his usual sales executive at ABC Co to discuss his intentions as he has not dealt in these investments before. However, the executive is out at lunch and so Brown is put through to another executive, Smith, a trader, with whom he has not previously spoken. Without hesitation Smith recommends several AIM company shares and traded options to Brown. Brown accepts the recommendations and instructs Smith to deal in the AIM shares. Smith goes to lunch and returns just before the close of business. He executes hurriedly buying orders with XYZ Co in several AIM company shares. The following morning, without further consultation with Brown, he transacts a traded option deal for him through another broker.

Analyse and discuss the regulatory issues arising from these transactions under the Conduct of Business Rules, and identify any breaches you suspect may have occurred.

_(10 marks)_

### Answer

a)

- (i) It would appear that the following rule breaches have, or may have, occurred:

- It is unclear if the original sale of shares was on an execution-only basis. If not, was suitability relevant, and was it considered? If the order was as a result of advice given by the firm, then Bell Jones is obliged to consider suitability.

- The contract note (confirmation) should have been despatched no later than the day following the transaction rather than two days later. On the contract note the fact that part of the transaction was as principal must be stated.

- The funds should have been despatched to Ruth without her having to request them.

   - When Ruth was told that the funds 'were available' they do not appear to have been put into a client account, which they should have been following settlement.

   - The provision of the investment advice might involve a number of rule breaches, although not all are clear from the limited information:

      1. Is there a written client agreement in place?

      2. Was the employee qualified to provide investment advice (approved person status)?

      3. What evidence is there that suitability was considered? Advising a widow to have her money working on the market is contrary to the advice that is usually given in these circumstances. (However, you must bear in mind that a widow is not necessarily either poor or lacking in investment expertise.)

      4. Are options suitable?

      5. The repeated emphasis by the employee may constitute undue pressure being placed on the customer.

   - The request was for £7,000 to be paid into Ruth's bank account. This implies that an arrangement was in force that would enable a direct transfer to be made. If this is so, Bell Jones should have organised the transfer rather than sending a cheque.

   - The information is unclear as to whether or not the firm was given discretion as to when to invest the £21,000 or was given an immediate dealing instruction. If the former was the case, then the firm acted properly in (belatedly) putting the money into a client bank account; whereas, if the latter was true, the firm has broken the timely execution rule.

   - The gilts should either have been registered in the name of Ruth Kendall or in the firm's nominee, in which case the certificate must be kept separate from any gilts owned by the firm in its own name. We have no information as to what happened.

   - The firm is not permitted to use the gilts as collateral (and if they were correctly registered in Ruth Kendall's name, the bank should not have accepted them as such).

- (ii) As regards recovering her money, it is unlikely that Ruth will be able to recover anything from the firm if it is in receivership. Depending upon the precise circumstances, she might have a claim against the firm's directors and/or the bank. As a last resort, she should be able to make a claim on the Financial Services Compensation Scheme, which was set up to assist eligible claimants who have lost money in the event of a firm's insolvency.

- b) The following are some of the regulatory issues and rule breaches that appear to be relevant:

- A firm must take reasonable steps to ensure that a personal recommendation, or a decision to trade, is suitable for its client. This involves obtaining appropriate information about the client's investment knowledge and experience, financial situation and investment objectives.

- The firm must have a reasonable basis for believing that a proposed transaction meets the client's investment objectives and is one in relation to which the client has the necessary experience and knowledge to understand the risks and is able financially to bear those risks.

- In the light of Brown's background, it is doubtful whether an investment in equity options (or, to a lesser extent, smaller AIM shares) would meet the required criteria for suitability.

- The rules on client order handling provide for the prompt, fair and expeditious execution of client orders. Concerns arise as a result of the delay in execution (going out to lunch first), which suggests that the orders were not handled promptly, fairly and expeditiously.

- In addition, the rule on best execution requires a firm to take all sufficient steps to obtain the best possible result for its client, taking into account the execution factors. The statement that buying orders were executed "hurriedly" suggests that the firm's obligations under the best execution rule may not have been fulfilled.

- There is no satisfactory explanation given for transacting a traded option deal on the following morning.

- Smith is not performing a certified function. As a trader, he would not need to have approved person status, and he would not be permitted to make a personal recommendation to a client.

## B35. *(Compulsory)*

### Question

Problems in demonstrating client suitability are a continuing source of comment and criticism of the industry by the FCA as financial regulator. Your director has told you, as the compliance officer in a small firm of financial advisers with a wide spread of clients, that he wants you to design and initiate a programme to ensure that your advisers adhere to all of the relevant regulations.

He adds that because of your firm's public commitment to the highest standards of integrity, he wants the programme to focus initially on ethical principles and considerations in the advisory process and then to ensure that it meets other regulatory requirements. Explain how you would design such a programme. Focus on the issues above, identifying key risk areas and demonstrate how you would deal with them to meet the task set by your director, using examples. _(20 marks)_

### Answer

The question was concerned with the design of a compliance programme for client suitability incorporating appropriate ethical elements. This could have been dealt with in a number of ways.

Candidates could have referred to the importance of the compliance and audit functions within a firm and the general functions and responsibilities involved. This could have included the general risks managed although this was not strictly necessary. Specific provision should then have been included with regard to relevant ethical standards and then suitability requirements.

Candidates could have referred to the FCA's general approach to ethics as set out in the draft Code of Ethics in CP09/18, Competence and Ethics document (CP10/12) and the Conduct Rules (COCON). The (then) FSA had issued a Discussion Paper on Ethics in October 2002. Reference may also have been made to Hector Sants' speech on 'Regulatory Culture' in October 2010 and FSA discussion paper on ethics in October 2002. Appropriate principles could also have been incorporated such as using those of the CISI Code.

Specific reference could have been made to particular FCA Handbook provisions concerning client relations and suitability including within PRIN, SYSC and COCON and COBS. Specific reference could also have been made to the FCA's Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC), Retail Distribution Review (RDR), Consumer Duty (PRIN 12), and similar initiatives.

Each of the key provisions within PRIN, SYSC and COCON concerning client relations should have been outlined and then the detailed requirements imposed under COBS 9 on suitability. These include general provisions (COBS 9.1), suitability assessments (COBS 9.2), guidance (COBS 9.3), suitability reports (COBS 9.4), record keeping and retention (COBS 9.5) as well as special provisions concerning stakeholder products (COBS 9.6), initial disclosure of information (Annex 1) and sales processes (Annex 2). Reference could also have been made to client classification in COBS 3.
