## 1. The General Approach to the Exam

The Diploma is the highest award available from the CISI to candidates that is based upon regulatory qualifications rather than upon investment, dealing or front-office activities of any kind. It is a professional level award and, therefore, in order to address it successfully you must think like a compliance officer. When reading the questions in the paper which present a scenario, always imagine what the person in the question is faced with, despite you not having the direct experience yourself, and analyse it from that point of view.

Remember that, on passing the Regulation and Compliance exam, you will enhance your attractiveness as a potential employee for all firms in the securities and financial services sector. Holders will be respected as having attained an outstanding level of competence and achievement.

Unsurprisingly, this exam concentrates on the management aspects of regulations and their impact on firms. There is less attention paid by the examiner to the very detailed knowledge, which may be more relevant at other examination levels. Mastery of detail is relevant and is expected in certain areas of the syllabus, but the important thing for candidates taking the CISI Regulation and Compliance exam is to be able to understand and communicate from an overall management viewpoint. Performance in the examination is likely to be enhanced by having practical experience and knowledge of the financial services sector.

There is only one objective for you and that is to pass the exam. This workbook has been written from that practical standpoint.

The purpose of the workbook is to enable candidates to know and understand regulations and be able to pass the exam. However, unlike the multiple choice question based exams, just reading the workbook will not in itself enable a candidate to pass the exam. You should aim to acquire a good knowledge and understanding of regulations and how to apply them in situations or scenarios as laid out in the exam questions.

## 2. Examination Structure

A three-hour-and-fifteen-minute examination, with the paper divided into three sections:

| Section | Format | Marks | Weight |
|---------|--------|-------|--------|
| **A** | Up to ten compulsory short answer questions | 30 | 30% |
| **B** | Choice of 4 practical/problem-solving questions; answer 2 (**one compulsory**) | 40 | 40% |
| **C** | Choice of 4 essay questions; answer 2 | 30 | 30% |

**Section B** marks are awarded to reflect not only technical knowledge, but the ability to apply that knowledge to the problem and to deal practically and effectively with the situation presented, **INCLUDING AN ETHICAL AND PROFESSIONAL RESPONSE TO THE SITUATION, BASED UPON THE CISI CODE OF CONDUCT, WHERE APPROPRIATE**.

**Section C** marks are awarded to reflect both knowledge and understanding, together with clarity of arguments presented.

## 3. Booking the Exam

The CISI makes full details available on its website, describing the exam booking process and timetable. Exams are held twice per year in June and December. There is a fee payable, and results are published in February and August.

## 4. How the Exam is Structured — Section by Section

### Section A

Section A normally comprises around ten **short-answer compulsory questions** and contributes a total of 30 marks, representing 30% of the total marks. It is really a test of one's knowledge. Most candidates tend to execute this section first; however, sections, and indeed questions, may be attempted in whatever sequence one wishes.

In Section A the examiner is looking for brief, factual answers, and it is advised that candidates express a concise yet clear answer in a few short sentences or statements. Questions in this section may require you to define or explain something, but there is no discussion element.

As facts are key in this section of the overall paper, when preparing for the examination you must therefore commit such knowledge to memory. Always write enough to ensure that the meaning is properly conveyed, but do not over-elaborate.

### Section B

In Section B, which is worth 40 marks (40%), candidates are required to address one compulsory case study, and then choose another question from a choice of three. This will test your ability to deal with a number of related problems arising from a scenario and data provided. This section provides the examiner with an opportunity to create a practical example and ask questions as to how you would analyse and approach a problem. The examiners do explicitly show how many marks are available for answering specific questions within the overall section. The indicated number of marks tells you where to devote your effort. Time spent in thinking and planning is vitally important. The examiners always like to see evidence of a plan being drafted by candidates here.

The case studies tend to present evidences (perhaps in the form of a table of information) which has to be analysed and conclusions then drawn in line with the questions asked.

Section B is noted for its ability to separate those candidates with sound depth of knowledge and relevant experience, from those without a solid framework upon which to structure their answers. It very much separates the wheat from the chaff across the cohort.

### Section C

Only in Section C are you given a free choice of questions. It requires you to select two questions from the four presented. Each one offers 15 marks, thus overall this is for 30 marks (30% of the total marks available). You may be asked to write short reports, memoranda or letters, with the examiners effectively reserving the right to request answers in whatever format they wish; so be prepared to be flexible and understand the differences in formats. If the question says 'write a report' then please do just that. Why do anything else?

These questions will be either discursive or draw upon specific technical areas of expertise which will include topical issues. Candidates' ability to communicate clearly and concisely will form an element of the assessment.

Section C will tend to include a question on current or future developments, such as the impact of new regulations on markets, industry or the impact of EU legislation; or on topical issues such as ethics in financial services or the changing focus and shape of regulations. It is therefore necessary to be able to put yourself (at least mentally) in the position of those who make decisions on compliance within firms (ie, the firm's compliance officer).

The questions in this section are often described as 'discursive'. The examiners are looking to assess whether or not you are able to discuss the points raised by the question, and they may also address future industry developments. In order to be able to do so, you will need both background and factual knowledge. Regularly reading the news as reported in the financial press is important. You should keep up to date on topics of importance and on developing issues. Questions are regularly set on topical issues.

The CISI website contains the last four exam papers and the Chief Examiners Report. Candidates should use these papers to get an understanding of the type of questions that will be asked in the exam.

## 5. Subject Areas

The following subjects have appeared in the exam for the last few years in Sections B and C:

- Regulated activities.
- Environmental, social and governance (ESG).
- Market abuse.
- Financial crime.
- Conduct of business (advice/suitability, financial promotions).
- Conduct risk.
- Senior Managers & Certification Regime (SM&CR).
- UK regulatory structure.
- EU legislation/global regulations.
- Ethics (business conduct).
- Enforcement.

There are many other subjects about which questions have been asked on a less frequent basis, and careful examination of previous exam papers (available on the CISI website) will stand you in good stead if you read through them. It is imperative that candidates read and understand the exam structure, as this will help them in their studies and approach to the exam.

Very occasionally, a one-off question will crop up which has never been seen before on some fairly esoteric topic. However, by and large, candidates can expect to find a fair set of questions in the exam paper, offering everyone a fair chance to achieve success in the exam. Not everyone will possess the necessary specific industry background to answer absolutely every Section C question. Therefore, it is important that candidates keep up to date with industry topical issues and discussions, which may appear in the exam.

## 6. Model Answers

Previous exam papers, and the chief examiner's reports (and answers), are available to view on the CISI website (under Qualifications > Past Papers and Reports).

To get an idea of what the examiner is expecting from candidates, what previous candidates did right and wrong in previous exams, and how to judge the length and structure of your answers, it is valuable to read the reports written after marking each set of papers by the chief examiner, also available on the CISI website (as above).

There is no correct model answer to any question. Indeed, when you look at some of the examiner's feedback documents, you will realise that it would not be possible to write all the suggested content for an answer in the time available. The examiner is looking for a well-structured answer that meets the standard required. Every candidate has their own unique experience, and the ability to bring to bear your practical experience will be to your benefit.

Sometimes questions are posed by examiners that ask candidates to present broad overviews. You have to be careful here if you have really deep knowledge of a subject; the selectivity that is required can only be addressed by planning against the specific question as set. Try to relate every element of the paragraph to the question concerned. For example, a candidate might see that a certain question is addressing regulatory issues surrounding hedge funds. Perhaps that candidate works in hedge funds and knows an enormous amount about them. However, the question will focus on a specific aspect concerning the regulation of hedge funds. It is evident, therefore, that there is a need to be specific and selective in the response.

## 7. Pass Mark

As with all its diploma papers, there is no explicit pass mark published by the CISI, and the decision is based upon the overall impression that the candidate's script makes on the examiner and on the Regulation and Compliance examination panel which sets the paper. While the CISI does not publish a pass mark, grades are awarded as shown below. **Think of 55% as being the required pass mark.**

- A — Distinction.
- B — Merit.
- C — Pass.
- D — Marginal fail.
- E — Fail.
- U — Unclassified.

A marginal fail represents a shortfall of no more than three marks beneath the required pass level. Candidates should aim to avoid being in this bracket, but nevertheless some will be. The examining panel will moderate all scripts in this segment and additional marks may be awarded. No marks will be deducted from the original mark awarded resulting from the moderation process.

## 8. What is the Examiner Looking For?

The successful candidate will demonstrate to the examiner and to the Regulation and Compliance panel that they have the ability to communicate and present a well-structured answer demonstrating knowledge of relevant issues, highlighting how processes fit together and drawing a coherent conclusion. The ability to demonstrate a practical application towards management of these issues will be expected.

The following summary list of thoughts may prove useful:

- Evidence of planning.
- A well-constructed answer which is a pleasure to read.
- Legible handwriting.
- Use of examples, diagrams and headings where appropriate.

**As has been said hundreds of times before, it is important for all candidates to read and understand the question and to answer the question as set. Do not stray into any other area and keep your answers relevant to the question posed by the examiners.** Furthermore, there is no point in representing the question in the written answer. Successful candidates will be able to demonstrate how the industry works and describe its 'moving parts' and how they operate, showing knowledge of the linkages. Therefore, the examiner is seeking a relatively high-level answer, but one that is able to draw together all the strands of knowledge that are required. It is not a test of your ability to show understanding of enormous degrees of in-depth detail. Detail can always be looked up in a work situation. It is the overall view that is important, together with a logical understanding of the pressures and responsibilities that fall upon compliance and operations managers.

Examiners report that they very often see **poor presentation skills** from candidates. Don't let yourself fall into that trap. Similarly, be able to be topical and quote examples. You are bound to have good experience upon which you can draw from your own job and roles that you have held within firms you have worked for, so prepare widely and undertake additional reading, which will enable you to present yourself in the finest possible shape for the written examination.

## 9. Keys to Success — Target Marks

| Section | Target | Strategy |
|---------|--------|----------|
| **A** (30 marks) | **20+ marks** | Factual. Requires knowledge gained through experience or revision. If you achieve 20+, you are well on your way to passing. |
| **B** (40 marks) | **~25 marks** | Compulsory question + one choice from three. The real test of whether a candidate can deliver. Case studies straddle UK/international regulatory knowledge, management skills, business approach and risk management. |
| **C** (30 marks) | **10+ per question** | Free choice of two from four. Demonstrate knowledge, views, know-how and practical experience against the backdrop of current industry challenges. |

In Section C, with two questions each worth 15 marks, 10 marks or more for each would see you through to passing the exam, providing that your scores in the other sections are satisfactory.

## 10. Studying and Preparation

Passing the examination will depend on your ability to prepare sufficiently well in the time available. This workbook is intended to support you in that process. In itself, it will not be sufficient. Students can take a training course provided by an accredited CISI training provider or prepare on their own as self-study. Whichever approach is selected by candidates, they will require outside reading and study in order, particularly, to keep abreast of current regulatory developments.

It is desirable for you to have, or to develop, an awareness of the big picture of the financial services sector in order to be ready for the exam. It is important to read articles in the financial press, both newspapers and industry magazines, such as the _Financial Times_ and _The Economist_. It is well worth reading articles from specialist publications, such as the CISI's _The Review_, law firms and regulatory practices of accountancy firms. Quite often, exam question topics will show some correlation with topical industry issues.

Preparation for the Regulation and Compliance examination requires a broad, diverse knowledge of markets and practices, and an ability to understand and implement processes, procedures and controls that reflect the diverse nature of the international markets.

Although not specifically required by the syllabus, the internet has a wealth of useful information, and search engines can be used to find information on key words and topics. The CISI syllabus gives examples of further reading and useful external websites.

**Remember that these exams are aimed to test higher skills, and successful candidates will be expected to show a keen interest in the subject matter and its development.**

## 11. Exam Technique

**Candidates must read the question, understand what is being asked and answer the questions being asked, not what they think it should mean. All too often, candidates do not answer the question being asked, wasting valuable time.**

Section A contains the most questions, but they are short and should be addressed concisely. Some questions may be for one mark only. There are also compulsory questions, and the failure to address any question will leave a gap in your ability to attain marks. A lot of the questions in Section A may ask you to define something, do a simple calculation or explain something straightforwardly. No single question should take that long and collectively this 30-mark section should be achievable within 45 minutes. There is no need to write plans in Section A. Do not exceed this time estimate, as it will eat into valuable planning and execution time for the other sections.

Section A is also quite likely to address aspects to do with risk and elements of regulation, but in a short concise manner. It is useful to utilise glossaries in order to learn and be able to define product names, well-known abbreviations and other industry jargon.

Sections B and C are intended to test the student's overall knowledge and management ability with respect to regulations. Questions can address the UK regulatory structure, but may also examine international regulatory regimes and approaches. You are recommended to bring your own experience to bear, and in the exam you should be able to demonstrate the ability to analyse information, to construct an opinion, and to be able to express your thoughts, conclusions and recommendations about scenarios posed. Again, as indicated repeatedly, the planning step for these questions is of great importance.

Check your work as you go, and certainly reread it to make sure that it all fits together properly and makes sense.

Be prepared to justify your opinions solidly. If you make assumptions, define what they are; otherwise the examiner will not know. In Section C, you will need to allow for reading time to address all questions from which you may choose. This will take a little bit of time and finally you will need to address two of the four questions set.

**Pay attention to the way you present your work.** Examiners have to mark the papers so make sure your handwriting is clear and legible. **Space your work carefully, use headings and do not write in lists or sets of bullet points**. Remember that your answers must be relevant to the question asked and refer to any of the important matters, including regulatory and risk issues as defined in the question.

## 12. Presentation of Your Scripts

As this is a written exam, a candidate's ability to use English effectively is essential. You are advised to take as much care as you can to ensure that your grammar, spelling and punctuation are addressed well. Professional exams require answers that are of a professional standard.

## 13. Sensible Use of Time

This is where planning helps. You do not want to run out of time so that you are unable to answer one of the, for example, Section C questions with 15 marks. Remember that **the examiner cannot award marks unless there is evidence in your work that knowledge exists and is demonstrated**. They cannot anticipate what you might have said, although a plan might indicate what you intended to write about.

Don't underestimate the time it takes to write a few brief pages of hand-written text. The accent must be on quality rather than quantity. People's capacities vary, of course, and it may be that your ability exceeds that of others, but don't be too ambitious. Planning will help you to concentrate on what is important and bring out those facts in a logical manner and in the format that is requested for the question you are answering.

### Recommended Time Allocation

| Section | Minimum | Maximum |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Section A** | 30 minutes | 45 minutes |
| **Section B** | 45 minutes | 60 minutes |
| **Section C** | — | 45 minutes per question |

These suggested times indicate that you need to be flexible, because some people will spend too long on Section A and put themselves under pressure in other sections. Some students take far longer than 60 minutes for Section B, but this will always compromise time available elsewhere and may lead to disappointment. It is most important to monitor and plan time carefully and allocate that time according to how many marks are available. For example, in Section A, if a question is worth 2 marks, then write a short answer, making it clear to the examiner that you understand the facts that they are looking for. Do not write half a page; this is pointless because only 2 marks are available.

You will need to develop your own style for time management based upon previous exam experience or indeed by practice exam writing, possibly using past questions or mock exams which accredited training providers can provide for you. It is also necessary not to forget that time should be managed and allocated carefully within a multi-part question, such as a case study which might have three or four subsections to it.

## 14. Types of Questions

**Section A** will always be a series of short compulsory questions to test a student's overall knowledge. These may include:

- **Definitions** – effectively of any word or industry phrase.

- **Names** (including abbreviations) – examples of this might be: what is Section 165 of FSMA, and how does it impact regulated firms?

- **Outlines or lists** – the examiner might ask you to state four types of regulated and permitted activities, or to list four defences against the charge of market abuse/insider dealing.

- **Similarities and differences** – the examiner might ask you to compare and contrast two related or similarly related services, for example comparing and contrasting the UK and the US regulatory approach and legislation in respect of insider dealing, market abuse or short selling.

**Section B:**

- **Case studies** – as indicated earlier, Section B will always take the form of a semi-authentic case study.

**Section C:**

- **Real-life case studies** – in Section C, you may be asked to comment on something that has really happened in the industry, for example, the change to the UK regulatory structure following Brexit, the rise and importance of 'ESG', increased scrutiny of conduct (both financial and non-financial misconduct), or other major stories.

- **Technical questions** – although it has been stated that a great deal of detailed knowledge is not generally required in the exam, it will be necessary for candidates to understand why certain technical matters are important and to be able to weigh up the scale of these impacts in an answer, and therefore, to draw the necessary conclusions from the questions asked.

- **Questions on topical issues** – these will be selected in order to pose questions about industry events, major developments or issues of debate in the industry. For example, recent exams have pointed at the issues presented by the change to UK regulatory structure post-Brexit, the rise and importance of ESG, and even on cryptoassets. A question might ask for benefits and disadvantages and then for your recommendations. If this is the case, you must make sure that you present a balanced view, and indeed have enough information and opinion to state your recommendations clearly. Should you be asked about priorities, it is important that you identify those and state with clarity how you would rank them and indeed how you would address them. Always seek to be as explicit as possible; this engenders clarity in your thought processes, avoids confusion and ambiguity and will gain you marks.

- Occasionally an exam question might make a **statement** or even include a **quotation** from a well-known individual or from a more theoretical origin. Candidates should be able to express their own view and be able to compose and state a satisfactory response. For example, Warren Buffett once famously said: _'Derivatives are financial weapons of mass destruction.'_ If you had to discuss that statement you would need to be able to present both sides of the argument and draw your own conclusion. Another example might be to express a view on the UK's approach to changing the regulatory landscape post-Brexit. You need to understand what this means and to have your own view about to what extent you agree with the statement.

- Many questions will focus on **topical issues**; you are, therefore, advised to read the financial press in order to keep up to date with major developments from the compliance angle.

## 15. Risk Management and Controls

Risk management is a key issue in regulation and compliance. In the exam, whenever you are faced with a question about risk, always contemplate how the risk can be controlled. The examiner may well ask you to identify risks and to put forward suggestions on how the risks can be mitigated and managed. This is symptomatic of real business life, where a compliance manager will be requested to provide information to other senior management colleagues in a firm, in order to state the operational challenges, opportunities or difficulties with respect to a potential business development.

## 16. Answer Planning

**It is important for students to plan their answers**, and examiners expect this particularly in Sections B and C. It is not necessary for Section A.

The purpose of a plan is to coordinate your thoughts so that there is no need to repeat your thinking when executing the writing of the answers, which can be laborious for many people. None of us is much used to writing longhand for the best part of three hours. A simple example plan is suggested below to illustrate a possible approach. You might decide to draw up a plan using shapes or arrows or analyse the question using a mind map. Many other people just prefer to make a bulleted list of their thoughts. Develop your own method that works for you.

Whatever decision you take, it is essential to spend two or three minutes, with a maximum of, say, five minutes, at the start of the time allotted to marshal your thoughts and to set in your answer script a working document for reference during the exercise of writing out the answer. Leave it in the answer script, but separated from the body of your actual answer. Do not obliterate it, as it will be to your advantage if the examiner can browse the plan, but make sure it is evidently marked as your plan and does not appear to form part of your actual answer. It may often be the case that you will decide not to state certain facts that you had thought of in the plan and to concentrate instead on those points that you feel carry greater weight in the answer. Examiners like to see plans, as indicated, but do not like to see candidates present a good plan and then be unable to go on to write an answer that fulfils the expectations of the plan. Planning is a tool to help you to set out a well-structured answer and thus maximise your chances of doing well in the exam.

This is purely an example to show a possible approach to drawing up an answer plan. It is anonymous and the question upon which it is based is not particularly important. What matters is that the plan should be simple, concise and quick to execute. It is a set of thoughts about the question you are analysing. It helps you to think clearly and to record those thoughts. From that point onwards, the plan becomes a source of reference to guide you as you write your answer. It saves you having to repeat your thought processes and allows you to concentrate on the relatively more laborious task of actually writing your answer down.
