## 2. The Real Problem

The textbook is a near-copy of the FCA Handbook. 500 pages, size 9 font, no pictures. 3 pages of text might contain 2–3 sentences you actually need to memorise — but:

- **You don't know which sentences they are**
- **Reading dense regulatory prose is extremely difficult with dyslexia**
- **You can't tell what's high priority vs. filler**
- **The knowledge levels (D, G, O) are meaningless without context** — what does "general understanding of 4 pages of detailed concepts" actually look like?
- **The 500-page past paper document has all the answers to what gets tested** — but you don't have time to read it either
- **You shut the laptop early because 10 pages of dense A4 takes weeks** and it's overwhelming

This tool fixes all of that.

## 4. The Core Feature — Tiered Distillation

This is the centrepiece. The thing that makes it worth using every day.

When you tell the AI *"I'm working on section 8.3 Conduct Rules today"*, it reads that section of the textbook and gives you:

### Tier 1 — MUST KNOW (exam essentials)
> The 3–4 core concepts you need to memorise cold. The stuff that, if you don't know it, you'll lose marks. Short, clear, no waffle.

### Tier 2 — SHOULD KNOW (supporting detail)
> Extra context that strengthens your answers. The kind of detail that turns a pass answer into a strong answer. Important but not make-or-break.

### Tier 3 — AWARENESS ONLY (don't waste time)
> Background material the textbook includes but the examiner doesn't test in depth. Know the name, know it exists, move on. The tool explicitly tells you: "Don't spend time on this."

### + Past Paper Alert
> At the bottom of every distillation: how often this topic appears in past papers, which exam sections it shows up in (A, B, or C), and links to specific example questions with model answers.

**Example output when you say *"Summarise SYSC 10 for me"*:**

```
SYSC 10 — Conflicts of Interest                    [Knowledge: G | Ch2 — 40%]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MUST KNOW
• A conflict of interest arises when a firm's interests (or one client's
  interests) clash with a duty owed to another client
• Firms must identify, prevent, manage, or disclose conflicts — in that
  priority order (prevention > management > disclosure as last resort)
• Chinese walls (information barriers) are the primary organisational tool
  — restrict information flow between departments
• SYSC 10.1.8R: if arrangements are insufficient, the firm must disclose
  the conflict to the client before acting

SHOULD KNOW
• Types: firm vs client, client vs client, personal interests of staff
• Conflicts policy must be in writing and appropriate to firm size/complexity
• Inducements link: anything that could create a conflict must be assessed
  under COBS inducements rules (cross-ref COBS 2.3A)
• Senior management responsibility: a director/senior manager must oversee
  the conflicts policy

AWARENESS ONLY
• Historical background on why MiFID II strengthened conflicts rules
• Detailed organisational examples in large investment banks (textbook
  filler — know the principle, not the case studies)

PAST PAPER ALERT — TESTED FREQUENTLY
Conflicts of interest appear in 14 past paper questions across all 3 sections.
Common combinations: conflicts + Chinese walls + inducements + ethics.
→ See: B12, B14, B26 (scenario), A7.3–A7.5 (short answer), C3.4 (essay)
Compulsory Q11 has tested this theme in multiple sittings.
```

**This is what you get for every section you work on.** The AI reads the textbook pages, cross-references the past papers, and gives you this tiered output instead of making you wade through the raw text yourself.

## 5. D / G / O — What They Actually Mean

The syllabus labels every topic as D (Detailed), G (General), or O (Overview). The textbook doesn't explain what that means in practice. This tool does — by cross-referencing the labels against 333 past paper questions to show you what the examiner *actually expects* at each level.

| Level | Label | What the examiner expects | What the tool does |
|-------|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **D** | Detailed | You can explain the rule, apply it to a scenario, and discuss implications. This is essay and scenario territory. | Full Tier 1 + Tier 2 breakdown. Deep treatment. Flags all related past paper questions. |
| **G** | General | You can describe the main principles and explain why they matter. You don't need to quote rule numbers verbatim. | Focused Tier 1. Moderate Tier 2. Shows you what G-level answers look like from past paper model answers. |
| **O** | Overview | You can name it, say what it does in one sentence, and move on. The examiner will never ask you to go deep. | Very short Tier 1 only (definitions). Explicitly says: "Don't spend more than 5 minutes on this." |

**The key insight:** A G-level topic in a 40%-weight chapter matters more than a D-level topic in a 2.5%-weight chapter. The tool calibrates both the knowledge level AND the chapter weight together. SYSC 10 labelled "G" in Chapter 2 (40%) gets more attention than a "D" topic in Chapter 4 (2.5%).

## 6. Past Paper Intelligence

The 333 past paper questions are the single best predictor of what will be on your exam. The same topics come up every year. The tool mines this data and surfaces it everywhere.

**For every topic, the tool tells you:**

- How many past paper questions test this topic (across Sections A, B, and C)
- Whether it appears in compulsory questions (the B11 ethics question comes up every sitting)
- Specific question numbers you can look at for practice
- Common topic combinations the examiner uses (e.g., market abuse + insider dealing + DTR always appear together)

**Top 5 most-tested topics (by question count):**

| Topic | Questions | Sections |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| UK regulatory structure (FSMA, FCA/PRA, permissions) | 52 | A, B, C |
| High-level standards (PRIN, SYSC, SM&CR) | 26 | A, B, C |
| Conduct of business (COBS, suitability, client categorisation) | 21 | A, B |
| Financial promotions (COBS 4, s21) | 13 | A, B |
| Market conduct (MAR, insider dealing) | 10 | A, B, C |

**These five topic areas account for the majority of Section A questions.** The tool makes sure you know this before you start studying.

## 7. What Gets Built

Two phases. Phase 1 is the foundation (content). Phase 2 is the simple interface.

### Phase 1 — Knowledge Base (in progress now)

Your 500-page textbook and 500-page past paper document get split into **~77 small, focused files:**

| What | Count | Purpose |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Topic files** | ~38 | One per exam topic. Contains the textbook content, cleaned up, with metadata tags (exam weight, knowledge level, priority, related questions). The AI reads these to generate your tiered summaries. |
| **Question bank files** | ~29 | All 333 past paper questions, grouped by topic, paired with model answers. Split into Section A (12 files), Section B (10 files), Section C (7 files). |
| **Reference files** | 4 | Syllabus, exam technique, examiner commentary, glossary. |
| **AI tutor rules** | 4 | Invisible config files that make Cursor's AI behave like a specialist CISI compliance tutor — it automatically knows exam weights, answers from the compliance officer POV, gives tiered output, and flags past paper frequency. |
| **Index + README** | 2 | Master map of everything + how to use the tool. |

**Chapter breakdown (what's in each folder):**

| Folder | Ch | Exam Weight | Files | Priority |
|--------|----|-------------|-------|----------|
| `part-1-regulatory-framework/` | 1 | 20% | 4 | High |
| `part-2-fca-handbook/` | 2 | 40% | 17 | Highest |
| `part-3-other-regulatory/` | 3 | 20% | 12 | High |
| `part-4-markets-exchanges/` | 4 | 2.5% | 1 | Low — skim only |
| `part-5-current-developments/` | 5 | 2.5% | 1 | Low — skim for essay awareness |
| `risk-in-financial-services/` | 6 | 15% | 2 | High — underpins compulsory B question |

**95% of the exam = Chapters 1 + 2 + 3 + 6.** The tool knows this and actively steers you.

### Phase 2 — The Simple Interface

A lightweight local web app. One command to start (`python run.py`). Opens in your browser. No accounts, no cloud, no internet needed.

**The interface has four screens. That's it.**

---

#### Screen 1: Topic List

A clean, simple list of every topic grouped by chapter. Each topic shows:

- Topic name
- Knowledge level badge (D / G / O)
- Chapter exam weight
- A checkbox: "Covered" (tick it when you've been through it)

No complex dashboards. No graphs. No analytics. Just a list you can scan and tick off as you go.

Optional: a "flagged for review" toggle if you want to mark something to come back to.

---

#### Screen 2: Study View

Click any topic in the list. You see:

1. **The distilled summary** — Tier 1 (MUST KNOW), Tier 2 (SHOULD KNOW), Tier 3 (AWARENESS ONLY)
2. **Past paper alert** — how often this topic is tested, with links to specific questions
3. **Your notes** — a text box where you jot down anything that clicked. Saves automatically.
4. **"Copy to AI Chat" button** — copies the topic content + your notes into a prompt you paste into Cursor Agent chat for a focused AI session

This is where you spend most of your time during content coverage (now through end of April).

---

#### Screen 3: Flashcards

For your May memorisation phase. Matches your existing study method (cover content → make flashcards → drill).

- **Create cards** manually — type a question and answer, tag it to a topic
- **Bulk create** — the tool gives you a prompt to paste into the AI. The AI generates flashcards from a topic's content. You paste the output back and the tool stores them.
- **Review mode** — question on screen → click to reveal answer → rate: Again / Hard / Good / Easy
- **Spaced repetition** — cards you get right appear less often. Cards you get wrong come back the same session. The tool always knows which cards are due today.
- **Filter by topic** — review cards for a specific section, chapter, or all flagged topics

---

#### Screen 4: Exam Practice

For your May practice paper phase. Timed practice in the exact exam format.

| Mode | Timer | What You Do |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Section A** | 4 min per question | Short-answer question appears. Write your answer. Reveal model answer. Self-mark. |
| **Section B** | 32 min per question | Scenario question with sub-parts. Answer scaffold provided (Issue → Rule → Application → Conclusion → Ethical Dimension). Write answer. Compare to model. |
| **Section C** | 25 min per question | Essay question. Scaffold provided (Intro → For → Against → Conclusion). Write answer. Compare to model. |
| **Full Mock** | 3h 15m | All three sections back-to-back. Time splits shown on screen. |

All your practice answers are saved so you can look back at them.

The tool knows that **all essay answers should be written from the POV of a compliance officer** (per your lecturer) and the AI will coach you on this.

---

#### Search

A search bar available on every screen. Type any statutory reference ("FSMA s.19", "COBS 9A", "SYSC 6.1") and find it instantly across all topic files, past papers, and your notes.

## 8. Current Regulatory Developments (Chapter 5)

The syllabus says you need to keep up with the FT, Economist, and FCA website. The textbook covers some of this, but it's a moving target.

**What the tool does:**

- Analyses past papers to identify what *types* of current development questions actually come up (regulatory reform, fintech, ESG — not random commodity prices)
- Focuses only on big-picture compliance and regulatory developments (FCA enforcement actions, new BNPL regulation, Edinburgh Reforms, crypto regulation) — not market news, not trading, not international economics
- Tags which developments are likely essay material for Section C
- Keeps it brief — this is 2.5% of the exam. You get a concise list of "things worth knowing about," not a news feed

## 9. The AI Tutor — What It Knows

Four invisible rule files configure Cursor's AI to behave as your personal compliance tutor. You don't set anything up — it works automatically when you open Agent chat in this project.

**The AI automatically:**

| Behaviour | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| Answers from the POV of a compliance officer | Your lecturer said so. All essay answers should take this perspective. |
| Gives tiered output (MUST KNOW / SHOULD KNOW / AWARENESS ONLY) | Because 3 pages of text with 2 key sentences is no use to you. |
| Tells you past paper frequency for every topic it explains | So you know if you're studying something that gets tested or something that never comes up. |
| Steers you away from low-weight chapters | If you ask about Ch4 or Ch5, it gives a concise answer and says: "This is 2.5% — know the definition and move on." |
| Goes deep on high-weight chapters | Ch2 (40%) gets thorough treatment. Ch6 (15%) gets priority. |
| Calibrates D/G/O against exam weight | A D-level topic in a 2.5% chapter gets less time than a G-level topic in a 40% chapter. |
| Cites specific files and past paper questions | Never makes things up. References the workbook and past papers by section. |
| Suggests practice questions after explaining a topic | "Now try these: A3.12, B14, C2.1" |
| Knows the exam format and technique | Section A = concise factual. Section B = IRAC + ethics. Section C = discursive essay from compliance officer POV. |

## 10. How You'll Use It — Fitted to Your Method

This is built around how you actually study, not how a study app thinks you should study.

### Now → End of April: Content Coverage Phase

Your goal: cover all the content and make notes on the core concepts.

**Daily workflow:**

1. Open Cursor. Open the chat panel.
2. Tell the AI what you're working on today:

> *"I'm working on section 8.3 Conduct Rules today. Give me the key points."*

3. The AI reads that section and gives you the three-tier breakdown: MUST KNOW, SHOULD KNOW, AWARENESS ONLY — plus past paper frequency.
4. You read the Tier 1 points. Make your handwritten notes from those. Dig into Tier 2 if needed.
5. If something doesn't make sense, ask follow-up questions:

> *"Explain what 'prescribed responsibilities' means under SM&CR. Give me an example."*

6. When you've got it, move on to the next section:

> *"Moving on to section 8.4 now. Summarise this for me."*

7. Tick it off in the topic list.

### May: Memorisation + Practice Phase

Your goal: memorise key concepts, make flashcards, hammer past papers.

**Daily workflow:**

1. Open the tool (`python run.py`). Go to Flashcards.
2. Review any cards that are due today.
3. Create new flashcards for topics you covered in April (use the bulk-create feature via AI).
4. Go to Exam Practice. Pick a section. Start the timer. Write your answer. Compare to the model answer.
5. Use the AI to mark your practice answers and get feedback:

> *"Here's my answer to B14. Mark it against the model answer and tell me what I missed."*
